**Core Message:** Criterium racers systematically overtrain anaerobically and undertrain aerobically, and this imbalance directly costs them sprint performance and race-finishing ability. Recovery of anaerobic capacity between race efforts is predominantly an aerobic process; neglecting aerobic base is self-defeating.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Build a strong aerobic base first; aerobic fitness directly supports repeat-sprint recovery in crits
- Limit dedicated anaerobic (above-VO2max) training to once per week or less during the base phase; in-season, reduce anaerobic training further ("less is more")
- Do not treat every training session as an opportunity to do max-effort sprints
- Once the race season starts, in-race efforts replace most dedicated anaerobic training; separate anaerobic sessions become redundant or counterproductive
- Aerobically-trained sprinters gain ~0.8 W/min through a crit (fatigue resistance); aerobically undertrained sprinters lose ~0.9 W/min — a real-world ~1.7 W/min spread across the field
- Creatine phosphate regeneration between efforts is largely a mitochondrial (aerobic) process — aerobic base directly refills the anaerobic "tank"

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- Data on fatigue curves through crits comes from Kolie's PhD thesis work; not yet published in peer-reviewed form at time of recording
- Effect size of aerobic base on sprint recovery is athlete-specific; elite sprinters with already-high aerobic fitness may not see the same marginal gains

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Doing more anaerobic training for crit success is a self-defeating strategy: it comes at the cost of aerobic base, which is what actually lets you repeat sprints
- ~80% of the energy to recover from a sprint comes from aerobic metabolism — not from storing more anaerobic capacity
- In-season, a crit racer is already doing more anaerobic work in races than is optimal for adaptation; adding training on top is likely harmful

**Evolving Views:**
- None explicitly stated; Kolie presents this as a settled conclusion from his research

**Core Message:** The best workout is sometimes not working out. Rest and recovery are not passive — they are active training inputs that drive adaptation. Chronic under-recovery is widespread among self-coached athletes and leads to plateaus and injuries.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Treat rest days and recovery weeks as intentional training decisions, not as failures or lost time
- Adam Pulford (CTS coach): build rest into the plan explicitly rather than waiting until the athlete is broken
- Recovery is where adaptation actually occurs; the training stimulus is only one side of the equation
- Identify whether fatigue is physical or psychological — each requires a different response
- Do not rely solely on objective metrics (power, HR) to assess recovery; subjective feel is valid and important
- When in doubt about whether to train or rest, defaulting to rest is rarely the wrong choice for recreational athletes
- CTS philosophy: periodized recovery, not just periodized training

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- Adam Pulford's perspective reflects CTS coaching philosophy; other coaches may structure recovery differently
- Individual recovery rates vary widely; there is no one-size-fits-all rest week frequency
- Athletes coming from high-volume backgrounds may need longer adjustment periods before they can trust rest as productive

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- "The best workout is not working out" — counterintuitive framing that recovery is a training act, not an absence of training
- Many athletes who feel undertrained are actually under-recovered; adding more volume is exactly the wrong response
- Psychological fatigue is as real an obstacle to adaptation as physical fatigue and requires rest, not "pushing through"

**Evolving Views:**
- Adam Pulford notes that the coaching profession has shifted toward taking recovery more seriously over the past decade; earlier periodization models undervalued it

### Episode 3 of 17

**File:** `1363710067-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-24-you-are-not-your-ctl.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1363710067
**Word count:** ~8,560
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

**Core Message:** CTL (Chronic Training Load) is a measure of training stress, not fitness. Using CTL as a proxy for fitness or as a training target leads to maladaptive behavior: chasing the number rather than the adaptation, and conflating accumulated fatigue with readiness to perform.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Do not set CTL as a training goal; set performance goals (power targets, event readiness) and let CTL be a byproduct
- A rising CTL can coexist with declining performance if recovery is insufficient — the number hides overtraining
- Rest and recovery improve performance but will temporarily drop CTL; this is correct and desirable
- Use CTL as a rough descriptive tool, not a prescriptive one
- Assess fitness through actual performance tests and subjective readiness, not through CTL trajectory

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- CTL has legitimate uses as a retrospective load-monitoring tool; the criticism is about misuse as a fitness target
- TSS/CTL models (Banister impulse-response) are simplified approximations of a complex physiological reality

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- A well-rested athlete performing better than a fatigued athlete with a higher CTL demonstrates that CTL does not equal fitness — yet many athletes chase CTL as though it does
- Taking a rest week while watching your CTL drop is psychologically difficult but physiologically correct; the discomfort is a measurement artifact, not a real fitness loss
- CTL can increase while an athlete is becoming overtrained — the metric is not just imprecise, it can actively mislead

**Evolving Views:**
- Implicit: the hosts suggest early adopters of TrainingPeaks-style analytics over-weighted CTL; the field has matured but the misuse remains widespread

### Episode 4 of 17

**File:** `1594046289-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-27-common-reasons-for-fitness-plateaus-and-their-fixes-with-rory-porteous.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1594046289
**Word count:** ~24,969
**Series:** Perspectives

**Core Message:** Fitness plateaus are almost always caused by one of a small number of correctable errors: insufficient recovery, wrong training intensities, failure to progress load, or unmanaged off-bike stress. Rory Porteous reviews these systematically with fixes.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Recovery ride maximum power should be genuinely below endurance pace — sub-100W for most athletes, not "easy endurance"
- Off-bike stressors (work, family, sleep debt) count as training load; a stressful day without riding still accumulates fatigue
- Do not do heavy lifting on recovery days; if strength training, schedule it on hard days
- Sleep is the #1 recovery intervention — prioritize over all supplements, gadgets, or protocols
- Eat more on rest days, not less; energy restriction on recovery days impairs adaptation
- Standard periodization structure: 3 weeks building, 1 week recovery; adjust to 2:1 if plateauing
- If plateauing, audit training intensity distribution first — many athletes cluster too much work in the "moderate" zone
- Fix recovery before adding volume; adding volume to an under-recovered state compounds the problem

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- Rory acknowledges sub-100W recovery ride targets are athlete-specific (based on FTP); the principle is "below your individual endurance floor"
- 3:1 periodization block structure is a starting heuristic, not a universal law; some athletes do better with 2:1 or 4:1
- Off-bike stress is hard to quantify; use subjective ratings rather than trying to assign TSS to life stress

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Eating more on rest days (not less) is counterintuitive but physiologically correct; muscle protein synthesis and glycogen replenishment are active processes requiring fuel
- A "recovery ride" done at endurance pace is not a recovery ride — it's a low-stimulus training ride; genuine recovery requires near-zero power output
- Many athletes interpret plateau as a signal to train harder; it is more often a signal to recover better

**Evolving Views:**
- Rory notes that recovery ride intensity guidance has become more conservative over time as coaches have recognized how much even "easy" riding accumulates stress

**File:** `1675129809-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-31-how-to-take-a-rest-day-and-how-to-avoid-screwing-it-up.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1675129809
**Word count:** ~21,814
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

**Core Message:** Rest days are frequently sabotaged by well-intentioned actions: "active recovery" rides that are too hard, heavy lifting, stress that carries physiological consequences, and under-fueling. A true rest day requires deliberate restraint across all domains.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- "Glass cranks" mental model: on a rest day, imagine your cranks are made of glass — any real pedaling force would break them. If you can't ride that gently, don't ride
- Do not sprint or lift heavy on recovery days — these are high-stress stimuli that prevent recovery
- Off-bike stressors (work deadlines, difficult conversations, poor sleep) are physiologically equivalent to moderate training load; account for them when assessing recovery
- Openers workouts (pre-race activation) are only appropriate for well-rested athletes; if you're fatigued, skip openers and just rest before the race
- Start a rest week early if accumulated fatigue signs appear before the scheduled rest week — do not wait for the calendar
- Recovery day ≠ a day you need to recover from; it is a day structured to maximize recovery from prior training

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- "Glass cranks" is a heuristic, not a physiological prescription; some athletes may benefit from very short, genuinely easy spins
- Pre-race openers recommendations vary by athlete; the principle is that they only help if underlying fatigue is low

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Many athletes believe an active recovery ride "flushes" lactate and accelerates recovery; the evidence for this mechanism is weak, and riding too hard on recovery days likely extends the recovery window
- Doing openers when fatigued can make race performance worse, not better — the pre-race ritual creates false confidence while adding stress
- A stressful workday without any riding should count against your training load budget; it is not "free" recovery time

**Evolving Views:**
- Hosts note increasing awareness among athletes that "more is more" does not apply to recovery; the field is moving toward more deliberate rest structures

**File:** `1799552662-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-37-pacing-and-programming-endurance-rides.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1799552662
**Word count:** ~23,033
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

**Core Message:** Endurance rides are frequently paced too hard, which accumulates disproportionate fatigue and pushes athletes above LT1 without the intensity needed to drive VO2max or FTP adaptations. Proper endurance ride pacing requires managing LT1 as a ceiling, not a target.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Target 60–80% of LT1 power for most of a long endurance ride; do not ride at LT1 for extended periods
- For a rider with a 350W LT1, this means riding at approximately 200–250W for the bulk of long rides
- LT1 drops roughly 10W after approximately 2.5 hours of endurance riding (based on Sprague study data); plan pacing strategy to account for this drift
- Fatigue accumulation is non-linear above LT1 — even 10W over LT1 for extended periods carries a disproportionate cost
- Plan interval training days first when building a training week, then fill remaining days with endurance; endurance rides are the flexible element
- Can ride 20–30 hours per week without overtraining if all rides are properly paced below LT1 ceiling
- FTP-range intervals placed within longer endurance rides can improve adaptation efficiency

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- LT1 is individual and requires testing to establish; using FTP percentages as a proxy for LT1 introduces error
- The 10W LT1 drop after 2.5 hours is a population-level finding; individual variation is substantial
- 20–30 h/week pacing guidance applies to athletes with established aerobic bases; beginners cannot sustain this volume safely

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Many athletes think riding "just at" LT1 optimizes endurance adaptations; the data suggest riding well below LT1 for most of the ride produces better outcomes with less fatigue cost
- Endurance rides paced at "comfortable" feel for trained athletes often exceed LT1 — feeling easy is not sufficient to confirm sub-LT1 pacing

**Evolving Views:**
- Rory Porteous notes that LT1-anchored endurance pacing has become more central to his coaching as he has seen how many athletes chronically over-pace long rides

**Core Message:** Race season training requires deliberately stepping back from hard training. A/B/C race prioritization, using B races as training, and scheduling mid-season breaks are essential to sustain performance across a long race calendar without accumulating unmanageable fatigue.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Assign every race A/B/C priority before the season; structure recovery and peak around A races only
- Use B races as training races: race hard, but don't taper or recover around them the way you would for A races
- C races are low-priority; may not warrant any change to training schedule around them
- Mid-season break (1–2 weeks of reduced load) is non-negotiable for athletes with dense race schedules; treat it as part of the periodization plan
- Easy volume (sub-LT1 endurance) maintains VO2max through race season even when hard training is reduced
- In-season rule of thumb: never pedal harder than feels good during a training ride; save maximal efforts for races
- Use sprint power as a fatigue indicator: if your normal opener sprint (e.g., 1,600–1,700W) is coming in at 1,500W, that's a signal to reduce load before the next race
- TTE (time to exhaustion at FTP) and FTP "feel" (whether FTP pace feels hard or manageable) are useful in-season fatigue indicators

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- Sprint power benchmarks are individual; establish a personal baseline during a well-rested period to use as a reference
- "Never pedal harder than feels good" is a heuristic for in-season training days, not a prescription to avoid all intensity — races are excluded
- Mid-season break timing should be based on schedule density and accumulated fatigue signs, not a fixed calendar date

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Most cyclists do too much hard training during race season, then wonder why they peak too early or fade in the second half; in-season training should be less hard than off-season training, not more
- Easy volume during race season is not "doing nothing" — it actively maintains aerobic base that would otherwise erode from racing stress alone

**Evolving Views:**
- Rory Porteous notes that athletes he coaches who resist mid-season breaks consistently underperform in late-season A races compared to those who take them

**File:** `1889036004-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-41-how-to-try-new-training-methods.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1889036004
**Word count:** ~16,717
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

**Core Message:** Athletes contemplating a new training method should conduct a gap analysis to determine whether the method actually addresses their specific limiter. If current training is working, don't change it. When adopting a new method, introduce it gradually rather than replacing everything at once — and always plan rest before stacking new stimuli.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Before adopting a new training method, identify the specific gap it is meant to close (e.g., "I want higher VO2max; this VO2max block protocol targets that")
- If current training is producing gains, do not change it; novelty is not a reason to disrupt a working plan
- Introduce new methods incrementally: for VO2max blocks, start with once per week for 1–3 weeks before increasing frequency
- Plan rest periods first when restructuring training around a new method; then plan hard work around those rest periods
- Plan for what could go wrong (illness, travel, work stress) and have a fallback structure ready
- Maintain the mindset that "the goal is not to see how much hard training you can get away with" — the goal is the minimum effective dose to produce the desired adaptation

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- Gap analysis requires honest self-assessment or a coach's external perspective; athletes frequently misidentify their limiters
- "If it's working, don't change it" applies at the program level; individual sessions should still be varied and progressive

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Chasing novelty in training methods (new intervals, new periodization schemes) when current training is effective is a form of self-sabotage — it resets adaptation progress
- The "all or nothing" trap: many athletes either stick rigidly to a failing plan or scrap everything when trying something new; the correct approach is partial integration
- Planning rest first and hard work second inverts the usual athlete approach (plan max hard work, rest when forced) and produces better long-term adaptation

**Evolving Views:**
- Rory notes that his coaching philosophy has shifted toward explicitly prescribing rest and recovery before prescribing hard work in any restructured training plan

**Core Message:** Sweet spot training (~90% FTP) is time-efficient and produces broad adaptations, but it is frequently over-used as a sole training method. It works best as a supplement to a full intensity distribution, not as the primary stimulus. Its high cumulative fatigue cost is underappreciated.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Sweet spot is defined as approximately 90% of FTP (not a range; a specific intensity anchor)
- Untrained athletes can typically sustain sweet spot for 40–60 minutes per session; well-trained athletes 1.5–2 hours
- Maximum effective sweet spot dose per session: approximately 2–3 hours (beyond this, fatigue cost outweighs marginal adaptation benefit)
- Over-under intervals (alternating just below and just above FTP) are an effective way to get sweet spot stimulus with more structure
- Use sweet spot as an add-on to a full training plan or as a maintenance intensity, not as the primary FTP-building tool
- Sweet spot is not time-efficient compared to other intensities when the full fatigue recovery cost is accounted for

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- James McKay notes sweet spot definitions vary across coaches and platforms (some use 88–93%, some use "tempo to threshold"); the 90% anchor is the hosts' preferred definition
- Sweet spot's relative value depends on training age and current fitness level; it is more appropriate for building base in newer athletes than for advanced athletes with established aerobic foundations

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Sweet spot is not actually time-efficient when recovery cost is factored in; the perception of efficiency comes from comparing it to low-intensity work, ignoring the recovery tax
- Many popular training plans built almost entirely on sweet spot produce good initial gains then plateau, because sweet spot does not develop either the aerobic floor (LT1) or the aerobic ceiling (VO2max) optimally
- Over-relying on sweet spot creates athletes who are "moderately good at everything and excellent at nothing"

**Evolving Views:**
- Hosts note that sweet spot became popular partly because structured training platforms (Zwift, TrainerRoad) can easily prescribe and track it; its dominance in online training plans reflects platform affordances more than optimal physiology

**Core Message:** A well-structured 12-week training plan has three blocks with increasing specificity, a defined rest week structure, and explicit VO2max block design principles. The thought experiment reveals what "simplest effective plan" actually requires.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- **12-week plan structure:**
  - Block 1 (weeks 1–4): Sweet spot progression — start at 60 min sweet spot per session, build to 110 min; establish FTP baseline
  - Block 2 (weeks 5–8): VO2max development with FTP maintenance sessions
  - Block 3 (weeks 9–12): Race prep — add anaerobic work while maintaining aerobic base; reduce sweet spot volume
- **Rest week structure:** Day off → recovery ride → endurance ride → openers workout → long endurance ride; rest week is not total inactivity

  - Target: accumulate 15 minutes at the physiological state associated with pVO2max (not necessarily at a fixed wattage)
  - 4-minute intervals are preferred for this goal
  - Accept up to 20W power decline across the interval set; larger drops indicate session is too long or too hard
  - Start VO2max work once per week for the first 1–3 weeks
- Build each block progressively; do not start Block 2 at the intensity level Block 1 ended at

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- The "simplest plan" framing is a thought experiment, not a universal prescription; athlete history and limiters should modify the template
- Rory emphasizes this is a starting structure; real athletes need more individualization especially in Block 3
- 20W decline threshold for VO2max intervals is a practical guideline, not a hard cutoff

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Structuring a training plan requires deciding rest week format before deciding hard workout format; most athletes plan workouts first and rest "fills in"
- A VO2max block that accumulates total time at a physiological state is more principled than prescribing fixed wattage; the wattage is a means to an end

**Evolving Views:**
- Rory Porteous notes that the "simplest plan" framing emerged from coaching athletes who had over-complicated their training; simplification itself is a form of progress

**Numeric ID:** 2061437404
**Word count:** ~14,537
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

**Core Message:** Intermediate cyclists (2–5 years of structured training, 10–30W/year FTP gain range) plateau not because they've hit biological limits but because they repeat the same plan, neglect recovery, or fail to develop training skills (pacing, progressive overload, readiness monitoring).

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Do not repeat the same annual training plan year after year; adaptation requires novel stimulus, and a plan that worked once provides diminishing returns
- Recovery is a major differentiator at the intermediate level — athletes who prioritize recovery advance faster than those who add volume or intensity
- Develop a "training skillset": learn to recognize early signs of overreaching (performance decline, motivation loss, sleep disruption) before they become overtraining
- Practice progressive overload deliberately: each training block should make measurable demands on the athlete that exceed the prior block
- Monitor whether training is producing the expected changes; if not, diagnose before proceeding
- Build derailment recovery as a skill: if illness, travel, or life disrupts training, have a protocol for returning to load safely rather than trying to "catch up"
- Intermediate definition: 2–5 years of structured training; FTP gains of 10–30W/year are normal at this stage

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- FTP gain rates are rough population benchmarks; individual variation is large, and gains depend on training quality, not just years
- "Derailment recovery" is a skill that takes time to develop; athletes early in their intermediate phase will make mistakes here

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Many intermediate athletes interpret slowing gains as needing more training; they actually need better training structure and more recovery
- Repeating a plan that worked in year 1 in year 3 is a mistake even if the plan was excellent; the athlete has adapted to it, and novelty itself drives adaptation
- Training skills (pacing, readiness assessment, overreaching detection) are undervalued relative to physical attributes; improving these skills often produces more gains than improving watts

**Evolving Views:**
- Rory Porteous notes that "training age" is a more useful concept than chronological age or CTL for diagnosing intermediate plateau issues

- HRV is a useful daily readiness signal but is inconsistent across individuals and measurement conditions; do not use it as the sole decision trigger for rest

- Sprint power as a readiness test requires an established personal baseline and consistent testing conditions (same warm-up, same protocol)

- The host "Fabiano" is the primary voice here alongside Kolie; specific coaching affiliations are not stated

- By the time most athletes feel they need a rest week, they already needed one a week ago; the subjective signal lags the physiological state by days
- Sprint power (a 5–10 second effort) is more sensitive to CNS fatigue than FTP-based tests and therefore a better early warning signal for accumulated fatigue
- Easy volume (recovery rides) can delay recovery rather than accelerate it when the nervous system is already suppressed — "active recovery" for a truly fatigued athlete may lengthen the hole

**Core Message:** Psychological barriers to rest are as significant as physiological ones. Fear of losing fitness, fear of weight gain, and social media comparison pressure cause athletes to under-rest. These fears are largely unfounded over rest week timescales, and acting on them sabotages adaptation.

**Core Message:** Auto-regulation ("vibes training") is a valuable skill that complements structured training, not a replacement for it. The best results come from blending structure with flexibility — knowing your priorities, using objective metrics to gauge readiness, and adjusting effort based on recovery status, life stress, and motivation. Most athletes benefit from 25–75% auto-regulated training depending on experience, schedule predictability, and self-awareness.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Maintain a prioritized list of training goals for the week/block (e.g., primary: FTP/TTE, secondary: sprint, tertiary: optional) so that when you auto-regulate, you know what to do first on good-legs days
- Use short FTP efforts (3–5 minutes) on days 3–5 after a hard block to objectively gauge recovery — if it feels easy and numbers are normal, you're ready; if you're counting down the seconds, rest more
- Use short sprints as a low-fatigue-cost readiness check — monitor peak power and how "snappy" the neural drive feels; a foreign-feeling motor pattern signals you need more rest
- When doing less structured workouts, still target a specific energy system per session — avoid defaulting to "kitchen sink" group-ride-style efforts that train everything shallowly
- For threshold work outdoors, aim for total time at FTP (e.g., "60 minutes at FTP, 10-minute minimum intervals") rather than rigid interval prescriptions — the physiological stimulus is effectively equivalent
- Accept ±5–10 watts of target power outdoors as "close enough"; don't expect erg-mode precision
- When RPE creeps up during threshold intervals, maintain power and set an absolute RPE ceiling (e.g., 8/10) rather than chasing a heart rate zone
- For VO2max efforts, breathing and perceived effort are the primary regulators; expect power to drop slightly across reps but keep pushing hard
- For anaerobic/sprint efforts, stop the session when you can no longer push hard — quality of effort matters more than completing all prescribed reps
- If in doubt about whether to train, swing a leg over the bike and assess during warm-up — but distinguish rest inertia from genuine fatigue
- When life stress is high, you likely have only one day of truly good legs — deploy them strategically (e.g., save for a key Saturday session rather than burning them mid-week)
- Undercooking training intensity by 5–20% is far better than overcooking by 10%; the recovery hole from overcooking is disproportionately deep
- When coming back from illness, set the bar low and rack up a string of small successes rather than ramping aggressively
- Track mood and motivation as key metrics — declining motivation often signals overreaching or under-fueling before performance metrics show it

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- Auto-regulation works best for athletes with deep training history who have calibrated their sense of effort; inexperienced athletes may misjudge recovery needs
- Highly motivated athletes risk doing too much on vibes; low-motivation athletes risk doing too little — both extremes need guardrails
- People with highly unpredictable schedules (medical professionals, shift workers, new parents) may benefit most from vibes-based training, but also need the most discipline around recovery
- Training camp vibes (e.g., riding hard in Spain for a week) are fine, but athletes with upcoming races need guardrails on intensity, duration, and altitude to control fatigue
- The "ride easy a lot, sometimes hard" heuristic is too vague to evaluate without knowing what "a lot" and "sometimes" mean for that individual

**Contrarian/Non-Obvious Claims:**
- Structured plan adherence of 100% is often suboptimal — some degree of auto-regulation typically produces better outcomes than rigid compliance
- Many athletes are shocked at how little structured training is needed for meaningful long-term progress; the minimum effective dose is lower than most expect
- The closer you are to race day, the more seriously you should take signs of fatigue (needing to drop power 5–10%) — the same signal in base phase barely matters
- Fitness gained months ago persists surprisingly well through high-stress periods as long as you maintain some riding; people rarely lose as much as they fear
- Tim Cusick's suggestion (referenced from earlier podcast): getting a coach for 1–3 years to learn the training rhythm before self-coaching is more effective than starting solo
- Skipping a hard workout almost never costs meaningful fitness, but forcing one when under-recovered frequently does — "when in doubt, sit it out"
