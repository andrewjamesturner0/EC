### Core Message
Kolie Moore reverse-engineered popular science-based hydration drink mixes and provides a DIY recipe using basic chemistry. The key principle is leveraging dual sugar transporters (glucose and fructose) in the gut to maximize water absorption, while keeping the solution hypotonic (around 4-5%). Sodium citrate is used instead of sodium chloride (table salt) because it is far more palatable during exercise. The recipe is customizable based on individual sweat rate, salt loss, taste preferences, and weather conditions.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Keep the drink solution at approximately 4% concentration (4 grams per 100 mL of water); the recipe ends up around 5% depending on salt content
- Use both dextrose and sucrose to take advantage of multiple gut sugar transporters, which also helps water absorption piggyback on sugar absorption
- Use sodium citrate instead of sodium chloride (table salt) as the primary sodium source -- it is "absolutely delicious" compared to unpalatable table salt during rides
- Most people lose 0.5 to 1.5 grams of sodium per liter of sweat
- Calcium loss is low priority at only 50-100 mg per liter of sweat; potassium can be skipped entirely if desired
- If you are a salty sweater (caked in salt after rides, sweat burns eyes), consider nearly doubling the sodium citrate amount
- Replace chloride through solid food (e.g., salted boiled potatoes) rather than trying to put it in the drink
- In hot weather, increase water to 550-700 mL per serving (vs. the baseline 500 mL) to keep the solution dilute
- In cold weather, reduce water to 400-450 mL per serving
- Grind freeze-dried fruit in a food processor while keeping it cold to prevent coating; store dry at room temperature
- Citric acid should be started at lower amounts and increased gradually; it is easy to overboard on
- Ascorbic acid (vitamin C) controls the "dryness" of the drink; skip it if you do not like that quality
- Skip coconut water -- it has too much potassium and not enough sodium for exercise hydration

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Individual sweat salt content varies based on heat adaptation and genetics; salty sweaters will "probably always be pretty salty sweaters"
- The ingredient source (Modernist Pantry) is explicitly noted as NOT a reputable NSF-tested facility; athletes subject to drug testing should source from WADA-compliant tested suppliers
- The body "craving what it needs" is presented as a useful but imperfect indicator ("we can't directly rely on this, but it's a pretty good indicator")

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Sodium chloride (table salt) is actually fairly unpalatable during exercise; sodium citrate is a superior form for drink mixes despite being less commonly used in commercial products
- Coconut water is not a good exercise hydration choice despite its health halo -- it has too much potassium and insufficient sodium
- Replacing chloride is better done through solid food than through the drink

### Evolving Views
None identified in this episode.

## Episode: 1258345612-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-12-a-deep-dive-into-ride-food-with-namrita-brooke.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1258345612
**Word count:** 16273
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Kolie Moore interviews nutritionist Namrita Brooke (PhD in carbohydrate/caffeine effects on fatigue) for a wide-ranging discussion on ride nutrition. The central thesis is that ride nutrition cannot be separated from the rest of the day's eating -- each ride is part of a continuous system, and fueling/recovery decisions compound across days. Gut training is real and trainable but must be approached gradually. Carbohydrate intake recommendations (60-120 g/hr) are not strongly dependent on body size but rather on training status, exercise intensity, and individual tolerance, and should be scaled based on what you are doing the next day.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Carbohydrate intake ranges by context:**
  - Low-intensity endurance rides: 30-60 grams of carbs per hour
  - Moderate intensity (tempo/sweet spot/threshold): at least 60 grams of carbs per hour, possibly more
  - Racing or high training load: 80-120 grams of carbs per hour, as tolerable
  - Short intense efforts (VO2max intervals, time trials, cyclocross): no intake needed during the effort itself if starting with full glycogen stores and adequate hydration; fuel before and after
- **Scale intake UP when you have a hard workout or race the next day**, even on easy days -- treat each day's fueling as preparation for the next
- **Daily carbohydrate targets:** 8-10 g/kg/day is "carb adequate"; high-end/high-volume athletes may need 10-12 g/kg/day
- **Carb loading protocol:** 10-12 g/kg/day for 3-5 days before a race if coming from a low-carb diet; less time needed if already eating carb-adequate
- **Emergency glycogen recovery:** if severely depleted, target 12 g/kg/day for the rest of that day
- **Glucose-to-fructose ratio:** earlier research suggested 2:1; newer research suggests 1:0.8 (glucose/maltodextrin to fructose) is more GI-tolerable
- **Above 60 g/hr requires multiple transportable carbs** -- straight glucose maxes out at ~1 g/min (~60 g/hr) oxidation rate
- **Gut training is real and multi-faceted:** it involves upregulating gut transporters (through consistent high-carb diet, ~8 g/kg/day), training gastric emptying (through eating bigger meals before training, increasing fluid rates during training), and must be done gradually
- **Gastric emptying tips:** larger boluses of fluid speed gastric emptying; fat and fiber slow it; heat and high exercise intensity slow it
- **Gels require significant water** to avoid GI distress -- "nearly half a bottle" of water per gel to dilute the hypertonic solution
- **Choose hypotonic drinks** over hypertonic ones for combining hydration with calorie intake; osmolality matters more than people realize -- all particles (electrolytes, amino acids, sugars) contribute
- **Early morning intense rides:** do NOT eat 20 minutes before the ride (risk of rebound hypoglycemia from insulin spike); instead eat immediately before or within 5 minutes of starting. Take 30 g carbs at start, another 30 g ~20 minutes later
- **Before-ride glycemic index strategy:** low glycemic meals well before the ride; high glycemic foods right before and during the ride
- **Solid food on the bike:** save potatoes, fruit, and whole foods for off-bike meals; for performance fueling use bars, drinks, chews, and gels because the carb density of whole foods is too low
- **For races, nibbling a bar over time is probably better than eating it all at once**, especially at high intensity
- **Bars:** choose oat/fruit-based bars (e.g., Clif bar) over nut/seed-heavy bars; focus on the ingredient list rather than exact fat/protein grams
- **Mountain bikers or athletes who cannot carry enough food:** use carbs in the drink (e.g., Scratch Super Fuel with 100 g carbs per serving); consider a streamlined hydration pack; weight penalty from carrying hydration is "inconsequential" compared to being fueled
- **Caffeine:** increases time to fatigue and improves cognitive performance after physical fatigue (Namrita's PhD finding); ~24/24 subjects in her study were responders; caffeine sensitivity is highly individual -- some people react to as little as 25-50 mg in a caffeinated gel
- **Candy as ride fuel (Haribo, Swedish fish):** the type of candy probably does not matter much; GI issues from candy are more likely due to insufficient fluid and sodium intake alongside concentrated sugars

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Nutrition is "so individual" -- tolerance varies by genetics, diet history, training status, and fitness level
- The 100-120 g/hr carbohydrate recommendation is based on trained elite male cyclists; less-trained athletes (e.g., ultra runners in one cited study) showed much lower oxidation rates at 90+ g/hr -- so the upper range is not universally applicable
- Carb oxidation capacity depends on training status, not body size or gender -- a well-trained 50 kg woman may need the same per-hour intake as a 100 kg man if training loads are comparable
- Rebound hypoglycemia from pre-ride eating "doesn't necessarily impact your performance" but does not feel good
- The Maurten hydrogel: the verdict is "still out" on whether it improves performance vs. non-hydrogel carbs; it "probably" helps with GI tolerance at very high intake rates (120 g/hr) but likely makes no difference at 30-60 g/hr
- Glycogen repletion from severe depletion takes 2-4 days, not overnight -- you cannot fix a bad fueling day with one big meal
- Transcript has minor corruption at line 677-678 (repeated "the" words) but content is otherwise intact

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Body size does NOT meaningfully change per-hour carbohydrate recommendations during racing -- training status and intensity matter more than weight (Namrita Brooke)
- The weight gained from carb loading (potentially 1+ kg from glycogen and water) is "not really a penalty" -- starting dehydrated or underfueled hurts performance far more than the extra weight (both hosts agree)
- Type 1 muscle fibers actually hypertrophy during a taper due to glycogen supercompensation and associated water storage (Kolie, referencing Andy Galpin citing a study on cross-country runners)
- You should eat MORE on easy days if you have a hard workout the next day -- the common approach of eating less on easy days is counterproductive (Namrita)
- "Training the gut" is not just about getting used to eating during rides -- it also involves eating a habitually high-carb diet (8+ g/kg/day) to keep gut transporters upregulated (Namrita)
- People who eat low-carb and then try to fuel hard during rides are fighting their own downregulated gut machinery (Namrita)

### Evolving Views
- Kolie acknowledges that he had not previously considered how much the NEXT day's training should influence TODAY's on-bike nutrition: "that's a perspective that I have not heard that much. And actually I haven't really considered that much myself."
- Namrita notes that the glucose:fructose ratio recommendation has shifted from the older 2:1 to a newer ~1:0.8, reflecting evolving research on GI tolerance.

## Episode: 1450793536-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-23-weight-loss-diets-for-endurance-performance-with-namrita-brooke.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1450793536
**Word count:** 20758
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Weight loss for endurance athletes should be approached through sustainable, moderate caloric deficits rather than fad diets, food rules, or aggressive restriction. Energy balance remains the fundamental mechanism of weight loss; hormones influence the desire to eat and move but do not override thermodynamics. The optimal strategy is to lose weight as slowly as possible while eating as many calories as possible, periodizing dieting to the off-season or base training phase and never during build or peak phases. Collaboration between a coach monitoring performance markers and a sports dietitian managing nutrition guidance produces the best outcomes, with the athlete's body feedback (RPE, sleep, mood, menstrual cycle) serving as critical guardrails against over-restriction.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Aim for a caloric deficit of only 200-250 calories per day when dieting; deficits over 500 calories/day caused lean mass loss even in resistance-trained subjects (Namrita, citing a resistance training study).
- Target a weight loss rate of 0.25-1.0 lb per week; up to 2 lbs/week only if starting body fat is very high. Namrita's rule of thumb: "Lose slowly eating as many calories as possible."
- Periodize dieting to the off-season or base training phase. Do not diet during build or peak phases. Kolie sometimes puts "no dieting" blocks (roughly 3-week periods) into Training Peaks for athletes.
- Carbohydrate intake for endurance athletes: 3-12 g/kg/day (total, including on-bike intake). This range is intensity- and volume-dependent.
- Protein intake for endurance athletes: 1.6-1.8 g/kg/day normally; increase to 2.0-2.4 g/kg/day when actively losing fat, when older, or both.
- Fat intake: minimum ~20% of total daily calories, or roughly 0.6-1.0 g/kg/day. Below this range, watch for suppressed sex drive, disrupted sleep, irritability, and hormonal issues.
- Distribute protein intake in roughly even doses throughout the day rather than concentrating it in one or two meals. Aim for 20-40 g protein after rides to support muscle protein synthesis.
- Fuel rides fully even while dieting. On-bike fueling is not the place to create deficits. Aim for 30-90 g carbs/hour depending on intensity, up to 90-100 g/hour for long or hard efforts. One example: a pro mountain biker went from ~3 gels total to 100 g/hour and reversed multi-day stage race fade.
- On-bike carb absorption is not body-weight dependent -- it depends on gut absorption capacity, intensity, and physiology, so the standard recommendations apply across body sizes.
- Use performance on the bike as a primary marker that a diet is too aggressive: if RPE rises relative to power, FTP effectively drops, or peak power declines during a small intended deficit, the athlete is over-restricting (Kolie).
- Create the deficit through a combination of slightly less food and slightly more movement, not exclusively from food restriction. Track NEAT (non-exercise activity thermogenesis) via daily step counts -- a declining step count while dieting signals the deficit is too large.
- To establish your maintenance calories: track food intake for a short period when weight has been stable. Use that average as the starting point, then reduce slightly.
- Use a BIA scale (bioelectrical impedance) to track trends in body composition over weeks/months rather than relying on bodyweight alone, which fluctuates with glycogen and water.
- After endurance exercise, the glycogen resynthesis window's first phase is insulin-independent. Athletes remain more insulin-sensitive for 12-48 hours post-exercise, making this an ideal window for carbohydrate intake (Namrita).
- For women: expect increased hunger and 2-12% higher resting energy expenditure in the luteal phase (post-ovulation, pre-menstruation). Do not fight this hunger -- fuel adequately to set up the early follicular phase for strong performance. Water retention of up to 5-7 lbs is normal in this phase.
- Track menstrual cycles. Loss of period is a late-stage indicator of chronic low energy availability -- by that point, significant harm (bone density loss, hormonal disruption) may already be occurring.
- Do not attempt recomposition (simultaneous fat loss + muscle gain) unless substantially untrained or very high body fat. For trained athletes, periodize these goals separately.
- High-intensity work (VO2max intervals, anaerobic capacity, sprints) is much harder to improve while in a caloric deficit because recovery time lengthens. Aerobic development is more compatible with moderate deficits.
- Work with a registered sports dietitian, not just a "nutritionist" (anyone can use that title). Look specifically for someone with endurance sport experience. Even 1-3 months of guided work can establish lasting habits.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- All calorie and macronutrient targets are estimates with significant error bars. Power meter kilojoule readings assume ~20-25% efficiency, which varies by individual and intensity (Kolie).
- Food tracking (e.g., MyFitnessPal) is inherently imprecise due to eyeballing portions, database errors, and unmeasured snacking (Namrita). Useful short-term for calibration but not recommended as a permanent practice due to both error and psychological risk.
- "Ideal race weight" is highly individual and genetically influenced. Do not chase arbitrary body fat percentages from charts. If an athlete has never been near a target body fat %, it is probably unrealistic (Namrita).
- Hormones influence desire to eat and move, making the behavioral side of energy balance harder than the physics implies. Ultra-processed foods in particular drive overconsumption.
- Younger athletes and juniors may need to gain weight, not lose it. Do not default to weight loss as a performance strategy (Namrita and Kolie).
- GLP-1 agonists and weight loss drugs: both host and guest recommend against their use for performance weight loss; these require physician oversight and are not shortcuts (Namrita and Kolie).
- The "overtraining vs. underfueling" debate is not either/or. Truly excessive training volume (e.g., 6 days/week of high intensity) can outstrip any reasonable fueling strategy (Kolie, from personal experience).
- Crash diets cause muscle mass loss and water loss in addition to fat loss, and can produce long-term hormonal, gut, and bone health consequences (Namrita).

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Insulin gets an undeserved bad reputation among healthy athletes. For exercising, healthy individuals, insulin is an essential anabolic hormone and not the cause of weight gain. The "carbohydrate-insulin model" is largely irrelevant to endurance athletes who are regularly exercising (Namrita).
- You should not try to create caloric deficits by restricting on-bike fueling. That is "such a small part of your day" and will only impair training quality (Namrita).
- Rest days are often when athletes are hungriest, and this is physiologically appropriate -- adaptation and repair require energy. Over-restricting on rest days undermines recovery (Namrita).
- Many endurance athletes chronically under-eat carbohydrates while over-eating fat, the opposite of what performance requires. Kolie reports routinely telling clients to double their food intake.
- An athlete put in a "calorie deficit" by simply shifting macronutrient composition (more carbs, less fat, same total calories roughly) lost weight without being in an intentional deficit -- the better fueling supported harder training and incidentally reduced fat intake (Namrita, case study).
- Basal metabolic rate does not meaningfully decline with age until about age 60, per Herman Pontzer's research in Nature (Kolie, citing the study).
- Riding more to lose weight often backfires: it increases appetite to the point where athletes gain weight instead (Kolie, client example of gaining 3 kg).

### Evolving Views
- Kolie notes that early in his coaching career he did not ask female athletes about their menstrual cycles and did not know he should. He now considers cycle tracking essential for female athlete health monitoring and encourages all female clients to log cycle data in Training Peaks.
- Namrita acknowledges that early in her career she "probably didn't tell some male athletes to eat enough," reflecting evolving understanding that low energy availability affects men too, though men lack the menstrual cycle as a warning signal.

## Episode: 1481215921-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-24-continuous-glucose-monitors-kristen-faulkner-and-athlete-health.txt

### Core Message
Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) are neither necessary nor sufficient for endurance sport performance, but they can be a useful supplementary tool when data is interpreted by qualified professionals in context. The panel (Namrita Brooke, RD; Tracy Carson, epidemiologist; Fabiano Araujo, MD/PhD) unanimously agrees CGMs do not provide a competitive advantage in racing, that the UCI ban is inconsistently reasoned (heart rate and temperature monitors are permitted), and that the real value of CGMs lies in off-bike 24-hour dietary pattern analysis rather than in-race decision-making. The episode uses Kristen Faulkner's Strade Bianche disqualification as a jumping-off point to discuss broader issues of athlete health, RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport), women's health in sport, and the risks of data fixation without professional guidance.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Do not rely on CGM data alone to guide fueling during races. Interstitial glucose (what CGMs measure) is not blood glucose -- it is an estimate with ~10% variance and a 5-10 minute delay. It does not reflect muscle glycogen stores, gastric emptying rate, or glucose uptake into working muscle (Namrita).
- Develop a fueling plan with a coach/dietitian before racing rather than reacting to real-time glucose readings. A gel every 15 minutes (as Faulkner reported doing) addresses on-bike fueling, but off-bike nutrition is where most athletes' deficits accumulate (all panelists agree).
- CGMs are most useful for off-bike, 24-hour dietary pattern analysis: observing how different foods, combinations, and timing affect glucose stability, energy levels, sleep quality, and subjective well-being (Namrita, Fabiano).
- If using a CGM, work with a qualified professional (sports dietitian, physician) to interpret the data. Without context from food diaries, training data, blood tests, and clinical history, CGM data is "meaningless" (Fabiano).
- Beware of "glucorexia" -- an obsessive focus on minimizing glucose spikes that leads to disordered eating patterns. Some practitioners are using CGM data to discourage healthy foods like oatmeal based on glucose spike appearance, which is misleading and potentially harmful (Namrita).
- Highly trained athletes have fewer glucose excursions (deviations from baseline) over 24 hours compared to recreational athletes. This means the same CGM readings may have different implications at different fitness levels (Tracy).
- A few days of low energy availability can decrease fasting blood glucose, but CGMs have not been validated as a proxy for low energy availability or RED-S. Low glucose is just one of many consequences of under-fueling, and fixing it is not as simple as eating more sugar (Tracy).
- Under-fueling is extremely common in endurance athletes. Kolie estimates at least half his clients have suffered from chronic insufficient caloric intake at some point, ranging from days to years.
- Multiple stressors beyond nutrition can disrupt the menstrual cycle: psychological stress, physical training load, and sleep deprivation. CGMs cannot capture or address these factors (Tracy).
- There is no substitute for good coaching. CGM race data typically just confirms what the athlete already felt (e.g., missed a feed, dropped a bottle). The fueling plan, training plan, and pacing plan should all be developed together beforehand (Namrita).

### Caveats & Qualifications
- CGMs measure interstitial glucose, not blood glucose. There is a ~10% margin and 5-10 minute time delay. During exercise, additional factors (dehydration, heat stress, gastric emptying rate, absorption rate, individual GLUT transporter variability) further distort the relationship between what you eat and what the sensor reads (Namrita).
- Research on CGMs in endurance sport is extremely limited: only 16 papers on PubMed for "continuous glucose monitoring and endurance training" and only 26 citations for "glycemic control and menstrual cycle" as of recording (Fabiano).
- Super Sapiens (the CGM brand Faulkner used) was not FDA-approved at time of recording; athletes using it had to be part of a research trial (Tracy).
- CGM data is siloed in separate platforms (e.g., Super Sapiens app) and does not integrate well with training platforms like Training Peaks, making contextual analysis impractical without significant manual effort (Fabiano).
- The sensors are expensive and not universally available (some brands only in Europe), creating equity concerns if racing use were permitted (Fabiano).
- The UCI rule banning metabolic sensors in competition (Article 1.3.006) prohibits the device itself, not just data recording. It allows heart rate, temperature, and sweat rate monitors, which is inconsistent (all panelists note this).
- Kolie compares CGMs to HRV monitoring: likely to follow a hype cycle where initial enthusiasm gives way to the realization that the data is hard to interpret meaningfully, especially since in well-trained athletes HRV seems to mostly reflect sleep quality rather than training readiness.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- CGMs provide no real-time performance advantage in racing because by the time low glucose is detected (with the interstitial delay), the athlete is already in trouble. Proactive fueling plans are far more effective than reactive glucose monitoring (Namrita, Kolie).
- The framing of CGMs as essential for women's health in sport is "way oversimplifying a complicated issue." Low energy availability involves far more than blood glucose -- if it were just about glucose, "we would just tell people to eat frosted flakes all day" (Namrita).
- Power meters provide more physiological insight than CGMs or most other wearable monitors for trained athletes (Kolie).
- The biggest value of a CGM may be off-bike dietary analysis rather than anything exercise-related. Fabiano found the most useful data came from observing post-workout meal choices and 24-hour glucose patterns, not from in-ride monitoring.
- Some dietitians are misusing CGM data to scare people away from healthy foods (e.g., "don't eat oatmeal, look at the glucose spike"), which is nutritionally irresponsible and potentially triggers disordered eating (Namrita).
- The research needed to validate CGMs for athlete health cannot be conducted if the devices are banned in competition -- creating a catch-22 where UCI demands evidence that cannot be gathered under their rules (Tracy).

### Evolving Views
- Kolie states he learned a lot from this conversation and that his initial hot takes became "it depends" after hearing from the panel. He entered the episode somewhat skeptical of CGMs (suspecting marketing hype from Super Sapiens) and came away with a more nuanced view that they have some off-bike utility but are far from essential.
- Kolie draws an explicit comparison to his earlier position that "there's no such thing as overtraining, there's only under-fueling" -- he now recognizes (informed by the previous episode with Namrita) that both can independently be true, and excessive training volume can outstrip any fueling strategy.

## Episode: 1966298519-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-36-the-consequences-of-chronic-underfueling-with-traci-carson.txt

### Core Message
Chronic low energy availability (LEA) is the central root cause of Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S), a framework encompassing 10 health outcomes including immune dysfunction, cardiovascular issues, menstrual irregularity, bone health deterioration, and mental health symptoms like anxiety and OCD-like tendencies. Guest Dr. Traci Carson (epidemiologist/researcher) and host Kolie Moore emphasize that LEA can occur at any body weight — athletes can be chronically underfueled without losing weight or even while gaining weight, because the body adapts by slowing metabolism, heart rate, and non-essential functions. Critically, even when total daily caloric intake appears adequate, poor distribution of calories throughout the day (e.g., underfueling around training and front- or back-loading meals) can still produce hormonal and endocrine consequences equivalent to overall underfueling. The episode stresses that bone damage from chronic LEA is largely irreversible, and that overtraining syndrome and RED-S have near-complete symptom overlap, with underfueling likely being a component of most overtraining cases.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Fuel adequately during long rides: taking in only 30 grams of carbs/hour during a 4-hour ride will impair next-day recovery and subsequent training quality (Carson).
- Aim for no less than 45 kcal per kilogram of fat-free mass per day as a general minimum for optimal body functioning (IOC consensus recommendation cited by Carson).
- Distribute calorie intake throughout the day, especially around training — do not save calories for one large meal later. A banana (110 cal) before a 2-hour session burning 1200 cal creates an 1100-calorie deficit by 9 AM that triggers stress responses even if dinner later brings the daily total to adequacy (Carson).
- If pursuing weight loss, limit dieting phases to approximately 6-8 weeks maximum, losing no more than 6-8 pounds per phase, then return to maintenance before resuming — treat body composition changes as a multi-year project, not a single-season effort (Moore, personal experience).
- Keep protein powder accessible to supplement meals that fall short on protein — Moore reports using it at 2 out of 3 meals.
- Monitor for RED-S warning signs: inconsistent performances, excessive bad training days, inability to function off the bike (couch-bound after workouts, brain fog, difficulty with basic tasks like showering or making lunch), moodiness, frequent illness, and irregular menstrual cycles (Carson/Moore).
- For women on IUDs who want to monitor cycle health: use basal body temperature tracking (e.g., Oura Ring) to detect ovulation — temperature rises by approximately 0.02 degrees at ovulation, requiring a precise thermometer or wearable (Carson).
- If a coach or team environment is encouraging disordered eating, fire the coach or seek external support (dietitian, therapist) as soon as possible — do not wait until the situation resolves itself (Carson/Moore).
- Seek a registered dietitian who specifically understands disordered eating, not just a generic sports dietitian, if the issue involves psychological barriers to eating more (Carson).
- Prioritize bone health from a young age — early damage to bone microarchitecture from LEA is largely permanent and cannot be fully repaired (Carson).

### Caveats & Qualifications
- There are currently no established thresholds for the magnitude or duration of energy deficit needed to trigger RED-S adaptations — it is highly bio-individual and unlikely to ever have a one-size-fits-all number (Carson).
- The 45 kcal/kg FFM/day figure is a broad general recommendation, not a precise individual target (Carson, citing IOC consensus).
- Diagnosing RED-S vs. overtraining syndrome vs. exercise-associated hypogonadism in males is extremely difficult due to massive symptom overlap — diagnosis is largely by exclusion (Moore, citing Stellingworth/Mountjoy/Burke paper).
- Calories in/calories out is "not that simple" — body composition and hormonal outcomes depend on nutrient timing throughout the day, not just daily totals (Carson).
- Hormonal birth control masks whether a natural menstrual cycle has resumed, making it a poor marker for recovery from LEA unless the athlete is specifically on an IUD and tracking ovulation via temperature (Carson).
- Menstrual cycle recovery from chronic LEA can take 3-6-12+ months and sometimes comes with weight gain, sometimes not — there is no one-size-fits-all timeline (Carson).
- The episode explicitly does not cover clinical eating disorders or how to broach the topic with someone suffering from one — the hosts disclaim clinical expertise (Moore/Carson).
- Moore's personal weight loss numbers (40 lbs over 3 years, 6-8 lbs per 8-week phase) are his individual experience and should not be taken as universal guidelines.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- You can be in chronic LEA and not lose weight, or even gain weight — body weight and external appearance do not correlate with fueling status. Athletes at any body fat percentage can be chronically underfueled (Carson).
- Overtraining syndrome and RED-S are "almost just a circle" as a Venn diagram — Carson argues that LEA/underfueling is almost always a component of overtraining, challenging the idea that they are distinct conditions.
- Moore pushes back on the coaching adage "there's no such thing as overtraining, only under-recovery" — he argues this is definitively false because training intensity and frequency can exceed any amount of recovery support (Moore).
- Even with adequate total daily calories, poor distribution of intake throughout the day (especially failing to fuel around training) can cause hormonal dysfunction including amenorrhea — total caloric balance is necessary but not sufficient (Carson, citing research on calorie distribution and menstrual cycle dysfunction).
- The cycling world gets a B/B+ on carbohydrate awareness but a C-minus to D on RED-S awareness specifically — the gap between fueling knowledge and RED-S recognition is large (Carson/Moore).
- Some professional teams still practice skin caliper testing and punitive food restriction (locking cupboards, reducing meals, extra miles for weight) — this is not just a historical problem (Moore).
- Getting your menstrual cycle back does not mean you are "cured" of RED-S — bone damage and other consequences persist and require continued attention (Carson/Moore).
- One bone stress fracture strongly predicts six more — the re-injury rate from LEA-related bone damage is significant and can become a lifelong cycle (Carson).

### Evolving Views
- Moore notes that the cycling world's approach to fueling is improving, particularly among women in domestic crit racing, and that some World Tour teams now have nutritionists and post encouragement signage about fueling — a shift from historical norms of restriction.
- Carson observes significant cultural progress since the Mary Cain/Nike story broke (which coincided with the start of her dissertation), particularly in media representation of diverse athlete body types, though both she and Moore grade the sport as still having major gaps in RED-S awareness.

## Episode: 2154769800-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-40-energy-expenditure-and-compensation-with-eric-trexler.txt

**Word count:** 21,545

### Core Message
Human energy expenditure is dynamic and adaptable, not a simple fixed equation. The body compensates for both increased exercise and caloric restriction through adaptive mechanisms that shrink energy deficits or surpluses over time — a concept formalized as the "constrained energy expenditure model." For endurance athletes, this means that naive calorie math (BMR + exercise kilojoules = total expenditure) is unreliable, and the best practical method for estimating energy needs is a 2-4 week empirical tracking approach using daily weight and intake data. Weight manipulation in cycling should follow a "get in, get out" philosophy similar to bodybuilding contest prep, rather than the common cycling practice of staying excessively lean for extended periods.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Use a 2-4 week "energy balance method" to estimate total daily energy expenditure: hold training load and caloric intake steady, weigh yourself every morning in standardized conditions, and calculate the slope of body weight change to infer actual expenditure. This is far more reliable than BMR calculators plus power meter kilojoules.
- Weigh yourself daily (not just start/end) to smooth out water weight fluctuations and get a reliable body weight trend line.
- If weight is stable at a given caloric intake, that intake approximates your total daily energy expenditure — but check for compensatory symptoms (low energy, low libido, feeling cold, sleep disturbance, amenorrhea, low T3) which indicate you may be weight-stable only because your body has downregulated expenditure.
- When dieting, expect weight loss to plateau as energy expenditure adapts downward. Plan to periodically recalculate your expenditure using the energy balance method and adjust caloric intake accordingly.
- Include resistance training (barbell, dumbbell, machine, or resistance band) during any weight loss phase. The best predictor of weight regain after a diet is how much fat-free mass was lost (Trexler, guest).
- For amateur cyclists, do the bulk of weight loss in the off-season when training intensity demands are lower, not during high-intensity in-season training.
- For professional cyclists, adopt a "get in, get out" approach to competition weight: reach race weight briefly for key events, then return to a higher sustainable baseline. Do not try to hold race weight for extended periods.
- When refeeding after a period of under-eating, expect the body to preferentially regain fat first, then fat-free mass. Ravenous appetite typically does not subside until fat-free mass is fully restored. Allow this process rather than fighting it.
- For ultra-endurance events (multi-day races), accept that you cannot fully fuel the effort in real time — you are "borrowing against future energy stores" and everyone loses weight. Focus on maximizing intake rather than trying to achieve energy balance during the event.
- Maximum sustainable caloric intake appears to top out at roughly 2.5x basal metabolic rate (some elite athletes may reach ~3x BMR). Use this as a planning ceiling for long-duration fueling strategies.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The 2.5x BMR "ceiling" on sustainable energy expenditure/intake is a population-level observation with biological variation; some elite athletes may push to ~3x BMR, possibly because this capacity is part of what makes them elite (Trexler).
- Non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT) is essentially impossible to measure directly — it is always calculated as the residual after subtracting other components, making it the "dark matter" of energy expenditure research with large error bars (Trexler).
- Power meter-based calorie estimates have inherent error because exercise energy expenditure is a metabolic process that cannot be perfectly inferred from external work output (Trexler).
- BMR prediction equations introduce error; if BMR is measured shortly after exercise, it may be inflated by residual metabolic effects lasting into the next day (Trexler).
- Wearable devices and consumer gadgets are not accurate enough for energy expenditure estimation — "none of them are where they need to be in terms of precision and accuracy" (Trexler).
- Low thyroid hormones, disrupted menstrual cycles, or other metabolic adaptations during heavy training are "not necessarily a problem until it's a problem" — it is a matter of magnitude, clinical relevance, and whether performance goals are being met (Trexler). Do not automatically pathologize these adaptations.
- The energy balance method requires honest, diligent food tracking for 2-4 weeks, which many athletes resist because they want immediate answers.
- Weight regain dynamics differ dramatically depending on starting context (e.g., obese individual losing weight vs. already-lean athlete cutting further). The Minnesota starvation experiment results should not be directly extrapolated to typical athletic weight cycling.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- You can be weight-stable and still experiencing significant energy compensation — weight stability does not mean you are adequately fueled. A cyclist maintaining weight on 3,400 kcal/day may actually need 3,700-3,800 kcal/day to function optimally if their body has downregulated via compensatory mechanisms (Kolie/Trexler).
- The day after an ultra-endurance event, even while highly sedentary, energy expenditure can reach 3x BMR — the metabolic cost of repair is far higher than most people estimate (Kolie, citing recent paper).
- The common cycling practice of maintaining very low body weight for entire seasons is more problematic than the bodybuilding approach of brief, targeted cuts — cycling culture lacks the "get in, get out" concept that bodybuilding has developed (Kolie).
- Dramatically increasing exercise volume does not proportionally increase total daily energy expenditure — the body compensates by reducing expenditure in other compartments, shrinking the expected energy deficit (Trexler, constrained energy expenditure model).
- Changes in mechanical/exercise efficiency at very low power outputs have been observed but largely disappear at training-relevant intensities — do not assume your body has become "more efficient" at hard efforts just because you have been training a lot (Trexler).
- Some athletes who are "stuck" at a given weight may simply need to eat more, not less. Increasing calories from 3,400 to 3,600 kcal/day can result in no weight gain but restoration of thyroid function, return of menstrual cycle, and improved well-being (Trexler/Kolie).

### Evolving Views
- Kolie mentions that his fascination with bodybuilding literature (MASS Research Review, Iron Culture podcast) has been "incredibly helpful" to him as a cycling coach, suggesting he has recently incorporated bodybuilding-style weight management principles (get in/get out, resistance training during cuts) into his cycling coaching approach — this represents a cross-pollination of ideas he appears to have adopted relatively recently.

### Core Message
Host Kolie Moore interviews Dr. Fabiano Araujo (medical doctor, twice-master, PhD, and Empirical Cycling coach) on managing illness for cyclists. The central message is that stopping training as soon as below-the-neck symptoms appear preserves immune system energy and shortens recovery time, whereas pushing through makes illness worse and can cost months of training. Nutrition during illness should prioritize carbohydrates, adequate protein, and healthy fats over dietary restrictions; supplements like vitamin C and D have conflicting evidence and offer little beyond a well-balanced diet. - **Neck check rule:** Stop ALL exercise (including gym work) if any below-the-neck symptoms are present: fever, myalgia (muscle pain), chest pain, or resting tachycardia — not just reduced-intensity alternatives
- Being sick causes up to a **25% reduction in endurance capacity** (e.g., a 100-minute TTE at 75% VO2max becomes ~75 minutes); RPE at any given power will be substantially elevated
- **Return to training after illness:** Begin only once all below-the-neck symptoms are gone; start with easy rides of ~30 minutes, progress by roughly 15 minutes per day based on athlete feedback, not a fixed timetable
- **Carbohydrate intake during illness:** Raise carbohydrate intake to support immune cells (which need glucose); avoid weight-loss diets or caloric deficits while sick
- Drinking **6% carbohydrate solutions** (e.g., sports drink) during training is associated with lower incidence of upper respiratory illness in studies — likely via combined hydration and carbohydrate effects
- **Protein:** No specific supplement (including glutamine) is consistently supported; the recommendation is a well-balanced diet, not extremes of very high or very low protein
- **Fats:** Ensure adequate healthy fats (mono/polyunsaturated — olive oil, avocado, wild fish) during illness; very low-fat dietary periods should be suspended until recovery
- **Avoid alcohol entirely during illness recovery:** There is no established safe minimum; even a single beer can trigger a full relapse (host's personal experience corroborated by Fabiano); alcohol impairs immune function for several hours
- **Probiotics** (most accessible form: yogurt) show promising evidence for reducing recurring respiratory illness; worth incorporating into regular diet
- **Hydration:** Illness increases risk of dehydration via reduced ADH (antidiuretic hormone) production and elevated protein catabolism; prioritize fluids alongside carbohydrates
- **Prevention:** Frequent hand washing, mask use indoors, avoid touching eyes/nose/mouth, do not share water bottles or towels, avoid binge drinking after competitions
- Write down subjective workout feel (beginning and end) to help coach/athlete identify when to reduce training load, especially since immune response varies person-to-person

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Most illness data in athletes comes from **elite Olympic athletes**, not amateurs or masters — amateur recovery typically takes longer due to life stress, less access to nutritional support, and less recovery resources
- The J-curve of training load vs. susceptibility to illness applies mainly to amateurs; elite athletes may follow an S-curve (more training = more protection at the highest levels)
- Evidence for vitamins C and D is equivocal in the literature (50/50 results); no consistent benefit established beyond a well-balanced diet
- Individual variation in immune response is high; there is no technology readily available to amateurs to directly measure immune function markers daily
- COVID-19 return-to-sport data is largely from non-athletic or older populations, making guidance for young trained athletes uncertain
- "Above the neck" symptoms (runny nose, sore throat, mild cough) are generally considered safe to train through lightly — this was not explicitly contradicted but context suggests caution even then

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- **There is no safe amount of alcohol** according to the literature Fabiano reviewed — not even moderate wine consumption — contrary to popular belief about moderate drinking being benign
- **Gym work during below-the-neck illness is not safer than cycling** — the common advice to "just do a shorter gym session instead" is not supported; full rest is recommended
- **Protein catabolism increases significantly during fever** because immune cells require amino acids and glucose to function; this is an underappreciated nutritional demand during illness
- The protective J-curve benefit of moderate training is largely for amateurs; elite athletes appear to have different (possibly better) immune regulation than amateurs training at high loads
- Weight loss during injury recovery (Remco Evenepoel's cited example) counterintuitively delays recovery because the body needs energy to repair tissue

## Episode: 2223337298-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-59-a-muscle-glycogen-paradox.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2223337298
**Word count:** 14404
**Series:** Watts Doc

### Core Message
Kolie Moore and Kyle Helson (returning guest, scientist with H-index 12) review a double-blind crossover study (N=8 recreationally active men, Louise Burke and David Bishop among authors) examining delayed post-exercise carbohydrate feeding. After a hard glycogen-depleting session (10×2 min at ~94–95% of ramp-test peak power), participants who received 2.4 g/kg carbs immediately after exercise performed ~30% more total high-intensity reps the following day than those whose carbohydrate intake was delayed by ~3 hours (even though total daily calories were identical and next-day muscle glycogen levels were essentially the same between groups). The paper's central paradox is that muscle glycogen replenishment was similar in both conditions by day two, yet performance differed substantially — suggesting that something beyond muscle glycogen (possibly liver glycogen, subcellular glycogen compartmentalization, or within-day energy deficits) drives this effect.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **The closer your next hard session is, the more critical immediate post-exercise carbohydrate intake becomes.** For back-to-back training days, same-day doubles, or multi-round track/race days, prioritize refueling as soon as possible after each effort
- **Practical target during the immediate refueling window:** The study used **2.4 g/kg carbohydrates distributed every ~20 minutes over the first 2 hours** post-exercise — this is specific to same-day or next-day hard sessions
- **The classical 30–60 minute "glycogen window" matters most when your next hard session is within hours**, not days; if next hard session is 4–5+ days away, timing of post-ride carbs is much less critical, as long as total daily intake is adequate
- **Don't over-focus on on-bike fueling at the expense of off-bike nutrition.** Athletes who fuel well during rides but then eat poorly the rest of the day (e.g., large salads, delayed meals) still accumulate large within-day energy deficits that impair recovery and next-day performance
- **Avoid deliberate within-day energy deficits on high-volume or back-to-back training days.** Within-day deficit research shows detrimental health and performance outcomes even when total 24-hour caloric intake is matched
- **On-bike fueling has evolved substantially:** Modern aggressive fueling (70–90 g/hour carbs) means returning from rides without acute hunger — contrasted with historical norms of 20 g every 30–40 minutes (~1 gel/hour); the higher range is now standard for well-fueled training
- **Getting some fructose off the bike** (fruit juice, mixed carbohydrate sources) may benefit liver glycogen replenishment specifically; nutritionists are reportedly recommending this — the study used glucose-only Lucozade, which may underestimate the benefit of mixed carb sources
- Glycogen molecules cannot cross cell membranes (they are too large); upper-body weight training does not meaningfully deplete leg glycogen — concurrent upper body lifting and cycling training can be scheduled without glycogen competition concerns
- Each gram of glycogen stores **~3–4 grams of water** — this explains significant body weight fluctuations (a few lbs/kg) during hard training blocks and tapering periods

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Study was in **recreationally active men** (~2.5+ hrs/week), not trained cyclists; trained athletes may recover faster, have higher baseline glycogen stores, or be less impacted by delayed feeding — unknown until replicated in trained populations
- **N=8** (one dropout from N=10): sample size was powered for molecular (PGC-1α mRNA) outcomes, not performance — however, the estimated effect size for performance reps was 2.03, well above conventional thresholds, so the performance finding is considered robust by the hosts
- The placebo used (zero-calorie Lucozade) may not have been perfectly blinded; participants could potentially have noticed taste differences
- Crossover washout period may not have fully eliminated carryover effects; participants who did the delayed condition first may have eaten more before returning for their second session
- **Muscle glycogen levels were ~10% lower in the delayed group** pre-day-2, but authors and Moore attribute this to normal day-to-day glycogen variation, not a meaningful mechanistic difference
- Whether liver glycogen was differentially replenished is unknown — liver glycogen is difficult to measure non-invasively and is not directly assessed in this study
- Kolie is explicit that he is not a nutritionist; advice on nutrition timing is based on coaching experience and reading the literature, not formal nutrition expertise

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- **Muscle glycogen replenishment was nearly identical between groups, yet performance differed by ~30%** — this is the central paradox; the conventional narrative that "if glycogen is replenished, performance will be restored" is directly undermined
- The canonical glycogen window concept (John Ivy) is partially validated — but the key insight is it matters specifically when training sessions are close together, not as a blanket rule for all athletes all the time
- **Within-day energy distribution matters independently of total daily calories** — two groups with identical total daily energy can have very different performance outcomes depending on when in the day they eat relative to training
- Gluconeogenesis cannot substitute for dietary carbohydrates at the rates needed for sports performance — the "carbs aren't essential because your body can make them" argument is described as disingenuous conflation of the term "essential"
- EPO (erythropoietin) is ~40% carbohydrate by weight (glycosylated protein) — inadequate carbohydrate availability could theoretically impair endogenous EPO function (though this is presented as an aside, not a primary claim)

### Evolving Views
- **The glycogen window concept has been substantially revised:** The early John Ivy literature (focused on 2–4 hour post-exercise windows and low-to-moderate intensity TTE tests) is now understood to have limited applicability except in specific high-frequency training scenarios; the broader wisdom has shifted from "always refuel within 30–60 min" to "total daily intake matters more, except when sessions are back-to-back"
- Moore notes that **fasted rides and deliberate bonking for training adaptation** were experimented with in early coaching career (~20g carbs/30–40 min norms); both he and Helson now consider this a failed experiment with no practical value
- The episode previews a planned sub-series examining **within-day energy deficits** and their health/performance consequences — indicating this is an actively evolving area of interest for the podcast

### Core Message
Kolie Moore interviews Tim Podlogar, exercise physiologist and performance nutritionist (formerly Bora-Hansgrohe, currently Tudor Pro Cycling and University of Exeter lecturer), on macronutrient planning, energy expenditure estimation, carbohydrate distribution, glycogen loading, and absorption rate testing. The central thesis is that energy expenditure estimation is fundamentally guesswork due to stacked assumptions (efficiency, off-bike activity, nutrition label error, absorption rates), and practitioners should calibrate over 1-2 months using body weight trends and recovery quality rather than trusting any single calculation. A key practical insight is that carbohydrate distribution across the day — especially the balance between on-bike and off-bike intake — has a profound effect on next-day glycogen stores and performance, and that blindly maximizing on-bike carb intake (e.g., 120g/hr) can paradoxically harm multi-day performance by leaving insufficient carbohydrate budget for recovery meals.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Expect a 1-2 month calibration period when starting with a nutritionist or new nutrition plan; adjust energy estimates iteratively based on body weight trends and recovery quality rather than trusting initial calculations
- Monitor body weight trends over at least a week rather than reacting to day-to-day fluctuations, which can be driven by water retention, training stress, salt intake, fiber changes, and menstrual cycle
- When estimating energy expenditure from bike kilojoules, recognize that efficiency assumptions (20% vs 25%) alone create ~900 kcal error on a 4,000 kJ ride; off-bike activity, nutrition label tolerances (~20%), and absorption inefficiency (85-95%) compound this further
- Think of carbohydrate intake in terms of a total daily energy budget: on-bike carbs come out of that budget, so consuming 120g/hr vs 90g/hr during a 4-hour ride means ~120g fewer carbs available for off-bike glycogen replenishment
- On-bike carbohydrate intake above ~60-70g/hr spares liver glycogen stores, but there is limited evidence it spares muscle glycogen; higher on-bike intake (e.g., 120 vs 90g/hr) primarily shifts substrate oxidation toward carbohydrate and away from fat rather than preserving more glycogen
- There is a dose-response relationship between post-ride carbohydrate intake and next-day glycogen stores; prioritize having sufficient carbohydrate budget remaining for recovery meals, especially in multi-day events
- For one-day races or events where next-day recovery does not matter, maximize on-bike carbohydrate intake — consider ramping from ~80-120g in the first hour up to higher rates in the final 2 hours
- Consume carbohydrates as soon as possible after training sessions; delayed post-exercise carbohydrate intake impairs next-day performance (~30% fewer intervals) even when muscle glycogen levels appear similar by the next day (Podlogar supports the "delayed recovery process" explanation)
- For weight loss, first establish a baseline energy balance over 1-2 months before creating a deficit; rushing into aggressive deficits risks iron problems, loss of menstrual period in females, impaired recovery, and inaccurate estimation of actual needs
- If a rider cannot physically eat enough solid food (common in grand tours), replace some solid carbohydrate sources (pasta, gnocchi) with liquid calories like juice or sugary drinks that go down more easily
- Do not obsess over long-term food tracking for amateurs; use a calibration period to learn portion sizes and body signals, then transition to intuitive eating guided by training demands
- For supplements, focus on vitamin D and ferritin (iron status) blood panels; aim for ~100% of recommended daily allowance for vitamins rather than megadosing
- For ferritin specifically, a sports nutritionist may want higher values than a doctor would require (doctors aim to prevent anemia; nutritionists aim to optimize performance, especially before altitude camps)
- Electrolyte replacement during exercise lasting less than 4 hours is probably unnecessary; daily electrolyte balance matters more than real-time replacement during rides
- If urinating excessively during rides, the most likely cause is drinking too much water rather than insufficient electrolytes; consider reducing fluid intake and adding electrolytes to what you do drink
- Sweat composition testing is of limited utility because results vary day-to-day based on heat acclimatization, intake, exercise intensity, and other factors

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Energy expenditure calculations are "purely guesswork" (Podlogar's words) due to the number of stacked assumptions; even doubly labeled water studies show 10-15% mismatch with actual energy intake in weight-stable riders
- Efficiency varies with exercise intensity (rises above the first threshold) and cannot be accurately measured above the second threshold using standard lab equipment; indoor lab tests may overestimate efficiency in outdoor riders unaccustomed to indoor cycling
- Body weight fluctuations of 1-2 kg can occur acutely from training stress and water retention without any change in energy balance — do not interpret these as meaningful fat gain or loss
- Energy compensation (reduced non-exercise activity during caloric deficit) is a real phenomenon; athletes dieting aggressively may unconsciously become sedentary off the bike, impairing recovery and household functioning

- Absorption rate testing (using isotope tracers during 2.5+ hours of lab cycling) is costly and methodologically demanding; results may confirm what experienced athletes already know intuitively about their limits
- There is currently no strong evidence that gut training increases maximal carbohydrate absorption rates (Podlogar: "we don't really see the evidence that it does")
- Nutrition label accuracy has ~20% allowed error, and weighing cooked vs raw pasta/rice introduces further measurement error; even a minute of different cooking time can meaningfully change the caloric content measurement of cooked grains
