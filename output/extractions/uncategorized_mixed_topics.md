## Episode 1

**Filename:** `643137612-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-9-strength-endurance-and-the-size-principle.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 643137612
**Word count:** ~8,000
**Series:** Watts Doc (episode 9)

### Core Message
This episode examines whether adding strength training to endurance training improves cycling performance through neural mechanisms rather than hypertrophy. A 16-week study of elite U23 cyclists compared a strength-plus-endurance group to an endurance-only group and found meaningful performance improvements in the combined group. The gains appeared to come from improved neural drive — specifically maximal voluntary contraction and type 2X to 2A fiber conversion — rather than added muscle mass. Crucially, VO2max and FTP did not change in the strength group, suggesting a separate mechanism of adaptation was responsible for the power gains.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Strength training combined with endurance training can raise 45-minute power output by approximately 8% in elite U23 cyclists over 16 weeks without changing VO2max or FTP
- Maximal voluntary contraction (MVC) improvements of 12–20% were observed in the strength-plus-endurance group
- The mechanism appears to be neural: improved motor unit recruitment, particularly type 2X fiber conversion to type 2A
- Strength training benefit is driven by high mechanical tension under stretch (eccentric loading), which cycling alone cannot replicate

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Subjects were elite U23 cyclists; extrapolation to amateur populations uncertain
- Study was 16 weeks; long-term effects beyond this window are unknown
- VO2max and FTP were unchanged — strength training appears to add a separate performance dimension rather than boosting the main aerobic metrics

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- The size principle dictates that higher-threshold motor units (type 2 fibers) are only recruited at high force demands; endurance cycling rarely produces the mechanical tension required to cause meaningful strength adaptation in these fibers
- Strength adaptations from cycling are largely absent because cycling is a concentric-only movement and muscles begin in a lengthened position only briefly — there is insufficient eccentric overload
- FTP improvements from strength training cannot be explained by aerobic mechanisms; the neural drive hypothesis (better recruitment efficiency) is more consistent with the data

### Evolving Views
Kolie expresses that the strength-training literature has shifted his thinking on what "training specificity" means — endurance training and strength training can address separate limiting factors simultaneously, and treating them as interchangeable or mutually exclusive is incorrect.

## Episode 2

**Filename:** `654554156-empiricalcyclingpodcast-the-watts-doc-10-can-fat-adaptive-diets-lead-to-better-performance-through-glycogen-sparing.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 654554156
**Word count:** ~7,000
**Series:** Watts Doc (episode 10)

### Core Message
This episode examines whether fat-adaptive diets can spare glycogen and thereby improve endurance performance. The central study involves elite race walkers assigned to a high-fat ketogenic diet, a high-carbohydrate diet, or a periodized diet over 3 weeks. The fat-adapted group started the 100km time trial with roughly half the glycogen stores of the carbohydrate group but ended up using the same absolute amount of glycogen — meaning glycogen sparing did occur at the biochemical level. Despite this, the fat group performed 40–70 watts worse on 1km and 4km time trials. Glycogen sparing from fat adaptation did not translate into better performance because the fat-adapted athletes could not sustain high intensity.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Do not use fat-adaptive diets as a performance strategy; they reduce high-intensity power output regardless of glycogen levels
- The relevant variable for performance at race intensity is not substrate availability but the capacity to generate power — and high-intensity power requires carbohydrate metabolism
- A follow-up paper ("crisis averted") showed that 2 weeks of carbohydrate reintroduction restored performance to baseline, but the fat group did not surpass the carb group — no catch-up or supercompensation effect

### Caveats & Qualifications
- 22 of 29 subjects were Olympic-level athletes; this is a well-powered study with meaningful external validity for elite endurance athletes
- Study duration was 3 weeks; very long-term (months or years) fat-adaptation effects were not examined
- The specific population was race walkers; some sport-specific substrate dynamics may differ slightly for cyclists

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Glycogen sparing is real — fat-adapted athletes do conserve carbohydrate — but this advantage is physiologically irrelevant if power output capacity at high intensity is simultaneously impaired
- The popular argument that "you have 80,000+ fat calories stored versus ~2,000 carb calories, so access more fat" ignores the rate-limiting role of carbohydrate oxidation for intensity above ~75% VO2max

### Evolving Views
Kolie notes that this episode was an early attempt to engage with fat adaptation literature rigorously; his subsequent episodes (Watts Doc 35 and 40) extend this argument deeper into molecular mechanisms.

## Episode 3

**Filename:** `662678975-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-1-training-for-cyclocross-season.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 662678975
**Word count:** ~2,000
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips (episode 1)

### Core Message
A short practical episode for athletes preparing for cyclocross season 4–6 weeks out. Kolie prescribes specific workouts for cyclocross preparation that target the short explosive efforts and the sustained threshold demands of the discipline. The emphasis is on race-specific neuromuscular work — starts, gear shifts under load, and technique — combined with maintaining aerobic base rather than loading high volumes of generic intensity.

### Key Training Recommendations
- 4–6 weeks out from cyclocross season: prioritize 3×10-second starts (simulate race start effort), 2×20-minute over-unders with gear shifts (simulate surges in race), and off-road technique sessions
- Maintain aerobic volume; do not abandon endurance riding for pure specificity work
- Technique work (dismounts, remounts, barriers) should be integrated as its own session rather than tacked onto the end of a hard interval session
- Gear shift over-unders: ride sweet spot, surge to threshold or above for 30–60 seconds, shift to harder gear, then recover back to sweet spot — repeat across 20-minute blocks

### Caveats & Qualifications
- This is the first Ten Minute Tips episode; the recommendations are based on coaching experience rather than citing specific studies
- Specific power targets depend on individual FTP; all recommendations are expressed relative to zones

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Short-duration cyclocross demands (45–60 minutes) are primarily aerobic, not anaerobic; most athletes underestimate how much aerobic capacity limits their results
- Technique practice is undertrained relative to its race impact; most amateur cyclocross athletes would gain more from improving dismount/remount skill than from adding another interval session

### Evolving Views
This is an early episode; Kolie later produces more detailed guidance on cyclocross preparation that moves away from zone-based prescriptions toward RPE and power-duration-based targets.

## Episode 4

**Filename:** `690199822-empiricalcyclingpodcast-the-watts-doc-12-can-fiber-type-predict-carb-or-fat-use.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 690199822
**Word count:** ~6,500
**Series:** Watts Doc (episode 12)

### Core Message
This episode challenges the popular model that slow-twitch fiber type reliably predicts fat oxidation and fast-twitch type predicts carbohydrate use. The evidence shows that 10–70% of fibers in trained athletes are hybrid types (expressing multiple myosin heavy chains), and that in trained endurance cyclists, substrate preference correlates more strongly with training history and relative intensity than with fiber type composition. A widely-cited Coyle study reported a 0.91 correlation between fiber type and fat oxidation, but Kolie argues this correlation was actually 0.87 when re-examined as correlation with intensity relative to FTP — meaning the relationship is largely mediated by training, not genetics.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Do not use fiber type (if known) as a basis for substrate use or training decisions; training adaptation is more important
- Relative exercise intensity — intensity as a fraction of your personal threshold — is a better predictor of substrate use than fiber type
- High-volume endurance training shifts fiber behavior toward greater aerobic capacity regardless of intrinsic fiber type distribution

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The literature on hybrid fiber types is still evolving and methodologically variable depending on whether fibers are classified histochemically or by myosin heavy chain expression
- The reinterpretation of the Coyle correlation is Kolie's own analysis and has not been formally published

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- The clean slow-twitch = fat burning / fast-twitch = carb burning narrative is a significant oversimplification not supported by fiber physiology in trained athletes
- Training changes fiber behavior more than it changes fiber type distribution; the same type 2A fiber in a well-trained cyclist will have much higher oxidative capacity than in a sedentary person

### Evolving Views
This episode is early in the podcast's engagement with fiber physiology; Watts Doc 37 later provides a more detailed look at oxidative capacity of fast-twitch fibers in cross-country skiers, substantially extending this argument.

## Episode 5

**Filename:** `782049499-empiricalcyclingpodcast-training-and-season-planning-of-pandemic-proportions.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 782049499
**Word count:** ~6,500
**Series:** Uncategorized (COVID-19 special)

### Core Message
Recorded during the COVID-19 lockdowns of spring 2020, this episode gives practical season planning guidance for cyclists whose events have been cancelled or whose training environments have changed. The core message is that an involuntary rest period at what was previously a peak time is an opportunity if managed correctly — either to consolidate rest or to launch a productive VO2max block. The episode also addresses motivation management and the risks of informal group rides during the pandemic.

### Key Training Recommendations
- If you were peaking for a spring event: take a genuine rest week, then reassess; do not panic-train to "preserve" fitness that no longer has a target
- If well isolated and not in a high-risk category: consider a hard VO2max training block (e.g., 4–6 weeks), as this is a rare opportunity to train without the distraction of upcoming events
- Double days (endurance in the afternoon after a morning session) are a viable tool for athletes with extra time; schedule the harder session first in a day, then the endurance session later
- Do not participate in informal group rides during lockdown — this was both a public health risk and a potential training mistake for an interrupted season
- Manage motivation by adjusting expectations; form without a target is just fatigue

### Caveats & Qualifications
- This episode was recorded in early COVID-19 (March–April 2020); some recommendations are specific to that circumstance
- Individual risk tolerance and living situation appropriately modulate whether a rest phase or training block is warranted

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- A forced break at a peak time is not inherently bad; athletes who entered the pandemic in a rested, un-peaked state may have more potential training runway than those who were peaking
- Most fitness losses during 2–4 weeks of rest are recoverable in 1–2 weeks of training; the psychological response to detraining is usually worse than the physiological reality

### Evolving Views
Kolie notes uncertainty about how long the pandemic and cancellations would last; subsequent planning guidance evolved as the timeline extended.

## Episode 6

**Filename:** `1160146492-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-34-why-keto-is-not-the-diet-youre-looking-for.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1160146492
**Word count:** ~9,500
**Series:** Watts Doc (episode 34)

### Core Message
This episode systematically dismantles the case for ketogenic diets in endurance performance, primarily through the Burke race walker studies. 22 of 29 participants in the main study were Olympic-level athletes; the 3-week dietary intervention showed that the ketogenic group did not improve on a 10km time trial while high-carbohydrate groups improved by 2–3 minutes. Keto specifically impaired running economy, meaning athletes required more oxygen for the same speed. A follow-up "crisis averted" paper showed that 2 weeks of carbohydrate reintroduction returned the keto group to their baseline — they regained what they lost but did not exceed the carb group's gains.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Do not use ketogenic diets for endurance performance improvement; the evidence is unambiguous that keto impairs high-intensity capacity
- Carbohydrates are required for performance at intensities above roughly the first lactate threshold; this is not a preference but a physiological requirement
- If an athlete has been doing keto and wants to return to carbohydrate-based fueling, approximately 2 weeks of reintroduction is sufficient to restore baseline performance

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Study subjects were race walkers (not cyclists); the metabolic demands are broadly comparable for high-intensity aerobic work but some sport-specific nuance exists
- 3-week dietary interventions may not represent full adaptation to a ketogenic state; however, advocates of keto typically claim 3–6 weeks is sufficient for adaptation
- Individual metabolic responses to dietary manipulation vary; the data represent group averages

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Keto impaired running economy — athletes using more oxygen per unit of work at the same speed — which directly contradicts the claim that fat adaptation makes athletes more metabolically efficient
- The "crisis averted" framing in the follow-up paper is striking: a real crisis would require a longer recovery, but even 2 weeks of carb reintroduction was enough to negate the keto impairment without producing any additional gains

### Evolving Views
Kolie connects this episode to his broader critique of fat-adaptation reasoning developed across Watts Doc 10, 35, and 40; each episode adds a different layer of the argument (substrate use, training adaptation, molecular signaling).

## Episode 7

**Filename:** `1178110471-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-35-fatmax-fallacies.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1178110471
**Word count:** ~10,500
**Series:** Watts Doc (episode 35)

### Core Message
This episode deconstructs two major fallacies associated with "fat max" training — the intensity at which fat oxidation is maximized. Fallacy 1: riding at fat max produces superior aerobic adaptation compared to other endurance riding. Fallacy 2: burning more fat during exercise causes meaningful fat mass reduction. Kolie argues that aerobic adaptation is driven by calcium signaling and AMPK from muscle contractions, not by substrate choice — a cell has no mechanism to preferentially adapt more because it is burning fat rather than carbohydrate. The fat mass argument fails because calories in/calories out is the primary driver of weight change, and the small caloric effects of fat oxidation rate during training are not meaningful.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Fat max training does not need to be targeted as a special zone; standard endurance riding at sustainable intensities accomplishes the same or greater adaptation
- In professional athletes, the fat max zone spans approximately 180–300W — a 120W range — making it practically impossible to "target" precisely
- Train for performance, not for substrate use; the adaptations that improve endurance also shift fat oxidation rates as a side effect, not the other way around

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The width of the fat max zone (180–300W range at the elite level) means individual variation is enormous; any fat max value is a rough central tendency
- The calcium/AMPK mechanism argument about adaptation is Kolie's synthesis of mechanistic literature; this is not directly tested in a single paper cited in this episode

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Riding at fat max does not give you special access to fat stores that other intensities deny; fat oxidation is not an adaptive training signal — it is simply what happens to be available as fuel
- A cell cannot distinguish "I should adapt because I'm burning fat" from "I should adapt because I'm burning glucose" — the adaptation signal comes from the mechanical stress and energy depletion, not from substrate composition
- Burning more fat during exercise does not cause more fat mass loss unless total caloric expenditure exceeds intake — the rate of oxidation during a 2-hour ride is trivial relative to daily caloric balance

### Evolving Views
Kolie notes that even well-intentioned fat oxidation research tends to conflate substrate use with adaptation, a confusion he explicitly tracks across multiple Watts Doc episodes.

- Design decisions at Zwift scale (millions of users) require conservative choices that sacrifice individual optimization for broad effectiveness and compliance

- Gaffney describes a 180-degree departure from Joe Friel-style rigid periodization toward athlete-centered, subjectively-regulated training — a shift that Kolie describes as uncommon in the broader coaching community

## Episode 9

**Filename:** `1233728686-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-37-your-fast-twitch-fibers-probably-as-aerobic-as-your-slow-twitch.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1233728686
**Word count:** ~14,671
**Series:** Watts Doc (episode 37)

### Core Message
This episode examines oxidative capacity in fast-twitch muscle fibers, challenging the assumption that type 2 fibers are inherently anaerobic. The key study involves cross-country skiers whose arms (40% type 1 fiber) and legs (58% type 1 fiber) were compared; despite the fiber type difference, citrate synthase activity was nearly identical (118 vs 111 units) across limbs, and HAD (hydroxyacyl-CoA dehydrogenase, a fat oxidation enzyme) was only 45% higher in legs. This shows that in well-trained athletes, type 2A fibers can match type 1 fibers in oxidative capacity. A 1987 paper is cited showing that at all exercise intensities — including sub-lactate threshold efforts — both type 1 AND type 2 fibers show metabolic signs of recruitment.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Do not assume your fast-twitch fibers are metabolically inferior; in trained athletes, type 2A fibers can have aerobic capacity equal to type 1
- The practical implication is that training volume and specificity develop aerobic capacity across all recruited fiber types, regardless of intrinsic fiber classification
- There is no need to specifically "train type 2 fibers aerobically" as a separate goal; general endurance training does this when loads are sufficient to recruit type 2 fibers

### Caveats & Qualifications
- These findings apply most clearly to well-trained endurance athletes (cross-country skiers); in sedentary populations, the type 1/type 2 oxidative capacity gap is much larger
- Fiber type classification by myosin heavy chain versus histochemical staining yields different proportions; the specific values cited depend on method
- The 1987 recruitment study uses metabolic markers as a proxy for fiber activation, not direct EMG measurement

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- The popular science claim that "slow-twitch fibers are aerobic and fast-twitch fibers are anaerobic" is a significant overstatement even in moderately trained populations, and nearly false in elite endurance athletes
- The cross-country skier data demonstrates that limb-specific training can equalize oxidative capacity across fiber types from very different starting points
- Sub-threshold intensities in trained athletes recruit fast-twitch fibers metabolically, contrary to the clean recruitment hierarchy implied by the size principle at steady-state intensities

### Evolving Views
This episode extends the arguments started in Watts Doc 12 (fiber type and substrate use) with more mechanistic evidence, and sets up subsequent episodes on p38 MAPK and low glycogen training by establishing that the adaptive signaling machinery is present in both fiber types.

## Episode 12

**Filename:** `1287323614-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-20-training-myths-part-1.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1287323614
**Word count:** ~9,193
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips (episode 20)

### Core Message
This episode debunks the training myth that big-gear, low-cadence cycling on the bike substitutes for strength training. Kolie explains the neuromechanical reasons why cycling cannot provide a genuine strength stimulus: cycling is a concentric-only movement, muscles begin in a lengthened position only briefly (not under the stretch required for eccentric tension), and the complex kinetic chain of the lower body during cycling dissipates mechanical load rather than concentrating it in a single muscle. Without large mechanical tension under stretch, there is no meaningful strength adaptation signal. Isometric contractions at high cadence are also insufficient.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Big-gear work on the bike does not substitute for strength training; if strength adaptation is a goal, use actual gym-based resistance training with eccentric loading (squats, deadlifts, lunges)
- Low cadence / big gear cycling can still be useful for neuromuscular cadence variety (as discussed in other episodes) but provides minimal force production adaptation
- Athletes who want both cycling performance and strength improvements should treat them as separate training modalities with separate stimuli

### Caveats & Qualifications
- This argument applies specifically to strength adaptation; big-gear cycling can still develop cadence patterns and specific neuromuscular recruitment pathways useful for cycling
- Some research suggests big-gear work may produce mild strength-related adaptations in untrained populations, but these appear minimal compared to traditional resistance training

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- The cycling-as-strength-training belief persists in coaching culture despite the clear biomechanical arguments against it; Kolie frames this as one of the most persistently wrong ideas in the sport
- Isometric contraction (holding high force at a fixed joint angle, as in cycling power peaks) is insufficient for strength gain compared to full range-of-motion eccentric loading
- The kinetic chain argument: force applied at the pedal is distributed across hip, knee, and ankle joints, reducing the stimulus at any single muscle group below the threshold for hypertrophy or strength adaptation

### Evolving Views
This is part 1 of a myths series; Kolie signals additional myths will be covered in future episodes. His strength training views are developed further in cluster 0 (Strength Training) episodes.

## Episode 13

**Filename:** `1338356839-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-17-evolution-vs-revolution-with-mikael-eriksson.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1338356839
**Word count:** ~20,240
**Series:** Perspectives (episode 17)

### Core Message
Scientific Triathlon podcast host Mikael Eriksson joins for a coaching philosophy exchange covering triathlon periodization, the science-practice gap in endurance coaching, aerotesting methodology, and the role of self-criticism in coach development. Mikael represents a deliberate, evidence-based approach to triathlon training that prioritizes zone 1 easy work, conservative hard sessions, and never training to absolute limits in hard sessions. The conversation explores where Kolie's cycling-specific approach and Mikael's triathlon approach converge and diverge.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Hard sessions should be performed at RPE 7–8, never to the absolute limit; leaving something in reserve is not timidity but essential for consistent quality across a training week
- Easy sessions should be genuinely easy — zone 1 — not the comfortable mid-range drift that many athletes default to
- Aerotesting with GPS and power meter simultaneously can achieve approximately 1% reliability for aerodynamic drag estimates with proper protocol; this is close enough for practical training and equipment decisions
- The science-coaching gap: coaches who study the literature carefully often find that optimal scientific protocols are impractical for real athletes with real lives; the best coaches synthesize science with pragmatic application
- Self-criticism is identified as the single most important trait for long-term coaching development

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Mikael's recommendations are framed around triathlon, where managing three disciplines simultaneously creates different recovery constraints than single-sport cycling
- Aerotesting reliability figures (~1%) are achievable under controlled conditions; real-world variability from wind, road surface, and fatigue can be much larger
- The "never to the limit" recommendation may not apply to race-specific practice or test efforts

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Conservative hard sessions (RPE 7–8, not to failure) may produce equivalent or superior adaptation to maximal-effort sessions because they allow sustainable training frequency without excessive recovery demand
- Self-criticism — the willingness to examine one's own mistakes and failures without ego protection — is identified as rarer and more valuable in coaches than technical knowledge
- Aerodynamic testing with consumer hardware is now close enough in reliability to distinguish meaningful equipment differences, democratizing aero optimization

### Evolving Views
Mikael and Kolie compare notes on how their respective coaching philosophies have evolved from rigid, plan-centric approaches toward more athlete-responsive, feedback-driven models.

## Episode 14

**Filename:** `1376019016-empiricalcyclingpodcast-400k-ama.txt`
**Numeric ID:** 1376019016
**Word count:** ~26,791
**Series:** Uncategorized (AMA milestone episode)

### Core Message
A Q&A episode celebrating 400,000 podcast downloads covers a wide range of training questions from Instagram followers. Topics include: the physiology of negative split endurance rides, fasted training, aerobic contribution at short durations, double day training structure, block vs. linear periodization, splitting intervals across two sessions, and the appropriate training approach for athletes with under 5 hours per week. Kolie's answers reflect his accumulated coaching experience and are consistently evidence-based.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Negative split endurance rides: physiologically sound because fat availability increases as a ride continues (as glycogen depletes, fat oxidation rises) and this conserves glycogen for the back half; not necessary to prescribe for everyone but has a defensible mechanism
- Fasted training: does not improve performance outcomes; may cause some athletes to train at lower intensities and miss adaptation signals; generally not recommended
- Aerobic contribution at 60 seconds or more: is substantial — even at durations of 60–90 seconds, aerobic metabolism contributes the majority of energy; athletes overestimate the anaerobic contribution at these durations
- Double days: sprint session first, then lift / endurance second is more effective than the reverse; separating the sessions by at least 2–3 hours allows partial recovery and allows both sessions to be of higher quality
- Block periodization vs. linear: block periodization (concentrated loading of one quality at a time) tends to produce better results for time-limited athletes and experienced athletes; linear periodization is fine for beginners
- Splitting intervals across two sessions: suboptimal but better than missing intervals entirely; quality of the second session will be lower, but the accumulated volume and stimulus are still partially effective
- Under 5 hours per week: prioritize enjoyment and consistency over structured periodization; marginal physiological gains from highly structured training are not worth the cognitive and motivational cost at this volume

### Caveats & Qualifications
- AMA format means questions vary in specificity; answers are necessarily general
- Individual athlete context (training history, goals, life stress) should modulate all of these general recommendations
- Fasted training data comes largely from short-term studies; very long-term effects are less certain

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Aerobic contribution even at 60 seconds is larger than most athletes believe, undermining the premise that "short intervals are purely anaerobic"
- For athletes under 5 hours per week, the primary intervention should be making training enjoyable enough to sustain rather than optimizing the physiological stimulus per hour
- Splitting intervals is presented pragmatically as "better than nothing" rather than condemned — an unusually non-dogmatic stance on training integrity

### Evolving Views
Kolie has evolved toward less prescriptive, more athlete-responsive coaching across the podcast's arc; this AMA reflects a notably practical and humble tone compared to earlier, more rule-based episodes.

**Core Message:**
FTP and TTE (time to exhaustion at FTP) are two independent, separable dimensions of threshold performance. It is possible to improve one without the other, or to improve TTE while FTP stays flat. Practitioners should measure and track both explicitly rather than treating a rising FTP as evidence of complete threshold development.

**Key Training Recommendations:**
- Measure TTE explicitly by testing how long the athlete can sustain FTP-level power; do not infer TTE from FTP alone
- TTE training (extended threshold work, e.g., long sweet-spot or sustained FTP intervals) can increase the duration an athlete can hold threshold power without raising FTP
- However, extended TTE training carries the risk of "entrenching" performance at a fixed FTP level — the athlete becomes more durable but does not get faster
- Once TTE is adequate for the athlete's goals (event demands), shift training emphasis toward raising FTP (VO2max work, higher intensity intervals)
- Avoid spending excessive time extending TTE far beyond what the target event requires; excess TTE work has diminishing returns relative to raising ceiling power
- After a TTE-focused block, allow sufficient recovery before FTP testing, as TTE work accumulates substantial fatigue

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- "Adequate" TTE is event-dependent: a 20-minute criterium needs far less TTE than a 3-hour road race or a gravel event
- Athletes with very short TTE (e.g., under 30 minutes at FTP) should prioritize building TTE before attempting to raise FTP, as the threshold itself is not stable enough to be a useful training target
- There is substantial individual variation in the trainability of TTE vs. FTP; some athletes improve TTE easily and FTP slowly, and vice versa
- TTE measurement requires a legitimate all-out effort at FTP, which has recovery costs and should not be done frequently

**Contrarian / Non-Obvious Claims:**
- A flat FTP over many months does not mean the athlete is not improving — they may be extending TTE significantly, which translates to better race results without a wattage increase
- Conversely, an athlete can raise FTP through VO2max work while TTE actually shortens — they become faster but less durable at their new threshold; this is an underappreciated failure mode
- Training to extend TTE can actually prevent FTP from rising by anchoring the athlete's physiology to a specific sustainable output rather than stimulating the higher-intensity adaptations that drive ceiling power up

**Evolving Views:**
- Hosts describe TTE as a relatively underappreciated metric in mainstream cycling culture, which tends to treat FTP as the primary summary statistic
- Emphasis on measuring both dimensions explicitly is presented as a more sophisticated, less common coaching practice

- Breathing-based proxies for VO2max work require some experience to calibrate — very unfit athletes may breathe hard at any intensity

**File:** `2158767849-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-62-why-ftp-isnt-for-setting-training-zones-and-alternative-approaches.txt`

**Core Message:**
FTP is a legitimate and useful performance metric for threshold training, fitness tracking, freshness assessment, and pacing — but it is the wrong reference point for setting over-threshold training prescriptions. - Even RPE-based calibration requires experience; newer athletes may not accurately perceive where their effort sits relative to physiological thresholds
- The 45-85% FTP range for LT1 is a broad empirical observation from coaching practice, not a precisely cited study number; individual outliers likely exist at both tails
- Over-threshold prescriptions based on eye-levels or RPE still require iteration and monitoring; there is no perfect formula

**Contrarian / Non-Obvious Claims:**
- FTP is explicitly not designed for zone setting across the full intensity spectrum — this contradicts how most training platforms (TrainerRoad, Zwift, Garmin) use FTP as the basis for all zone calculations

### Episode: Ten Minute Tips 70 — Fitness Beyond FTP
**File:** `2243291618-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-70-fitness-beyond-ftp.txt`

**Core Message:**
FTP and VO2max are necessary but insufficient for racing success. Each discipline (road racing, criteriums, cyclocross, mountain bike cross-country, ultra/gravel) has specific fitness demands that require targeted training beyond general aerobic preparation. Gap analysis — comparing an athlete's current fitness profile against the specific demands of their target event and role — is the individualization framework that coaches use to identify what to train next.

**Key Training Recommendations:**

*General principles:*
- General aerobic prep (endurance + FTP + VO2max) is the assumed foundation; discipline-specific training builds on top of it
- Specific workouts carry high fatigue cost and cannot be done continuously; timing relative to target races is critical
- Strength training (particularly heavy lifting) is broadly applicable: clients with minimal >120% FTP training experience often see PR 1-minute powers after starting strength work, even before increasing high-intensity cycling volume

*Road racing / criteriums:*
- Repeated short hard efforts: 30/30s, 40/20s, 20/20s, 15/15s — paced as hard as you can sustain consistently for the full set (e.g., 10-minute block); the goal is a flat power curve across all efforts, not a PR first effort
- Build set duration from 3-4 minutes up to 40-60 minutes to match race duration
- Over-under threshold intervals: sitting at or just below threshold with 10-30 second punches above, then recovering back to threshold — replicates crit dynamics
- Sprints from race speed (25-30 mph), not from a standing start — the mechanical demand of a sprint at speed is qualitatively different from a slow-speed sprint
- Pre-fatigued efforts (threshold into hard effort into sprint): replicates the late-race physiological state

*Cyclocross / cross-country mountain bike:*
- VO2max efforts (3-5 min) to prepare for the hard first 15 minutes; settle into a sustainable pace thereafter
- Hot laps (course-specific repeated efforts with longer recoveries) are the most transferable training for CX/XCO; 5-10 second short sharp efforts replicate off-road effort profile better than 30/30s
- Skills work under fatigue: practice technical riding (cornering, descending, obstacles) while already in the red — this is specifically missing from most athletes' training
- Hard start followed by settling: practice going all-out at the beginning then finding threshold pace (reverse of road race dynamics)

*Ultra / gravel:*
- TTE (time to exhaustion at FTP): increasing how long an athlete can sustain threshold power is the most time-efficient proxy for ultra performance improvement when total ride duration is time-limited
- Consecutive long-day blocks: 2-3 consecutive days of multi-hour riding at low-moderate intensity — the consecutive aspect reveals nutritional and recovery deficits that single long days do not
- Sweet spot over 5-6+ hour rides: building the ability to sustain sweet spot for extended durations in a single session
- Nutrition practice is explicitly listed as a training intervention: dialing in fueling strategy, gas station food options for multi-day events, and carbohydrate intake volume are trainable skills

*Applies to all disciplines:*
- Sprint training (short efforts, neuromuscular) — applicable to any mass-start race regardless of discipline
- Race-skill work: pack skills, cornering, drafting, reading the race; watching race footage; going to clinics; having experienced athletes share their tactics — framed as having a bigger impact on results for many athletes than any fitness improvement
- Opportunity cost analysis: always weigh whether additional specific training (e.g., sprint work) is worth displacing general aerobic training; for aerobically undertrained athletes, more FTP/VO2max work typically outweighs specificity

**Caveats & Qualifications:**
- The Hickson-style repeated hard-effort workouts (group ride + threshold + sprints) are reserved for occasional use; doing them every week leads to breakdown
- Normalized power being low in CX is expected and does not indicate a poor training week — off-road courses with many short efforts produce low NP even when the race felt very hard
- Ultra race wins are heavily determined by non-fitness factors (navigation, bike mechanicals, nutrition execution, pacing, sleep strategy) — an average FTP with excellent non-fitness execution often beats a high FTP with poor execution

**Contrarian / Non-Obvious Claims:**
- This type of repeated hard effort training (e.g., 30/30s, hot laps) better predicts race outcomes than FTP for criteriums and CX — Cory explicitly states that between two athletes with the same FTP, the one with higher 30/30s power will almost always have better race results
- Dehydration amplifies EAMC risk by increasing relative exercise intensity as seen by the muscles — even without being a direct cause, it is a two-to-three-step-removed predictor of cramping; ties back to Episode 59
- Motivation as a fatigue indicator: Erica's comment that the word "motivation" in workout notes was Cory's signal she was approaching the edge — intrinsic motivation dip is presented as an early fatigue signal, not a character flaw

**Evolving Views:**
- Coaches express that the FTP-centric conversation in cycling media obscures how much of racing performance is discipline-specific and FTP-agnostic
- Gap analysis (comparing athlete profile to event demands) is presented as the standard individualization tool that most self-coached athletes lack; this is the argument for coaching beyond providing a workout library
