## Episode: 1943996043-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-43-off-season-breaks-and-massive-listener-qa.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1943996043
**Word count:** 22,828
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
This roundtable of Empirical Cycling coaches (Kolie Moore, James, Megan, Erica, GC, Corey) discusses how to approach the off-season break, emphasizing that effective rest is the goal — not just time off. The panel stresses that the purpose of the off-season is mental and physical recharge, not the preservation of fitness, and that athletes (especially masters) who skip genuine rest often pay for it later in the season. The episode also covers a wide listener Q&A on FTP testing, strength training RPE limits, sprint and anaerobic capacity timing, volume vs. intensity, and coaching philosophy.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Off-season activity level:** Masters athletes (50s–60s) should aim for ~6–8 hours/week at moderate intensity during their off-season to minimize fitness loss; fitness is harder to rebuild with age.
- **Off-season gym:** You can go straight into strength training after the cycling season ends without waiting for a bike-specific rest period; gym work does not appear to impede recovery from accumulated cycling fatigue.
- **Strength training RPE cap:** Keep heavy strength sessions at RPE 7 (out of 10) routinely. Going to RPE 9–10 every session requires a rest week every third week and leads to a required month of rest after 3–4 months. The minimum effective dose cited is 3–6 sets at ~RPE 7 at 1–6 reps. Coach Kolie's personal maintenance dose: 3 sets of 1 rep per week of single-leg squat at RPE 6–7 is sufficient to maintain strength.
- **FTP indoors vs. outdoors:** Expect indoor FTP to run ~10–15 watts lower than outdoor for many athletes (one client example: 305W outdoor / 290W a struggle indoors). Substitute sweet spot for FTP work indoors rather than trying to force target outdoor FTP watts.
- **Sprint training timing:** Sprint efforts (keep them ≤10 seconds for true peak power development) can be trained year-round if it is a weakness; from a periodization standpoint, ~6 weeks before racing is sufficient if it is not a primary limiter.
- **Anaerobic capacity timing:** Anaerobic capacity peaks quickly — expect 4–6 workouts to near-max it if you have been doing some intensity, 6–10 from scratch. It also decays quickly, requiring ~1 session/week or every 1.5 weeks to maintain. Recommendation: build it close to target races rather than early in the season when you'd burn a lot of sessions just maintaining it.
- **Volume vs. intensity:** Extra time should almost always be filled with volume, not intensity. The intensity any individual can handle is relatively capped; volume capacity can be substantially grown. The more aerobic volume you carry, the larger the response to any subsequent interval work.
- **Recovery ride pacing:** Recovery rides done at ~65% FTP (e.g., 200W for a ~300W FTP athlete) are already too hard for true recovery. A 200W recovery ride on a 300W FTP is flagged as a top-tier coaching mistake.
- **Monitoring fatigue via good days:** Watch whether your good days are rare. If they are, that is a sign of accumulated fatigue; good days should not be few and far between.
- **Post-off-season ramp-up cue:** Wait until you are burning to train again, then wait "a little bit more." Use the itch-to-return as the start signal, not the end of a fixed calendar period.
- **Training backwards from goal race:** For self-coached athletes, start from the goal race date and work backwards to determine how much off-season you can actually afford.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Off-season rest duration varies enormously by individual: a low-volume rider might need only 1–2 weeks; a high-volume athlete who raced heavily may need a month or more.
- Physical fatigue may take longer to shed than mental fatigue — an athlete who feels motivated may still be physically depleted.
- The ~10-watt indoor vs. outdoor FTP gap is athlete-specific; heat, inertia differences in the trainer, and pedaling pattern all contribute.
- Anaerobic capacity as a limiter may be structural (muscle mass, rate-of-force development) rather than purely trainable; skinny climbers have a hard ceiling here.
- The "ride at the pace you could do for 8 hours" endurance cue is not appropriate for athletes already doing 6+ hour rides, who may actually be able to self-destruct at "8-hour pace."
- Whether 10 days off counts as an off-season depends entirely on what training looked like before it and what follows it. If training looks identical before and after, it was just a rest week, not an off-season.
- Altitude training: ~100 hours at altitude yield approximately 1% hematocrit improvement (rule of thumb, acknowledged as approximate). Adaptation disappears quickly after returning to sea level.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- You can likely begin heavy-ish strength training immediately after your race season ends without needing a bike-specific recovery window first — gym work seems well-tolerated even on accumulated cycling fatigue.
- Typical FTP loss over the off-season is at most ~10 watts, and often zero. Significant loss from off-season rest is rare. Coaches often see athletes increase FTP after rest, partly because pre-rest FTP was suppressed by fatigue.
- A client with a very short training age had a 5-minute power only 2–3 watts off his PR after a two-week rest — the fitness security blanket fear is largely unjustified.
- Sprints for peak power development should be ≤10 seconds; 30-second "sprints" in the scientific literature are not sprints in the peak-power sense and do not develop peak power meaningfully.
- Well-trained athletes see progressively smaller adaptations to high-intensity and sprint-intensity training; endurance training adaptations extend beyond what high-intensity training provides at advanced training ages (citing a meta-analysis discussed in the episode).

### Evolving Views
- Coach Kolie notes that for athletes who lose fitness easily, a small amount (~2 rides/week) of low-intensity activity during the off-season is preferred over complete rest — this represents a nuanced departure from a blanket "take full time off" prescription.
- James describes an ongoing n=1 self-experiment: after two years building from ~6–7 hours/week to ~17–18 hours/week, he found that day-to-day and within-ride intensity tolerance improved substantially — suggesting that the relationship between volume and intensity capacity is bidirectional and trainable.

## Episode: 2073000300-empiricalcyclingpodcast-the-minute-tips-54-the-truth-about-junk-miles.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2073000300
**Word count:** 12,691
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
Kolie Moore, GC, and Gediminas examine the concept of "junk miles" from multiple angles, ultimately arguing that the term is largely a red herring. Their working definition of genuinely junk training is: "anything that adds fatigue without any real benefit" — and they note that mental health, enjoyment, and motivation are legitimate benefits. The episode examines how riding too hard on "easy" days leads to a gradual "fatigue security blanket" that athletes fail to recognize until months later. Volume is positioned as a major driver of adaptation at any intensity, and enjoyment is defended as valid training rationale.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Pace endurance rides by duration, not zone:** The longer the ride, the lower the watts should be. GC's coaching cue: ask athletes how many watts they can average for 3 hours (~200W typical), then whether they can hold that for 5–6 hours. The answer is usually no. The alternative — 150W for 5–6 hours — is almost always achievable and is a better endurance stimulus. 5–6 hours at 150W > 3 hours at 200W for endurance development.
- **Training camp / big volume week pacing:** When doubling weekly volume (e.g., 10 hours → 20–25 hours), pace it easy enough to be sustainable. Kolie recommends athletes who do 10 hours/week can stretch to ~150–200% of normal volume (15–20 hours) for a camp week. Athletes doing 15 hours regularly might stretch to ~22–25 hours. Athletes doing climbing camps should target the lower end of those ranges because terrain enforces tempo effort. Pace such that you finish the week without needing more than 2–3 days of recovery.
- **Fitness measure after a volume week:** Don't expect a large FTP bump; expect a "more aerobic" feeling and improved fatigue resistance. These are harder to measure but are genuine adaptations.
- **FTP maintenance during race season:** A 6×5-minute FTP maintenance workout is recommended for mid-race-season to keep threshold sharp without excessive fatigue. Kolie describes this as his personal favorite maintenance session: "Give it a shot — it hits what you need without adding too much fatigue."
- **Group rides — apply them purposefully:** Group rides are not automatically junk. Value depends on: (1) skill gap — pack handling, cornering, and race-pace accelerations cannot be fully replicated in structured training; (2) fitness level — less-trained athletes get real fitness from group rides; more-trained athletes may find structured training delivers more stimulus per hour. In Miami flat group rides (50+ riders), even well-trained athletes may only average ~150W in the middle of the pack — closer to junk than training. The prescription: if you're going to do a group ride as a structured workout substitute, make it genuinely hard, not a social roll.
- **Periodize fun intentionally:** Explicitly schedule unstructured rides as legitimate training. After 4–5 months of consistent winter structured training, going for group rides or fun rides is appropriate and beneficial. Trying to maintain perfect structure year-round increases stress to the point where the net cost exceeds the training benefit.
- **Endurance rides paced too hard — chronic fatigue pattern:** Riding endurance at 70–75% FTP (often 200W regardless of FTP) accumulates chronic fatigue over months, not days. The warning sign is comment tone in training logs shifting gradually from "felt great, could have done more" to "glad I survived." Monitoring qualitative feedback over months is essential.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- "Junk miles" is highly context-dependent: the same ride could be junk for a well-trained athlete but valuable for a newer one.
- Climbing vacations / high-tempo volume weeks: the quality of endurance miles matters — flat easy miles vs. rolling terrain at forced tempo are not equivalent. Adjust target volume downward when terrain will push intensity higher.
- "Riding too hard on commutes" is a real category of junk: one athlete with a 360W FTP was averaging ~280W twice daily on a 30-minute commute, five days a week. Coach could not convince him to ride easier; he burnt out and quit competitive cycling before the end of the training block.
- Perfect TrainingPeaks compliance (all green) should raise a coach's suspicion, not admiration. Gediminas describes achieving 100% compliance in his first coached year and being mentally and physically destroyed by end of season, needing a month off and still not wanting to look at the bike.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Total training volume is a major driver of adaptation and intensity is "almost irrelevant" for the adaptations from endurance riding specifically (referencing an AMPK meta-analysis). Practical implication: not being in a specific zone does not make an easy ride junk.
- The 75% FTP line between "Zone 2" and "Zone 3" is arbitrary; training zones are descriptive, not prescriptive. Allen Coggan, who popularized the zones, reportedly believed that endurance rides below 65% FTP were not worth doing — the coaches explicitly call this "absolute bullshit."
- One to two training rides after a pandemic lockdown caused athletes who stopped commuting to lose noticeable fitness — suggesting those commutes, which most would label "junk miles," were providing meaningful aerobic stimulus.
- "Tempo = sweet spot = FTP" for early-phase training adaptations: in the first months of a build, all of these intensities lead to roughly equivalent mitochondrial adaptations (based on whole-muscle homogenate data). Which one you use matters less than whether you do them consistently and progress them.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie identifies the "fatigue security blanket" as a dangerous cognitive pattern: athletes who have trained heavily develop a sense that accumulated fatigue = fitness, and double down on volume when fitness slides instead of pulling back. Coaches must counter this by pointing to longitudinal qualitative feedback (comment tone, subjective feel), not just power numbers.
- GC notes that growing up in Miami's group ride culture — daily unsanctioned street races with 50+ people — shaped an early bias toward group rides as training, which structured training systematically outperformed. His evolution: use group rides with specific purpose (e.g., attacking to generate a hard interval) rather than as passive training.

## Episode: 2150935608-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-61-coach-qa-on-limiters-low-volume-rest-weeks-resuming-training-under-optimized-training-more.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2150935608

### Core Message
A coach roundtable (Kolie, Megan, Gediminas, Alex, Erica) tackles listener questions about limiters, big volume weeks, under-optimized training practices, and the pitfalls of sporadic intervals. The overarching theme is that most recreational athletes under-optimize the fundamentals — nutrition, sleep, qualitative self-monitoring, and mental engagement with hard efforts — while over-optimizing arbitrary metrics. The episode is one of the most comprehensive discussions of recovery micro-practices and the "macro vs. micro" training mindset in the podcast.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Big volume weeks — safe ranges:**
  - Athletes doing 10 hours/week can safely stretch to ~15 hours (150% of normal) for a camp week; going to 20–25 hours is likely too much without a coach directing recovery.
  - Athletes doing 15 hours/week can stretch to ~22–25 hours.
  - If a climbing destination forces higher terrain intensity, reduce total hours target further.
  - Practical preparation: do all grocery shopping before the week begins; buy lots of energy-dense food (explicit recommendation: stock ice cream and high-calorie snacks). Coming home from a 6-hour ride to an empty fridge causes athletes to skip eating and crash.
- **Sporadic vs. structured intervals:** Sporadic "ride hard when I feel like it" intervals that accumulate the same time-in-zone as structured intervals can produce similar acute adaptations, BUT: (1) they cannot be progressively overloaded in a systematic way, (2) coaches cannot identify the cause when they stop working, (3) athletes cannot assess whether they are improving their TTE at a given power. Recommendation: structured intervals allow a logical framework for progression and diagnosis.
- **Under-optimized practice #1 — nutrition:**
  - Do not let >5–6 hours elapse without eating on a training day. Athletes who skip lunch and train in the evening will not catch up on fueling by workout time.
  - Keep non-perishable snacks (e.g., Larabars, granola bars) at your desk or in your bag for days when food access is unpredictable.
  - Endurance athletes doing intermittent fasting: the literature may show similar strength/hypertrophy gains for intermittent fasting in sedentary populations, but fasting with high-energy training (thousands of additional kCal/day) is genuinely harmful for recovery and long-term adaptation.
- **Under-optimized practice #2 — sleep environment:** During heat waves, actively cool the bedroom. Sleeping too warm impairs recovery and causes workout quality to drop noticeably within days. Use AC at night, even if culturally it feels unusual. For racing travel: bring earplugs, an eye mask, and a neck pillow.
- **Under-optimized practice #3 — qualitative self-monitoring:** Self-coached athletes almost never formally track qualitative signals (mood, motivation, perceived effort, sleep quality, general stress). Coaches ask for this information from athletes 20–50% of the time in consultations. Recommendation: log a brief daily comment on how you feel, not just power numbers. Trends in comment tone over 2–3 months are among the most actionable coaching data available.
- **Under-optimized practice #4 — mental engagement during hard efforts:** During VO2max, sprint, and threshold efforts, actively engage with the effort — cadence, power production, breathing — rather than just trying to survive it. This engagement may produce meaningfully higher watts and better race-readiness for suffering. For strength training: the final reps closest to failure are the most valuable; slow down and lean into those reps rather than rushing through.
- **Under-optimized practice #5 — macro-level perspective:** Individual bad workouts (especially in heat or high-stress weeks) should not trigger doubt about the entire season. The relevant signal is the trend over weeks and the whole training block, not any single session.
- **Interval progression rule:** To prevent plateau from sporadic training, intervals must be progressively lengthened over time. Example: if FTP intervals start at 8 minutes, advance to 10 → 12 → 15 minutes. Stagnating at the same time-in-zone at the same power is the most common reason sporadic training stops working.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Doubling volume for a camp week works for athletes who are otherwise consistent. An inconsistent athlete suddenly doing 20 hours may not recover quickly enough to resume training.
- The "all roads lead to PGC-1α" principle (any training stress drives aerobic adaptation) is described as "roughly true but one-dimensional" — useful as a mindset anchor but not a complete framework.
- VO2max zone time-in-zone from a group ride or cyclocross race does not equal focused VO2max intervals; heart rate high without sustained progressive effort does not drive VO2max adaptation in well-trained athletes.
- Sporadic intervals can work as a limited method, especially for advanced athletes who use them within a logical framework (e.g., timed hot-lap circuits with tracked normalized power and consistent route).
- Strength and hypertrophy gains are roughly equivalent between intermittent fasting and normal eating in strength/hypertrophy literature — but this does not transfer to endurance athletes with large daily energy expenditures.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Coach Kolie states he has reduced formal FTP testing for athletes because a well-designed training plan already surfaces fitness trends — testing burns energy that is better saved for race day. For athletes with consistently structured training, direct testing may be unnecessary.
- When a client has a history of performing better after more rest than planned, Kolie's strategy is to deliberately program less and see benefits. One example: a client who repeatedly auto-regulated rest against the plan and went on to UCI podiums — the coach credits learning the athlete's individual rest needs from observing that auto-regulation.
- Nutrition is framed as the highest-leverage under-optimization for most recreational cyclists, above gadgets, training plans, or interval design.

### Evolving Views
- Gediminas describes a personal lesson from his first coached year: 100% training plan compliance led to end-of-season physical and mental breakdown, requiring a month off and a loss of enthusiasm for the sport. He now immediately flags clients who show all-green TrainingPeaks calendars as potential over-compliers, not model athletes.
- Alex notes that off-the-shelf training plans often consist of "random hard riding every week" with no progressive focus or coherent framework — a pattern he identifies as one of the most common traps athletes fall into before seeking professional coaching.

## Episode: 2269775624-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-73-things-we.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2269775624
**Word count:** 9,888
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
Kolie Moore, Erica, and Will discuss things they wish they had known earlier about training, racing, and coaching, followed by curated listener submissions on the same topic. The episode converges on three core regrets: (1) massively under-fueling on-bike in their formative years (both absolute calories and carbohydrates specifically), (2) treating training plans as rigid mandates rather than flexible frameworks, and (3) in racing, not using process goals rather than result goals to accelerate skill development. On the coaching side, the panel emphasizes that individual differences in motivation and compliance style are as important as physiology.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **On-bike fueling (historical anchor):** The old standard of ~25g carbohydrate per hour (e.g., one gel every 45 minutes) is inadequate. Modern recommendation: eat enough that you return home feeling roughly the same as when you left. The goal is to not dig an energy hole during the ride. Practical cue: if you are thinking about what you'll eat for dinner during a 3-hour ride, you are not eating enough.
- **Carbohydrates on-bike:** Fueling adequately on long rides allows the same session length with less recovery cost, meaning more consistent quality training over weeks. The coaches link inadequate on-bike fueling directly to poor training week consistency and prolonged fatigue.
- **Auto-regulation:** Training plans are described as "written in sand" — a stiff breeze (illness, work stress, injury) should move them without guilt. Key recommendation: if energy, sleep, or external stressors signal it is not a good day to train hard, move the interval session 1–2 days forward rather than forcing it. The coaches estimate that forcing a bad session causes more harm than moving it does.
- **Weight management for cyclists:** Race weight is highly individual. Kolie describes reaching his lightest weight ever (approximately 155 lbs at ~8% body fat, compared to his optimal racing weight of ~173 lbs) and PR'ing a local climb that day, then being "garbage for two months" as his body rebounded. The lesson: aggressive weight loss below an individual optimum delivers a short-lived performance peak at the cost of extended underperformance. His personal experience suggests 8% body fat was his absolute floor; best racing was at 173 lbs.
- **Strength training for masters athletes (50+ female example):** Strength training is "very, very, very underrated" for late-30s and older cyclists, both for health reasons and performance. It appears especially beneficial for athletes who do most of their riding in the FTP-and-below range (i.e., not much high-intensity) — the neural recruitment stimulus from heavy lifting transfers to bike performance for this group.
- **Interval progression — TTE focus:** Extending time-to-exhaustion (TTE) at sweet spot / threshold power was identified as a key personal training breakthrough (Erica). Progression: extend interval durations as fueling improves and fitness builds.
- **LT1 training skepticism:** Kolie states he is an "LT1 training skeptic." His position: LT1 is best raised by doing lots of hours well below it, not by targeting LT1 specifically. He has never observed a case where LT1-specific work produced a transformational response that other endurance riding would not have. He describes current LT1 training interest as a "training fad."
- **Racing — process goals over result goals:** When learning new race skills (sprinting, breakaway timing, lead-out mechanics), deliberately set process goals rather than result goals. Example from Kolie: when learning to sprint, contest every preem to practice set-up, then sit up in the final. Teams learning lead-outs should accept a regression in individual results over half a season to build the mechanics correctly — the investment pays off.
- **Performance variability — normal range:** Some week-to-week and day-to-day variation in FTP/power output is expected. However, an FTP that seems to swing 20+ watts regularly is a flag, not just normal variability — something systematic is off (fatigue, nutrition, sleep). Persistent downward trends over months are a yellow/red flag regardless of magnitude.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The coaches note that "what a cyclist should look like" as a body type ideal is still present in cycling culture, even if less prominent than a decade ago — weight management messaging has improved but has not disappeared.
- The flexibility benefit of training plan auto-regulation is athlete-dependent: some athletes genuinely thrive on rigid structure (it reduces their stress); coaches should not impose an auto-regulation philosophy on athletes who find structure intrinsically motivating.
- "Mental toughness" from deliberately hard type-2-fun rides may have some value for developing mental resilience, but the coaches are skeptical it is worth seeking out proactively; it tends to find you anyway via bad weather, mechanical issues, etc.
- Eating more on-bike does not automatically mean eating less off-bike; one listener submission noted that eating more while riding actually led to weight loss — the coaches speculate this may be because under-fueling on the bike leads to compensatory overeating and mental stress eating afterwards.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- "Hours are more important than miles" — framing endurance training by duration rather than distance is explicitly advocated, with running cited as a sport that mostly does it wrong (calculating in miles). Duration directly reflects physiologic stimulus; miles depend on terrain, grade, and speed.
- Eating more on-bike can lead to weight loss off-bike (listener observation, coaches acknowledge as plausible — under-fueling during training may cause stress-driven overeating afterward).
- There are only ~5 physiologically distinct workout types worth doing — Tim Cusick's framing cited approvingly. The implication is that elaborate coach-designed workouts are often just variations on a very small underlying set of stimuli, and complexity does not equal effectiveness.
- "I need less compliance from you" — Kolie has told clients directly that too-high compliance is a red flag. Training plans are not designed to be executed perfectly every session; auto-regulation is part of the design.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie identifies a past error in early coaching: not knowing how to incorporate qualitative athlete feedback into plan adjustments, and not teaching athletes to read their own signals. His coaching philosophy has shifted toward explicitly coaching interoception — the ability to recognize and act on internal physical and mental signals — as a core skill.
- Will describes significant racing development coming from a single 20-minute Pro race at Athens Twilight Criterium: more learning in 20 minutes than in the previous 10 years of racing. The lesson drawn: pushing into unfamiliar, high-stakes situations accelerates tactical development faster than years of comfortable racing.
- The panel collectively describes the shift away from fasted training and fat-burning-zone mythology (dominant ~2010–2015) as a genuine improvement in endurance sports culture, while noting it has not entirely disappeared.
