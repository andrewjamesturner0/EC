### Core Message
This is the inaugural episode of the podcast, focused on how to test FTP accurately and why most common testing methods systematically overestimate FTP for anaerobic riders. Kolie argues that the standard Hunter Allen 20-minute test (take 95% of 20-min power) is too inflated for anaerobic or sprint-oriented riders, whose 20-min power may be significantly above their actual aerobic threshold. The "correct" FTP is the inflection point on the power curve above which fatigue accumulates faster and below which it accumulates slower. The preferred approach is a longer, open-ended test (40–70 minutes) performed at a sustainable pace, letting the athlete feel out threshold by RPE rather than chasing a number.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Use a longer FTP test: ride 40–70 minutes at what feels sustainable. Start deliberately below perceived FTP for the first 10–20 minutes, then ramp gently to the edge of "I can hold this for a long time."
- Do NOT use the 2×8-minute test or ramp test as your primary FTP tool if you have significant anaerobic power. For anaerobic riders, the 8-minute test can produce FTPs that are 15–50 watts above actual threshold (host's own data: ~270 W FTP from an 8-min test that yielded 310–320 W).
- The 20-minute test without the 5-minute blowout is particularly problematic for anaerobic riders, who may get an FTP that is 88–90% of 20-minute power rather than the assumed 95%.
- FTP intervals should be assigned at ~10 watts below FTP to reduce fatigue and allow more total training volume.
- If your FTP is correctly set, a 2×20-minute FTP workout should be very accomplishable, not soul-crushing. If it feels near-impossible, your FTP is likely set too high.
- TTE (time to exhaustion at FTP) ranges from ~30 minutes in early training to 70+ minutes in well-trained athletes; this variability makes "FTP = one-hour power" an oversimplification.
- Look for the inflection point in the power curve when assessing FTP, not a fixed percentage of a short test.
- Ramp tests are even more unreliable than 8-minute tests for FTP; they require blood lactate data to be valid, as used by Team Sky (who were not just "doing a ramp test on their trainer").
- If someone sets a new long effort in a race or long ride, that effort can serve as an FTP data point when examined in context of the full power curve in WKO4/5.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The 95% factor for the 20-minute test only applies on average; for aerobic-dominant riders (ultra-endurance, triathletes who rarely go over threshold), the correction factor may actually be smaller (20-min power closer to real FTP). For highly anaerobic riders, the correction may need to be 90–92%.
- FTP is not always one hour; it is "approximately one hour" in the Coggan definition, and can range 30–80+ minutes depending on training state.
- Power meter error of ±2% (worst case) or ±0.5% (SRM) means that worrying about 5-watt differences in FTP is largely noise; train in zones, not to single-watt precision.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Many athletes who think they "prefer sweet spot because FTP feels too hard" are actually riding at their real FTP — because their FTP has been overestimated and sweet spot (90–95% of inflated FTP) equals their true threshold.
- The ramp test — despite being used by Team Sky — was used alongside blood lactate in a lab context to find VO2max, not FTP. Using it alone on a trainer to set FTP is a misapplication.
- A well-executed longer FTP test (45–60+ min) should not feel like a pro TT rider collapsing at the line. It should feel like a sustained hard effort that only becomes truly difficult in the last several minutes.

- None identified in this episode (first podcast episode; no prior views to revise).

## Episode: 601928829-empiricalcyclingpodcast-the-watts-doc-3-is-there-anything-special-about-2x20.txt

### Core Message
The 2×20-minute workout is a solid default FTP session, but its value comes from the metabolic time spent at threshold intensity rather than any magic in the "20 minutes" number. The first 2–3 minutes of any interval are spent with heart rate and oxygen delivery lagging behind power output, meaning a 20-minute interval yields only ~15–17 minutes of true metabolic FTP work. This creates a minimum effective interval duration of about 10 minutes. The bigger principle is that continuous FTP work is superior to fragmented FTP work, and progressive overload must come primarily from increasing total time in zone rather than increasing power.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Minimum effective duration for an FTP interval is approximately 10 minutes (which yields ~7 minutes of true metabolic FTP work); intervals shorter than this provide diminishing returns.
- Total metabolic time at FTP intensity per session should be at least 30 minutes. 2×20 (yielding ~33–35 min true metabolic work) meets this threshold.
- Progress by adding time, not power: go from 2×20 → 3×15 → 3×20 → 4×15 → 4×20, etc. Resist the temptation to increase watts.
- Continuous intervals are better than fragmented ones: 1×40 > 2×20 > 4×10, even at slightly lower power. Example: 1×40 min at 230–235 W is approximately as effective as 2×20 at 245 W (given 250 W FTP).
- Assign FTP intervals at ~95–97%, not 100%, to reduce fatigue and allow consistent quality across all intervals in the session.
- Pace the first interval conservatively — going 10–15 W over FTP on interval 1 will leave you 10–15 W under FTP on interval 2, defeating the purpose of the workout.
- For time-crunched athletes (40–60 min available): prefer fewer, longer continuous intervals over many short ones. With only 30 min of work time after warm-up, a single 30-minute effort at 90–95% FTP is optimal.
- For high-volume athletes (2–3 hr rides): embed FTP intervals inside longer rides (e.g., ride 1 hr easy, 2×20 in the middle, 1 hr easy). As TTE extends, scale to 3×30 or even 1×80+ for very high responders.
- Recovery between FTP intervals: keep legs moving at a pace just enough to preserve warmup benefit; for high-volume long rides, can raise recovery to 50–70% FTP to add training stress.
- Do not increase power during a training block; increase time and retest FTP at block end.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The progression to very long single FTP intervals (1×60, 1×80+) is only appropriate for highly experienced athletes with many hours per week. Beginners attempting this will have a "really bad day."
- Training styles of elite athletes do not necessarily transfer to intermediate or beginner athletes.
- Going over FTP (even just 3–5%) causes blood lactate to rise, accelerating glycogen depletion. This is not a better training stimulus — it just depletes you faster and curtails total time at the right metabolic intensity.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Riding 10 Watts over FTP for an FTP interval is no better than riding at FTP, and likely worse, because it reduces total completable time at the target metabolic state.
- The 2×20 structure is good largely because it's the minimum dose that provides enough true metabolic time at FTP. It's not that 20 minutes has physiological magic — it's that you can't go much shorter and still get adequate stimulus.
- Training at 95% FTP produces essentially the same metabolic stimulus as 100% FTP, but with lower fatigue — meaning more total training work can be accumulated.

- None identified in this episode.

## Episode: 631172070-empiricalcyclingpodcast-the-watts-doc-8-ftp-vs-critical-power.txt
**Numeric ID:** 631172070
**Word count:** 5820
**Series:** Watts Doc

### Core Message
FTP (anchored to maximal lactate steady state, MLSS) and critical power (CP) are attempting to measure the same physiological inflection point but via different models, and they frequently do not agree numerically. CP is a mathematical model valid for interpolation across ~1–20-minute efforts but breaks down when extrapolated to longer durations. FTP is superior for setting training zones below threshold because it directly reflects a physiological state (constant maximum blood lactate). Using CP in place of FTP to assign threshold intervals will systematically over-prescribe intensity for athletes with high anaerobic capacity, making workouts incomplete or impossible.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Use FTP (MLSS-derived), not critical power, to set threshold training zones. If CP overestimates, FTP-based intervals at 3×20 or 2×30 watts will become unsustainable.
- A real-world example: Kyle's CP in 2016 was 304 W vs. his actual FTP of 285–290 W — a ~15–20 W gap. At 300 W (CP), his threshold intervals would have been "very hard if not impossible."
- CP can be useful above FTP (e.g., to interpolate 3-minute to 10-minute powers for VO2max work), but should not be used to anchor zone-based training.
- FTP as trained via intervals updates continuously as TTE extends — it is a "living model" requiring periodic retesting, not a static number.
- iLevels (WKO4/5) account for individual variation above FTP where CP has historically failed, and are preferred for assigning all zones.
- A 5–15 W difference between CP and FTP may be statistically insignificant in a research sense but practically significant for training: 7% error causes cascading training zone problems.
- FTP tests that rely on accurate long-duration steady-state efforts (40–70 min) are more valid than CP model estimates for training zone purposes.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Some studies find CP and FTP statistically equivalent; others find CP significantly higher. The results depend on the subjects' anaerobic profiles. On average, one well-designed study (trained subjects) found CP = 278 W vs. FTP = 239 W — a 39 W gap.
- CP is valid for interpolation within its tested duration range (e.g., 1–20 min); only extrapolation beyond that range is problematic.
- Using CP from Golden Cheetah (which fits it automatically to all ride data) is particularly risky because it uses only the athlete's best efforts and overlays them on the mean-max curve as if they are achievable every day.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- The widespread adoption of CP in Golden Cheetah is largely accidental — it's easy to program and therefore was included. This created a perception of scientific equivalence with FTP that is not always warranted.
- CP was designed as a laboratory measurement tool for physiological research, not as a field tool for coaches to prescribe workouts. FTP was specifically designed for that purpose.
- The literature on CP vs. MLSS is cherry-pickable — you can find papers supporting either equivalence or large differences. The robust studies with trained athletes tend to find CP higher.

## Episode: 726050497-empiricalcyclingpodcast-the-watts-doc-14-effective-ftp-training.txt
**Numeric ID:** 726050497
**Word count:** 5495
**Series:** Watts Doc

### Core Message
The single governing principle of FTP training is progressive overload, and the most practical and safe form of overload is adding time to intervals, not increasing power. FTP has two dimensions — watts and duration (TTE) — and chasing TTE extension is more reliable than weekly power increments that may not exceed power meter noise. A key study (Billat et al., masters runners) showed that six weeks of FTP-paced intervals, progressively increasing total time from 30 to 60 minutes per session at 2×/week, raised TTE from 44 to 63 minutes and velocity (proxy for watts) — supporting both the approach and the training structure.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Start each FTP training block with an FTP test that gives both watts and TTE. Use TTE to determine starting interval volume.
- TTE 40–45 min (very common): start at 4×10 min with 3-min rests. Progression: 3×15 → 2×20 → 2×30 → 1×40 → 1×60.
- TTE 55–65 min: start at 6×10 or 3×15. Progression: 5×12 → 6×12 → reduce rest periods over weeks.
- Progress session volume from ~30 min total FTP time (week 1) toward 60 min total over a 6-week block, mirroring the Billat study protocol.
- Going over FTP does NOT improve FTP faster — the aerobic adaptation signals are not enhanced by going over threshold, only by accumulating time at or below it. 3×10 at 105% FTP ≈ 3×10 at 100% FTP, but you hold 100% FTP much longer, so 100% produces more total aerobic stimulus.
- "Over-unders" or FTP with bursts (10-second surges every 5 minutes) can provide additional metabolic load without requiring a power retest or exceeding TTE. Vary surge frequency or duration to increase load progressively.
- FTP training raises both FTP wattage and TTE. In the Billat study, TTE went from 44 to 63 minutes (+43%) at a higher absolute pace. VO2max also rose from 66.6 to 68.8 mL/kg/min (+3.3%).
- Sweet spot training (if used) requires more interval time than FTP intervals to match the adaptation stimulus. If 2×20 at FTP is the baseline, sweet spot might need 2×30 or 2×35 to produce equivalent fatigue and adaptation.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- FTP training does not always raise VO2max, especially in already well-trained athletes. Expect it mainly in those new to structured threshold work.
- At FTP, carbohydrate use averages ~83% (RER ~0.93) in this population and does not change after training at the same absolute intensity. What changes is that old FTP now feels easier (lower % of new FTP), shifting fat use upward.
- Programs that stagnate (e.g., standard TrainerRoad plans) typically stall because they never increase total time in zone — they repeat the same intervals indefinitely, providing no progressive overload.
- TTE improvements can be dramatic in athletes who have never done FTP work; gains become smaller and slower in already-trained athletes.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- "One hour FTP" is not achievable for most amateur cyclists. The coach's observation: most amateur cyclists can hold their FTP for 35–45 minutes before TTE is specifically trained. "One hour power" is therefore not FTP for most people — it is below FTP.
- Staying at the same power and duration indefinitely is one of the most common reasons FTP progress stalls (same dose, no overload — like staying on the same weight in the gym indefinitely).

### Core Message
This listener Q&A episode covers practical questions about improving FTP, including: whether sweet spot or VO2max work can substitute for direct FTP training (answer: generally no); how much FTP error is tolerable (5–10 W); minimum FTP maintenance dose; training by terrain; the FTP/VO2max relationship; and why FTP work benefits even riders who never race near threshold. The central theme is that FTP is the aerobic foundation for all cycling performance, and that nothing replaces direct threshold training for raising it.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Best way to improve FTP: ride at FTP. Over-unders at threshold are the next best tool. VO2max work does NOT reliably raise FTP — in coached athletes, FTP only rises after returning to dedicated FTP training.
- VO2max work can raise short-duration test scores (ramp test, 8-minute test) without raising actual FTP, especially if the test protocol has significant anaerobic contribution.
- FTP maintenance (minimum dose): one interval session per week at 90–100% FTP. For TTE of ~60 min, 3×20 is sufficient. If doing VO2max blocks (3–4 weeks), FTP typically does not decrease and may tick up a few watts — this is the best scenario for FTP maintenance without dedicated threshold sessions.
- Tolerable FTP error: ±5–10 W. At high FTPs (350+ W), 5–10 W is a small percentage; at lower FTPs (100–150 W), it matters more. Always set training targets 5–10 W below FTP to avoid inadvertent over-prescription.
- TT bike FTP is typically 10–15 W lower than road bike FTP; this is a real difference that requires its own calibration.
- Standing vs. seated FTP: does not warrant two separate FTPs unless you race exclusively in one position (e.g., Contador-style standing climbing intervals). For most athletes, train as you race.
- Continuous intervals (4×15 with 5-min rests) are better than the same total volume fragmented more (e.g., 60×1 min). Minimum effective continuous interval: ~10 minutes. 60×1-min FTP intervals would produce "almost none" of the adaptation of 4×15.
- FTP work benefits sprinters and criterium racers even though they "never use FTP watts in races." Higher FTP means surges to 130–140% FTP (the race intensity) are now a smaller fraction of what you can sustain aerobically, meaning faster recovery between attacks. A sprinter with 280 W FTP spending race time at 330–350 W is working largely anaerobically; raising FTP to 300 W nearly halves the distance to those watts aerobically.
- For beginners: getting a coach accelerates FTP gains dramatically. Example cited: athlete went from 180 W to 230–240 W FTP within a couple of months.
- For flatland vs. mountain terrain: prescribe free-form targets (e.g., "accumulate 80 minutes at FTP across the ride") for mountainous terrain rather than structured intervals.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Garmin's VO2max estimate is off by a minimum of 5–10%; ignore it as a training guide. WKO4/5 VO2max estimate is more accurate but still imperfect.
- FTP does not change meaningfully week to week. A perceived drop of 20 W in interval performance is typically accumulated fatigue, not a true FTP decline. FTP cannot change physiologically that fast.
- One-minute work (anaerobic capacity) reaches adaptation ceiling within 6–8 weeks; FTP work can be trained more continuously and cumulatively.
- Time-in-zone over FTP is not a reliable FTP estimator. Well-trained racers can spend enormous time over FTP in a race without that indicating their FTP is underestimated.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Doing a lot of one-minute intervals will make your ramp test FTP score go up without actually raising FTP — this is "gaming the test." You're studying for the test (anaerobic contribution to the test), not improving the quality (aerobic capacity) it's supposed to measure.
- Sweet spot is not really "lower fatigue" training — if set correctly, sweet spot fatigue ≈ FTP fatigue for comparable time. The real fatigue difference disappears when you account for needing ~10 minutes more per interval at sweet spot to match the FTP stimulus.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie notes that his holistic aerobic training philosophy (not compartmentalizing FTP vs. VO2max vs. base) has led to much less FTP detraining in his athletes over time compared to his earlier, more compartmentalized approach.

### Core Message
Recorded ~3 years after the original FTP testing article, this episode revisits FTP testing in light of additional coaching experience (including a full pandemic training year with no racing). New issues addressed: the ramp test systematically overestimates FTP on average; CP is not equivalent to MLSS/FTP; erg mode testing has specific drawbacks; prolonged FTP training at a fixed wattage can "wear a rut" in perceived effort that disguises real fitness gains; and the WKO5 model provides an alternative pathway to FTP estimation when formal testing is impractical.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Experienced athletes (coached) often go straight to an open-ended progressive FTP test rather than the stepwise progression in the original article. The coach starts them ~20 W below estimated FTP for the first 10–15 minutes, then asks them to ramp slowly to find that sustainable edge.
- Avoid jumping from a "comfortable" pace to full perceived FTP without a gradual ramp — this fails ~28 of 30 attempts in coaching experience.
- Erg mode for FTP testing: acceptable if using it to generate data for the WKO5 power curve model, but it defeats the purpose of learning to feel out the threshold inflection point by RPE.
- A "brute-force" binary-search erg test (ride at suspected FTP ±10 W, see which side fatigues faster) is theoretically accurate but impractically time-consuming. Do not attempt.
- Retest FTP every 4–8 weeks under normal conditions. Less frequent testing is appropriate for highly trained athletes where 5–10 W/year is the expected gain. Do NOT simply add 5 W every 4–8 weeks without testing — FTP may not have moved.
- To avoid "wearing a rut" in RPE when sticking with one FTP wattage for a long block: use a 15–25-minute open-ended test occasionally (not a fixed 20-minute test), or use Zwift racing in categories slightly above your ability to force a genuine maximal sustained effort.
- WKO5 model-based FTP tracking: ensure the power curve is well-populated at 10–15 sec, 30–60 sec, 3–6 min, 10–30 min, and 60–90 min time ranges. Regular "model maintenance" efforts at these durations allow modeled FTP to update between formal tests.
- When FTP has gone up 15–20 W during a training block but the test returns the same number, look to race power files, Strava KOMs, or long over-under intervals for the true inflection point in the power curve — the model will show the new FTP even if the test did not.
- Heart rate is not a reliable FTP test metric: FTP heart rate can drift from 160 to 175 BPM across a single test effort and is highly variable day to day. Never use a target HR to define FTP.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Lactate-based FTP testing: varies significantly based on protocol (3-min vs. 10-min steps, fed vs. fasted, hydrated vs. dehydrated). The author has done enough lactate tests that he no longer finds them valuable except when external parties (teams, etc.) require them. A good lactate test requires multiple long-step protocols that are extremely time-consuming.
- FTP is physiologically a range, not a sharp line — the inflection point in the power curve is a curve itself. Picking the midpoint and erring slightly low is preferable to a precise high estimate.
- Critical power in 2020 papers is confirmed to be significantly higher than MLSS/FTP on average, with a reported "gray zone" between CP and MLSS. Working in this gray zone will produce faster fatigue than expected from either model.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Ramp tests systematically overestimate FTP: Jim Arnold's data (40-min TT vs. ramp FTP) showed an average +17 W bias with an upper agreement limit of +45 W. Rory's group of 16 cyclists showed an average +18 W bias, with multiple individuals overestimated by 30–50 W.
- A year without racing (2020 pandemic) is uniquely bad for accurate FTP testing because the natural "forget-and-retest" cycle that breaks the perceived-effort rut normally happens through racing. Without that, athletes get stuck in the same perceived-effort rut and underestimate true gains.
- The correct definition of FTP is the inflection point at which above it you fatigue faster and below it you fatigue slower — regardless of what that corresponds to in minutes or lactate concentration. The "60-minute" definition creates more problems than it solves.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie no longer recommends the stepwise progression protocol from his 2018 Training Peaks article for athletes he coaches directly — he moves them straight to the final open-ended version of the test.
- More reliance on the WKO5 model for day-to-day FTP estimation compared to earlier, more test-centric approaches.

## Episode: 1324590415-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-39-why-you-probably-cant-hold-your-ftp-for-an-hour.txt

### Core Message
This episode builds on a deep reading of the Billat et al. paper (masters endurance runners doing FTP interval blocks) to argue that (1) most cyclists cannot hold their FTP for one hour — the realistic average is ~40–45 minutes TTE; (2) the "FTP = one hour power" definition is historically motivated but physiologically arbitrary; and (3) the correct training response is extensive FTP work (progressively extending time in zone) not intensive FTP work (raising power). The episode makes a metabolic case for why extending TTE is mechanistically linked to improved lactate clearance, carnitine transport, and mitochondrial density.

### Key Training Recommendations
- The practical average TTE for amateur cyclists is ~40–45 minutes, not 60. Training to extend TTE toward 60 minutes (and beyond) is a primary goal of FTP blocks.
- The Billat study protocol (mimicking what Kolie prescribes): 2 sessions/week, starting at 3×10 and 2×15 in week 1, progressing to 3×20 and 2×30 in week 6. Total time in zone went from 30 to 60 minutes over 6 weeks. TTE went from 45 to 63 minutes (+40%).
- This protocol (FTP intervals twice weekly, progressive time) is the template for all FTP blocks. There is no "wrong" way to increment — adding one interval, adding 2 minutes to each interval, or mixing interval lengths are all valid as long as total time in zone increases.
- High-volume athletes (long slow distance base): adding FTP work on top of maintained aerobic volume produces the largest TTE gains. The Billat subjects had 10 years of 5×/week LSD running. The FTP intervention was added on top of that, not in place of it.
- Metabolic basis for extending TTE: lower blood lactate at minute 10 of a TTE test predicts longer TTE (R² = 0.69). Lower lactate means better mitochondrial oxidation of lactate (keeping it inside the cell and converting it to energy before it spills into the blood). This is what extensive FTP training develops.
- Fat oxidation increases with FTP training (not by reducing carb use at FTP, but by enabling more fat use at sub-FTP intensities once FTP rises, because old FTP intensities are now lower % of the new FTP).

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Study population: masters runners (43–51 yrs, VO2max ~55, half-marathon 83 min). Carbohydrate use at FTP averaged ~80–83% (RER ~0.93) pre- and post-training — did not change at the same absolute intensity. This appears to contradict the "FTP training increases fat burning at FTP" belief; the benefit is at intensities that were previously FTP but are now sub-FTP.
- Highest TTE ever reliably tested in Kolie's coaching practice: ~83 minutes in three athletes simultaneously. This is exceptional.
- Highest FTP relative to VO2max typically seen: ~90%. Average across the scientific literature: ~75–85%. FTP at 85% of VO2max in this study is high but within published ranges.
- Note: this paper has multiple Whisper transcription glitches (repeated sentences) in the transcript around lines 174–285 and 575–577.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- "FTP equals one hour power" is an arbitrary convention from the original Training and Racing with a Power Meter book — and even there the two authors (Coggan vs. Allen) give slightly different definitions on consecutive pages. There is nothing physiologically special about 60 minutes.
- Doing an honest one-hour TT to test FTP will "fuck up your training for weeks" (as an early coach warned Kolie) because most athletes' TTE is shorter than 60 minutes — meaning a true one-hour TT effort would require riding above FTP for large portions of the test.
- The TTE metric in WKO4 (released 2015) was an implicit acknowledgment that the one-hour definition was wrong, but Coggan had to field years of "one hour" questions defending it.

### Evolving Views
- The coach's view on FTP training philosophy was largely formed by this paper, which he credits with inspiring the podcast itself. The key insight — chase TTE extension, not wattage — has remained consistent throughout the series.

- The 20-minute test (95% correction) is better than the ramp test, but the range of valid correction factors is roughly 88–97% depending on athlete type. Aerobic-dominant athletes (triathletes, ultra-endurance) need only a small correction (FTP near 95–97% of 20-min power). Highly anaerobic athletes may need 88–90%.
- Critical power testing can be useful but still overestimates by ~20 W on average vs. MLSS in a study comparison (CP = 253 ± 44 W vs. MLSS = 233 ± 42 W, average bias +20 W). Requires two separate max-effort test days and cannot be done easily in the field.
- Time-in-zone over FTP: not a reliable FTP estimator. Well-trained racers (e.g., Rory: spent 25% of an 8.5-hour event above FTP) do not need FTP readjusted because of this.
- Normalized power as FTP indicator: better than time-in-zone but requires course-normalized circumstances (consistent pedaling; no short, frequent sprints that inflate NP). NP within 10 W of average power on a quasi-steady effort is a reasonable indicator for retesting.
- The open-ended RPE-guided test works because FTP is intuitively perceptible: going 10 W over feels disproportionately harder; going 10 W under feels dramatically easier. Use this asymmetry as the key test cue.
- Preferred test target: within 10 W of true FTP. When uncertain, go low — slightly under-prescribing FTP is far less damaging than over-prescribing.
- Using best race power to estimate FTP: "by far the worst way." Race-derived power is too confounded by course, tactics, and effort distribution to be reliable. A controlled test is always preferable.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Ramp test results are gameable: doing 2 months of anaerobic capacity work can raise ramp test FTP by 30+ W without any aerobic improvement. Example: one coached athlete who deliberately did anaerobic-only training had ramp test FTP of 260 W vs. actual FTP of 221 W (+39 W, +18%).
- Blood lactate testing is only as good as the protocol. Common field protocols (3-minute step tests, simultaneous VO2max ramping) have too many confounders (hydration, fasting state, warmup quality, step duration) to be reliable without expert supervision. Good MLSS testing requires multiple long-step days and is very time-consuming.
- FTP correction factors (e.g., 75% of ramp test end power) have been proposed but the cited range (72–78%) is extremely narrow and ignores individual variation.
- All FTP test methods are models representing a phenomenon (the fatigue inflection point) — none are the phenomenon itself. The power meter itself introduces ≥0.5–2% error before any testing methodology is considered.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- A Kolie Moore test (open-ended, RPE-guided) is more accurate than a ramp test or 8-minute test for most riders and produces usable training data even when executed imperfectly.
- Very highly trained road racers (400+ W FTP) tend to have their 20-minute power and FTP nearly converge, making the standard 95% correction close to correct for them. The testing problem is worst for intermediate anaerobic riders.
- The 5-minute blowout before the 20-minute test is largely ignored by athletes and may increase test variability even when performed — one paper found it had negligible effect on the 20-minute result at a population level.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie says he did not intend for the open-ended test to become known as "the Kolie Moore test" and would prefer it simply be called a better test. He added the multi-step progression protocol in the published article for a general audience, but with coached athletes he skips straight to the final open-ended version.

## Episode: 2175917922-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-57-finding-ftp-by-feel-is-easier-than-you-think.txt

### Core Message
This episode makes the philosophical and empirical case that FTP can be reliably found by RPE (perceived exertion), supported by two papers. The first (older RPE ramp study) showed R = 0.88 correlation between RPE 13–14/20 and lactate threshold, though with ±30 W limits of agreement (due to coarse 30 W steps). The second, more recent paper compared self-selected intensity (RPE-guided 30-min TT) vs. critical power vs. MLSS and found the RPE test matched MLSS to within ±10 W in 11 of 13 participants (average zero bias; CP overestimated by +20 W). The conclusion is that athletes intuitively know their threshold, that the FTP phenomenon is directly observable, and that all tests are models representing this observable phenomenon — some more accurately than others.

### Key Training Recommendations
- You can find your FTP by feel, without a formal test. Key RPE cues: (1) if you increase power by 10 W and it feels disproportionately harder, you are at or above FTP; (2) if you decrease power by 10 W and it feels dramatically easier, you are at FTP; (3) if your breathing goes ragged, you are above FTP.
- RPE anchor technique: recall your easiest possible cycling effort (RPE ~7–8/20), your sprint maximum (RPE ~19–20/20), and find threshold as the intensity where the effort feels sustainable for a long time but distinctly harder than tempo — roughly RPE 13–14/20, or ~7/10 on a 10-point scale.
- Athletes with prior aerobic sport experience (swimming, running) tend to nail the threshold feel quickly. Athletes with strong preconceived notions about FTP often have more difficulty because they need to get out of their own heads.
- With coaching and RPE feedback, some athletes can hold within ±5 W of FTP across every interval without looking at the power meter.
- The "Kolie Moore test" is essentially an operationalization of this RPE guidance: start below FTP, use a time goal (40–70 minutes) as the anchor, and ramp slowly until finding the intensity where +10 W feels disproportionately harder.
- All FTP tests are models. When a test returns a value that doesn't match your RPE, trust the RPE — not the model.
- After establishing a reliable RPE for threshold, most experienced athletes can skip formal testing for months and simply set intervals by feel, then periodically verify with power data.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The second paper used 30-minute sustained efforts, which is on the short side for TTE at threshold (average TTE is ~40–45 min). Slightly longer tests would likely have produced even tighter RPE-vs-MLSS agreement.
- RPE-to-FTP correspondence is not universal: Kolie's own 20-minute test consistently put his FTP at ~90% (vs. the assumed 95%) because of his high anaerobic capacity. Kyle has the same issue. RPE calibration requires some prior threshold experience to work well.
- Paper 1 protocol had 30 W step increments, creating a ±30 W limit of agreement — this is a methodological artifact, not a true reflection of RPE accuracy.
- The RPE scale (Borg 6–20) was explicitly stated by Borg himself not to be taken literally as heart rate × 10. It is a descriptive anchor tool, not a precise physiological ruler.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- The phenomenon of FTP (the fatigue inflection point) is directly perceptible by the athlete and does not require any measurement instrument to find. All power meter-based tests are abstractions representing this phenomenon, not the phenomenon itself. When the model disagrees with lived experience, the model may be wrong.
- Critical power overestimates MLSS (FTP) by an average of ~20 W in this study's population, confirming earlier findings. RPE-selected intensity was a better predictor of MLSS than critical power.
- The best argument for the open-ended RPE-guided FTP test is not philosophical but empirical: in peer-reviewed data, self-selected RPE intensity matched MLSS within 10 W in 11/13 participants. This is tighter agreement than ramp tests or critical power for most populations.

### Evolving Views
- The publication of the 2016 paper (critical power vs. self-selected intensity vs. MLSS) confirmed Kolie's independently developed approach to FTP testing — a case of "convergent evolution." He began using open-ended RPE-guided tests around 2015, a year before this paper was published.
- Kolie now states explicitly that he values RPE and athlete feedback as primary data, not secondary — and that he regrets time spent on short-form tests early in his coaching career.

## Episode: 2283684374-ten-minute-tips-74-ftp.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2283684374
**Word count:** 11412
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
This three-coach roundtable (Kolie, Erica, Fabiano) enumerates common FTP-building mistakes, organized as a practical checklist. The central themes are: test FTP by feel rather than relying on short-effort proxies; ensure sessions are well-fueled and properly rested before you execute threshold work; be skeptical of lactate testing as a training tool; do not assume FTP is changing when fatigue or underfueling may be the real variable; and understand when to stop chasing FTP gains and shift toward TTE extension or VO2max work. A recurring emphasis is that FTP is a stable physiological marker that should function as a fatigue barometer, not a day-to-day fluctuating number.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Test FTP by feel using the open-ended RPE-guided approach: start conservatively, gradually ramp to the sustainable edge, and trust that a well-calibrated athlete can find their FTP within ~5 W in about 10 minutes. Do not predetermine the target number before the effort.
- Do not extrapolate FTP from non-FTP efforts. Building sweet spot to 90 minutes or completing long sweet spot work does not confirm FTP has risen. Only riding at FTP tests FTP.
- Come into FTP sessions with appropriate rest. Physical fatigue or high life stress will skew RPE and produce an unreliable threshold calibration.
- Use minimum interval durations of approximately 12 minutes for threshold work; shorter intervals risk drifting into VO2max territory and inflate perceived FTP. Progress interval duration over the block.
- Fuel threshold sessions adequately. Two-by-20 at threshold is a large kilojoule day and requires carbohydrate both during and after the session. Poor fueling will cause sessions to fail and may lead to the false conclusion that the FTP block "didn't work."
- Hydrate aggressively before morning sessions — eight hours of fasting and overnight breathing causes significant fluid loss. Consider evening carbohydrate loading as a compensatory strategy.
- Control indoor heat buildup. 65–75°F (18–24°C) indoor temperature without aggressive cooling will degrade interval quality significantly. Use fans and ice water actively; expect 10–15 W reduction in sustainable power if cooling is inadequate.
- When transitioning from indoor to outdoor training in spring, recalibrate your RPE/threshold perception for the changed thermal environment and physics. Terrain type (flat vs. climbing) can shift FTP expression by 10–20 W for some athletes.
- Do not attempt to build FTP continuously through a busy race schedule. Racing disrupts the controlled training dose and recovery predictability required for progressive FTP overload.
- Start each threshold interval conservatively — 5–10 W below the prescription. Build from there. The last interval (or an extension of the final interval) is the appropriate place to test the ceiling, not the first.
- When FTP stops rising, shift focus to TTE extension (longer intervals at the same watts) before moving to VO2max work. Then, if needed, raise the "roof" with VO2 work to open space for a higher FTP later.
- Track FTP as a fatigue barometer. A 20 W apparent drop mid-week almost always reflects accumulated fatigue, underfueling, or heat — not a true fitness decline. Use this as a recovery signal, not a retest trigger.
- For extensive vs. intensive FTP work: prefer extensive (time-in-zone) for building TTE. Reserve intensive work for situations where a new higher FTP needs to be consolidated or validated.
- To prevent RPE rut when FTP improves, be aware that 15–20 W higher watts will feel unfamiliar. Push through this brief adjustment period; TTE may temporarily drop and then rebuild at the new level.
- Do a short FTP test effort in the warmup (~2 minutes) to get a daily read on freshness before committing to full interval intensity. Adjust the session accordingly.
- Vary training with VO2max, sweet spot, and other efforts to maintain mental engagement and avoid the diminishing returns and opportunity cost of exclusive FTP focus.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Lactate testing uses population averages (e.g., 4 mmol/L as threshold, 2 mmol/L as LT1) that do not apply reliably to individuals. In coaching practice, the fixed-concentration method has underestimated threshold by 30–40 W in individual athletes. A world-champion track cyclist was told his threshold was far lower than his actual 40–50 minute sustainable power because his lactate profile was atypical. Kolie considers routine field lactate testing "a waste of time and money" given that performance is easier to measure directly.
- CPEX (cardiopulmonary exercise testing) and other lab-based threshold protocols also commonly overestimate threshold in practice. When athletes attempt 2×20 at lab-derived numbers and fail the second interval, that is a clear sign of overestimation.
- Metabolomics testing (discussed as an emerging option) requires ~4 tests per year at ~$250 each; the coaches find this cost-prohibitive and not yet proven for practical training zone prescription.
- Seasonal factors (sleep quality worsening in heat, accumulated fatigue near season apex, increased outdoor volume) all affect the rate and nature of FTP adaptation and should be accounted for when evaluating a block's results.
- At very low fitness levels (e.g., after months of easy riding), FTP expression is compressed: the range between sustainable and maximal short-duration power is narrow, making RPE-based threshold identification genuinely difficult.
- For athletes on an energy deficit or morning training in a fasted/dehydrated state, RPE is unreliable and FTP intervals may fail for nutritional reasons unrelated to true fitness.
- Terrain specificity is real for some athletes: individuals may show a 10–20 W difference between flat and climbing FTP, requiring separate calibration if their event demands are terrain-specific.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Lactate testing has too many valid methodologies (at least 10 published approaches) to be considered a reliable gold standard. The multiplicity of methods is itself evidence that none of them work reliably. (Kolie's position, shared by Fabiano.)
- The 2×8-minute test at 90% correction — popular at the time Erica began coaching — can overestimate FTP by up to 60 W for highly anaerobic riders (Kolie's own data: ~330 W 2×8 result vs. ~270 W actual FTP).
- An athlete can feel their FTP going up during a training block without testing. The block itself — when intervals are executed near threshold — functions as continuous testing. Formal retesting every 4–8 weeks is not required to detect progress.
- Doing FTP training three times per week is not necessary and may be counterproductive. Kolie reports that over the last 2–3 years he has given athletes less FTP training than listeners would expect, with no reduction in FTP gains or TTE improvements — and possibly larger gains.
- The notion that FTP intervals must be "erg mode perfect" or "clean" to count is incorrect. Normalized power within ~1 W of average power on an outdoor interval is a high-quality effort; natural stochasticity of outdoor riding is not a reason to dismiss the session.
- FTP is not the endpoint of a training career. A well-chosen variety of efforts — including VO2max, sweet spot, and race-specific work — is almost certainly more effective than single-minded FTP block accumulation for most cyclists.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie notes that in the last 2–3 years he has moved away from heavy FTP block emphasis and has given athletes a more varied training diet. He reports this has not reduced FTP progress and may have improved it — representing a practical evolution from the podcast's earlier, more FTP-focused recommendations.
- The panel collectively notes the recurring pattern in exercise physiology of new testing methods (lactate, CPEX, metabolomics) cycling through popularity before returning to the same conclusion: direct performance measurement is more reliable. Fabiano: "Physiology is not changing, so you don't need to reinvent the wheel."
