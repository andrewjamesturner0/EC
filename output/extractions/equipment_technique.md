## Episode: 611724549-empiricalcyclingpodcast-talking-to-aston-lambie.txt
**Numeric ID:** 611724549
**Word count:** 8,326
**Series:** Uncategorized (early episode, pre-series format)

### Core Message
This is an interview with Ashton Lambie, US individual pursuit record holder and team pursuit national team member, conducted by Kolie Moore and Kyle Helson. The episode covers Lambie's background in gravel and track cycling, his experience racing with the British-registered "hoo-ha/Watt Bike" squad, and what he learned about aerodynamics and equipment optimization from that team. A recurring theme is that there is no arbitrary threshold of ability below which equipment optimization doesn't matter — every detail counts at every level. The episode also touches on team pursuit tactics, gear selection, and Lambie's view that strength work in the gym made a meaningful difference to his riding.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Gear/crank selection:** Lambie rides 165mm cranks on all bikes — road, track, and TT — as a consistent baseline.
- **Equipment optimization philosophy:** No level of detail is too small to optimize. The hoo-ha team attitude was that every piece of equipment on the bike should be optimized for speed, from ceramic bearings to aero-tested fabrics to helmets. There is no "get faster first, then worry about equipment" threshold — the line is arbitrary.
- **Helmet selection:** The fastest helmet is rider-specific; there is no universally fastest aero helmet. The team tested positions before committing to a helmet, because some helmets (e.g., a POC model discussed) are very fast but only in a narrow position window. Even the Giro required position optimization before the POC could replace it.
- **Aero testing:** The team used an Alpha Mantis system with a formal testing protocol. A structured protocol for what you're testing, how you're testing it, and what the numbers mean is necessary — you can't "just throw something in and call it aero testing." Lambie helped bring testing protocols back to the US national team.
- **Team pursuit lineup strategy:** A team pursuit change (rider swap at front) costs ~0.1 seconds per lap; minimize changes. Optimal goal: go through the full lineup twice. Rider position should account for aerobic vs. anaerobic profile, how many turns each rider can contribute, draft compatibility (some riders are harder to sit on), and waterline matching.
- **Ergogenic supplementation:** Lambie brought sodium bicarb and caffeine protocols back from the hoo-ha team to USAC. Sodium bicarb dose was approximately 50–60g total over a day of three rounds of racing (Cambridge World Cup). Caffeine approached ~1g total across a full race day at Cambridge.
- **Pacing (individual pursuit):** Target pace was 53s per km (5:03/4km), with the coach walking a line on the track. Lambie's world record attempt was paced at 5:03, needing 5:02 to break the record; he aimed to ride slightly ahead of the line. Power output for the record ride was approximately 480–500W.
- **Strength training:** Lambie wishes he had started gym work earlier. Specifically, deadlifts: "It's a really similar motion to starting and I feel like if I'm really primed for a good hard deadlift and thinking about that at the start, it's always just flying out of the gate." Gym work also improved stability in the saddle and power application.
- **Periodization for track cyclists:** Off-season gravel and bikepacking (high volume, no intensity ceiling) functions as a base period. The track/intensity season is built on top of that aerobic foundation. Starting intensity too early and extending the season too long led to deterioration by Worlds in a prior season.
- **FTP context:** Lambie reports FTP of approximately 380W; emphasizes this is not exceptional relative to other national team members (some at 400–410W), suggesting individual pursuit performance comes from factors beyond FTP alone.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Aero testing was done on the track (not wind tunnel) for the Watt Bike program — position was dialed via track runs, not CFD or tunnel work.
- The "plug and play" bike fit Lambie did (sliding onto a teammate's TT bike with saddle swap only) worked coincidentally because their proportions were compatible; this is not a general approach.
- Sodium bicarb GI tolerance is highly individual; the team had a protocol for delivery and timing that worked. At Cambridge with three rounds in one day, even with the protocol, Lambie felt ill from cumulative dose.
- Team pursuit lineup is complicated and constantly evolving — "nobody is ever solid in their position."

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- The idea of "get better before you worry about equipment" is an arbitrary line. Pro teams don't show up to world cups on steel road bikes; by that logic, every incremental step up in equipment optimization is equally justified regardless of the athlete's current level.
- John Archibald (C-level IP world record holder at time of recording) reportedly rarely exceeds 1,000W in a pursuit start — yet produces one of the fastest IPs ever recorded — because his massive aerobic engine means his heart rate actually *decreases* as a pursuit progresses. Start power is almost irrelevant for a true aerobic engine at this level.
- Qualifying rounds at world cups: elite teams sometimes strategically choose *where* to seed themselves rather than going all-out in qualifications, because first-round (qualification) times are sometimes *faster* than finals times due to strategic sandbagging.

### Evolving Views
- Lambie acknowledges he became aware of his own limitations in TT bike handling and steering, describing it as "probably the hardest thing for me" transitioning to road TT position for team pursuit — because he had never raced time trials before. This represents an identified gap, not a resolved one.
- The US national team began aero testing in Colorado Springs but needed a proper protocol before the Alpha Mantis system could be used effectively — Lambie helped import that structure from the hoo-ha team.

## Episode: 1169482870-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-5-coaching-the-process-with-cory-lockwood.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1169482870
**Word count:** 15,174
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
This episode introduces Corey Lockwood as a new Empirical Cycling coach. The conversation primarily covers Lockwood's background in motocross racing and road cycling, his experience with a pseudoscientific coach (the "Semper Poro" system), and his philosophy on what makes good coaching. The most technically dense section covers criterium cornering technique and how Lockwood's motocross background informs line selection and power management in bunch races. There is substantial material on how bike setup (tire pressure, wheel depth, stem length, crank length) affects cornering and power application.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Criterium cornering — line selection (exit-speed focus):** Riders who lack explosive sprint acceleration should prioritize late-apex lines to maximize exit velocity. Remove all riders from the mental picture first, find every opportunity to pedal through turns, then add road furniture, then add rider dynamics. The goal: stay seated and spin up, never having to sprint out of the saddle at 800W from a corner.
- **Late apex technique for non-sprinters:** Brake a little earlier, open the turn up, lay the bike over, pedal into and out of the turn. This preserves seated power output (400–500W seated vs. 800W standing) and allows momentum maintenance.
- **Cut-and-thrust (square-up) lines for sprinters:** Hard brake into a corner, pivot inside, sprint out. This costs energy but suits riders with high peak power. Not appropriate for riders who must rely on sustained power.
- **Sensing the limit of grip:** Feel feedback through handlebars and outside pedal (countersteer loading); you can hear the tire at the limit. Read road surface changes (chip seal to asphalt, concrete to asphalt) before reaching limit conditions — surface transitions change traction coefficients mid-corner.
- **Bike setup variables that matter for racing:** Tire width, wheel depth, PSI, and contact patch all affect cornering traction and spin-up speed. Crank length matters for pedaling through tight-radius turns. Stem length and bar width affect sprint power generation. These details matter as much on a road bike as on a motocross bike.
- **Wheel depth for light riders on flat TTs:** Choose deepest wheels you can handle given your weight and expected crosswind. A lighter rider with lower CdA can achieve the same velocity as a heavier rider at higher watts if the aerodynamic advantage is real. Focus on position quality — a rider at 300W in a dialed position can beat a rider at 400W with poor aero.
- **TT gearing:** A 60T chainring (1x) was described as a legitimate TT option to avoid spinning out on descents and false flats, allowing a lower cadence (70–80 RPM) "diesel" mode where the rider can reduce power output, lower breathing, and still carry speed. On hilly courses, revert to a 53/39 or compact.
- **Fabric vs. skin in TTs:** Not universal — depends heavily on the specific suit. A cheap, slow skin suit may be slower than shaved skin. Only testing reveals the answer. Importantly: smoother surfaces are not always faster aerodynamically (golf ball effect), and body hair aero effects are genuinely uncertain.
- **FTP workout discipline:** Never add power during threshold workouts based on feel. Threshold wattage should stay constant; re-test to confirm gains. Most athletes who "feel good" and add power mid-threshold-workout are either fatiguing themselves unnecessarily or are wrong about the gain.
- **Athlete understanding of workouts:** Athletes who understand why a workout is prescribed execute it better — they operate from a performance mindset rather than a fear/survival mindset. This appears to produce better physiological outcomes from the same workout.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Lockwood is explicit that aerodynamics is outside his area of deep expertise — he defers to aerodynamicists and notes he has limits to his knowledge on fabric vs. skin questions.
- Cornering line choice is engine-dependent: the optimal line for a diesel non-sprinter is completely different from the optimal line for a sprinter. There is no universally correct criterium line.
- Threshold FTP development: early in training it tends to go up; later in training it typically doesn't. Understanding this prevents athletes from adding too much power prematurely.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Motorcycle racing (motocross) provides a highly transferable fitness base to road cycling — it produces aerobic/anaerobic fitness, muscular strength, and the mental discipline of sustained high-intensity efforts (160–180 BPM with brief recovery windows), which translates directly to TT fitness. Lockwood's FTP was never below ~370W when he started cycling.
- Conventional "ride with people faster than you" advice is often misapplied — it only works if the pace differential is manageable. If you're hanging on for dear life every ride, you're not learning, you're just accumulating fatigue.
- "One size fits all" coaching programs have fundamental holes because physiological and mental individuality is too large to accommodate with generic programming. What worked for the coach may not work for the athlete, and using the coach's own racing success to validate the methodology is a logical trap.

### Evolving Views
- None identified in this episode.

## Episode: 1239908659-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-10-a-guide-to-time-trialing-with-cory-lockwood.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1239908659
**Word count:** 12,401
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Corey Lockwood and Kolie Moore have a focused conversation on time trialing technique, equipment, and strategy. This is one of the most technically specific TT episodes in the series, covering position philosophy, gearing, cadence management, equipment selection for different course profiles, and the aerodynamics of fabric versus skin. Lockwood draws heavily on his motocross background to explain course-reading, line selection, and the mental approach to attacking each section of a TT course.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **TT position philosophy:** Comfort and power output should not be sacrificed for extreme aero position. A TT position that is radically different from your road bike will reduce both power output and time to exhaustion. The goal is to make your best power position aerodynamic, not to sacrifice power for drag reduction. A rider can test and verify this: on a wind tunnel or on the road, an extremely aggressive position sometimes produces *slower* speeds than a moderately aero position because the power loss outweighs the drag savings.
- **Aero bar stiffness:** Cheap or flexible aero bars/pads that move 0.5 cm per bump undermine position confidence, change hip angle mid-ride, and make it impossible to lock in a repeatable position. Carbon one-piece or 3D-printed bars that don't flex are worth the investment — they allow a tighter, more consistent position.
- **Cadence management in TTs:** Cadence is a tool to generate and manage speed, not a fixed target. Use higher cadence (above normal) to accelerate and spin up to speed; drop to your comfortable steady-state cadence to maintain. "Normal" cadence is wherever breathing rhythm feels balanced with leg pressure — whether 80, 85, or 90 RPM. Spinning out your ceiling (overly high cadence) elevates heart rate and is hard to bring back down; reserve high cadence for deliberate acceleration phases.
- **Gearing for flat TTs:** A 60T chainring is viable for experienced TT riders to avoid spinning out on flats/descents, to maintain a straighter chain line (less drivetrain loss), and to enable a controlled low-cadence "diesel" mode on false flats without spiking breathing. Riders with a 60T can continue turning over the gear when others are spinning out and super-tucking.
- **Gearing for hilly TTs:** Revert to 53/39 or compact. The 60T becomes a liability on climbs.
- **Course-reading methodology:** Break a TT course into 15–20 sections, each with its own pacing strategy, body position, and cadence plan. On out-and-back courses, use the lap function to time your gap to the turnaround — hit lap at a landmark before the 180, then clock it when you see your competitor pass the same point. This gives you a precise gap in seconds throughout the race.
- **Turnaround pacing:** Do not over-brake at cone turnarounds. Carrying 3–4 mph more speed through a turnaround versus braking too hard matters significantly over a full TT. Every 10 seconds of over-braking accumulates.
- **Lightweight riders on flat TTs:** Focus on CdA optimization — a small rider at 300W with excellent position can match a large rider at 400W if CdA is proportionally smaller. Choose deep-section wheels up to the wind/weight management limit. Target hillier TT courses when possible to leverage w/kg advantage.
- **Fabric vs. skin revisited:** Some suits are fast, some are slow — you must test. Not all skin suits are created equal. The best suits require either knowing the right people or a large budget.
- **Course recon mindset:** Riding a familiar training road and reconning the same road as a TT course require a different mental mode. Recon means thinking about cracks, wind effects, gradients, sight lines, and braking points — not just riding.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Making yourself "really small" doesn't automatically mean smoother airflow — aerodynamics is nuanced enough that extremely aggressive positions can create turbulence that negates the frontal area reduction. Lockwood defers to aerodynamicists on specifics.
- Everything in TT aerodynamics should be tested. Intuitive guesses about what looks fast are often wrong — Lockwood cites velodrome testing where a "worse" position was measured faster.
- Cadence preferences are highly individual. Whether "normal" is 80 or 90 RPM varies by rider. The key is knowing your own number and using that as the baseline.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Flat out-and-back TTs that appear to be "boring" are actually richly technical when broken down into micro-sections. A Moriarty-style course has 15–20 distinct tactical segments. Riders who don't see this are leaving time on the course.
- Different TT courses suit fundamentally different rider types. The Knoxville nationals course (multiple 180° cone turns, frequent re-acceleration) favors riders who can do repeated 3–5 minute very high intensity efforts and recover quickly. It does not suit riders with a large sustained FTP and low peak power — those riders need a momentum-carrying strategy that minimizes sprinting out of the saddle.
- The "10 on the back" bailout gear in a time trial (Lockwood's 60T chainring means a 10T cog is needed to spin out) is about managing breathing and recovery on downhills and false flats, not about climbing.

### Evolving Views
- None identified in this episode.

## Episode: 1406970607-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-21-behind-the-new-american-40km-itt-record-with-cory-lockwood.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1406970607
**Word count:** 19,659
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
This episode covers Corey Lockwood's successful attempt at the US 40km ITT national record at Moriarty, New Mexico (elevation ~6,500 ft), set November 5, 2022, with a time of 44:54:59. The conversation covers altitude acclimatization, equipment choices made within real-world budget constraints, aero testing methodology without a wind tunnel, TT pacing strategy on an out-and-back course, training structure leading to the record, and the value of VO2max work as a prerequisite to threshold development. It is one of the richest single episodes in this cluster for actionable equipment and technique detail.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Altitude acclimatization (individual variation):** Lockwood needs approximately 6–7 days at altitude to feel comfortable after coming from sea level. Pattern: day 1 okay, day 2 bad, day 3 very flat, days 4–5 improving, days 6–7 good. This is based on 10–15+ years of experience training and living at 4,000–6,000 ft. At 7,000–8,000 ft the response is harder and longer. This is distinct from published averages — individual experience is the only reliable guide.
- **Aero bar selection — stiffness priority:** Lockwood specifically credits the Aero Coach bars chosen for the record attempt as a "critical component" — they lock in the position so that elbow pads don't move on bumps. When the front end flexes, hip angle changes and head position shifts, making the position non-repeatable and non-confidence-inspiring. Rigid one-piece or carbon aero bars justify their cost.
- **Low-budget aero prep methodology:** Without a wind tunnel, test aerodynamics by: (1) riding a consistent flat road segment, (2) changing only one variable at a time, (3) comparing average speed at matched RPE and power, (4) using auditory and tactile feedback (wind noise, pressure on the suit, head load) as a proxy for drag. Return to baseline if the change doesn't help. Build an evidence base one tweak at a time. "Rate of perceived exertion wind tunnel."
- **Pacing an out-and-back TT (negatively split with wind):** Day 1 attempt at Moriarty: tailwind on the way out, produced a fast outbound split, but Lockwood was "blown up" ~10 minutes before the finish on the way back and missed the record. Day 2: near-calm outbound (slight headwind), paced more conservatively out (~390–410W avg estimated), got 10 seconds faster to the turnaround than day 1, then benefited from slight tailwind on the return and was ~10 seconds ahead of splits throughout. Lesson: knowing the course prevents over-commitment on the outbound when wind conditions are likely to reverse.
- **Moriarty course technical section:** A slight rise out of the turnaround looks manageable but is deceptive at 30+ mph. Going too hard in that 5–10 minute section costs 10+ minutes of compromised pace on the far side of the knoll. Treat it as a patient section — be "fast and aero" through it rather than hammering.
- **Power at altitude for TT pacing:** Estimated power: ~390–410W outbound average, ~420–440W return average (20-minute return section). Some power loss at 6,500 ft vs. sea level, but less than many expect for a well-adapted athlete.
- **Gear selection for TTs — 60T chainring:** Running a 60T chainring with a 10T minimum cog allows controlled low-cadence pacing on false flats (70 RPM "diesel mode" at ~300W), avoiding the breathing spike that comes from excessive leg speed. Lockwood carries the gear deliberately into sections where he wants to recover speed without elevating heart rate. "Turning over a big diesel gear" on a false flat allows recovery while still carrying 34–35 mph.
- **Cadence and gear interaction:** Lockwood typically runs ~95 RPM consistently in training. For TT pacing, he selects cadence deliberately by terrain: accelerate at higher cadence, manage speed at steady-state cadence, recover on false flats by dropping to 70–80 RPM in a big gear. The ability to select precisely 2–3 RPM differences across gears is critical for squeezing seconds out of split times.
- **Training structure for a record attempt:** The months before the Moriarty record attempt included: 2 weeks completely off the bike, a gym adaptation phase (RPE-based, basic lifts, ankle mobility), then gradual build with easy riding and increasing tempo and over-under work, then heavy gym (twice per week) concurrent with longer endurance hours (25–30 hrs/week), then transition to a VO2max block (6 sessions/week, 3 double-days/week, ~40 min of VO2 stimulus per day split across 2 sessions, 3-week block). FTP work followed after the VO2 block.
- **VO2max session execution on trainer:** Use the flywheel to spin up to 120 RPM first, then add load (resistance mode). Shift down the cassette to maintain 110–115 RPM for the interval duration. The last 60 seconds at maximum sustainable effort is where the adaptation stimulus peaks. For aerobic athletes, starting hard and holding high cadence is critical — dropping to lower cadence lets heart rate recover and reduces the stimulus. Target ~110–115 RPM, not 120 (Lockwood found 105 was insufficient to keep heart rate elevated without recruiting larger motor units that fatigue faster).
- **VO2max session dose for non-elite athletes:** 1–2 sessions per week of 3–4 minute intervals is sufficient stimulus for most amateurs. 6 sessions/week (Lockwood's protocol) is appropriate only at his training volume and only after a properly structured aerobic base.
- **Rest after race season:** Lockwood took 2 months after the record attempt before FTP testing. Upon testing, FTP was within a couple percent of peak in-season values. Top-end athletes' power numbers are within ~1% year-round; the primary adaptation from rest and rebuilding is endurance depth, not peak watts. Omitting off-season rest eliminates the "headroom" needed to absorb subsequent training load.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Altitude adaptation time is highly individual. 6–7 days worked for Lockwood because of 10–15 years at altitude. Athletes without this background may need significantly longer.
- Power meter on Lockwood's TT bike was unreliable — all power figures for the record attempt are estimates based on training data and perceived effort, not direct measurement. This is explicitly flagged.
- 60T chainring and 10T minimum cog produces extreme chain angle on the smallest sprockets — "chain bind" is a real concern. The tradeoff is accepted because the Lockwood rarely uses those gears.
- VO2max block intensity is "really hard" — the word "savage" is used. Athletes not properly prepared (full aerobic base, strength base, proper rest period) will not respond to this block — they will simply accumulate fatigue.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- An out-and-back TT is not boring — it consists of 15–20 distinct tactical sections each requiring different power, cadence, body position, and strategy. Athletes who don't break down the course this way are leaving significant time available.
- First-day record attempts often fail not from insufficient fitness but from insufficient course knowledge — specifically, not knowing which sections allow over-commitment and which sections demand patience.
- Riding too hard in endurance training ("muddying" the zones) is one of the primary training errors even experienced athletes make. It prevents FTP development because high-intensity work can't be executed from a fatigued baseline. The training effect becomes "steady state and tempo" with a ceiling that never rises.
- VO2max work can be genuinely difficult for aerobic specialists — because their cardiovascular system recovers so fast, they must spin at 110+ RPM to prevent the heart rate from dropping mid-interval. Sprinters have the opposite problem (too much muscle recruitment, blow up immediately).

### Evolving Views
- Lockwood explicitly identifies the "muddy training" pattern — riding endurance too hard, doing too many interval sessions per week (3–5), and not periodizing gym work — as mistakes he made for years before working with Moore. This is a changed view based on accumulated experience.
- He also notes that he used to do FTP intervals as 4x8 min and "a bazillion" 10-minute efforts rather than a single 60-minute effort. The insight: "if you want to ride your bike for 60 minutes, go ride for 60 minutes." Moving from 4x8 to continuous TTE-style threshold work was a major change.

## Episode: 1658393151-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-31-being-faster-than-your-watts-with-giancarlo-bianchi.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1658393151
**Word count:** 26,224
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Giancarlo Bianchi ("GC"), a 20+ year amateur road racer based in Boulder, CO, discusses how to win races with limited power — specifically, FTP 260–290W at ~140 lbs (roughly 4.1–4.3 w/kg), yet regularly beating riders with >5 w/kg. The episode covers race intelligence, aerodynamic position on a road bike, equipment upgrades, course/competition reconnaissance, reading body language, pacing breakaways, and using normalized power as a race management tool. Though the primary focus is tactics, there is a substantial equipment and technique thread running throughout.

### Core Message (continued)
GC's core argument is that FTP gets you "a seat at the table" — after that, everything is chess. On flat and rolling courses where his low CdA and good TTE can be leveraged, he consistently beats much stronger riders by positioning better, conserving energy, and attacking at moments of collective fatigue.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Aerodynamic position on a road bike (most impactful upgrade):** Ride the hoods with 90-degree elbows, head down (the head is "one of the worst aerodynamic shapes" and sticking it up wastes significant watts), and a flat back. Many riders are "sails" in crosswinds — an aero road position makes drafting behind GC difficult, which is a competitive advantage.
- **Equipment priority — deep wheels over weight savings:** GC argues deep-section wheels (50–60mm) are the single most impactful equipment upgrade for most riders. The weight penalty (approximately half a pound vs. 30mm wheels) translates to seconds on a local climb. The aerodynamic gain at speeds above ~13 mph is far larger. Even on a 6–7% climb where top riders average 14–15 minutes, aerodynamics still meaningfully apply at sections where speed exceeds 13 mph.
- **Skin suit adoption:** GC was wearing skin suits in road races by ~2010–2014, before this was common in US amateur racing. He notes the aerodynamic advantage is real at racing speeds and also that skin suits are more comfortable to race in.
- **Course reconnaissance (race-mode mindset):** Riding a training route and reconning it as a race course require different mental modes. Recon means identifying cracks, sight lines, gradient changes, wind exposure, and where to position before key sections. GC reconned the Colorado State Road Race championship course even though it was a route he regularly trained on.
- **Wind reconnaissance:** Understanding not just wind direction but how wind interacts with course geometry is critical. A tailwind section followed by a crosswind turn creates a race-defining moment — the group is traveling fast with the tail, and as soon as the turn arrives, the crosswind creates echelon dynamics that shatter the field. Knowing this in advance allows pre-positioning in the top 5 before the turn. GC describes winning a Florida race twice using exactly this knowledge.
- **Competition reconnaissance:** Use road-results.com, Strava (public profiles), and ZwiftPower to build a pre-race profile of competitors. Review recent training, recent race results, and course performance data. Look at what courses they've done well on, what their power profile looks like (ZwiftPower), and how they looked in the days leading up to the race (training volume, type). GC identified a competitor at Masters Nats by recognizing his kit from a Strava photo.
- **Normalized power as a race management tool:** GC displays normalized power during races. When NP approaches his FTP, he knows the race is about to reach a decisive moment — regardless of how tired he feels. This recalibrated his internal gauge after his TTE extended significantly, because he felt less tired at the same NP than before, but the race dynamics were the same. He uses this metric to time position moves and anticipate attacks.
- **Pacing a solo breakaway:** GC soloed for ~40 minutes at the Colorado State Road Race, winning by approximately a wheel length. Key tools: (1) lap function on the computer, clocking a gap measurement at a landmark each time around, (2) fueling at 40g gel every 20 minutes (~120g carbs/hour) regardless of race pace, maintaining fueling even during slow sections, (3) knowing the last hill on the course is short but steep, and committing full power there to carry momentum into the downhill/final turn.
- **Reading body language of competitors:** Watch breathing rate (especially relative to your own after a hard section), shoulder/hip rock, bar grip tension, shoe tightening, swig of water before a climb — these are "tells." If their breathing is harder than yours after the same effort, they are more fatigued. Use this information to select the attack moment.
- **Fuel consumption for training days:** GC eats "hundreds of grams of carbs" for breakfast before a big FTP interval day, fuels every 15–20 minutes on the bike. "One–two–three breakfasts" starting with a simple carbohydrate snack at 5 AM, then a substantial main breakfast before training. Night-before carb loading is also important. This is the same fueling he uses for national championships or climbing stages.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- GC's tactical advantages (low CdA, good TTE, high repeatability in 2–6 minute efforts at ~344W/5.3 w/kg) are specific to his physiology. His advantage evaporates on pure climbing races.
- Normalized power as a race cue is highly individual — GC's FTP and typical race NP profile are specific to him. On some courses, NP approaches FTP quickly even at a moderate pace; on others it doesn't. The principle can generalize, but the specific number doesn't.
- Deep wheels on light riders require careful selection in crosswinds — weight-to-wheel-depth matching matters. This is the same caveat Lockwood raised in the Perspectives 10 episode.
- Competition stalking via Strava/ZwiftPower assumes public profiles. Power numbers from ZwiftPower may not match outdoor performance, but they reveal power profile shape (which durations a rider is strong at).

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- GC has beaten riders with >5 w/kg "often" — at FTP of roughly 4.1–4.3 w/kg. The mechanism is energy conservation, aerodynamic efficiency, and tactical intelligence, not physiological superiority. This is a concrete lived example of the "FTP gets you a seat at the table" principle.
- Reconning a course you already train on regularly is still valuable — because the mental mode when training ("enjoy the scenery, do your interval") is fundamentally different from the mental mode required to analyze the course as a racing surface.
- Attacking the man who is tired but still in the breakaway with you is often a mistake. Keeping a tired breakaway companion means they do some rotation work and provide some draft. Attacking them prematurely (as GC did at Albuquerque Masters Nats) burns matches, may cause cramping, and removes a useful domestique. Keep them until the final kilometer; then sprint.
- Breakaways are only as strong as the weakest link. If someone stops pulling, do not attack them — instead, get to the front and use the "rotate fingers" signal to rebuild cooperation. Attacking kills the breakaway for everyone.

### Evolving Views
- GC changed his assessment of attacking the South Florida rider at Albuquerque Nats after debriefing with Moore: he shouldn't have attacked him, because a tired companion is still useful for draft and occasional pulls. He came to understand this only after that race.
- After working with Moore for approximately two years, GC's training shifted from strictly polarized (easy or 3x8) to include tempo and over-under work, VO2max sessions (which he had never done before), and better gym periodization. His "depth of fitness" — TTE rather than peak FTP — improved substantially, and he noticed this primarily by the fact that the race's pace felt easier than his internal monitor expected, causing him to miss a move he would previously have recognized.

## Episode: 1884216615-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-40-training-mistakes-every-beginner-makes-and-solutions.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1884216615
**Word count:** 17,085
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
Kolie Moore and Kyle Helson discuss common training mistakes made by beginner cyclists (defined as less than ~2 years experience). The episode is primarily about training process, mindset, and information hygiene rather than equipment or technique per se. However, it contains several directly relevant practical recommendations, particularly around FTP identification by feel, how dogmatic "rules" in cycling impede rational equipment decisions, and how to use race participation as a learning tool for technique and tactics. The episode ends with listener questions that address FTP testing frequency, training zone use, and rookie errors.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Avoid dogmatic equipment "rules":** Cycling has a tradition of gatekeeping rules (e.g., "only use little ring in winter," clothing rules, sock height rules) that are arbitrary and unhelpful to new riders. These rules have no physiological basis. New cyclists should feel free to optimize equipment for their actual needs rather than adhering to tradition.
- **Don't wait to race until "fit enough":** Racing frequently at lower stakes teaches technique, peloton mechanics (how to move up, how to read a move, when to sprint) that no amount of FTP-building training can substitute. The skills of bike racing — positioning, handling in a bunch, reading the peloton — only develop through race exposure.
- **FTP identification by feel:** Learn FTP by feel before relying on testing. FTP is the hardest effort that feels sustainable: muscular (not lungs-burning), breathing is hard but not "running away," you can hold it but wouldn't want to hold it for more than 30–40 minutes at untrained level. Knowing FTP by feel is more valuable than a number because: (1) it works in any sport, (2) it serves as a fitness indicator when the number drops without explanation (red flag for fatigue).
- **Don't over-test FTP:** Testing once a week is a common beginner error — it's fatiguing and the data isn't actionable at that frequency. Testing should happen after a meaningful training block, not weekly.
- **Training zones: be close, not precise:** Being 5W off in a zone is irrelevant. Being 60–80W off (e.g., because a lactate test was poorly done or misinterpreted) is damaging. The primary source of zone error in beginners is not imprecision but miscalibration — ensure the FTP estimate is ballpark correct before worrying about zone precision.
- **Lactate testing is often not worth the cost for amateurs:** The same person's lactate threshold can be interpreted 8 different ways by 4 different practitioners. The largest discrepancy seen by Moore between lactate test result and actual FTP was 60W (lactate test underestimated by 60W). A power meter and honest self-assessment provides more actionable data than most lactate tests for amateurs.
- **Volume as sole training driver is a mistake:** More hours is not always better. New gains are rapid and can come from almost any training stimulus. Once noob gains plateau, volume alone stops working — structured training is required. Doing 6 days/week of VO2max and anaerobic work (a listener error) for two years produces no improvement and likely net harm.
- **Riding endurance too hard:** Going hard on endurance rides prevents adequate intensity in interval sessions. This creates "muddy" training where you're always at medium intensity and nothing is optimally trained. (Corroborated by Lockwood's experience discussed in Perspectives 21.)
- **Low-volume training still requires rest:** Total volume is not the only driver of fatigue. A 6-hour week of hard group rides and racing requires the same rest principles as a 20-hour week of mixed intensity.
- **Using racing as training and learning:** Race at local crits, Tuesday worlds, training races — this is where you expand your "decision tree." Try attacking from the gun, waiting for the sprint, going solo — whatever you wouldn't risk at a big event. Low-stakes results, high-stakes learning.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Lactate tests done carefully, with good protocol and a skilled interpreter, can corroborate power meter data perfectly. The issue is consistency of execution and interpretation, not the test itself.
- "Ride with people faster than you" is conditionally good advice — if the pace differential is manageable and you can learn from the experience. If you're getting dropped and hanging on for 4 hours, no learning is happening and you're just accumulating fatigue.
- The right balance of fun vs. structured training is genuinely individual. Someone who values the weekly group ride highly enough to keep it even at the cost of some fitness gain is making a valid personal choice — the coach's job is to make sure the trade-off is explicit, not to eliminate the group ride.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Focusing on FTP metrics as the primary goal can impede race performance. Riders who build a high FTP and then go race often discover that top-end repeatability, pacing skill, and positional technique are the real limiters — not FTP. The teeter-totter effect: focusing on one aspect of fitness often transiently reduces another.
- New gains from any training stimulus are so large that a beginner cannot distinguish between a good training protocol and a mediocre one for months. This means beginner success stories validate almost any coaching methodology, including pseudoscientific ones. High responders make bad coaches' programs look good.
- Heart rate decoupling in summer is often just heat and dehydration, not a real indicator of declining fitness. Confusing environmental effects for fitness signals is a common beginner error with data.

- Prioritize aerodynamic improvements (helmet, position, bike fit, wind tunnel time) over lightweight wheels for performance gains

- Real-world spoke and hub weights rotate at different radii with different moments of inertia; the model is intentionally simplified for illustration

### Core Message
Kolie Moore and Kyle Helson provide a comprehensive guide to Everesting (climbing 8,848 meters of elevation in a single ride on the same hill). They frame it as a borderline ultra-endurance event averaging ~12 hours for most athletes, and emphasize that logistics and preparation matter more than raw fitness. The episode covers hill selection, gearing, nutrition planning, mechanical prep, emergency planning, bike position, key training sessions (extensive sweet spot, endurance rides, low-cadence work), recon rides, periodization, pacing strategy, and kit selection. The central thesis is: control every variable you can, do at least two recon rides, pace conservatively, and eat far more than you think you need.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Do at least 1-2 recon rides on the actual hill: first at 4-5 hours (6-8 weeks out), second at 60-75% of full attempt duration (3-4 weeks out)
- Build your weekly long ride up to 6-8 hours; optionally do one 12-hour flat ride for confidence and pacing practice
- Key sweet spot workout: ~90% FTP for 15-minute reps with 5-10 minute rests, repeated extensively to build muscular endurance for sustained climbing
- Alternative sweet spot structure: 20-30 minutes of sweet spot every hour for 3-6 hours
- Build low-cadence climbing tolerance gradually: start with 1 minute low cadence / 4 minutes normal cadence within sweet spot or FTP intervals, add 30-60 seconds of low cadence per session over time
- Low-cadence work should be done at 80-100% FTP
- Choose gearing so you can still climb at 50-100 watts below your target pace; have at least 1-2 easier gears than your preferred gear as bail-out options
- Use a gearing calculator (e.g., Sheldon Brown's) to match gear ratio to target speed and preferred cadence
- Consider mountain bike cranksets on road bikes for access to smaller chainrings (e.g., 34T or 38T front ring) -- common practice for extreme hill climbs like Mount Washington
- Strengthen your core for sustained climbing: good mornings, Romanian deadlifts for low back; trap bar or farmer's carries for core stabilization
- Get a bike fit 1-2 months before your attempt, specifically at the gradient of your target climb; steep gradients close hip angle and strain the lower back
- If trainer-bound, set bike to the average slope of your target hill to simulate position specificity
- Start your Everesting attempt easier than you think you need to -- most files show steady power decline; conservative pacing dramatically increases odds of finishing
- Take short breaks as needed for bathroom, food, mental reset -- don't destroy yourself for the sake of continuity
- Plan for ~93 g/hr of carbohydrates during the ride (based on example: 300W FTP, 75 kg rider, 11-hour completion, 8,200 kJ, ~50% carb utilization)
- Include protein in your nutrition plan to feel satiated -- don't rely solely on gels and sugar for 12 hours
- Eat early and eat often; it takes longer to replenish glycogen than to burn through it
- Never try new foods on race day or even the night before; test everything on recon rides and long training rides
- Do a full mechanical once-over of your bike a week before the attempt, not the night before
- Bring spare tubes, tires, wheels, cables, and possibly a whole spare bike; know how to fix common failures
- Have an emergency plan: ride with a friend if possible; know cell service coverage on the hill; carry emergency contact info; tell someone your plan and check-in schedule
- Wear your most comfortable, proven shorts/kit -- not new or untested gear
- Estimate total time conservatively and build in buffer for breaks; if you run out of food, stop

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Average Everesting times are 9-13 hours; records are held by elite/former-pro athletes (Emma Pooley 8:53 women's, Rona McLaughlin 7:04 men's) -- don't benchmark yourself against pros
- If your longest ride is normally 2-3 hours, you need significant volume buildup before attempting 12 hours
- Recovery from a 12-hour ride can take 1-2 weeks if you're wrecked, or 2-3 days if you're adapted to long efforts
- Low-cadence training affects individuals differently; some tolerate it well, some find it very fatiguing -- monitor your response
- Steeper hills are generally better (less wind resistance, more energy fighting gravity), but only if you have appropriate gearing -- don't attempt 18% on a 39/28
- Shorter hill repeats (~half-mile climbs) keep you warmer and more mentally engaged vs. long climbs with extended descents requiring jackets; personal preference matters
- The Everesting.cc website has specific rules including a kinetic gain rule and the requirement that all elevation must come from repeats of the same hill (no loops, no ride-to elevation)
- If feeling tired at any point during training, you need rest, not more training

- Logistics and preparation are more important than training for Everesting success (Moore: "this is more important than the training aspect")
- This is one event where weight savings legitimately matter because you are fighting gravity at 6-8 mph with negligible aerodynamic drag -- the opposite of the rotating weight episode's conclusion for criterium racing
- Many first-time Everesters start their first reps way too hard and find out within a couple reps that it's unsustainable

- Don't overlook protein during ultra-endurance efforts; the "empty gut" feeling from gels-only nutrition is a real performance limiter even when carb math says you should be fine

### Core Message
Kolie Moore presents three progressive drills to develop sprint technique on the bike, grounded in the physics of Newton's third law: when you push hard on the pedals, the bike pushes you back, so sprinting requires both force production and force stabilization. The drills progress from big-gear force stabilization, to small-gear high-cadence bike-rocking technique, to a full-power sprint that integrates both skills. The key insight is that sprinting is a technique-limited activity, not just a power-limited one, and specific drill work with proper cues can unlock existing power.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Drill 1 -- Big Gear Drill (Force Stabilization):** Start from 20-30 RPM on flat ground or slight uphill; sprint standing for 10-15 seconds; aim to reach no higher than 60-70 RPM; keep core and arms very tight to stabilize force between feet and hands; do this standing (not seated) to remove saddle as a stabilization crutch
- **Drill 2 -- Small Gear Drill (High Cadence / Bike Rocking):** Use small chainring and medium-to-small cassette cog; start standing at ~90 RPM and gradually increase cadence until technique breaks down; naturally rock the bike back and forth underneath you; skilled riders can reach ~190-200 RPM standing; optimal duration is 10-20 seconds to allow neural patterning to develop; primary cue is "relax" -- muscles must relax quickly between pedal strokes to contract quickly; watch for chain slap at high speeds
- **Drill 3 -- Max Power / Race Speed Drill:** Coast down a hill to 20-25 mph (~40 kph); on flat ground, stand and sprint in the right gear; aim to hit peak power within 1-2 seconds of standing; feel the integration of Drill 1 (tight core/arms stabilizing force) and Drill 2 (relaxed, fluid pedaling at speed); faster sprinters can start at ~30 mph / 50 kph; keep your head up and look where you're going
- Incorporate 1-2 of these drills into interval ride warmups, 2-3 times per week
- For a dedicated drill session, do a couple big-gear drills, then small-gear drills, then finish with max-power sprints -- progression on the same day reinforces neural patterning
- Total sprint time in a quality session: ~60-90 seconds total
- Do these drills when fresh -- in the first 60-90 minutes of a longer ride
- Quality over quantity for sprint work; if your sprint is ~1800W, keep drill volume low to avoid digging a fatigue hole; for 800-1200W sprinters, a couple of warmup sprints is fine
- If the saddle bumps your thighs when standing and rocking the bike, your fit is probably too short -- a longer cockpit keeps center of gravity properly centered and gives room to move; this is especially common on the track
- Cadence and gearing optimization for sprinting will be covered in a future Watts Doc episode

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Watch out for knee problems with the big-gear drill (high torque at low cadence)
- Chain slap is a real risk during the small-gear high-cadence drill
- The host notes he can only do ~190 RPM standing for a couple seconds; his sweet spot for the small-gear drill is 10-15 seconds at ~170-175 RPM -- adjust to your own ability
- Sprint technique work should be done fresh; fatigued sprinting teaches bad motor patterns
- Riders with ~1800W sprints need to manage drill volume more carefully than riders at 800-1200W

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- The primary cue for high-cadence sprinting is "relax" (and the second cue is "really relax") -- being too tight locks up muscles and prevents the rapid contraction-relaxation cycle needed for high RPM; this is counterintuitive since sprinting feels like it should be about maximal tension
- Sprinting is as much a stabilization problem (Newton's third law -- the bike is trying to launch you off) as it is a force production problem; without proper core/arm tension, force "leaks" through torso rotation instead of going into the pedals
- After doing small-gear drills at 170 RPM, normal sprint cadence (110-120 RPM) feels slow, making it easier to think and refine technique at race speeds -- a deliberate perceptual training effect
- Bike fit for sprinting (especially track) should have a long cockpit to keep center of gravity between contact points; many riders, especially track riders, have fits that are too short for effective standing sprints

### Core Message
This comprehensive episode covers how to adapt training for track cycling across the spectrum from pure sprinters to endurance track racers. The central thesis is that aerobic fitness is the foundation for nearly all track events lasting over about one minute, and that the kilo (~1 min) represents the overlap zone where both sprinters and enduros can be competitive. For pure sprinters (kilo and shorter), the modern approach emphasizes getting "prison big" (strong enough to push massive gears) combined with developing anaerobic capacity, not just peak power. For endurance track events, all standard aerobic training principles apply, with modifications for high cadence, fixed-gear technique, and race-specific surging. Track technique (banking comfort, standing starts, flying 200 lines, fixed-gear pedaling, gearing strategy) is a distinct and important training component that cannot be replicated on the road.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Aerobic foundation for events over 1 minute:** For individual pursuit (4 min) and longer, apply the same base-building, FTP, and VO2max training as you would for any aerobic cycling discipline. There is no special "track aerobic" methodology.
- **Volume for endurance track riders:** Aim for 20-25 hours/week for pursuit-level riders; diminishing returns on VO2max improvements become severe beyond that. More volume generally enhances adaptations from targeted FTP and VO2max sessions.
- **High-cadence VO2max work** is highly track-specific training; prioritize it for endurance track racers over hill repeats.
- **Modify over-unders for track specificity:** Take the "overs" at 120-130 RPM to simulate race-pace accelerations. Start with small cadence increases (e.g., 100 to 105-110 RPM) if high cadence is uncomfortable.
- **Do NOT do all FTP workouts at high cadence** -- that is unnecessary. Modify only specific sessions (over-unders, race-specific intervals) for cadence.
- **"Death sentence intervals" for track race prep:** 30 seconds over / 30-60 seconds under, with overs at race-specific cadence (120-150 RPM for enduros), paced so you are completely emptied by the final rep. Two to three sets per session. This is harder than most track races.
- **Standing starts:** Practice regularly with short reps (even just 5 pedal strokes, then roll around easy). Fold 1-2 standing starts into the beginning of other sessions without replacing the main workout. Do NOT practice standing starts on a road bike with derailleur gears (chain can drop, causing crashes).
- **Flying 200 practice:** Technique-heavy; changes by track geometry. Ask experienced riders about lines and entry points for each specific track.
- **Practice the height-speed relationship** on the track: snake up and down the banking while paying attention to speed gained/lost at different heights.
- **Individual pursuit prep session example:** Warm up, do a couple of standing starts on race gear, then 2-3 efforts of 2-4K at race pace or full gas, rolling in from the rail. As race approaches, progress to 3-6 full-gas standing-start pursuits. Stop at 3-3.5K to keep something in reserve for subsequent reps.
- **Do NOT do all pursuit training uphill on the road** -- the cadence, aero position, and speed conditions are too different; this approach consistently fails to transfer for individual pursuit.
- **For sprinters, strength training is primary:** "Prison big" (Metty Cordi's philosophy). Focus on getting as strong as possible, including both bilateral and unilateral leg work. Unilateral work (single-leg press, single-leg squat) has proven particularly effective for on-bike transfer.
- **Bilateral deficit:** Expect single-leg lifts at ~70-80% of bilateral max (e.g., single-leg squat 275 lbs vs. back squat 360 lbs). Both bilateral and unilateral work are needed; do not rely exclusively on one.
- **Gearing strategy:** Keep a log of gears used for different events, who was there, and what worked. Match gear to expected race dynamics (breakaway = bigger gear for steady effort; surgy field race = smaller gear for acceleration).
- **Between efforts at the track:** Sprinters should rest as completely as possible (sit in the infield, do nothing for 30+ minutes). Endurance riders generally benefit from easy spinning between efforts.
- **Neural drive / freshness for sprint work:** Do not schedule full-gas sprint or high-cadence sessions the day after heavy legs. If you get less than ~7.5 hours of sleep before a planned hard day, push the session to the next day.
- **Weekly track league can substitute for intensity days** in training. Don't stack VO2max or anaerobic work the day before a track league night. Adjust surrounding training to keep quality high.
- **For beginners:** Don't overhaul training to start racing track. If you're already a crit racer, your fitness base is a good starting point. Focus on getting comfortable on the track, riding fixed gear, and learning track etiquette.
- **If gym strength gains aren't translating after a year of focused work (~50 watts improvement in sprint):** Consider shifting focus to aerobic development, VO2max, repeatability, and racing strategy instead.
- **Repeatability for points race accelerations:** Higher FTP and better aerobic endurance are the primary drivers. Add intermittent-effort training (not just steady-state) as race-specific supplement.
- **Improving leg speed / reducing spin-outs:** Practice on rollers; be very fresh for high-cadence neural work. May gain 10-15, possibly 20 RPM on the cadence at which peak power occurs, but top-end leg speed is largely a natural attribute.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- **Pure sprinters (kilo and shorter) probably do not need long endurance rides**, though they're fine to do for fun. Jeffrey Hoogland does occasional 2-4 week road blocks at 100-150W average but these disappear near major competitions.

- For VO2max training, cyclocross and track racing are NOT good substitutes for dedicated VO2max intervals, even though heart rate may be near max for 30-50 minutes. "No matter how many cyclocross races you do, chances are your VO2max is not going to [improve optimally]."
- Track sprinting is "kind of a whole different game" from road or endurance track -- training methods don't fully cross over.
- The "if you're not fast we probably can't make you fast" adage is unfortunately largely true for top-end leg speed and rate of force development. Strength is more trainable than leg speed.
- Contrast training and methods borrowed from track-and-field sprinting do work for track-and-field sprinting but have limited transfer to cycling sprinting in the hosts' experience.
- Depth drops and box jumps are useful benchmarking/scouting tools for explosivity but have not correlated strongly with on-bike improvements once an athlete is well-trained.
