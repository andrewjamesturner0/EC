## Episode: 625992387-empiricalcyclingpodcast-the-watts-doc-7-what-is-base-really.txt
**Numeric ID:** 625992387
**Word count:** ~4,300
**Series:** Watts Doc

### Core Message
The episode challenges the conventional understanding of "base training" as months of low-intensity zone 2 miles. Kolie and Kyle argue that many of the adaptations attributed to zone 2 base miles can also be obtained from threshold or VO2max work, and that the actual benefit of traditional base seasons is often rest and recovery from the prior season rather than the development of a new aerobic foundation. "Base" is reframed as the cumulative effect of all focused aerobic work across a season and a career, not specifically low-intensity mileage. The "bigger base, higher peak" pyramid metaphor is replaced with a "food plate" model where threshold, VO2max, and endurance work all contribute.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Build your longest ride up to the duration of your longest race (Friel recommendation endorsed as "pretty solid advice").
- Endurance rides should use a negative split — start at lower power (e.g., 150–170 W for a planned 200 W average) and build throughout. One Reddit user reported smashing all multi-hour power records by simply starting easier.
- To make endurance rides effective, they must challenge your endurance limit: if you can comfortably sustain 4 hours at 150 W, target 4.25–4.5 hours to push the adaptation.
- For athletes who are time-limited and have a training camp or long event approaching, VO2max intervals are an effective preparation tool: they raise the power you can sustain on subsequent long rides.
- A 90–120 minute steady endurance ride is excellent as a maintenance/between-race day workout (e.g., between a Friday and Sunday criterium).
- Friel recommends a minimum ~2-hour ride for zone 2 adaptations, but this is only useful if 2 hours is actually challenging for the athlete.
- If time-crunched, increase ride intensity rather than doing very short zone 2 rides. Zone 2 rides shorter than what you can easily handle provide minimal adaptation.
- Once you hit a duration ceiling, shift progressive overload to increasing power output on endurance rides, not just extending duration.
- Intervals can be started earlier than tradition suggests — there is no evidence that athletes must complete months of zone 2 before doing threshold intervals.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The advice about riding long applies to athletes racing long events. Time-Crunched Cyclist author Chris Carmichael is quoted: if your longest ride is 1–1.5 hours, race races that aren't longer than that.
- Elite professional cyclists are "genetic freaks" — their training is hard to screw up regardless, and their practices should not be blindly copied by amateurs.
- Scientific studies on base training typically use recreationally active or average amateur populations; results may not transfer to well-trained athletes.
- The physiological claims behind "you must ride easy to build capillaries" are incorrect, but the practice of easy riding in the off-season still produces real rest and recovery benefits.
- Endurance pace (zone 2) and VO2max training do produce subtly different adaptations even though both cause aerobic improvements. They cannot be exactly equated.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Months of zone 2 riding work "literally false, metaphorically true" — the real benefit is recovery from the previous season, not building an aerobic base.
- Zones 1–5 produce roughly the same tick-marks on the Coggan physiological adaptation chart; "aerobic is aerobic" and different intensities yield similar adaptations.
- A person with a "big base" has simply done a lot of focused aerobic work at all intensities over time — not a lot of zone 2 miles specifically.
- "Going slow makes you slow" is probably not true in a strict sense, but "you need to go slow to go fast" is only true if interpreted as "you need adequate rest."
- Performing extensive zone 2 before starting intervals is not a universal prerequisite; it's fine to begin intervals earlier as long as recovery is managed.

### Evolving Views
- None identified in this episode beyond the general thesis reframing what "base" means — this appears to be a settled position for the hosts rather than an update to a prior view.

## Episode: 1199788219-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-17-sweetspot-vs-polarized-is-kayfabe.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1199788219
**Word count:** ~17,000
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
The episode argues that the "sweet spot vs. polarized" debate is a false commercial dichotomy (kayfabe) that obscures more useful thinking about training. Both are merely tools, and starting from a training modality rather than from an athlete's specific limiters and desired adaptations is backwards. The hosts advocate for thinking first about what physiological quality needs development (VO2max, threshold, endurance, anaerobic capacity), then selecting the methods that develop it. Sweet spot training's primary benefit is its favorable stimulus-to-fatigue ratio and ease of measuring progress via FTP. The episode also argues that sweet spot is essentially threshold training, meaning the large body of lactate threshold literature fully applies to it even though it's not labeled "sweet spot" in research.

### Key Training Recommendations
- For time-crunched athletes (8 hours/week or less), increasing intensity (up to sweet spot / threshold) is necessary to elicit sufficient adaptation, since easy volume alone will be insufficient.
- Sweet spot training becomes increasingly demanding at higher fitness levels: once you can do 3x20 comfortably, you must progress to 3x30, 3x40, or 2x40+ to continue gaining.
- For threshold/sweet spot work, target an RPE of 7–8: if you're "gritting your teeth and really digging deep," you're at a 9 and that's too hard for this intensity domain. Do not white-knuckle the last 3 minutes.
- Progress sweet spot intervals asymmetrically if needed: a 25-minute + 20-minute session is a valid step before 25+25, then 30+25, etc.
- Time-in-zone must be accumulated within a single session or at minimum within a single ride; splitting it between a morning session and an afternoon session does not count as the same stimulus.
- For VO2max work, the stimulus is the intensity, not the duration. Aim for 15–20 total interval minutes per session (12–15 to start); going beyond ~23 total minutes is unusual. Individual intervals can range from 2 to 8 minutes depending on athlete.
- Athletes with VO2max in the mid-high 90s ml/kg/min may need more endurance training, not more VO2max training.
- Hard workouts: target 7–8 RPE for threshold/sweet spot, 9–9.5 for VO2max-type efforts, essentially never a true 10. Limit hard days to 2–3 per week (rarely 4), with genuinely easy recovery between.
- Do not pre-plan training zone distributions (e.g., "80/20" or "all sweet spot"). Ask instead: how many hard days per week can I recover from, and how hard should they be?
- Polarized approaches are most relevant for athletes who can sustain very high training volumes with proper easy riding between hard sessions.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- "80/20" polarized distribution may mean only 4 hard sessions in a 20-session block, which is not much for advanced athletes who may need more specific stimulus.
- Sweet spot training does produce real metabolic fitness: it increases the sustainable metabolic load (threshold) and extends TTE, with some athletes able to hold threshold power for 80+ minutes after long-term training combining sweet spot and endurance.
- Both sweet spot and polarized training approaches are subject to confirmation bias. If someone burns out on sweet spot and rests, the perceived improvement from "switching to polarized" may be mostly the rest.
- Testing frequently (e.g., every 4–8 weeks) is already over-testing by most standards; rely on modeled FTP data (e.g., WKO5) and RPE cues rather than constant testing.
- Many people set their sweet spot power too high because their FTP is overestimated, making what they call "sweet spot" actually threshold.
- Measurement bias: 5-minute power includes a significant anaerobic contribution and is a poor proxy for VO2max gains; FTP/TTE are more reliable progress metrics for threshold-focused training.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- "Sweet spot vs. polarized" is a commercial construct ("kayfabe") — the debate is more useful for selling training plans than for guiding individual training.
- Sweet spot training is basically threshold training; the absence of a "sweet spot" label in peer-reviewed literature does not mean there is no scientific evidence for it — it's all the LT/LT2/MLSS literature.
- The mechanism of progressive overload differs by training domain: for VO2max work it's intensity (like weight training stimulus = load), not time-in-zone; for sweet spot/threshold it's extending time-in-zone.
- An individual workout rarely matters; what matters is the mechanism of progressive overload and whether you are consistently embracing it.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie mentions he has "not followed [Seiler's polarized model] since it was very popular a couple of years ago" and acknowledges uncertainty about whether Seiler has updated his recommendations, flagging his commentary on polarized as potentially outdated.

## Episode: 1270352152-empiricalcyclingpodcast-300k-time-crunched-training.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1270352152
**Word count:** ~25,000
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips (300K special episode)

### Core Message
This milestone episode addresses training for time-crunched athletes and offers a frank assessment: at around 4–6 hours per week of training, athletes will plateau relatively quickly, and intensity cannot fully substitute for volume over the long term. Kolie states that approximately 14–15 hours per week is the minimum he has observed for year-on-year FTP improvements. The episode covers how to structure limited training time, the role of TSS, the primacy of life stress management, and how to progress threshold and VO2max work within a tight schedule. It also addresses the "two-a-day" approach and the sleep vs. training trade-off.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Manage life stress first: training hard on top of high psychological stress produces poor quality efforts and may not generate the peripheral adaptive signals (calcium flux, NADH/ATP stress, etc.) needed for adaptation.
- At low volumes (4–6 h/week), more is always more: going from 4 to 6 hours is a 50% increase in stimulus. At these volumes, adding even one endurance ride per week (if easy enough) matters greatly.
- Two hard sessions per week (7–8/10 RPE or harder) is the right starting point for most time-crunched athletes; sometimes 3, rarely 4.
- A hard day better than a hard day: favor 2–3 hard sessions with full recovery days over 5 moderate sessions where you never fully recover.
- For threshold work (sweet spot to FTP), add time-in-zone gradually: e.g., start with 2x20, progress to 2x22.5, 2x25, then consolidate toward longer single intervals. Time-in-zone must be accumulated within a single ride, not split across morning and afternoon sessions.
- For VO2max work, the key is intensity not duration: aim for 15–20 total interval minutes per session, ensuring you are "gasping like a fish." Individual intervals: 2 minutes minimum, up to 5–6 minutes max for most athletes.
- Do not pre-plan your training distribution (80/20, etc.) — ask instead whether the stimulus is working and whether you're recovering from it.
- RPE decoupling is a key indicator of off-bike stress or inadequate recovery: if a normally-manageable 2x20 threshold workout feels like grinding through at a 9/10, back off.
- Sleep: 98–99.5% of recovery is eating and sleeping. Do not consistently sacrifice sleep for extra training time; a sleep deficit compounds faster than expected.
- Two-a-day workouts are a valid way to increase volume when single long rides are impossible.
- One 4–5 hour ride per week is sufficient to keep most athletes progressing in endurance fitness, even if weekday rides are short.
- Avoid "makeup" workouts: if you missed Tuesday's VO2max session, do not try to stack it on top of Saturday's planned workout.
- If recovery from a hard ride takes more than 2–3 days at your current low training volume, your body is not adapted to volume and a single hard effort is disproportionately depleting — a sign to build general volume first.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The 14–15 hours/week threshold for year-on-year improvement is a coaching observation, not a universal law; genetics and prior training history affect this substantially.
- Athletes who have trained at high volume previously can maintain near-permanent aerobic adaptations on much lower volume; the "minimum dose" to maintain past fitness is much smaller than the dose required to build new fitness.
- Athletes with high genetic endowment may compete at high levels on low volume — do not use their results as normative expectations.
- TSS is a useful rough tool but creates false equivalences: two 30-second maximal sprints will normalize to a high TSS but are not equivalent to an hour at FTP in aerobic terms. Weekly intensity factor optimization is a path toward overtraining.
- Zone 2 is best understood as "the hardest pace that still feels easy" — the actual wattage is secondary. Athletes should not peg it at the top of zone 2 by the Coggan table. Even riding 20–30 W below what they think is their LT1 is fine; there is no observed adaptation difference.
- Sweet spot cannot fully substitute for long rides in race preparation for events like Unbound 200 or similar ultra-endurance events.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- At low training volumes, higher-intensity training taps out faster than people expect (the "ryanodyne receptor fragmentation" effect that produces calcium leak and drives adaptation in novices is absent in well-trained athletes — they must "do it the old-fashioned way and just ride a lot").
- People who are low-volume often recover from hard efforts more slowly than people doing 20+ hours/week — higher chronic training load improves the body's capacity to absorb subsequent stress.
- Athletes in excellent aerobic condition frequently feel post-maximal-effort like they "could have gone harder" — this is a sign of good aerobic fitness, not under-effort.
- When zone 2 is prescribed at 2 mmol/L blood lactate (a common LT1 proxy), many well-trained athletes end up riding at low sweet spot intensities, making every "endurance" ride functionally a tempo session — this is a common setup for overreaching.
- The LT1 / zone 2 transition does not have a power-curve fingerprint — Andy Coggan designed the zone as a descriptive range, not a prescriptive target, to cover where most athletes' genuine endurance pace would fall.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie acknowledges that "volume is more and more the key driver of continuous aerobic adaptation" with each year he coaches, updating from an earlier emphasis primarily on interval quality.
- Kolie notes that his views on base training for low-volume athletes are tentative and may "easily change week to year."

## Episode: 1333403119-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-23-how-much-ftp-training-is-too-much.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1333403119
**Word count:** ~13,500
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
The episode follows up on the prior TTE episode by addressing whether FTP training is over-emphasized in amateur cycling. The central argument is that FTP improvement requires a three-legged stool of FTP training, VO2max training, and endurance training in combination — exclusive focus on FTP intervals will plateau eventually because VO2max sets the ceiling on FTP, and endurance volume supports the aerobic base that allows both to rise. FTP training is "necessary but not sufficient" for most race goals. The episode also addresses how to diagnose whether FTP or another metric (sprint, anaerobic capacity, pack skills) is actually the binding limiter in race performance.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Do not rely exclusively on 2x20 FTP intervals for fitness. Combine FTP training with VO2max work and endurance volume for continued improvement.
- Diagnose your race limiter before choosing what to train: if you are dropped on an early fresh hill, sprint/anaerobic capacity may be the limiter; if you blow up late in a race, endurance and FTP are more likely limiters.
- If your sprint at the end of a race is within a couple percent of your fresh sprint peak, sprint training is not the priority. If it's reduced by 30–50%, aerobic endurance is likely the limiter.
- FTP training at a correct target should feel hard but manageable — RPE 7–8. You should not have to "summon demons" to complete intervals. If you are, your FTP is overestimated.
- Raising power arbitrarily in FTP sessions without a felt sense of where threshold actually is is risky and unreliable. Athletes who can feel their threshold accurately can self-adjust; others should not raise targets without testing.
- Mechanism of FTP increase: FTP training is a duration-based stimulus for well-trained athletes (extend time-in-zone). VO2max training raises the ceiling. Endurance volume supports both. Focus shifts depending on which is currently limiting.
- Anaerobic capacity (W') improvements come relatively quickly but plateau; threshold and VO2max take longer but extend further.
- TTE (time-to-exhaustion at FTP) is often the more actionable lever than FTP wattage itself — extending TTE from 35 minutes toward 60+ minutes is a common and realistic goal.
- When time-limited, there is only so much FTP work you can add to compensate — the ryanodyne receptor noob-gains effect that makes HIT work so well for beginners does not persist indefinitely.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- A 200 W FTP with a giant sprint may still win match sprints; FTP alone does not determine race outcomes.
- Early in training (off the couch), FTP work, VO2max work, and even endurance all raise FTP comparably — it is primarily a plasma volume effect at that stage.
- FTP tests with significant anaerobic contribution (20-min test, ramp test, 2x8) can be "taught to," meaning the score can rise from anaerobic capacity improvements rather than aerobic ones. Use caution when interpreting test results.
- There is "a ton of hype" around FTP as a metric, but it is nonetheless a genuinely important aerobic marker; the hype does not invalidate the metric.
- Not all cyclists should emphasize FTP: specialists (sprinters, track pursuers) have different needs, and some race formats (crits, track) weight other capabilities heavily.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Simply doing 2x20 all the time will help most people maintain FTP but not continue improving it — the adaptation from threshold-only training is a duration-based stimulus that plateaus once TTE is extended.
- For a rider with poor race results who has excellent 1-minute and sprint power, the fix is almost always more threshold/endurance training, not more sprint work — the sprint isn't the problem; the aerobic base is.
- FTP training popularity may be partly explained by immediate measurability: it produces visible week-to-week improvement signals that longer-term adaptations (VO2max ceiling, lifetime aerobic base) do not.

### Evolving Views
- None identified in this episode.

## Episode: 1396150891-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-25-whats-so-special-about-zone-2.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1396150891
**Word count:** ~23,000
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
Responding to a client consultation question and forum discussions, this episode argues that zone 2 has no unique physiological adaptation that cannot be stimulated by riding at higher intensities — it's a continuum, and "threshold training is endurance training" (Andy Coggan's words, endorsed by Kolie). What is special about zone 2 is what it *allows* within a training plan: because it accumulates relatively little fatigue, large volumes can be stacked without compromising recovery for quality interval sessions. At very high volumes (20–30 h/week), the "pro zone 2 approach" makes sense because the fatigue from that volume itself limits how much high-intensity work can be stacked on top. For most athletes riding 10–15 hours/week, there is no obligation to include dedicated zone 2 if other work is being done well.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Endurance riding (zone 2 / LSD) is best defined as "the hardest pace that still feels easy" and should pass the talk test, regardless of power number. Do not peg it at the top of the zone 2 range by power table.
- Riding 20–30 W below your apparent LT1 is fine; Kolie reports no observed adaptation difference between riding at 60% of FTP versus 45–50% of FTP for athletes whose LT1 is around 60–65% of FTP.
- For pro athletes (20–30 h/week) under high volume, maintain endurance riding during a VO2max block but ride easier than usual — doing so maintains FTP and VO2max even when no hard intervals are happening.
- Endurance riding is a highly effective maintenance tool: when fatigued pro athletes switch to easy endurance-only training, their threshold and VO2max numbers recover over time without any interval work.
- On a trainer, break up endurance rides with cadence variation (e.g., 10 minutes at 10 rpm above normal, then 10 rpm below, repeat) rather than adding intensity.
- When transitioning from 3-hour to 6-hour long rides, add 30 minutes per week conservatively or 60 minutes per week aggressively — either is fine for most athletes.
- Include preemptive rest weeks when increasing volume dramatically. A coach's job is often to pull the leash (reduce training) rather than crack the whip.
- If only doing 2–3 rides of 2 hours per week, those rides do not need to be zone 2. Threshold or VO2max work in that slot still provides endurance-continuum adaptation. Zone 2 is not mandatory for short schedules.
- Consistency (80% effort every session) outperforms intermittent crushing (100%) followed by rest (0%).

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The lower limit of zone 2 adaptation is unknown; there is no established minimum wattage. What seems clear is that riding "too easy" (literally chainless or zero resistance) would not adapt, but once pedaling meaningfully, very low intensities still provide adaptation.
- Very high volume (15–30 h/week at zone 2) does accumulate real fatigue and limits quality interval capacity. The "zone 2 is free/costless fatigue" narrative is only true at moderate volumes.
- Zone 2 defined by 2 mmol/L blood lactate puts many trained athletes at low sweet spot — this is a common misapplication that turns endurance rides into tempo rides with associated fatigue accumulation.
- There is no strict correlation between LT1 power (relative to FTP) and fiber type; training volume appears to be the larger predictor of LT1 position.
- Zone 2 pace varies greatly between athletes; the number in the Coggan zone table is a convenience, not a physiological fingerprint.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Zone 2 is not special in terms of adaptations; it is special in terms of what it permits in a training week — low fatigue, high stackability.
- Copying Pogacar's zone 2 training plan would not make an amateur competitive; his genetics are the differentiating factor, not the zone 2 volume.
- A retired pro or someone with many years of high-volume training can maintain very high fitness on surprisingly low volume because many aerobic adaptations become semi-permanent.
- Zone 2 physiologically is best defined as "a training intensity that does not accumulate much undue fatigue" — its defining feature is what it *doesn't* do (accumulate fatigue), not a specific fuel-usage or fiber-recruitment pattern.
- "Junk miles" is a myth when applied to easy riding; truly junk effort is moderate-but-not-structured riding (sprinting from stoplights, tempo fartlek commutes) that creates fatigue without targeted adaptation.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie explicitly flags that his views on zone 2 physiology are still forming: "between now and whenever I've thought about it for a year or two, you're going to see an evolution of thought" on what zone 2 means physiologically.
- He has historically referred to endurance riding as "endurance training" rather than "zone 2" to avoid prescription-by-zone-number; this naming preference is confirmed here as intentional.

## Episode: 1514086555-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-28-ftp-and-vo2max-thoughts-and-listener-questions.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1514086555
**Word count:** ~23,000
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
Following up on the VO2max podcast series, this episode clarifies programming details for VO2max and FTP blocks, addresses common mistakes, and answers listener questions. The key messages are: (1) before beginning a VO2max block, an athlete should be at or near their previously-established aerobic fitness (best achieved via a period of endurance training, not by immediately jumping into hard intervals after time off); (2) recovery after a VO2max block takes far longer than most athletes expect — 2–8 weeks to see full FTP improvements — and beginning a new training phase after only 3–4 days of rest is a common error; (3) FTP training does not necessarily raise FTP, especially in trained athletes, because it is primarily a duration-based stimulus, not an intensity-based one; and (4) volume is the primary long-term driver of FTP development, with most athletes being volume-limited rather than intensity-limited in their FTP improvements.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Before a dedicated VO2max block, bring aerobic fitness to a prior baseline first — ideally via a period of higher-than-normal endurance volume. Getting off the couch and doing VO2max work immediately accumulates fatigue faster than adaptation can occur.
- VO2max block structure: 2 hard workouts per week is the right starting point; a "mixed block" (1 VO2max + 1 threshold workout per week) is a valid alternative when pure VO2max work stalls.
- One possible block stacking: 2–3 consecutive hard days (Thu/Fri/Sat), then easy Sunday, then recovery Mon–Wed. Not the only valid approach.
- After a VO2max block, allow full recovery until legs feel good (often 1–2+ weeks of easy riding), then test with a 3–5 minute maximal effort to assess FTP changes. Do not test after 3–4 days.
- Post-block FTP check: expect 50/50 chance of seeing the improvement immediately vs. still being fatigued. If fatigued, take more easy days. Minimum expected gain after a good block for high-volume athletes: 15 W immediately plus another 10–15 W over the following 2 weeks. Lower-volume athletes will see smaller gains.
- For VO2max intervals: minimum interval duration ~2 minutes; a range of 2–8 minutes is useful. Total interval time per session 15–20 minutes; no benefit observed from going beyond ~23 minutes. Starting intervals "not quite so hard" when fresh prevents blowing up the first effort.
- High cadence during VO2max intervals reduces muscular fatigue and is particularly recommended for double-day sessions.
- FTP training: do not summon the "pass/fail at a specific wattage" mindset. If FTP workouts feel very hard, your FTP target may be overestimated. Use RPE and heart rate alongside power.
- Volume is the primary driver for most athletes stuck on FTP plateaus: clients riding 10–12 h/week rarely see consistent FTP improvements; those at 20–30 h/week almost always do.
- When volume plateaus are unavoidable, shift focus: improve sprint, pack-handling skills, repeated-effort capacity, or race craft rather than continuing to chase FTP in a context where volume limits it.
- Consider an alternative season structure for volume-limited athletes: stay at 95% fitness all season and go race more, rather than trying to peak 2–3 times.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- There is no single "correct" way to periodize VO2max and FTP work — mixing modalities in the same block can work; dedicated sequential blocks can work. The key is recovery and measuring output.
- The post-block FTP improvement lag is real but poorly studied; it may relate to the mitochondrial biogenesis lag described in the dysfunctional mitochondria episode (referenced but not numbered in this transcript).
- Dedicated VO2max blocks are not always appropriate — skip them if there is insufficient runway for recovery before a target race.
- "Noob gains" from FTP training are primarily plasma volume effects and apply to all aerobic training modalities equally — the real mechanism is not threshold-specific.
- FTP test anxiety can deflate results; informal threshold discovery (ride 10 minutes at a spot, build to threshold feel, stop at any point) is often a better approach than formal testing for anxious athletes.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- FTP training does not necessarily raise FTP in trained athletes — it is primarily a duration-based stimulus, more like extending TTE than lifting a ceiling. The ceiling is VO2max.
- Early FTP improvements from just about any aerobic training are largely plasma volume gains, not threshold-specific adaptations.
- Most athletes are volume-limited in their FTP development, not intensity-limited — adding more hard intervals when volume is already low is the wrong prescription.
- When FTP improvements stall, the correct response is often to change the season strategy (race fresher, more often) rather than add more training stress.

### Evolving Views
- The 2–8 week post-VO2max-block recovery window was updated from the 2023 season of coaching data: "for a lot of them, it was not the average full recovery time…anywhere from two to six to eight weeks." This represents an updating of expectations compared to a more optimistic prior assumption.
- Kolie comments he "used to" give athletes their old coach's advice to aim for 400 W FTP quickly, and acknowledges this was naive.

## Episode: 1560449302-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-29-training-myths-part-3-volume.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1560449302
**Word count:** ~26,000
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
This myths episode addresses several common training volume misconceptions. Myth 1: you can overtrain on volume but not intensity — false; you can overtrain on intensity at surprisingly low volumes if riding hard on every session. Myth 2: women cannot do as much training volume as men — false; Kolie has never coached a woman who couldn't match or exceed men's volume capacity, and the apparent gap is due to shorter race distances and second jobs, not physiology. Myth 3: you can replace high volume with high intensity — 90% myth, 10% true; works to a limited degree at sub-threshold intensities but definitely not at or above threshold. Myth 4: just spinning easy is junk miles — false; all easy aerobic riding provides central adaptations (plasma volume, cardiac output) regardless of intensity; what's actually "junk" is semi-hard, unfocused riding that creates fatigue without targeted adaptation. Myth 5: you don't need to train a ton of hours to be fast — has the largest grain of truth (genetics and training history are huge), but for most athletes, volume is the primary long-term driver of aerobic performance.

### Key Training Recommendations
- If prone to overtraining on volume, the most common cause is riding endurance too hard (e.g., because LT1 was assigned at 2 mmol/L, placing it at low sweet spot). Fix: ride endurance pace genuinely easy, passing the talk test. Athletes should feel like they're "pedaling but not really doing any work."
- A new client's first camp/block experience should be confirmatory: if an athlete does a 20-hour easy week and reports no soreness and feeling great, the diagnosis is that they had been riding their endurance too hard all along — the solution is to ride genuinely easier.
- For high-volume blocks or camps: keep intensity very low ("suspiciously easy"). Eating and recovering adequately allows volume accumulation without overtraining.
- Commuting by bike at low intensity (try-not-to-sweat intensity) is a legitimate endurance stimulus and can meaningfully add to weekly training volume. Pandemic data: athletes who stopped bike commuting lost ~10 W FTP.
- Two-a-days are a valid volume-accumulation strategy; if an athlete can ride AM and PM, the accumulated contractions produce aerobic adaptation.
- Hiking, walking, and other low-impact aerobic cross-training provides central (cardiovascular) adaptations that transfer to cycling; not an exact replacement but meaningfully complementary.
- For athletes where FTP is plateaued: the solution may not be more training. Consider whether race experience, pack skills, repeated-effort capacity, or sprint improvement would produce better outcomes.
- If doing a VO2max block with high volume simultaneously, keep endurance intensity very low and manage nutrition carefully (high energy demands at 20–30 h/week + VO2max intervals).

### Caveats & Qualifications
- "You can overtrain on volume but not intensity" as a myth has a grain of truth: many people overtrain on volume because they accidentally ride their endurance too hard, making every session moderate-intensity; they don't experience this with pure HIT because they'd never sustain 5 days/week of maximal VO2max work.
- Intensity substitution for volume: can work for sub-threshold zones (e.g., 4-hour endurance ride → 2-hour slightly higher endurance ride is an imperfect but acceptable substitution). Definitively does not work for converting long endurance rides into over-threshold interval sessions — different stimuli, disproportionately high fatigue.
- Scientific literature on HIT vs. volume training typically uses subjects who are in a state of readiness for noob gains, which inflates the apparent efficacy of HIT and makes results hard to generalize to well-trained athletes.
- The "genetics" caveat for low-volume fast athletes: some are just gifted. Some are coasting on past training history. Some are highly experienced tactically. Don't assume their training is the differentiating factor.
- Even with very favorable genetics, training adaptation rate and magnitude still vary significantly — some gifted athletes respond explosively to any stimulus; coaching these athletes requires care not to overload them.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- "Going kind of hard all the time" — not committing to easy or hard — is the closest thing to actual junk miles: enough fatigue to accumulate, not enough stimulus to adapt meaningfully.
- Sprinting from stoplights during commutes is close to junk in the purest sense: stimulus-to-fatigue ratio of near zero.
- Women's apparent training volume limitations are almost entirely explained by shorter race distances and second-job demands rather than physiological capacity — the physiology appears equivalent.
- The "high volume" training done by elite women (e.g., 30–35 h/week off-season for Van Vluten) is consistent with physiological parity, not an outlier.
- A sedentary VO2max in the high 60s (vs. population average ~40 for men) means some athletes "start halfway to home plate" — their training load is not the primary differentiator in their performance.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie's position on the necessity of volume has hardened over time: he is more confident than in earlier episodes that year-on-year improvement at training volumes below ~14–15 h/week is rare for most athletes, and that the typical response to stalled FTP should be increased volume (when feasible), not more intensity.
- He flags that the question of whether women can do as much volume as men is one he has answered empirically through coaching (they can and often do more) rather than from the literature, and that historical race calendar differences are the confounding variable.
