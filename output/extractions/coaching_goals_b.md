## Episode: 2012769243-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-48-avoiding-over-optimization.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2012769243
**Word count:** 20,904
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
This coach roundtable (7+ Empirical Cycling coaches) focuses on avoiding over-optimization and instead building optimal habits for the "99%." The central argument is that consistency, realistic goal-setting, and sustainable long-term planning produce better outcomes than obsessing over marginal gains or chasing perfect training execution. The episode warns against the "all-or-nothing" mentality and the vicious cycle of ramping hard then burning out. Coaches argue that stress management — across work, family, sleep, and training — is the real driver of periodization.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Goal-setting:** Use SMART goals; the "achievable" criterion matters most. Don't attempt to go from 20th to winning a race in one year — aim to crack top 10 if you're typically 15th–20th.
- **Process goals over outcome goals:** Set measurable in-race process goals (e.g., improving peloton positioning every lap, tracked by DS video at feed zones) rather than only fitness outcome goals.
- **FTP expectations:** Expect roughly 10–15 W of FTP gain in two training blocks; 30 W in a year is exceptional. A client wanting to go from 250 W to 400 W FTP should be told realistic gains are ~10–30 W/year.
- **Mid-season breaks:** Schedule 7 days fully off mid-season (suggested around May and June for double-season racers). ~50% of athletes feel immediately stronger on return; ~50% need a couple of weeks to regain feel. Allow 1–2 retraining weeks after the break.
- **All-or-nothing clients:** Give achievable minimums rather than aspirational targets. If a client can't complete 5–6 hr rides, prescribe 2–3 hr rides; if weekly targets are being missed, prescribe 30–45 min "momentum days" to maintain the habit.
- **Recovery ride discipline:** IFs consistently at or above 0.75–0.80 across all rides is a red flag. Easy riding should dip well below 0.6 IF. The floor of ride intensity matters: if nothing in a training file goes below 0.5 or 0.6, training is too hard globally.
- **Volume priority:** Generally prioritize volume over intensity for long-term road performance, but note that some athletes (e.g., those who dislike long rides or are very time-limited) may benefit from intensity-priority with noted genetic exceptions.
- **Endurance ride intensity:** For a 400 W FTP rider, zone-two endurance rides at ~200 W (IF ~0.50) are appropriate; rarely exceed ~0.60 IF for endurance rides. Going above LT1 on endurance rides compromises recovery for interval sessions.
- **Early morning ride fueling:** Eat within 30 minutes before riding to top off liver glycogen (muscle glycogen is available but liver glycogen depletes overnight). Consider liquid calories (fruit juice, smoothies) if solid food is hard before early rides. CGM can help individuals fine-tune their carb timing relative to exercise start.
- **Climbing specificity:** If you struggle to hold power on climbs but are fine on flats, elevate the front wheel on the trainer (e.g., brick under front wheel), do tempo/sweet spot work in that position, and progressively build. Start with just 10 min and build to an hour-long tempo session.
- **Strength/cross-training:** Include upper-body pushing/pulling and rotation movements (e.g., Russian twists, swimming, rock climbing/bouldering). These improve the anterior-posterior muscle imbalance from cycling. 10–15 min of yoga post-ride to open hip flexors is beneficial.
- **Tracking workout quality:** Log RPE, motivation, soreness, stress in training software on the day it happens — not weeks later. Subjective data loses precision quickly.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Volume/FTP improvements are non-linear (log-linear curve): large gains early, diminishing returns as training matures.
- Individual variation is wide — stress recovery rates differ person to person; what works for one (e.g., a high zone-two LT1 athlete like James at ~350 W) is not generalizable.
- Off-season training goals should account for loss of summer motivators (group rides, daylight); don't plan winter volume assuming summer-level motivation.
- Amateur riders should not model their training on world-tour pros, who may train sub-maximally because they race constantly; pros riding LT1-focused training partly reflects overestimated threshold targets and race frequency, not a universal prescription.
- 30-min rides: value depends on the individual. For some it is a "momentum day" win; for others with high travel/commute burden, a walk may provide equivalent benefit with less logistical stress.
- Comparing yourself to friends or pros on Strava is counterproductive; their volume reflects their genetics, not a prescription for yours.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Doing intervals at 3000 kJ endpoint is **not** equivalent training for race fatigue resistance — endurance-pace 3000 kJ rides allowed athletes to hit great numbers that they could NOT replicate after 3000 kJ of actual race-stress riding. Race-specific accumulated hard work, not kilojoule totals, is the relevant metric.
- Positioning and race craft can be worth more than 10 extra FTP watts. Coaches on the call said they'd happily sacrifice some FTP for the ability to enter a final corner in 1st–2nd wheel with complete reliability.
- The Norwegian 4×4 method (mentioned as a listener question) was dismissed in one sentence: the hosts' first Google result cited Joe Rogan and Andrew Huberman, and they moved on.
- High-zone-two training at LT1 for pros may be producing burnout and mental fatigue — the effort to maintain precise zone 2 riding solo is psychologically taxing after years of training, and may be driving down threshold training volume in pro programs.
- Fernando Alonso parallel: "Driving at 90% is sometimes faster than 100% because the car is unstable at the limit." Training at 80–90% adherence consistently for years likely outperforms training at 100% for blocks followed by crashes.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie's first encounter with high job-stress derailing training (2016–17 coaching experience): previously assumed training dose was the main lever; this case showed that work stress at an 8–10/10 level neutralized all training adaptations completely. Since then: stress management is treated as co-equal with training prescription.
- The hosts have moved away from prescribing intervals at a specific kilojoule endpoint (early fatigue-resistance approach from mid-2010s), in favor of structuring hard efforts later in interval sessions or on subsequent days.

## Episode: 2110075104-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-57-our-coaches-answer-your-training-questions.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2110075104
**Word count:** 14,844
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
A second multi-coach roundtable answers listener questions on mid-season breaks, managing burnout/disappointment, U23 overtraining, intensity vs. volume balance, managing high work stress, pre/post-workout habits, athlete–coach communication, and what cyclists over-emphasize. The throughline is that consistency, honest athlete–coach communication, and realistic expectations matter far more than optimizing any single training variable. Coaches repeatedly return to the idea that process goals buffer against the inevitable disappointments of outcome-based race results.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Mid-season breaks:** Take at minimum 1 full week off mid-season; within 2–3 weeks of return most athletes are back to pre-break levels or higher. Use a break as an "insurance policy" — don't wait until performance visibly drops; schedule it proactively. An athlete who peaked at Hilo (late April), took a full week off, and did 2 easy weeks returned "absolutely flying."
- **Managing burnout/disappointment:** Enter the season with pre-set process goals. If you can evaluate "how did I execute my process goals?" you have a meaningful outcome measure regardless of race placement.
- **Work stress management:** During high work-stress periods, decide between improving or maintaining fitness — not both. The minimal maintenance dose is much lower than most people assume. Drop FTP targets by 10–20 W during high-stress periods to avoid training at an overestimated threshold. During work travel, prioritize not getting sick; do easy riding for fun on short trips; add protein to diet; stay hydrated.
- **Intensity vs. volume:** Volume should dominate year-round with intensity added strategically — but never reduce volume so far that interval quality collapses. Adding more volume requires: (1) pacing endurance rides easy enough, (2) adequate fueling during and after rides. The ceiling where volume is no longer the main limiting factor for FTP improvements is roughly 18–20 hr/week.
- **U23/junior overtraining:** Young athletes are as susceptible to overtraining as adults, and often more so because of school stress, poor nutrition access, social pressure, and the belief that they can "outwork" their way through problems. Treat a 16–21 year-old as if they have a 50–60 hr/week high-stress job; adjust volume and intensity accordingly.
- **Pre-workout habits:** Know your carb intake plan per hour before starting; have snacks or bottle strategy established; check weather and kit; plan route so interval sections are not interrupted by traffic lights or descents; read the workout carefully before beginning.
- **Post-workout habits:** Log a workout comment before the details fade (even if emotions need to settle for tone); consume recovery nutrition promptly; use post-ride comments to track patterns of fatigue, stress, and diet.
- **Auto-regulation under stress:** On high-stress days, approach training with explicit permission to skip intervals, move them, or ride easy. If legs feel bad but you want to move them, go; if you hit good legs, consider advancing the next day's workout. Stress manifestation in performance is highly unpredictable day-to-day during stressful periods — accept that variability rather than fighting it.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Volume hours by racing category (from a USA Cycling survey, n=543) show rough trends: Pro men ~17.5 hr/wk, Cat 1 men ~11.5 hr/wk, Cat 2 ~9 hr/wk, Cat 3 ~7.5 hr/wk, Cat 4/5 ~5.5 hr/wk; women follow similar pattern at lower hours. These are **observational correlations, not prescriptions** — higher-category riders ride more because they enjoy it more, not necessarily because the hours caused the category.
- There are only 15 Cat 1 men and 29 pro men in the study, so data is noisy at the top end.
- Less than 100 of the 543 survey respondents had power meter data; only 25 women had power data. Treat power comparisons from this study with caution.
- One-third of training days will be bad (rule-of-thirds, from running literature): normalizing this prevents athletes from catastrophizing poor sessions.
- Improvements after 16 weeks of solid training are still "very small incremental steps" — managing this expectation prevents disappointment from being mistaken for evidence the plan isn't working.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Cyclists over-emphasize power output and should focus more on speed and race outcomes — aerodynamics, bike handling, and descending can matter as much as watts.
- Focusing too much on genetics ("I don't have the talent") is counterproductive. The person with the best cycling genetics alive today is probably "on a couch eating chips" — most people have never discovered their full potential.
- "You don't lose, you learn" — experience in racing at current fitness level accumulates even in losing seasons and creates the race wisdom that eventually produces wins.
- Cyclists tend to over-obsess about diet/weight (possibly orthorexia): for most amateur athletes, just fueling workouts adequately and meeting caloric needs is sufficient. Constant concern about "eating too much" is more harmful than occasional excess.
- Over many years, even if your FTP doesn't improve, **bad days become less frequent and less severe** — this is a real performance improvement that doesn't show up in peak power numbers.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie: moved away from formal testing. Testing is now rare; training file trends and race performance are used instead. The "noob gains" phase (two phases: first gains from any training, second gains from smart training) eventually ends and monthly tests stop improving consistently.
- Multiple coaches independently report that asking athletes to ride easier is harder (requires more trust) than asking them to go hard — and that easy riding is often where athletes are most resistant to coaching guidance.

## Episode: 2196447155-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-65-why-training-or-racing-experience-shouldnt-determine-training-volume.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2196447155
**Word count:** 15,327
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
This roundtable argues that racing category and training history should not dictate how much volume an athlete does — because volume-to-performance relationships are log-linear (diminishing returns), and survivorship bias means the athletes you compare yourself to represent only those who stuck with the sport. The episode uses a USA Cycling observational survey to examine hours-per-week by category, and concludes these are correlations not prescriptions. The core message: compare your progress only to your own history, not to others; year-over-year consistency compounds more than any single "correct" training recipe.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Volume and FTP:** Doubling volume from 10 hr/wk to 15 hr/wk is "almost guaranteed" to improve training outcomes, but not proportionally — going from 10 to 15 hr/wk will not yield 50% more FTP. One coach went from 10 hr/wk to 20 hr/wk in Spain for 3 months, expected 30–40 W FTP gain, and got ~20 W instead.
- **James's data point:** Mid-300s FTP at moderate volume; gets to ~400 W only with ~1200–1300 hr/year plus stacked intervals. Key difference was not FTP but **durability** — LT1 improved substantially, allowing cruising at higher power with lower physiological cost, and better legs deep into races (others slow down more).
- **Rory's data point:** Went from 200 hr/year to 600+ hr/year over ~5 years (early 30s). FTP and power numbers improved via consistent stacked volume; the main subjective change was endurance — 12 hr/wk eventually feeling "easy."
- **Megan's data point:** Prior to switching to longer efforts and more volume, she was dropped at pro-level races (Joe Martin 2022) with good short-power numbers. Volume + longer threshold/sweet spot work transformed her race endurance and repeat-effort capacity. 6-hr rides made 3×30 min sweet spot feel manageable; pre-base anaerobic efforts would leave her "absolutely floored" where now she can do many more of them.
- **Time-in-zone progression:** Add 5 minutes per session (roughly), but auto-regulate — reach the sensation of "wheels coming off" at the end of a time-to-exhaustion effort and extend by 5–10 min beyond last session. Do not rigidly prescribe; feel-based progression is better than rote addition.
- **At what point does volume stop being the main factor?** Approximately 18–20 hr/week of consistent training is where volume stops being the primary limiter of aerobic capacity improvements. Below that, more volume almost always helps. Above it, intensity quality and recovery become the primary variables.
- **Multi-year improvement:** Consistency year-over-year compounds even with modest per-year gains. A client (Cole Tambury) was Cat 3 when coaching started; after years of consistent improvement, is now making lead groups at national-level races like Gila. Year-by-year gains of ~10 W FTP × 5 years = +50 W, which is significant.
- **For untrained/low-volume athletes:** Any intensity level will produce improvements; the higher the intensity, the faster the initial gains — but too much intensity too early produces accumulated fatigue that blocks the harder work needed later in the season.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Survey (Journal of Science and Cycling, n=543 USA Cycling athletes) caveats: selection/survivorship bias; only 25 women had power data; only 15 Cat 1 men in sample. Treat as exploratory, not prescriptive.
- Cat 5–Pro power-to-weight by category (approximate, from survey): Men Cat 5 ~3.8–4.0 W/kg, Cat 4 ~4.2 W/kg, Cat 3 ~4.4 W/kg, Cat 1/2 ~5 W/kg; Women Cat 5–3 ~3.3–3.5 W/kg, Cat 1/2 ~4.4 W/kg. These are **averages with wide variance**, not requirements.
- "Everyone can get to 5 W/kg if they just try hard enough" — dismissed as survivorship bias. The listener asking is self-selecting into a sample of people who stuck with cycling.
- Baseline VO2max and training response rate are genetically distinct traits; where you start does not reliably predict where you end up. Coaches have seen some athletes add >100 W FTP during coaching.
- Off-season rest matters as much as training: a client who had never taken a true off-season break (no full off weeks in years) was advised that without periodic full rest, adaptation accumulation stalls.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Your racing category does not determine your training volume needs — nor does doing more volume guarantee moving up a category. Tadej Pogacar at 3 hr/wk would "probably still be in the pro category."
- Reverse-engineering a pro's Strava to build your training plan is "one of the worst ways" to train. There is "very little correlation between the quality of training data and absolute performance" — coaches see 3.2 W/kg athletes with exemplary training data alongside 5.3 W/kg athletes doing objectively questionable things in their first year.
- Social media posts about training are a biased sample: people only post their good workouts and hide bad ones. Megan confirmed she hid files when she was struggling at 200 W in bad patches.
- People with one year of experience "repeated 10 times" (i.e., doing the same thing without learning) make no progress — honest assessment of training quality (not just hours) is required for multi-year improvement.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie: previously believed natural talent alone determined outcomes ("if someone was going to be good, they'd get good no matter what training they did"). Now believes training quality can meaningfully cap or enable potential — you can screw up training enough to prevent reaching your genetic ceiling.
- Multiple coaches: initially skeptical of a "truly off-season break" concept for well-trained athletes; now routinely prescribe mandatory off-season breaks after seeing repeated evidence that athletes without rest stop adapting.

## Episode: 2201678679-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-66-the-best-and-worst-training-habits.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2201678679
**Word count:** 13,507
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
Coaches Kolie, Gediminas, and Erica present their lists of best and worst training habits in a structured format, oriented toward early fall habit-building for the following season. The best habits cluster around: establishing routines that conserve mental energy, managing expectations to avoid over-optimism or over-pessimism, focusing intentionally during workouts, planning nutrition and logistics in advance, trusting coaches while asking honest questions, and reading internal fatigue signals. The worst habits mirror these: inconsistency, expecting breakthrough gains from small changes, testing too frequently, under-fueling, and ignoring Whisper-transcript-level health metrics like sleep routine and screen time.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Routine/consistency (Erica):** Establish a fixed training time to remove decision fatigue. Removing barriers to entry (kit laid out, nutrition prepped, bike ready) dramatically improves adherence. The habit of always riding the same time of day requires far less motivation than spontaneous scheduling.
- **Routine/consistency (Gediminas):** Always having simple carb sources available (buy sugar at any grocery store) means you're never stopped from riding by lack of specific nutrition products.
- **Don't over-optimize consistency:** Too much consistency is also bad — clients who never miss a ride but also never recover properly (e.g., pushing recovery rides to 65% FTP instead of 50%) plateau. Rest days and easy days must genuinely be easy.
- **Focus before and during workouts (Erica):** Before a VO2 session, visualize the road segments, expected sensations, and equipment choices. During intervals, clear your head of distractions; put the phone down. The goal is intentional presence, not total relaxation.
- **Pre-race routine (Gediminas):** Having a repeatable pre-race ritual — what you eat, when you warm up, what you do at the venue — reduces anxiety because it converts the controllable parts of race day into automatic habits.
- **Visualization (Gediminas):** Visualize likely race scenarios, including things that can go wrong (mechanicals, surges, adverse weather), so those events don't trigger panic when they happen. Also includes having realistic pacing targets anchored to actual prior performance data, not aspirational FTP estimates.
- **Long-term focus (Kolie):** Avoid short-term testing addiction. Don't expect every ride to yield better numbers. Testing infrequently (only when there's strong reason to believe something has improved) prevents test anxiety and premature conclusions. Coaches look at trends in training files, not individual test snapshots.
- **Nutrition habits (Gediminas):** Don't guess your on-bike carb intake and "hope it works on race day." Test fueling in training, under conditions similar to race day (temperature, duration, effort). 90 or 120 g/hr is only as good as your gut testing has validated it to be. Separately: track daily nutrition for 1–2 weeks with a barcode-scanning app to get a realistic baseline.
- **Trust the coach (Gediminas/Erica):** Tell your coach how you actually feel, not what you think they want to hear. Coaches are not impressed by perfect workout execution — they are impressed by honest communication. For the first year with a coach, default to executing the plan; then ask questions and customize.
- **Auto-regulation / reading internal cues (Kolie):** Develop the ability to distinguish good-leg days (push harder) from bad-leg days (ride easy or skip intervals). For athletes with chaotic schedules, a good approach is: identify the best day each week for a hard session, and be flexible about which day that lands on. "Play whack-a-mole with your legs."
- **Sleep routine (Erica/Kolie):** Consistent sleep/wake time matters potentially more than sleep quality alone (circadian rhythm benefits from regularity). Limit screen exposure and stimulating content before bed; lower bedroom temperature to facilitate sleep onset; avoid caffeine after noon.
- **Health consciousness — do some, don't overdo (Kolie):** Bone density supplementation via running, hiking, or weighted exercise is good; but weighted bras, excessive gym frequency, and orthorexic eating patterns are counterproductive. Post-ride nutrition must include adequate carbs — all-vegetable recovery meals are too high in fiber and too low in available carbohydrates to support recovery.
- **Keep fun in training (Kolie):** Training plans are "written in sand" — they can and should be modified. Group rides, adventure rides, stupid-fun rides are part of the optimal long-term training system because they drive motivation and adherence.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The "rule of thirds" (from running literature, shared by coach Katie): roughly 1/3 of training days will feel good, 1/3 okay, 1/3 bad. Accepting this prevents catastrophizing. An unusually high frequency of bad days is a signal worth investigating, not a baseline.
- FTP gains are logarithmic: fast initial gains slow to a handful of watts per year for most trained athletes. Peak best-ever FTP from a good block should not become the default expectation — it was a peak, not a floor.
- A previously coached person performing consistently at their current level may see their worst days become less frequent even without absolute power improvements — this is real progress that doesn't show in peak numbers.
- Testing too early (after insufficient rest) or too often is a common error that increases test anxiety and provides misleading data. Coaches rarely formally test now; training file trends are more informative.
- "Two phases of noob gains" (Gediminas): first gains happen regardless of training quality; second wave happens once training becomes systematic. After both phases, the compulsion to test every month should subside.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Riding easier is harder (in a psychological sense) than riding harder. Going hard daily feels productive; going easy requires trusting the process and often generates more coaching pushback than any other recommendation.
- Some athletes feel best with chronic fatigue in their legs (accumulated fatigue "feeling of training") and perform objectively poorly when well-rested. The data (power meter) says they're better rested; their subjective experience says otherwise. The data is right.
- Auto-regulation separates good training from just okay training more than any other single factor: "What separates great from just okay training is the ability to adjust based on how you're feeling."

### Evolving Views
- Kolie: Started out thinking formal testing was essential; now rarely tests athletes formally. "I let the training be my guide." This is a significant shift from early-career coaching practice.
- Gediminas: Previously loved testing (when still in "noob gains" phase and seeing monthly improvements); stopped once the second wave of gains leveled off, and now only tests when there is strong reason to expect an improvement.

## Episode: 2215377053-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-67-making-the-most-of-winter-training.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2215377053
**Word count:** 14,012
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
Kolie and coach James McKay discuss how to individualize winter/off-season training planning. The central argument is that "what should my winter look like" has no universal answer — it depends on race calendar start date, how much fatigue you've accumulated, how quickly you lose and regain fitness, what you're motivated by, and life constraints. They walk through common winter questions (intervals or not, gym work, body composition, periodization style) and consistently land on: use the minimum effective training stimulus, monitor whether FTP is actually moving, and don't let the optimization instinct override fun and long-term sustainability.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **When to start intervals in winter:** Determined by when you need to be in shape, working backward. Example: for track Nationals in July, intervals were delayed until January–February; for spring road racing (Feb–April), intervals were started in November with sweet spot and over-unders. There is no universal date.
- **When NOT to do intervals immediately post-season:** If coming off heavy racing with a lot of intensity (especially if also managing weight), prioritize shedding cortisol, allowing fat mass to recover, and reducing chronic stress before adding structured intensity. Listen to motivation levels.
- **Gym work in off-season:** Reasonable gym load (2× per week) should not count as "hard training." Energy cost is low; the stimulus is different. Starting with 2–4 sets per muscle group is sufficient; 10–15 total weekly sets for the lower body is reasonable for most cyclists. James PR'd his sprint power with only 4–6 working sets per session twice per week. Key mistake coaches see: too many exercises per session (e.g., squat + deadlift + single-leg press + lunge, 4 sets each = 32 sets/week, which causes persistent soreness and compromises bike training).
- **Minimum effective dose for strength (neurological gains):** As few as 1–3 sets at 7–9 RPE per exercise, done once per week. Strength gains (neural benefits for peak power) can be achieved with very low dose; hypertrophy requires more sets but is less critical for cyclists. Strength training is compatible with in-season training at low doses year-round.
- **Interval type in early winter:** Tempo, sweet spot, and over-unders are the appropriate starting point for most athletes. Some athletes (like James) need VO2max work earlier in winter because tempo/sweet spot doesn't recapture lost VO2max fitness as effectively for them — this is individual pattern recognition from their training history.
- **Minimum training principle:** Use the least intense training stimulus necessary and monitor whether FTP is moving. "Feel" your FTP by riding close to threshold frequently (2–3 times/week) and detect whether you're progressing. If 2–3×20 sweet spot is not moving the needle, consider adding VO2 work — but you don't need a full VO2 block.
- **Volume indoors:** When limited to trainer riding, reduce volume and increase intensity density. Doing shorter, focused interval sessions is a reasonable trade for outdoor long rides. Reverse periodization (harder in winter, more volume in summer when outdoors is feasible) is valid — it emerges naturally from these constraints.
- **Zwift racing:** Acceptable as once-per-week hard ride and checks the fun box; every 2–3 weeks is probably maximum sustainable dose without compromising other training. One Zwift race ≈ one hard interval session in terms of training stress.
- **Body composition in winter:** Weight loss should be planned, communicated with a coach, and paired with reduced interval intensity. During a caloric deficit: muscle glycogen stores are lower, recovery is slower, and energy availability constrains training output. Drop from 2 interval sessions/week to 1 if dieting, or reduce duration/intensity of each session. An athlete secretly dieting while having their training ratcheted up will "be against the ropes" — the coach will eventually detect it from failed sessions and depleted feedback.
- **Using enforced breaks strategically:** If you have a planned Christmas break or vacation, use the training weeks leading into it to experiment with something new (e.g., harder block periodization, new interval format), then use the break as the recovery runway. Alternatively, use the break for fun/social riding and start fresh afterward.
- **Metrics for when to start training:** (1) The "stairs test" — when you stop dreading stairs and start running up them, you're ready. (2) Body weight returning to normal after ultra/stage races. (3) Training history: athletes who lose a lot of fitness detraining may want to maintain 5 hr/week easy riding through the off-season rather than full rest. (4) Age: for 50+ athletes, longer off-seasons can make regaining fitness harder; consider shorter breaks with light maintenance riding.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Even after a few weeks of detraining, some athletes lose 40 W FTP (James: mid-300s → ~400 W in peak condition; loses the high end quickly when off the bike). This can be distressing but is normal and recoup-able.
- "Zone 2 base season" percentage splits are not meaningful as a pre-planned target. Volume is more important than zone precision; riding at 40% FTP still contributes to endurance adaptation. Don't compulsively pedal to stay "in zone 2" at the expense of time or enjoyment.
- Pros who appear to do low-volume, light training are often near their genetic limit: less training is needed because any stimulus gets them to their ceiling. Amateurs are nowhere near their ceiling and cannot use pro off-season training as a template.
- Australians/Kiwis chasing both summer seasons (Aus + Euro) have essentially no viable off-season window and must make compromises. Be realistic and less ambitious with training during the "winter" window in such cases.
- Ultra event recovery takes longer than expected; literature shows fatigue markers still present 2+ weeks after ultra completion.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Periodization is not about following a canonical sequence (zone 2 → tempo → threshold → VO2 → specificity). It is just "the best training for the constraints of your life at this time." Some athletes properly train harder in fall/winter than in summer because that's when their life allows it.
- The "do only zone 2 in base" narrative is overrated: if you're relatively detrained, **any** intensity level improves your aerobic fitness. Applying the minimum effective dose principle, you can determine individually which stimulus is actually necessary.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie and James: acknowledged that previous coaching attempts to use tempo/sweet spot to bring James back from detraining were ineffective — discovering that James specifically needs early VO2max work to recapture lost power is a classic example of individual pattern recognition overriding general prescriptions.
- General: the podcast notes that winter training recommendations have shifted away from rigid periodization templates toward individualized "what does your life allow, and what's your race calendar?" planning.

## Episode: 2252506076-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-71-avoiding-panic-training-starts-now.txt
**Numeric ID:** 2252506076
**Word count:** 12,683
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips

### Core Message
Recorded in late January, this episode (Kolie, Megan, Gediminas) defines panic training, catalogs its causes, and provides concrete strategies for avoidance and pivoting. Panic training is defined as cramming training in response to feeling underprepared for an upcoming event — typically producing more harm than benefit. The episode argues that setting intermediate process milestones throughout a season, maintaining realistic expectations, planning for disruption (illness, life stress), and pivoting to achievable goals when things go sideways are the real solutions. Panic training is diagnosed as a mismatch between expectations and reality — and adjusting expectations is almost always the better lever than adjusting training volume.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Avoiding panic training (intermediate milestones):** For Unbound 200, set a process goal: complete at least one 6+ hr ride per month from March through June. Failing to track and hit these milestones leads to a two-week-out realization that your longest ride is 3 hours. Milestones can also be: number of group rides done, nutrition protocol tested in training, specific equipment validated.
- **The no-makeup-training rule:** If a session is missed, do not stack it onto the following week. Either push it to next week's equivalent slot or drop it. Adding a fourth interval session to a week that already has three is likely to push performance backward, not forward.
- **When panic training is almost acceptable (~6+ weeks out):** If the reason for the schedule crunch has resolved (illness cleared, life stress reduced), doing a single long ride 6 weeks out is probably net positive even if it doesn't fully close the gap. Below 2 weeks out: no panic training at all.
- **How to pivot when panic training temptation hits:** (1) Adjust expectations for the event (finish > podium); (2) change the event type (pivot from Unbound 200 to shorter crits if endurance prep is behind); (3) change training focus to fast-ROI modalities (see below).
- **Fast-ROI training when compressed for time:**
  - Endurance/TTE training (extending sweet spot and over-under intervals): improves relatively quickly.
  - Anaerobic capacity / repeat sprint work (30-30s, 30–60 sec max efforts): "easy come, easy go" — you can reach crit shape in 2–4 weeks of this work even from low training base, as long as recovery is adequate. Kolie did this in one year of family stress and reached great crit form in 3 weeks of nothing but short, hard, repeated efforts.
  - **What doesn't come back quickly:** FTP / threshold fitness — do not expect FTP gains in a compressed 2–4 week block; accept 80–90% of previous race fitness as the realistic ceiling.
- **Best practices if you decide to panic train:** Focus only on the highest-ROI modalities for your specific event type; do not try to do everything. Match the training type to what's most fun/motivating (motivation drives execution quality).
- **Improving FTP, VO2max, FRC, and sprint simultaneously:** Do nothing for 3–4 months, then start any high-intensity protocol for 4–8 weeks. All four will improve simultaneously until adaptation plateaus. Then pivot to a different protocol.
- **Minimum effective dose (how to find yours):** Reduce training load until you stop improving — that's the floor. Alternatively, look back in your history for periods of lower training (finals stress, vacation) where you maintained fitness and returned to intervals successfully: that regime was at or near your minimum effective dose.
- **Planning for disruption:** Build the assumption of 2–3 weeks of lost training into any season plan. Nearly every athlete gets sick at least once in winter. Assess past seasons: "How much did I typically lose?" and design the schedule assuming a similar hit this year. Monthly calendar notes (e.g., "March: threshold focus; May: VO2max block") are a low-overhead way to stay on track.
- **Confidence building rides:** Near race day, a single long ride that you were anxious about completing can boost confidence even if physiologically minimal. Other examples: riding in the dark to get comfortable for overnight ultra sections; doing a ride the evening before and then heading out again the next morning to simulate multi-day event transitions.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Social media-driven panic ("everyone is in Mallorca training 20 hours"): what you see is a biased sample. That person doing a 20 hr week in Mallorca may be overdoing it and will pay in March–April.
- Program hopping as a panic response (switching from slow-build to "miracle intervals" when behind): almost never works because the same life stressor that prevented training is also impairing recovery for the new program.
- Under-fueling and panic training are often co-morbid. Panic about watts/kg leads to simultaneous caloric restriction, which worsens recovery and makes training quality even worse. The combination is "the last thing you absolutely need to do in the last 1–3 months before your goal race."
- Panic training in one out of ten cases will actually work (the disruption was mild enough and the athlete recovers well enough). In nine of ten, it either maintains or degrades fitness vs. a conservative approach.
- Training is an inverted-U function: more training helps up to a point, then performance starts degrading. Panic training usually means starting on the downslope of that U.
- For athletes with persistent energy deficits from under-fueling: lying around like a slug after rides, difficulty doing basic tasks post-ride, and chronic low mood are acute signs. These can drift for months before becoming obvious — "diagnosis by treatment" (eat more, see if you feel better) is often faster and cheaper than lab testing.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Being "undercooked and motivated" at the start line is often better than being "well-trained and exhausted." Mental freshness and excitement for the event matter more than the last 2–3 weeks of training volume.
- Panic training is not really a training problem — it is a **goal and expectation management problem.** "Panic training is a result of refusing to adjust your expectations or goals."
- Treating a race you're undertrained for as "a fun vacation" rather than a competitive effort is not admitting defeat — it is the strategic choice that best preserves long-term motivation and enjoyment of the sport.
- Everyone's athletes get sick at least once in winter. Normalizing illness-related disruption prevents the "I'm so far behind everyone else" panic spiral.
