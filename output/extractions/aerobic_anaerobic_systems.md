### Core Message
This 100K listens AMA covers a wide range of training questions submitted via Reddit, centering on the relationship between aerobic and anaerobic systems. The core argument is that most cyclists systematically underestimate how aerobic their performance requirements are — even criterium racing, short climbs, and repeated sprint work primarily come down to aerobic capacity. Kolie argues that anaerobic capacity is best thought of as "polish" that comes quickly and goes quickly, while aerobic development takes years and drives long-term performance gains. Several Q&A exchanges develop the idea that TTE extension at FTP is driven by improved fat oxidation, not just glycogen storage, and that VO2 max work (not FTP work) is often the key lever for well-trained athletes.

### Key Training Recommendations
- For athletes who get dropped in criteriums or on punchy climbs: the limiter is usually aerobic power, not anaerobic capacity. The fix is raising FTP (aerobic base), not adding more sprint or anaerobic interval sessions. A 400W FTP rider would not have been taxed anaerobically in the same situation.
- Stochastic "breakaway-style" tempo rides — varying effort naturally with terrain rather than steady Zone 3 — are preferred over prescribed steady-state Zone 3. These serve as good aerobic workouts (60–90 min) while training repeated accelerations. Kolie attributes substantial cross fitness gains over "2–3 weeks" from this approach.
- Steady-state Zone 3 is rarely prescribed as focused training; it is assigned only in rest weeks for ~1 hour or less. A pro doing 6 × 90-minute Zone 3 blocks was identified as training sub-optimally.
- FTP maintenance in non-cycling off-season: do as much riding as possible without compromising the priority sport. Without specific FTP training but with activity, FTP can likely be maintained within 5–10W for roughly 1–2 months before significant decline.
- For athletes with a high proportion of Type II muscle fibers: watch fatigue more carefully from sprint efforts — they can dig a much deeper hole than the power output suggests. Also, carbohydrate burn from hard efforts is not 1:1 with kilojoules on the power meter; actual carbohydrate cost is higher for high-intensity work.
- Anaerobic work capacity (AWC): treat it like polish — focus on it only for a few weeks before racing. Doing repeated anaerobic intervals all year without a long aerobic base does not build AWC in the long term; muscle mass and peak power are the real long-term limiters of AWC.
- TTE extension at FTP: driven in large part by improved fat oxidation at FTP (not purely glycogen storage). Athletes after a good winter base can achieve TTEs of 65–85 minutes. When TTE reaches ~60+ minutes, that is a good time to begin VO2 max work, which will then raise FTP and shorten TTE, beginning the cycle again.
- FTP training does not increase FTP in well-trained athletes; VO2 max work does. This was offered as the "most controversial training opinion."
- VO2 max block recovery: expect improvements to appear 40% of the time immediately, 25% of the time within 2–3 weeks of finishing the block; for most athletes full benefits take 1–2 months to materialize. Do not begin a second VO2 max block before full recovery from the first.
- For combined cycling + marathon training: maintain as much cycling volume as possible without compromising run training; it will not fully preserve FTP but is better than nothing.
- FTP interval progression: track TTE separately from FTP; progress by adding interval time (e.g., 4×10 → 4×12 → 3×15). Also track sweet spot TTE and over-under progressions as separate parallel tracking variables.
- For Type II fiber-dominant athletes seeking to maintain endurance during weight-training off-season: ride 6 hours/day on weekends if possible alongside twice-weekly lifting. Separate riding from lifting by time (avoid cycling immediately after lifting to protect hypertrophy via mTOR interference).
- Sprint development (snap): 10–15 second standing sprints from ~39×16–17, starting at 60–70 rpm, targeting a cadence range of 70–130+ rpm. This trains neuromuscular coordination and can produce a small (~0.1–0.2 m/s) bump in Msmax within 1–2 weeks for untrained sprinters.
- Pedaling time, not power, is the key stimulus for Zone 2 adaptation. Indoor vs outdoor equivalency is approximately 1:1 when pedaling time is matched. Athletes should minimize coasting on outdoor endurance rides.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- FTP maintenance during time off depends heavily on the individual and age; young athletes sometimes return from 2-week breaks and lose essentially nothing.
- Zone 3 training may not be inefficient for highly trained athletes whose LT1 is naturally at 80–85% FTP; the zone description becomes inapplicable to them.
- The "50% more time on the trainer" rule is dismissed as unfounded; the real comparison factor is pedaling time.
- Coggin's training zones were designed to be descriptive (after the fact) rather than prescriptive (during training).
- Blood lactate at MLSS (OBLA standard ~4.0 mmol/L) is a population average; individual ranges are roughly 2–8+ mmol/L. Prescribing training from a population-average lactate number is unreliable.
- Machine learning / auto-coach systems are limited by inability to account for non-training stressors, individual variation in biomarkers, and the fundamental problem that even exhaustive power datasets converge toward Gaussian distributions that miss outliers.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Doing anaerobic intervals all year without aerobic base will not improve you at anaerobic events; "if you do aerobic work really well for 10 years, you'll be a really well-trained endurance athlete. If you do anaerobic work for 10 years without anything else, you're not going to be that good at anything."
- FTP training alone, for well-trained athletes, does not increase FTP; VO2 max work is the lever.
- A good winter period of low-intensity endurance training can produce TTEs of 65–85 minutes in the first spring FTP test, which then allows a VO2 max block to push FTP to a new ceiling.
- Sprint (anaerobic) efforts are not purely anaerobic: a 1300W sprinter still needs to sit down and recover aerobically after 15-second efforts; a 900W "climber" type doing the same sprint workout shows far less aerobic distress, demonstrating that the hole dug is less about fiber type and more about the absolute cellular disturbance from maximum motor unit recruitment.
- Burning fat at FTP is more common than often assumed; data from ketogenic athletes shows 50–67% of energy at FTP from fat oxidation. The mechanism of FTP does not preclude fat burning.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie evolved away from prescribing steady Zone 3 rides in favor of stochastic tempo and cleaner aerobic/hard efforts.
- Previous approach emphasized posterior chain strength for cyclists; he now considers this largely bro-science given the limited role of hamstrings in knee-flexed cycling mechanics.

### Core Message
This "Best Of 2020 Instagram AMA" episode covers a wide range of metabolic and systems-level questions, with heavy emphasis on fat and carbohydrate metabolism, the aerobic–anaerobic continuum, and what "fat adaptation" actually means physiologically. A through-line is that many popular distinctions (aerobic vs. anaerobic, Zone 2 bottom vs. top, fat-adapted vs. carb-dependent) are either misunderstood or less actionable than commonly believed. Several questions directly address the continuum of glycolysis, lactate production, and fat oxidation at and around threshold.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Fat adaptation via training (not diet) does not impair carbohydrate utilization for high-power efforts. Normal endurance + sprint training keeps metabolic flexibility intact. Dietary fat adaptation (keto/LCHF) suppresses carbohydrate use, and this is detrimental for sprint and high-intensity performance.
- Zone 2 should be paced by RPE, not by wattage or a strict percentage of FTP. Going to the top of Zone 2 (e.g., 1W below Zone 3) is counterproductive — accumulates disproportionate fatigue. For well-trained athletes, "Zone 2" RPE can correspond to as high as 80–82% FTP.
- Zone 2 minimum effective dose: "more than you're currently doing" — progressive overload logic applies. A 6-hour ride is better than a 4-hour ride if both are at appropriate RPE. If you can take vacation weeks and ride 25 hours, do it; people still see aerobic benefits from large but inconsistent volume spikes.
- The efficiency factor (normalized power ÷ heart rate) is the best single metric for tracking short-term cardiac stroke volume progression on endurance rides (apples-to-apples comparison between similar rides over weeks/months).
- For sweet spot intervals: the total interval time matters more than continuity. Effective dose should approach what you can hold continuously at that intensity — if sweet spot TTE is 90 minutes, targeting ~80–90 minutes of total interval time at sweet spot is appropriate. Effort should feel hard in the last 10–15 minutes but not impossible.
- To maintain strength gains during race season: maintain ~1.5 g protein/kg body weight/day; do a couple of sub-maximal sets of squats or deadlifts (3–5 reps at ~70–80% effort) once per week; include some sprint work once a week. No need to reach absolute maximum loads to maintain already-acquired muscle mass.
- Recovery weeks: ride the bike but cap intensity at ~6/10 RPE. The only time Kolie prescribes steady-state Zone 3 is during recovery weeks (30–90 minutes at a wide power range) to re-activate the hormonal working-mode before the next hard block. Include openers (short high-intensity efforts) in the days coming out of a rest week to avoid rest inertia.
- The biggest overlooked low-hanging fruit: sleep and rest. Recovery is "99% eating and sleeping." All recovery gadgets (ice baths, compression pants, cupping) are marginal; if they reduce stress or improve confidence, fine, but they are not the signal.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Increasing muscle mass without additional capillarization will slightly reduce FTP relative to VO2 max — but this effect is small unless endurance training is completely abandoned.
- Lactate production does not indicate anaerobic metabolism per se; almost all metabolic pathways are technically "without oxygen" at some step. Even at threshold, significant aerobic processes continue; lactate is also a fuel in the Krebs cycle. The term "anaerobic threshold" is historically misleading.
- At a true 1-minute all-out effort, approximately 50% of energy is anaerobic (first seconds entirely so, transitioning to mostly aerobic by minute 4–5+ of max efforts). The cardiovascular system — transport, not utilization — is the major VO2 max limiter.
- HRV as a daily readiness metric: not reliable for well-trained endurance athletes. In Kolie's athletes, HRV often improves during fatigue accumulation and tanks during rest weeks — opposite of the expected pattern. Off-bike stressors (alcohol, sleep quality, heat) affect HRV more than training load at that fitness level.
- VO2 max testing (lab or Garmin estimates): not necessary for most amateur athletes; power data alone is sufficient. Testing may be useful for elite athletes where fine-grained comparisons of FTP vs. VO2 max power matter.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- "Almost all forms of metabolism are actually anaerobic" — even the Krebs cycle contains no oxygen in its direct steps; O2 is only consumed in the electron transport chain. Lactate is not proof of anaerobic metabolism.
- Fat oxidation during cycling originates from whole-body adipose tissue mobilized systemically (not spot fat near active muscles) AND from intramuscular triglycerides (IMTGs); these are mobilized by the same signal. You cannot direct fat loss to a specific region.
- Endomorphs, mesomorphs, and ectomorphs are meaningless in predicting athletic ability or training response; body structure reveals nothing about heart size, fiber type, neural drive, or power generation.
- During a taper, slow-twitch fibers actually hypertrophy temporarily by filling with glycogen. In highly trained endurance athletes, slow-twitch fibers can be larger than fast-twitch fibers.
- Athletes who stop training after an injury/break and come back after a month nearly universally come back stronger, thanks to the rest itself. This held repeatedly in Kolie's athlete roster.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie evolved away from prescribing strict Zone 2 wattage windows toward RPE-based endurance pacing after seeing athletes accumulate too much fatigue at "the top of Zone 2."
- Lactate and VO2 max laboratory testing moved from "probably useful for most athletes" to "not important for most amateurs" as power-meter analysis proved sufficient in practice.

## Episode: 1087518463-empiricalcyclingpodcast-instagram-and-reddit-ama.txt

### Core Message
This AMA covers the mechanics of several training constructs — over-unders, fat metabolism at threshold, training older athletes, anaerobic repeatability, and intensity distribution — while returning repeatedly to the core theme that training distribution should emerge from physiological principles and individual need rather than from preset schemas like 80/20. A notable segment addresses whether and how over-unders work, with a practical prescription for low-cadence overs as a method for recruiting larger motor units. Kolie also provides a concise framework on why even short track events are primarily aerobic.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Over-unders structure: start with 30 seconds over FTP (20–30W above), then 4.5 minutes under (at tempo watts). Do not make the overs and unders symmetrical in duration. The "under" portion should allow genuine recovery; if you are "hanging on by the skin of your teeth" during the under, the prescription is too hard. Total time over threshold across a 1–2 hour race is typically no more than ~25 minutes; over-under prescriptions should respect this scale.
- Low-cadence over-unders: do the "over" portion at low cadence (obligate recruitment of larger motor units), limit overs to 10–60 seconds, then do unders at normal cadence for recovery. This is an advanced technique — useful primarily for athletes who specifically lack large motor unit oxidative capacity. Start with 2 sets, work up to 4.
- Anaerobic repeatability workout: 20–30 second max-effort seated efforts, rest 2–3 minutes, 4–6 repetitions. Start with 2 sets, build to 4. For longer anaerobic capacity work: 3-minute on / 3-minute off or 2-minute on / 4-minute off. Key indicator of need: poor repeatability of VO2 max and higher efforts despite high volume likely indicates insufficient work in the larger motor units.
- FTP work when TTE is short: if you specifically do not want to extend TTE (e.g., for 5–20 minute events), keep doing long rides for aerobic stimulus, prioritize VO2 max work, and do some FTP maintenance (e.g., 2×20) without continually extending time in zone.
- Once TTE at FTP reaches ~60 minutes or more, shift to VO2 max work — this is a reliable time to push FTP upward.
- Fasted training: do not combine fasted riding with FTP intervals. Fasted rides are a "strong spice" with some benefit for fat adaptation; limit to once or twice per week maximum, never for high-intensity sessions. When doing fasted rides, use tempo-type (not threshold) intensity. Do not go isocalorically deficient — maintain total caloric intake with fat and protein.
- For improving FTP when FTP ≈ 80–82% of VO2 max watts: do VO2 max intervals, not more FTP work.
- Training intensity distribution: do not target a preset distribution (e.g., 80/20, pyramidal). When training goes well, the distribution that emerges often looks pyramidal on inspection, but that was not the design target. Focus on physiological systems, not percentages.
- 5–10 minute track events are still highly aerobic. Even track endurance riders must develop strong aerobic bases. Anaerobic and sprint capacity matters too, but the aerobic system supplies the dominant portion of energy. Newer track categories run shorter races but as you advance, events lengthen dramatically (40km points races equivalent to a criterium in duration and demand).
- Older athletes (declining VO2 max): incorporate weight training for bone health and muscle retention, but don't assume switching to "anaerobic focus" will be more productive — individual potential varies.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- 80/20 training is a tool, not a law; Kolie does not calculate distributions — he responds to recovery rates and adaptation signals.
- Fasted rides are primarily appropriate for athletes who have already maximized training volume; for most athletes, just riding more is the more effective intervention.
- Eating more does NOT interrupt aerobic adaptation. Eating during FTP intervals enables longer intervals and more total stimulus, which produces better adaptation than fasted shorter intervals.
- Training repeatability metrics: compare the same interval set over time (not just races); variability index (normalized power ÷ average power) of >1.2 indicates stochastic race-like demands where repeatability matters.
- Whether sweet spot counts as a "high-intensity" workout depends on whether it is being used in a building context (yes, it counts) or as maintenance (no, it may not need to count against the weekly hard-workout budget).

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Eating food does not blunt aerobic adaptations — this directly contradicts a popular "train-low" hypothesis. Longer intervals with food produce more total adaptation than shorter fasted intervals.
- Fat burning high-fat diet claim rebutted: increased fat oxidation on a high-fat diet comes primarily from ingested dietary fat, not mobilized adipose stores. Actual endogenous fat mobilization remains approximately the same; caloric deficit (not dietary fat composition) drives adipose fat loss.
- For repeatability deficits: the cause is likely insufficient work in larger motor units (not lack of anaerobic capacity per se). The prescription is specific repeatability intervals, not more VO2 max volume.

### Evolving Views
- Low-cadence over-unders: Kolie used them heavily for several years with many athletes and has since moved away from them as a default — they work for some athletes in specific circumstances but are not universally warranted.

## Episode: 1211797429-empiricalcyclingpodcast-2021-best-of-instagram-ama-questions.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1211797429
**Word count:** 20,278
**Series:** Uncategorized (AMA / Q&A — Best Of)

### Core Message
This Best of 2021 Instagram AMA is one of the most physiologically detailed AMA episodes, covering RPE calibration, heart rate decoupling, FTP stability, periodization of VO2 max vs threshold blocks, anaerobic capacity training philosophy, VLAmax, protein requirements for aerobic adaptation, and long-term athlete development. A recurring theme is that aerobic development remains the primary lever for almost all cyclist archetypes, including those competing in short track events, criteriums, or hill climbs. The episode also develops a nuanced view of FTP behavior under fatigue (TTE contracts, not FTP itself, for most athletes) and contrasts the aerobic demands of crits vs. the common belief that they are primarily anaerobic.

### Key Training Recommendations
- RPE calibration for endurance rides: LT1 ("Zone 2") pace is the hardest pace at which you can ride without having to think about it — talk-test passing, mentally easy, fun. RPE ~4–5/10. FTP-level intensity is RPE ~7/10 — hard but not excruciating, requiring focus but not psyching-up; if you find yourself needing to mentally grind hard to maintain watts, you are over threshold.
- Over-unders prescription: begin conservatively — 30W over FTP for 30 seconds max / then down to tempo for 4–5 minutes. Do NOT use symmetrical over/under durations. Aim for ~20–30 minutes total time over threshold in the set. If the "under" period doesn't allow recovery, the prescription is wrong.
- FTP training structure: 2–3 high-intensity sessions per week is a reasonable starting point; sweet spot in a build context counts as one of those sessions. Effective sweet spot means progressive overload in time-in-zone (2×20 → 2×22.5 → 2×25 → 4×15, etc.).
- VO2 max block structure: 7–12 VO2 max workouts in 2–3 weeks constitutes a focused block. Allow up to 2 weeks of recovery afterward before benefits fully appear; do not stack another hard block before recovery is confirmed.
- Mixing VO2 max and threshold in the same block: acceptable when timeline is compromised, but makes it harder to isolate which variable is driving FTP gains. One practical sequencing: sprinkle VO2 max efforts into the final weeks of a threshold block as a bridge into a pure VO2 max block.
- Anaerobic capacity: if "historically poor" anaerobic capacity is a limiter for target races (punchy/hilly events), the long-term solution is off-bike strength training to build muscle, not year-round anaerobic intervals. Year-round anaerobic capacity work leads to burnout and does not produce long-term gains beyond what a 4–6 week pre-race block can provide. Strength training off-season, then transition to specific capacity work closer to racing season.
- For repeatability training: 10-second on / 50-second off, 3×10 minutes total (i.e., 10 repetitions per 10-minute set), then increase time-in-zone. Or: all-out repeated efforts to failure at supra-threshold with near-complete rest. Core rationale: "replenishment of cellular homeostasis is aerobic" — FTP is simply the highest power output that can be maintained almost purely aerobically, so you need aerobic base for repeatability, not just anaerobic capacity work.
- Low-carb endurance rides for TTE extension: a secondary tool (not the primary). Assign a low-intensity ride the day after a hard workout or race (low glycogen state naturally) and keep riding. This can produce a "huge TTE" effect. Hard to do correctly — maintaining isocaloric intake is the primary difficulty. Use under coaching guidance.
- Protein for aerobic adaptation: minimum ~1 g/kg/day; better target is ≥1.5 g/kg/day for aerobic endurance athletes doing high-intensity work. All aerobic adaptations (capillary growth, mitochondrial biogenesis, myosin heavy chain synthesis) require protein synthesis.
- FTP maintenance when returning from a break: do some maintenance of "middle intensity" (sweet spot, threshold) every 2 weeks or so — 2×20 sweet spot is sufficient. For VO2 max maintenance: general aerobic riding (even low-intensity endurance) maintains VO2 max — you do not need specific VO2 max workouts to avoid VO2 max deconditioning.
- For aerobic events on the track (5–20 min, elimination races, points races): do not neglect aerobic base. Even sub-3:30 women's individual pursuit requires huge VO2 max; Van Vluten won silver at track worlds by virtue of her aerobic engine, not anaerobic training. Filippo Ganna exemplifies both aerobic monster and anaerobic monster simultaneously.
- Aerobic training needed for all events longer than ~35 seconds: "unless you are doing pure track sprinting, aerobic is very good and likely will help all of your athletic endeavors."
- VLAmax and crits: VLAmax is reduced by aerobic training (more mitochondria = more lactate combustion). A declining VLAmax from aerobic training is not a problem for criterium performance — it means more aerobic power, and replenishment of phosphocreatine / cellular homeostasis is nearly entirely aerobic. A VLAmax of 0.31 is likely not the crit limiter; improving aerobic capacity will help more. If truly low on anaerobic side, short high-intensity work (30/30s, 15/15s for 3–6 minute sets) can raise VLAmax.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- FTP does not change day to day for most athletes under fatigue; TTE contracts instead (e.g., from 50 minutes to 30 minutes when fatigued). If a training plan forces workouts at a wattage that feels like a different person's FTP, the athlete needs rest, not a reduced FTP target.
- Heart rate decoupling is nearly universal and is not a signal to stop a ride, change training, or hold back. Multiple non-fitness causes (heat, dehydration, adrenaline, insufficient food) drive decoupling. RPE matching the intended endurance intensity matters more than HR decoupling percentage.
- FTP as % of VO2 max watts: conceptually valid but not highly actionable for day-to-day coaching; Garmin VO2 max estimates are unreliable; WKO5 does it better but still has significant error margins. Use trend data (year over year), not daily values.
- Elite riders do not all train polarized; at the team level, training distribution varies by event, phase, and individual. When inspecting well-run training programs retrospectively, they often look pyramidal, but this was not by design.
- Cadence and aerobic adaptation: higher cadence increases total pedal strokes per unit time but likely doesn't increase aerobic stimulus meaningfully — the shorter contraction duration offsets the higher frequency. Integrated time is approximately equivalent.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Anaerobic work does not "kill" mitochondria, but training that is too anaerobic-heavy (insufficient endurance volume) leads to low mitochondrial density. Lactate testing of athletes who train primarily at high intensity with little endurance work reveals elevated lactate levels at sub-threshold powers — they lack enough mitochondria to combust the lactate being produced.
- VLAmax decreasing with aerobic training is a feature, not a bug for criterium racers.
- The canonical view that "you should train at VO2 max intensity to raise VO2 max" is partially wrong for all training levels: general endurance riding maintains VO2 max, and specific VO2 max intervals are needed only to push above current capacity, not to avoid deconditioning.
- High VLAmax and high FTP are not mutually exclusive: Andre Greipel-type athletes (2000W sprint, 400W FTP) exist; the two systems are driven by partially independent mechanisms.
- Anaerobic capacity is best built through muscle mass gains in the gym, not through year-round interval work.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie now prefers a focused, isolated VO2 max block (rather than concurrent VO2 max + threshold) when the training calendar allows, though mixed blocks are common in practice.
- In 2020 (no racing year), observing athletes' unconstrained aerobic development solidified his view that long-term aerobic development is consistently under-prioritized when racing forces taper phases that interrupt aerobic momentum.

### Core Message
This Ten Minute Tips episode uses the "it always depends" running joke as the entry point for a genuine discussion of training individualization. Kolie and Kyle (a physicist) draw parallels between scientific null results and training experiments — both require iterative adjustment, and "it depends" is the mark of an expert recognizing the full dimensionality of a problem. The episode develops several case studies of where seemingly universal training rules ("ride more," "do VO2 max work") are indeed not universal, and identifies specific physiological dimensions that require individualization: tapering needs, training response rate, recovery rate, and the interaction of life stress with training load. The aerobic/anaerobic dimension surfaces in the context of VO2 max potential and whether a given athlete still has headroom to raise it.

### Key Training Recommendations
- VO2 max individualization: not every athlete needs to train VO2 max. If VO2 max is already very high (e.g., ~80–85), the marginal gain from additional VO2 max work may be low, and the "teeter-totter" effect (gains in one system trade off with the other) can appear for truly ceiling-limited athletes. For most amateurs, there is still headroom, so VO2 max work is worth trying.
- VO2 max block duration: eight weeks is not a universal block length. Check for signs of improvement 2–3 weeks in; if no hints of getting faster, the block may not be the right stimulus — consider adjusting or ending it earlier.
- "Ride more" is not always the right advice: if endurance rides are executed too hard (i.e., intensity during Zone 2 is too high), adding more of that riding makes things worse, not better. The qualifier is execution quality, not just volume.
- TTE extension with FTP work: building out TTE is a fairly universal principle applicable even to athletes with 7 W/kg FTP — the FTP number may not move further, but TTE can still be extended.
- Tapering is one of the most highly individual training variables. Some athletes need hard legs going into races (via preceding race/hard effort); others respond to classical rest tapers. Experiment early to identify the pattern.
- For athletes with alternating high/low training weeks (e.g., 10-hour weeks alternating with unconstrained weeks): use the high-volume weeks — the aerobic stimulus from those weeks is real and beneficial even if consistency is irregular. The underlying principle of "more aerobic stimulus = better long-term adaptation" applies; week-to-week variation is not catastrophic.
- Day-to-day individualization by coaches: check athlete data at least once daily; delete or modify training weeks when life stressors peak (stressful work, illness, moving, family demands). The mental and physical stress toll goes into the same pool.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Rules of thumb for training (polarized, sweet-spot-based, "always do long ride Sunday," etc.) are like Newton's law of gravity — a simplified model of a more complex underlying reality. They work for many situations but break down at the extremes.
- A standard VO2 max block (e.g., prescribed as 8 weeks) should not be followed blindly if no improvement is visible by weeks 2–3; the body of training work, not the precise weekly structure, is what drives adaptation.
- "Everyone should ride more" has a hidden qualifier: only if the additional riding is executed at the right intensity (genuine aerobic RPE, not excessive). Junk miles at marginally elevated intensity accumulate fatigue without providing proportionate adaptation.
- Individual recovery rate from blocks, races, and even single hard sessions varies enormously. Some athletes recover from a 12+ hour race in 4–5 days; others need 2 weeks. Do not assume standard recovery timelines.
- Whether a 280W/80kg athlete can reach 4 W/kg truly depends: body composition, height, training history, and unknown genetic ceiling all interact. Diet-body composition changes (if substantial) may provide more headroom than additional training stress.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- A high FTP (e.g., 400W) does not indicate zero remaining improvement potential in VO2 max, but the benefit of further VO2 max work may be demonstrable only through direct experimentation with the individual athlete.
- Fiber size is not fixed by genetics or by fast- vs. slow-twitch type: in highly trained endurance athletes, slow-twitch fibers can be larger than fast-twitch fibers. During a taper, slow-twitch fibers hypertrophy further as glycogen fills them.
- A training plan going "better than expected" does not necessarily mean you should push harder — the right response may instead be to maintain course and monitor for hidden fatigue accumulating beneath the positive surface.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie notes ongoing evolution in how strictly to follow preset periodization templates; increasing experience has led him away from multi-week advance programming toward short (3–4 week) windows with active daily adjustment.

### Core Message
This episode debunks several training myths: (1) that sprinters and non-sprinters need fundamentally different aerobic training, (2) that crit racers should focus primarily on anaerobic efforts, and (3) that you don't need to train FTP if you're not a time trialist. Kolie Moore (host) and Kyle Helson (co-host) argue that aerobic training signals are the same regardless of fiber type or sprint ability, and that the real differentiator for sprinters is managing recovery and fatigue rather than prescribing different aerobic workouts. They emphasize that race-specific training has its place but should not dominate a training plan, and that long-term development requires a full "rainbow" of workout types across the intensity spectrum.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Prioritize aerobic fitness development even if you are a natural sprinter or crit racer; being able to finish the race is the prerequisite for using your sprint.
- For natural sprinters, find the minimum dose of sprint-specific work (sprints, moto pacing, high-cadence work, explosive gym work) needed to feel good legs on race day, rather than maximizing sprint volume.
- Separate sprint training sessions from aerobic sessions rather than combining them (e.g., hard aerobic workout followed by sprints in the same session).
- If results plateau, try adding more rest and doing less volume before adding more work -- especially if you've been training hard for weeks or months.
- Use a "rainbow" of workout types (endurance, sweet spot, FTP, VO2max, sprints) rather than relying exclusively on any single method or purely race-specific training.
- Consider periodization with multiple short general-to-specific cycles (Aldo Sassi style) rather than one long linear periodization, especially for riders near genetic ceiling.
- Assess your biggest limiter honestly: if you have a big sprint but can't finish races, prioritize aerobic development; if you are aerobically strong but always finish 10th, add more races and work on tactical skills.
- For sprinters approaching target races, slightly reduce FTP/sweet-spot volume to control fatigue, but don't eliminate it entirely.
- Allow sufficient rest before FTP or performance testing -- avoid testing within days of a VO2max block or hard group ride; fatigue masks fitness.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Sprinters who carry high aerobic training loads will typically lose some watts on their peak sprint; the more and harder they train aerobically, the more pronounced this effect. Recovery is the key to managing this trade-off.
- Fiber type characteristics from textbooks are far more nuanced than commonly presented; science in the last 3-5 years has shown the traditional tables are oversimplified.
- A "good sprint" does not necessarily indicate fast-twitch fiber dominance -- rate of force development and gear selection (cadence) are confounding factors. Some sprinters peak at 90 RPM, others at 140 RPM.
- Type 2 fibers in cross-country skiers were shown to be nearly as good at fat oxidation as type 1 fibers -- they could simply produce more force (referenced Watts Doc episode on metabolism).
- Personal experience of what produces "best legs" for race day is more important than any generalized coaching prescription, including the hosts' own advice.
- "If you have empirical evidence that your legs are good after you do this, that, and the other thing, and you can win a race -- don't let anybody tell you different, including me." (Kolie Moore)

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Developing sprint power does not preclude developing aerobic power, and vice versa -- the common belief in a trade-off is largely a myth. Matthew van der Poel is cited as evidence that both can coexist at elite level.
- Natural sprinters often need MORE aerobic training and LESS sprint-specific work than people assume, not the other way around. The minimum effective dose of sprint work is often quite small for natural sprinters.
- The aerobic signaling pathways are identical in sprinters and non-sprinters -- "you're not a different species."
- Tactical and positional skills (hiding in the wind, cornering, reading the race) can produce podium results for riders who on paper shouldn't be top-five, and are often more undertrained than physiology.
- Fatigue from Vanderpoel's spring classics campaign to Giro to Tour showed peak 10-second power declining by ~150 watts per block -- "fatigue is the ultimate watt block."
- Highly race-specific training (only doing race simulations) is probably the least efficient way to get better, analogous to trying to break the 4-minute mile by only running full miles daily.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie references seeing "way more often than I thought I ever would" that riders test FTP without adequate rest, suggesting his coaching consulting experience has shifted his awareness of how widespread inadequate recovery is.
- The hosts note that early predictions that Vanderpoel and van Aert would never succeed on the road (because cyclocross is "short" and "sprinty") were proven completely wrong, reinforcing their thesis that aerobic engines underlie success across disciplines.
