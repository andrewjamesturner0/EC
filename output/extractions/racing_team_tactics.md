## Episode: 636044361-empiricalcyclingpodcast-student-union-1-collegiate-nationals-and-the-art-of-the-ttt.txt
**Numeric ID:** 636044361
**Word count:** 7,921
**Series:** Student Union (uncategorized/collegiate)

### Core Message
This episode covers collegiate nationals through interviews with MIT cyclists Emma Edwards, Liam Fenlon, and Burke Ozturk, with a heavy focus on the team time trial (TTT). MIT has exceptional institutional memory for the TTT developed over many years. Heat at the Augusta nationals course was a major performance factor even for athletes who had done targeted heat adaptation. Effective team communication and flexible pre-race planning were cited as decisive factors in the women's team winning.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Do 2–3 dedicated TTT practice sessions before nationals; use them to calibrate pull duration, aero position, and communication protocols — not just fitness
- In the TTT, assign shorter/no pulls to the weakest or tallest (most wind-exposed) rider in early laps so they can save energy for finishing climbs
- Seat the lightest/smallest rider behind the tallest rider to maximize draft and save energy for the end
- On a finish-line climb, conserve earlier in the race so the weakest finisher can still contribute to the three-rider minimum
- Heat prep: pre-ride courses the day before; use cold water bottles, ice in jerseys; still underestimate heat impact in 80°F+ humidity
- For crit cornering: racing on unfamiliar/neutral equipment (different tires) requires deliberate re-assessment of corner entry speeds — don't assume parity with your own setup
- Strava-segment repeated testing is a valid substitute for wind-tunnel aero testing when tunnel access is unavailable

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Heat adaptation (sauna sessions, trainer without fan) reduced but did not eliminate performance drop in 80°F+ humidity — Liam noted going into the red "much earlier than expected"
- TTT rotation order has no perfect answer when riders have unequal fitness; solutions involve trading off comfort for collective speed
- Races are partly determined by who gets caught behind crashes, not just fitness — Burke's both road race and crit outcomes were crash-related
- Varsity vs. club collegiate fields race very differently: varsity fields use full team tactics, club fields are more chaotic with riders racing individually

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Burke (host): "What is a break? A break is a team time trial, just not with teammates." The TTT is framed as the purest test of team racing, more controllable than road races precisely because you can plan every factor.
- Emma: Communication in the TTT was the biggest success factor, more than fitness — "something's going to go wrong; being flexible and communicative was super, super important"
- Institutional knowledge (multi-year accumulated TTT protocols, commands, pull durations) is presented as a bigger competitive advantage than any single training session

### Evolving Views
- None identified in this episode; this is primarily a race-report/interview episode.

## Episode: 659340143-empiricalcyclingpodcast-race-report-2019-cascade-classic.txt
**Numeric ID:** 659340143
**Word count:** 5,385
**Series:** Uncategorized (race report)

### Core Message
Emma Edwards reports on her Cascade Classic stage race experience racing for the composite Amy D Foundation team. The episode covers gravel-stage tactics, stage-race GC management for the first time, and the importance of team communication and opportunistic attacking. A coaching intervention on race nutrition (eating 100 g/hr) produced an immediate improvement in results mid-race. This was Emma's first race where she could genuinely compete for a GC result, contrasting with previous bad luck (flats at Colorado Classic and Joe Martin).

### Key Training Recommendations
- Eat 100 g carbohydrate/hr during stage races; Kolie notes this was the single biggest intervention that unlocked a UCI podium for Ingvar Omarsson in a similar situation
- Pre-ride the course before race day where possible; however, driving it (vs. riding it) can distort difficulty perception and lead to poor race strategy (Emma misjudged difficulty of stage 1 by driving it)
- Stay near the front of a strung-out peloton into gravel/technical sections — being mid-pack or behind led to being caught by crashes and unable to respond to attacks
- Team communication at stage races: "This is where you should look out for an opportunity to attack if you're feeling good" — general plans plus freedom to react, not rigid prescriptions
- For a GC rider in late stages: when a key rival's group is ahead and closing a gap, communicate with a team captain to help limit time losses, even if she cannot close it fully
- Commit to Tubeless tires for gravel stages to survive tread cuts that would puncture tubed setups

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Emma's success (7th GC, top 15 every day) came partly from avoiding flats and mechanicals — Kolie frames clean racing luck as a precondition for performance
- Descending skills are rate-limiting for riders without mountain experience: Emma "had to burn a match trying to get back on" after getting gapped on a long wet descent
- Tire choice matters for survival in gravel sections: multiple competitors flatted on the same section Emma survived on tubeless

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Without any specific GC-targeted workout prep between collegiate nationals and Cascade, Emma performed well across five days; Kolie says "there was really nothing special — just making sure she would be in the right physical and mental state." Implies fitness from collegiate season transferred directly.
- For a crit stage with a hill, attacking and animating the race repeatedly (even with no win) serves a team goal of disrupting sprinters and creating opportunities for stronger climbers

## Episode: 985640092-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-the-challenges-of-self-coaching-with-alex-carmona.txt
**Numeric ID:** 985640092
**Word count:** 7,937
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Alex Carmona (Empirical Cycling coach) discusses his journey from coached athlete to self-coached athlete to coach. The central themes are self-coaching challenges (accountability, objectivity, flexibility vs. structure), race intelligence as a separate skill from fitness, and the importance of mentors. Alex argues that knowing your own strengths and weaknesses — both physiological and tactical — is the fundamental prerequisite for racing improvement, beyond simple fitness gains.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Study courses and opponents before races: "Do the extra leg work. If you want to win a race, you know it's only these rare freaks that just show up and ride away and win." Course knowledge = tactical advantage
- Map your winning scenario before a race and work backwards: "What's your winning scenario, then let's work backwards. So it really does require a good analysis of somebody's skill set and also their mindset"
- If you have a modest sprint (~600 W), you cannot get separation in a men's crit field — you must follow an attack or "coax somebody, give them a gel to attack" to get out
- Self-coaching requires writing out a periodized plan (events, goals, timeline) in advance; without the structure of a pre-set plan, flexibility degrades into unstructured riding
- When structuring self-coaching, treat intervals like a program by RPE — "not only intervals by RPE, but a whole program by RPE almost" — adjusting based on daily feel rather than rigid watts
- Get to the front of a field before key terrain features: "There's gonna be a climb here, well then you got to make sure you're at the front of the group going to that climb"

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Self-coaching works best if you have strong self-discipline and have been coached for long enough to internalize training structure; it can easily drift toward Strava comparison and unstructured riding
- Downside of self-coaching: missing exposure to methods you wouldn't think of yourself; "if you're getting coached by someone else, you're going to be doing stuff that you didn't think of"
- Race results alone don't tell you what to fix — you have to "pay attention to those outcomes and think about what they mean"

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- "People often say there'll be like a lull 15, 20 minutes into a race [crit]. It just didn't." High-level crits are full gas from the gun — the assumption of a neutral period is wrong at the national level.
- Tactical intelligence is a trainable skill: watching YouTube videos of crits to understand sprint setups and breakaway coverage is legitimate preparation, separate from fitness work

### Evolving Views
- Alex evolved from thinking that showing up to races every weekend and riding hard would produce improvement, to realizing that at Cat 2+ you need to "understand the races better, understand the courses better, understand who you're racing against better" — fitness alone plateaus; race craft must be actively developed.

## Episode: 1138654516-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-3-usa-crits-2021-with-alex-carmona-and-taylor-warren.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1138654516
**Word count:** 15,021
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Alex Carmona and Taylor Warren (CS Velo road captain) debrief a full USA Crits series season, their first extended foray into high-level crit racing after careers focused on road and stage racing. Core message: criterium success is dominated by cornering skill and positioning; fitness matters but is secondary to being able to float in the top 10–15 wheels. At the USA Crit level, teams like Legion control the race speed from lap 5, creating a peloton train system that requires full team coordination to penetrate. Season management across a crit calendar requires building fitness before the season, not during it.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Cornering practice is the #1 crit-specific skill**: find a quiet downhill at race speed (30+ mph) and practice corner entry, apex timing, and exit lines repeatedly — "it doesn't matter if you can corner well at 20, you've got to do it at 30"
- A rider with a 300 W FTP can finish top 5–10 in national-level crits if they can hold top 15 wheels all race — positioning saves more energy than fitness
- In USA Crits on flat courses, passing someone at 36 mph requires ~1,500 W; you need a substantial sprint to contest pure flat finishes
- For hilly crits (Athens, Winston-Salem): anaerobic repeatability to do 500–600 W every lap is more important than peak sprint power; smaller/lighter riders favored
- Intensity distribution across a crit season: ~60–65% of training should be aerobic/endurance riding; let the races supply the high intensity (VO2, anaerobic) — "let the races really be the intensity and kind of fill it in around"
- Build the "reservoir" of fitness in the off-season: "about 80% of the gains you're gonna make is probably gonna be in the off-season when you're not racing where you can actually do a proper build"
- Openers for evening crits: morning opener (tempo, some low-threshold efforts, a few high-cadence sprints) works well, especially after travel days; day-before easy spin also effective
- Tire setup for crits: Taylor ran Vittoria Corsa G2 25mm/latex at 80 front/85 rear (65–67 kg); mid-season switched to Continental GP5000 28mm at ~75 front/80 rear and felt better; Alex ran Pirelli P Zeros 24mm (measured 26–27mm on wide rim) tubeless at 70 front/75 rear (~150 lb body weight)
- Recovery during crit season: prioritize sleep and nutrition above all; compression/massage are secondary; incorporate 1–2 mid-season break weeks; don't skip entire race weekends but be selective about which ones

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Cornering with people around you (5-wide, accordion effect) is a separate skill from cornering alone; without pack-riding context the skill doesn't fully transfer
- Radio/earpiece communication in crits is often impractical at high speed; half the time riders pull the earpiece out because they "can barely hear anything"
- Travel stress and logistics fatigue accumulate significantly; working full-time during a crit series requires treating travel recovery as a training variable
- Warmup for crits: highly individual; staging for 45 min after a 20-min warmup is counterproductive; some riders (Alex) found warmups mostly arbitrary

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Taylor: "Where you are 10 to go is most likely where you're going to finish plus or minus one or two spots" in UHC/Legion-controlled races; position at lap 10-to-go is the race within the race
- The "crit beef" / social media drama is primarily theater — in person, the same riders are "pretty chill," respectful of each other, and safety-conscious because they race together every weekend
- At Athens Twilight (150 starters, 25 finishers), finishing the race is itself a meaningful accomplishment; the attrition separates the result from the preparation

### Evolving Views
- Both Alex and Taylor shifted from thinking road racers can "just pivot" to crit racing toward recognizing that crit racing requires years of accumulated skill building; they each got their teeth kicked in early in the season and only felt competent by the final events.
- Taylor revised his view on fueling: previously did 40–60 g carb/hr on endurance rides, often starting with no food; moved to 90–100 g/hr deliberately and saw sustained fitness trends upward across the whole season (discussed again in the Perspectives 29 episode).

## Episode: 1174414534-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-6-2021-season-review-and-2022-planning-with-ingvar-omarsson.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1174414534
**Word count:** 16,257
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Season review and 2022 planning call with Ingvar Omarsson, an elite Icelandic privateer cyclist competing across road, gravel, and MTB (UCI top-50 ranking at time of recording). Main themes: how to structure a season around peak races rather than maximizing race count; how a mid-season nutrition intervention (eating 100 g/hr instead of 3 gels for a long stage) directly produced a first UCI podium; how early-season fatigue from racing depresses training volume and delays late-season fitness; and the value of an intentional off-season with gym work for athletes who normally ride year-round.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Do not race in January if your first A-race is May — use that time for VO2 max blocks and strength work instead; "doing a race just to do a race" sacrifices training quality
- Race fewer events: quality over quantity — Ingvar's coach explicitly recommended racing less in 2022 after overloading 2021
- Eat 100 g carbohydrate/hr during long race stages and endurance rides; for Ingvar, going from minimal fueling to this standard produced his first UCI podium within days
- After a multi-week VO2 max block (which Ingvar described as 3 weeks, same workouts each week, ~20–25 hrs/week): plan follow-on aerobic volume (17–20 hr weeks) to maintain fitness; the adaptations persist through easy maintenance riding
- An intentional off-season (novel for a career-long year-round rider) allows targeted strength/gym work that is incompatible with a VO2 max training block
- Sequencing: strength phase in Nov–Dec → aerobic build + VO2 block Jan–Feb → sharpen for first A-race (May); threshold work in March–April before target peaks
- For bunch sprint situations (tactical): have patience, "reduce your odds" by staying in the pack until the final laps — don't attack if you have the aerobic engine to win from a small group sprint; but do practice sprinting even if you're strong enough to solo, to develop the skill

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Ingvar notes that his best form came in spring (after the VO2 block) but dropped in June due to training volume reduction from racing and then having a newborn — fitness is load-dependent and can fade mid-season without volume maintenance
- Carrying fatigue from late-season racing into November compressed the off-season; starting the prep cycle 6 weeks later than ideal is a real cost
- Being a privateer gives schedule freedom but requires self-management of race selection, logistics, and sponsor obligations that team riders don't face
- Early-season stage races (Lanzarote, Casablanca, Andalusia) in January are training races; factoring them as A-races creates a peak-too-early problem

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Kolie: "I hope next season you can race a little less. Because I know that we could get your form a little better." — More racing does not equal better results; this is stated explicitly as a structural recommendation for a top-50 UCI rider
- Ingvar: Getting second at the Rift (24 seconds behind Pete Stetna) felt better than every gold medal in Iceland — perspective on the importance of racing at the right level, not just winning
- A VO2 max block that leaves you unable to eat pizza (Ingvar's anecdote about staring at pizza unable to motivate yourself to eat it) is at the appropriate intensity; stopping too early is a waste

### Evolving Views
- Ingvar evolved from never having an off-season (one off-season in his career, from a motorbike accident in 2015) to deliberately planning one; he acknowledges deep discomfort with losing fitness intentionally but recognizes the long-term benefit
- Ingvar changed from winning races predominantly solo (attacking off the front) to practicing bunch sprint tactics — won the Icelandic road national championship in a bunch sprint for the first time in years, realizing he could do it when he committed to the tactic instead of defaulting to soloing

## Episode: 1266057934-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-13-overtraining-sucks-with-katie-aman.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1266057934
**Word count:** 19,329
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Katie Aman (Empirical Cycling coach and former elite road racer) discusses her overtraining experiences in swimming and cycling, providing a first-person account of how relative energy deficiency (RED-S / underfueling) can coexist with strong race results, and how to recognize and recover from overtraining. The episode connects nutrition, weight loss, chronic fatigue, and injury risk as an interconnected system. Coaching tips focus on communication, reading athletes' subjective fatigue signals, and the "less is more" philosophy of interval work.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Endurance pace should be suspiciously easy: Katie was prescribed 174 W as endurance pace when her actual aerobic ceiling was ~130 W — 44 W too high, leading to chronic fatigue and post-ride exhaustion; Kolie revised downward based on direct assessment
- Do 2–3 hard interval sessions per week maximum; 5–6 per week leads to cumulative fatigue without additional adaptation — "less is more"
- Hard days hard, easy days truly easy (80 W for Katie on easy days = ~1/3 of threshold); polarized distribution reduces overtraining risk
- Do not restrict carbohydrates on the bike even when attempting body weight management off the bike; off-bike minor caloric reduction is compatible with performance, on-bike restriction is not
- During injury recovery: eat freely to support healing; attempting to lose weight during a broken collarbone repair delayed recovery in Remco's reported case — reversed here as a cautionary example
- When athletes show signs of deep fatigue (high RPE at normal watts, loss of motivation, inability to sustain repeated threshold intervals, soreness/weakness when accelerating from a standstill or going upstairs), pull back before the athlete reports it — these are early-warning signs
- For women: losing menstrual cycle is a significant RED-S warning sign requiring immediate nutrition and recovery attention, not normalizing

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Katie's overtraining was invisible to external observers and generated positive race results (national podium, stage race GC contention) simultaneously — overtraining does not always mean bad performances immediately
- Gaining back 10–15 lbs after injury and returning to racing showed 200 W improvement in sprint power (600 → ~800 W) — the weight was suppressing top-end output
- Swimming training culture (high volume, no power metrics, doubles culture) predisposed her to ignoring fatigue signals; cyclists from swimming backgrounds may carry similar habits
- The Whisper transcript for this file has some repetition artifacts (repeated phrases) from approximately line 480 onward; content is still extractable

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Performing well while overtrained (Katie won multiple elite races, was 26th at nationals, got 7th at Green Mountain stage race while at a dangerously low bodyweight) — "if you're performing well, you may still be chronically underfueled and overtraining"
- Forced rest from injury can paradoxically produce the best fitness in years afterward (Kate Courtney 2018, Vanderpoel post-Olympics cited); the recovery debt from prior overtraining is finally paid
- "Do you really have to go to every local race? Racing every Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday, Wednesday — that's a recipe for fatigue." Voluntary rest from racing is underutilized as a recovery tool

### Evolving Views
- Katie evolved from a "heroic effort" mindset (missing friends' birthdays for swimming, treating every rest as failure) to explicitly recommending "less is more" as a coach — the hardest shift for athletes is giving themselves permission to rest
- Kolie updated his coaching process to "ask athletes everything — warm-up, food, water, leave no stone unturned" after missing a key underfueling pattern in Ingvar's stage race; this came from coaching feedback in this episode's context

## Episode: 1622992359-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-28-training-takes-and-season-retrospective-with-maeghan-easler.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1622992359
**Word count:** 17,106
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Megan Eastler (pro road cyclist and new Empirical Cycling coach) reviews her breakthrough season after switching from a high-intensity-heavy program to Kolie's higher-volume, lower-intensity approach. The key intervention was increasing weekly volume from ~13 hrs to ~20 hrs (sometimes 30+) and replacing multiple weekly VO2/30-30 sessions with structured endurance + single weekly hard session. She discusses stage race tactics (Redlands, Gila, Joe Martin), TT performance, and the philosophical error of coaching athletes to "not fight for the win."

### Key Training Recommendations
- Replace 2–3 weekly 30-30 sessions (a previous program's approach "to prepare for racing surges") with higher aerobic volume — going from 13 hr/week average to 20–21 hr/week average produced significantly better results across a full season
- Limit hard sessions to one per week when building for big stage races; "three 30-30 sessions in one week is too much for me"; one hard session per week is enough
- Long ride target: 7 hrs as a sweet spot; 8 hrs starts to feel like "a job"; 6 hrs not enough for pro-level endurance development
- Train athletes as if they're fighting for the win — "if you don't have a FTP interval until you start working with [coach], you've missed a major training stimulus"; low-ambition training produces low-ambition results
- For GC racing: stage 1 is often the worst day (first-time-at-race jitters, positioning errors, altitude adjustment); do not panic; the body often performs better on days 3–5 if managed well
- In road sprints and crit sprints: when you have the fitness to be in the front group, believe in it — "if I'm in this group and I'm not getting dropped, I should probably sprint"; don't overthink it

### Caveats & Qualifications
- High-intensity prep ("preparing for surges") may make sense for a short criterium like the Highlands circuit race but is counterproductive for sustained 20-min climbs at Redlands Oak Glen or multi-day stage racing
- Some riders tolerate low-volume/high-intensity well if they have very deep multi-year training history; Megan does not think this was her situation
- Being in fourth GC at Gila was surprising and required a specific tactical awareness (protecting time gaps on the last day) that Megan had to learn in real time

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Megan's previous coach: "You're not going to be fighting for the win, so let's prepare for you to be pack fill." — Kolie and Megan dismiss this explicitly: training to not-lose produces not-losing results; training to win produces winning capacity
- High volume in women is not inherently over-training; Megan averaged 21 hrs/week with a peak of 32.5 hrs and describes her year as consistent and progressively improving — dispels assumptions about women needing lower loads

### Evolving Views
- Megan's view on training changed fundamentally: from "ride hard, get faster" (multiple high-intensity sessions per week) to recognizing that aerobic volume is the missing foundation
- As a new coach, Megan learned to read fatigue signals in others precisely because she experienced what over-intensity felt like personally: "I know how much would put me overboard"

## Episode: 1630053015-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-29-managing-fitness-and-rest-during-race-season-with-taylor-warren.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1630053015
**Word count:** 20,774
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Taylor Warren (CS Velo road captain) discusses a breakthrough year: PRs at 13+ years into racing, attributed to higher on-bike volume, dramatically better fueling (90–100 g carb/hr on endurance rides), and a mid-season training block of 6 weeks at 20+ hrs/week. He articulates how to manage fitness across a dense stage-race calendar (50+ race days) through intelligent recovery prioritization, RPE-based training, and a compressed off-season aerobic build.

### Key Training Recommendations
- Push carbohydrate intake to 90–100+ g/hr on endurance rides (even easy ones); Taylor used DIY lemonade in 2L bottles: 100–150 g table sugar + lemon juice + 2 g sea salt per bottle; tubeless setup allowed lower pressure for hot, dusty Boise training (180–220 W rides at 60% FTP)
- Endurance rides should average 180–185 W (for Taylor's ~300+ W FTP) — "as fast as I can that still feels easy"; confirmed that RPE-guided endurance pace naturally stabilizes around the same value across very different-length days
- Mid-season break: take 1 week off entirely (Taylor did ~5 hrs of easy mountain bike riding); then do a 6-week volume block (20+ hrs every week); "I would recommend to just about everyone taking a week off in the middle of summer"
- During a heavy race calendar (Redlands → Gila → Joe Martin = 14 race days in 25 days): prioritize recovery over training; threshold sessions only in 2-week gaps between races; no VO2 max work — racing provides it
- The off-season build is where 80% of fitness gains happen; maintaining is relatively easy during race season; build the reservoir before the season
- Signals of readiness to train hard again after racing blocks: motivation to go hard ("I want to train hard" or slight boredom with easy riding); absence of grumpiness when accelerating or going upstairs; not the reverse — if someone refuses to push above threshold it's a signal to give more easy volume
- Travel management: sync to destination time zone immediately on arrival; eye mask + familiar pillow; normalize the schedule rather than tracking home time zone; adjust circadian rhythm by getting up early rather than going to bed late (if morning person)
- RPE-based training: "heart rate and power are objective quantifiers of something subjective, which is RPE. Power and heart rate are roundabout ways of training what you're actually trying to train." Use RPE as primary guide, especially for endurance

### Caveats & Qualifications
- 100 g/hr table-sugar bottles may cause gut distress for some; Taylor tolerates high sugar concentrations well; individual gut testing required
- "Maintaining is relatively easy, whereas building is quite hard" — fitness can decline slightly over a race-heavy stretch even with correct recovery, especially if threshold isn't touched for months
- Joe Martin crit (late in a very busy stage race) was Taylor's worst result of the year — body doesn't respond well to stage racing when not trained for it; team was built for stage racing but still unprepared for the crit demands
- Team logistics quality significantly modulates travel stress; bad accommodations, absent food prep, uncertain transfers compound fatigue exponentially

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Taylor: "If you're going to get the same adaptation from riding 180 watts as from 220 watts for endurance rides — and most research shows that's probably the case — then the 220-watt ride just burns more kilojoules and you're more tired, with nothing to show for it."
- "200 watts" as an endurance target is a human ego/round-number artifact — the body doesn't care about round numbers; the actual aerobically appropriate pace for many 250–350 W FTP riders is 160–185 W
- Riding 30-hour weeks (140 + 180 mile back-to-back days) at a very easy pace produces PRs in sustained power — the volume itself, independent of intensity, is a performance driver

### Evolving Views
- Taylor shifted from individual-session thinking ("I put a lot of weight into single individual training sessions") to block-level thinking: "every training session is a drop in the water bucket — it's much more beneficial to have that consistent drip than a big splash"
- Taylor updated his fueling practice from 40–60 g/hr to 90–100 g/hr; the accumulated recovery and energy effect across a whole training block, not just a single ride, was the distinguishing result

## Episode: 1688639271-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-31-race-specific-intensity-and-tapering-with-alex-carmona.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1688639271
**Word count:** 23,728
**Series:** Perspectives

### Core Message
Kolie Moore and Alex Carmona (Empirical Cycling coach) discuss race-specific preparation, progression from general to specific intensity, and tapering for one-day races and stage races. The episode is the most technically detailed in the cluster on the topic of translating off-season fitness into race readiness. Key insight: group rides are the single best first race-prep session because they test both physiology and handling simultaneously. Fatigue monitoring (primarily motivation-based) determines when to progress from easy to specific training.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **First race-prep session**: group ride — tests both physiology and bike-handling skills simultaneously; also reveals weaknesses (forgotten cornering skills, dehydration habits, eating on bike)
- **Progression of race-specific work**: capacity first (30-30s: 30 sec full gas / 30 sec rest × 6 reps × 2–3 sets with 6-min rest between sets), then power with incomplete rest (2–5 min hard / 2–5 min rest), then maximum power with full recovery (30 sec absolute max / 20 min full recovery × 2–3), then race-specific duration at race-specific effort
- 30-30 workouts: can scale sets (1 set for early season, 6 sets when fit), can scale intensity (not always full gas), can morph into other formats; extremely versatile race-prep tool
- Embed threshold intervals: 15–20 min at threshold with 10–30 sec jumps thrown in (100 W over threshold) — mimics "go hard, settle back in, stay hard" dynamics of actual road racing
- **Race-specific prep for different disciplines**: For cyclocross, start with one weekly skills session in August (mounts/dismounts/barriers/off-cambers at sub-race pace); add fitness-specific CX intervals only in second season+ when skills are established — beginners need more skills sessions, experienced riders need fewer
- For criteriums: if you can hold top 10–15 wheels, every race becomes manageable; position training (group rides, practice positioning in the pack) matters as much as fitness
- **Tapering**: varies by individual — some athletes need "thrashing the legs" (3 hard days) going into races; others need full rest; reading this requires knowing each athlete's response pattern
- Signal for readiness after racing block: boredom with easy riding / wanting to go hard; inability to push above threshold voluntarily is a sign more easy volume is still needed first
- Don't confuse fatigue-induced performance drop with getting unfit — they look identical; the correct response is the opposite (more rest, not more training)

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Most people do too much race-specific work year-round; the question is "what's the reasonable dose" not "what's the absolute maximum"
- Some riders (rare) can go straight from threshold/sweet-spot work into races without any sprint/repeat training — this is individual; most people need the progression described above
- Cross-practices: for experienced riders, weekly Wednesday "hammer" cross practices are often too much; a skills-only session at recovery pace once a week may be more appropriate
- Stage race pre-race prep: travel stress and sleeping in unfamiliar places cannot be discounted; arriving early (staying the night before in a proper accommodation) and having logistical simplicity reduces stress load

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Alex: The best first race-prep workout is "a Saturday morning group ride" — not a structured interval session; the dual benefit of physiology + skills testing cannot be replicated on a trainer

### Core Message
Cole Tamburri (PhD student, CU Boulder; longtime Empirical Cycling client) discusses a multi-year journey from ~276 W FTP (20-min test, 2018) to ~400 W FTP, becoming competitive in Colorado elite road racing and UCI races (Gila, GMSR). ### Key Training Recommendations
- Strength training: approximately once per week year-round; not heavy squats every week — more like maintenance lifts; weight lifting introduced after collarbone healing (2021) and maintained since
- For criterium skills: you cannot substitute fitness for positioning — "it doesn't matter how high your FTP is if you can't position in the field, read the field, and sprint"; race craft must be practiced directly in races
- GMSR crit tactic: the race effectively starts from staging, not the start line — be on the front row behind the moto after the neutral lap; "you could have that goal but everybody knows that about that race"
- In Gila stage racing: stage 1 is often your worst stage at an unfamiliar race — survive it; performance improves from stage 2 onward as you learn course, road feel, field dynamics
- At Gila, time trial on day 3 revealed true fitness level — finishing in the top 25% of a UCI field confirmed that stage race fitness (vs. single-day) was a genuine strength
- For multi-day stage races: "taking care of yourself over four or five days and having the mental attitude to push through" — pacing, eating, and sleep quality across stages matters as much as any single-day power
- Train to win, not to survive: "there are races in which I could get away with bad pack work and positioning with fitness... and situations where I could get away with not having a super high top end by going off the front. [But] that's not going to work in the GMSR crit."

### Caveats & Qualifications
- Cole went from 145 lbs / 6'2" (underweight state with good climbing results) to ~165 lbs and was 9 minutes faster up Mount Greylock — weight gain while eating adequately produced both power and speed gains, not just mass
- RED-S / low energy availability affects men too; Cole references hearing rhetoric in cycling that frames low libido/hormonal suppression as a "badge of honor" — this is a warning sign, not a goal
- "Fastest way to be fast is to be happy, rested, and well-fed" — summary of what changed across the 5-year progression; deceptively simple but resisted in practice

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- People who get good fast (easy cat-up trajectory) often struggle more at the elite level than people who struggled early — the early struggle builds the "how do I approach this tactically" mindset; "I've never thought I'd be here, so every time I make the selection it's great"
