## Episode: 936171130-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-27-training-repeated-sprint-ability.txt
**Numeric ID:** 936171130
**Word count:** 13,058
**Series:** Watts Doc (episode 27)

### Core Message
This episode argues that FTP is an incomplete measure of cycling performance and that training repeated or stochastic efforts — which recruit larger motor units — requires specific targeted work distinct from steady-state threshold training. The hosts present a physiological framework for "repeated sprint ability" (RSA) as a separate endurance quality from FTP-based fitness. The key insight is that large motor units are typically under-trained aerobically because they are seldom recruited in daily life or steady-state rides, and that making them more aerobically capable is the mechanism behind getting better at criteriums, cyclocross, and mountain bike racing. Several specific workout types and tracking methods are discussed, and the episode resolves an apparent contradiction with earlier anti-high-intensity content by clarifying that the workouts here are medium-hard, not short/maximal.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Stochastic tempo rides:** Ride a course aiming to cover it as fast as possible — push harder on hills, recover on descents, like an impromptu ITT on rolling terrain. Duration can be 1–4 hours. Good for both road and off-road. Also called "fartlek tempo." Goal is to accumulate kilojoules while varying power naturally.
- **Sweet spot over-unders:** 10–60 seconds (sometimes up to a minute) over threshold, followed by 1–5 minutes under threshold. A specific example given: 30 seconds at 5–8 minute power, then 2 minutes at sweet spot power. Progressed as 2×20 or 3×20 sets. Considered universally doable and recoverable.
- **Intermittent efforts (15-15s, 30-30s, etc.):** Typically 15 seconds on / 15 seconds off, for 10+ minutes per set. Start with 3×10 and work up to 3×20 or 2×30. No strict power target — pace yourself so you can finish the intervals feeling worked but not wrecked. Do not blow out the first efforts. Kolie notes doing these at ~400–450 W on a ~260 W FTP.
- **RST (Repeated Sprint Training):** Absolute max seated or standing sprints, 5 seconds every 30–60 seconds, or 10 seconds every minute. Progression mirrors 15-15s. Designed for criterium, cyclocross, and mountain bike racers. Not the same as classic track sprinter workouts (which are 3–5 max efforts with full recovery over 2–3 hours).
- **FTP with accelerations / sprints on top:** Hold FTP-level power and add sprint accelerations every 2–5 minutes (or at intervals). Watch the peak power of accelerations across the workout; if they fall from ~900 to ~450 W by the end, larger motor units are fatiguing and need more training. Aim to peg the acceleration target at a level you can sustain more or less consistently rather than starting too high and fading hard.
- **Test sessions:** 1 hour of 15-15s (or 30-15s, 10-20s) or 10-second sprints every minute for an hour — effective both for building and testing this fitness type.
- **Cadence manipulation:** Low cadence is occasionally used for athletes who habitually climb at lower cadences. Otherwise, Kolie does not usually assign low-cadence work at tempo/sweet spot. High cadence noted for VO2 max work elsewhere.
- **Recovery management:** If someone is not feeling snappy or cannot accelerate well, reduce or remove the hard sprint portions but keep steady sweet spot work.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- RSA adaptation can take months to years; some people are naturally poor at repeated sprints and the training takes longer to stick.
- Athletes who are naturally strong sprinters can dig very deep holes and need extra caution — they need more recovery per session.
- Normalized power is a poor metric for stochastic training: it is course-dependent, can be "NP busted" by short hard efforts, and can read 20–30 W above actual threshold from a criterium with 30–40 second efforts. Do not use it to adjust FTP.
- Stochastic or RST-type work must be individualized: "how much recovery does somebody need" is the primary variable that determines frequency.
- Cadence manipulation (specifically low cadence) at tempo/sweet spot is occasionally useful for specific cases (e.g., someone habitually overgeared on climbs) but Kolie generally does not assign it broadly.
- If FTP is overestimated, adding accelerations to an FTP interval becomes very hard very fast and is not productive.
- For mountain bikers: off-road endurance riding does provide the stochastic stimulus, but flat road rides provide more consistent pedaling and specific aerobic adaptation. For those already excellent at repeated efforts, off-road riding is not as necessary for this quality specifically.
- Tracking stochastic fitness: use fixed test courses (ideally race-terrain specific), specific interval test sessions, or races/workouts where you watch how peak sprint power degrades over time.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Fiber type and metabolic adaptation are decoupled: a study on cross-country skiers found that their type 2 (fast-twitch) fibers had fat oxidative capacity comparable to their type 1 fibers. Metabolic adaptations are not inextricably linked to fiber type — it depends on training demands.
- A triathlete with a 400 W FTP may do fine in a cat 5 criterium without any repeated-sprint training; the importance of RSA training is relative to the competitive level.
- With FTP with sprints, it is better to peg the acceleration at a power you can sustain than to start high and fade, even though "starting high and fading" can be more race-like in some contexts. Consistency of motor unit recruitment matters more.
- Aerobic training of large motor units (which improves "fresh sprint" ability at the end of a race) is one of the main benefits of this type of work — not just because it trains the sprint itself, but because those motor units preserve their own glycogen better when they are more aerobically capable.
- Coaching most people, Kolie includes intermittent efforts at some level for virtually the entire year — even during "FTP blocks." Rare to have three purely steady-state FTP sessions in a week.

### Evolving Views
- This episode explicitly addresses an irony: earlier Wattstock #2 ("Why You're Probably Training Too Hard for Crits") cautioned against short, high-intensity work year-round. Kolie clarifies here that the caution was specifically against very short VO2max/anaerobic capacity work done all year, not against the medium-hard intermittent and over-under work described in this episode, which can and should be done more regularly.

## Episode: 1164677890-empiricalcyclingpodcast-200k-the-best-and-worst-workouts.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1164677890
**Word count:** 12,678
**Series:** Uncategorized (200k milestone episode)

### Core Message
A structured debate between Kolie and Kyle on their personal "best" and "worst" training workouts, generated for the 200,000 listens milestone. Both hosts independently compiled lists without seeing each other's, then compared and discussed. The episode is explicitly framed as opinionated rather than prescriptive, and the hosts acknowledge that a "bad" workout for one athlete may work well for another. The core themes are: avoid workouts assigned as percentages of FTP for intensity zones (because FTP-to-VO2max ratio varies hugely between athletes), avoid pass-fail extremes that don't teach pacing or RPE, favor workouts that are progressive and build on success, and prize boredom-busting variety for long trainer rides.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Bad workout #1 — VO2max intervals as a percentage of FTP:** Both hosts listed this independently. Doing 4×6 min at 110–120% FTP is often too easy for athletes with high VO2max-to-FTP ratios (five-minute power can be 140–150% of FTP). The fix: either use WKO5 iLevels based on an actual power curve, or just go genuinely max on VO2max intervals. "You know you're doing VO2max right if you're breathing really, really, really hard."
- **Bad workout #2 — Low-cadence "strength training" on the bike (e.g., 5×5 at 60 RPM on big gear):** Even at VO2max-level powers, force on the cranks is still an order of magnitude below leg press or squat. This does not substitute for actual strength training.
- **Bad workout #3 — Going to true failure in a workout:** Specifically, workouts where the athlete cannot complete them due to an FTP set too high (e.g., starting 3×30 at 95% but actually riding above FTP), or a wildly aggressive jump in progression (e.g., 2×25 → 4×30 with no build). The fix: use open-ended intervals with an RPE-based stop point, not a binary pass-fail. Stop when the exercise is no longer generating adaptation, not when physically unable to continue.
- **Bad workout #4 — 2×20 all-out on both intervals:** Generates too much fatigue for marginal additional physiological stimulus over a sweet spot workout. The cumulative fatigue is not worth the marginal benefit.
- **Bad workout #5 — Overly precise workouts:** e.g., 738 W for 28 seconds × 6 (directly copied from WKO5 optimized intervals). Power meter error and day-to-day variation make this kind of precision meaningless. Round intervals to sensible numbers and let them evolve as fitness changes.
- **Bad workout #6 — 40-20s at 130% FTP:** Even at 130% FTP, for a well-trained athlete the work only becomes hard in the last couple of reps of 10 minutes. The fix: pace yourself without a target, and progress the workout over weeks by watching average power on the ons.
- **Bad workout #7 — 16×1 min on / 1 min off all-out (Revolver):** Quality collapses after the first 2–3 reps with only 1-minute recovery. The fix: shorten the set to 3–6 minutes of 30-30s (or similar), stop while quality is still good.
- **Bad workout #8 — Recovery rides at 1 W below zone 2:** Failing the spirit of recovery rides by treating training zones as light switches.
- **Good workout #1 — Adding 5 min per rep to your last FTP interval session:** Time-based progression is a safe and effective way to build FTP work. Err on the side of more time at the same power before raising watts.
- **Good workout #2 — Threshold progression with alternating power:** A "boiling frog" approach to high-threshold training: 2 min tempo / 3 min threshold / 3 min tempo / 2 min threshold, done as 2×30 or 3×30. Good for athletes scared of their threshold number. Makes the minutes go by faster and reduces mental stress.
- **Good workout #3 — High cadence VO2max efforts:** Kolie lists this as a staple. One listener broke through a plateau and gained ~10 W FTP after 3 weeks. Details not fully elaborated here (covered in other episodes).
- **Good workout #4 — Long low-intensity group ride (5–6 hours at conversational pace):** Underrated; forces true low-intensity riding and is socially enjoyable.
- **Good workout #5 — Fartlek/stochastic tempo:** Ride a course aiming for speed rather than specific power. Natural variation, race-prep value, fun.
- **Good workout #6 — 30-30s (2–3 sets of 3–6 minutes):** Short enough that quality stays high throughout, hard enough to genuinely stress anaerobic capacity. Average or normalized power for a 30-30 set should be "in the over-FTP range" with normalized power near 5-min max power range.
- **Good workout #7 — Zone 2 ride with 5–10 second out-of-saddle sprints every few minutes:** Good for crit sprinters doing long base rides; there may be a paper suggesting occasional sprints enhance aerobic adaptation; doesn't improve peak sprint power but makes a long ride bearable.
- **Good workout #8 — Distributing an FTP workout after pre-fatigue (with caveats):** Doing a KJ burn first, then finishing with e.g. 20-30 min at FTP. Context-dependent; the pre-fatigue must genuinely simulate race fatigue (i.e., hard enough that you might fail the final effort). Not a beginner workout.
- **Tracking FTP without tests:** Kolie recommends using the WKO5 PD model (last 30–60 days of data) to track FTP movement in sections of the power curve rather than formal testing. "Testing is training and training is testing."

### Caveats & Qualifications
- All "bad" workouts can work for some athletes under some conditions. The hosts frame them as generally suboptimal, not categorically forbidden.
- The ramp test for FTP is estimated to accurately predict FTP for ~40–55% of riders due to the large individual variation in anaerobic capacity relative to FTP. Ramp test duration also affects peak power: ~6-min ramps don't reach VO2max; ~11-12 min ramps also miss it; the sweet spot is roughly 8-10 minutes.
- Polarized training ("hard all the time") leads to burnout in athletes who've been training for a year or more without rest periods or lower-intensity phases. Multiple coach-to-athlete examples cited.
- Microburst intervals (e.g., 40-20s) must go very long (10+ minutes) before they feel genuinely hard for fit athletes if pegged to FTP. Self-paced or time-based tracking is better.
- The pre-fatigue FTP workout is unreliable because kilojoule burn rate is highly individual (aerobic monsters can burn 3,000 kJ in 3 hours at endurance pace; others need 7–8 hours).

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Low-cadence "strength endurance" intervals were originated by Aldo Sassi (a coach associated with doping-era teams). Many athletes report they work well for climbing feel, but they do not substitute for actual strength training by any measurable force comparison.
- The original Coggan power zones were intended to be **descriptive** (to categorize work after the fact) not **prescriptive** (to assign targets). Using fixed percentages like 120% of FTP for VO2max work is a misapplication of the original intent.
- For 30-30s as anaerobic capacity work, the recovery time (the "off") is critical: if recovery is too short (e.g., 10-sec off), quality collapses in reps 3–4. Chris Hoy's book describes old GB Cycling track sessions of "25 flying 100s" before they had proper coaching — athletes were vomiting by rep 6. Lesson: more is not better, quality of reps matters.

### Evolving Views
- None identified in this episode. The hosts explicitly note they haven't seen each other's lists and are discovering overlap live, but no view reversals are expressed.

## Episode: 1253680522-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-19-ftp-testing-shortcuts.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1253680522
**Word count:** 11,107
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips (episode 19)

### Core Message
This episode addresses athletes and coaches who struggle with formal FTP testing — due to anxiety, bad days, or poor pacing — and presents two practical "shortcut" methods for finding threshold without a formal test. The first method is a blind-paced workout (no power or HR feedback) of 3×15 or 2×20 at perceived threshold. The second is a progressive intensity exploration: do 5–10 min efforts at increasing power values (+15 W steps) starting ~60 W below estimated threshold, and identify the crossover point where effort becomes unsustainable. The episode also includes a substantial discussion of the physiology of threshold (EMG inflection points, motor unit recruitment), the psychological pressures around testing, and how to use WKO5 model estimates as a third option. A key meta-point: FTP is important but not the sole determinant of race performance; over-fixating on it is a coaching failure mode.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Blind-paced 3×15 or 2×20 (with 5–10 min rest):** Cover power meter and HR monitor. Ride purely by feel at what you know is threshold effort. Review average power afterward. Works "93 times out of 100" to produce accurate FTP even after a failed formal test two days prior. Best for athletes already experienced with threshold feel.
- **Progressive intensity exploration (stepping up intervals):** Do 5–15 min efforts starting ~60 W below estimated FTP, add 15 W per step. For athletes with a big sprint (high anaerobic capacity), use 10–15 min efforts rather than 5–8 min, as they can mask threshold for longer. Stop when the effort crosses from "breathing hard but controlled" to "breathing uncontrolled and clearly unsustainable." The crossover point is threshold. Kolie used this on himself: thought threshold was 200 W, discovered it was 190 W after only 15–18 min at 200 W felt clearly over threshold.
- **WKO5 model as fallback:** Use the last 30–60 days of data with the PD model. 30-day look-back may lose important peaks; 90-day is the default but may include stale data. Adjust the look-back window until the model seems to reflect current fitness. Noted as plus-or-minus 10–15 W accuracy.
- **Do not test near A-priority races:** Risk of both psychologically bad outcomes (confidence hit) and "wasting a good day."
- **Round down when in doubt:** If FTP movement is <10 W, leave it unchanged. Power meter error alone (~2% for a 400 W FTP = 8 W) makes small changes meaningless.
- **Allow athletes in good tune to self-adjust threshold workouts:** A very small subset of athletes can incrementally raise their threshold target during a block (by feel) rather than waiting for a formal test. Works only when RPE calibration is excellent.
- **Let threshold workouts feel like threshold:** FTP intervals should always feel like FTP — not like survival. If four-by-eight feels catastrophically hard, the FTP is set too high, not that the athlete needs to "push through."
- **Rest before testing:** 4–5 easy days is usually sufficient; no need for a full taper. Abort the test if legs don't feel good after a proper warm-up.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- The stepping-up exploration may not cross threshold clearly for athletes with high anaerobic capacity (sprinters can sustain moderately above-threshold power for 15–20 minutes before the system collapses). These athletes need longer individual steps.
- FTP can have 20–30 W day-to-day variation due to accumulated life stress, sleep, and other factors. Training against the "good days" leads to chronic overreaching.
- Some athletes have FTP that is effectively ~30 W lower than their best-day test result, and training at the inflated number consistently leads to failure and frustration; training at the lower (true consistent) number produces faster long-term gains.
- WKO5 FTP estimates often come in ~10 minutes short on TTE (time to exhaustion), not just on the power value.
- FTP tests should not be done when genuinely fatigued. A bad test result in a fatigued state is meaningless but may still cause psychological harm.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- For athletes with high anaerobic capacity relative to FTP (sprinters, track athletes), going slightly over threshold can feel fine for 15–20 minutes before "collapsing" suddenly — very different from aerobic-dominant athletes who feel the crossover quickly. Kolie's own example: at 190 W FTP, felt fine at 200 W for 15–18 minutes, then suddenly couldn't continue.
- FTP testing anxiety exists even at professional and near-professional levels — including athletes going to nationals or international races who are more nervous about testing than competing.
- A successful FTP test can sometimes be counterproductive if used as a confidence boost going into a race, because it may lead to overestimating available matches.
- The FTP discovered by blind-pacing 2×20 can come in notably lower than a formally executed test, and that lower number is often the more actionable training value.

### Evolving Views
- None identified in this episode. However, Kolie notes he now assigns threshold within ~10 W bands (previously within 5 W), reflecting a shift toward less precision in target assignment.

## Episode: 1440628345-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-22-the-case-against-erg-mode.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1440628345
**Word count:** 16,161
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips (episode 22)

### Core Message
Despite the clickbait title, the episode makes a nuanced case against routine ERG mode use, primarily centered on the loss of the RPE feedback loop. The hosts argue that knowing how efforts feel — and being able to adjust power in real time based on fatigue — is more important than hitting a prescribed number. ERG mode is presented as a tool that disables this feedback loop, leading to pass-fail thinking, inability to detect early fatigue signals, and poor pacing skill development. There are identified legitimate uses for ERG mode (recovery rides at confirmed wattage, Zwift racing, fartlek on artificial courses, remedial pacing for beginners, and simulating climbing for flat-country athletes). The episode is notably critical of VO2max intervals programmed as percentage-of-FTP power targets in ERG mode, and includes a substantial discussion of hard-start VO2max intervals and their physiology.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Endurance rides in ERG mode: not recommended** unless LT1 is precisely known by RPE and the athlete can set the watt target conservatively and adjust it. The LT1 range across athletes spans 50–85% of FTP — a huge individual range. Even on the same athlete, LT1 shifts ~30–40 W based on fatigue/day-to-day state. Starting an endurance ride at a fixed ERG watt that is 30–40 W above true LT1 on that day accumulates fatigue and shortens the ride. Kolie recommends not turning on ERG for at least the first hour of an endurance ride.
- **VO2max intervals in ERG mode: strongly not recommended** because they require absolute maximal effort. Pre-programmed targets are based on FTP percentages, which are individual — "VO2max power" can be 130–150% of FTP for athletes with a high anaerobic capacity. A referenced Jim Arnold blog post on hard-start VO2max intervals showed only ~80–95% VO2max achieved at a predetermined plateau, not 100%. Full exertion is required for the training stimulus.
- **Anaerobic capacity intervals / sprints in ERG mode: not recommended.** For 30-second max efforts, power naturally starts ~1500 W and may fall to ~700 W (a 2× drop). Pre-programming that profile is nearly impossible and ignores day-to-day recovery. The duration of a hard effort matters as much as the peak power; cutting it short because you can't sustain a fixed ERG watt defeats the workout.
- **Good uses of ERG mode:**
  - Recovery rides at a confirmed easy wattage (good for athletes who tend to go too hard).
  - Zwift / RGT races with hills — simulates low-inertia climbing for flat-country athletes.
  - Fartlek-tempo on rolling virtual courses (ride for speed, not watt target).
  - Remedial pacing education: a "training wheels" use for athletes who cannot self-regulate. Should be graduated past.
  - Optionally, during the steady portions of zone 2 or zone 3 rides, once LT1 is empirically known, to reduce cognitive load.
- **Developing RPE calibration as a primary skill:** Kolie says a large majority of his coached athletes could have their workouts described only as "do FTP" / "do sweet spot" / "do VO2s" without any power targets and would "nail it every single time." This is the goal state.
- **Pacing skill for sprint and anaerobic efforts:** Before a max effort, do short 5-second exploratory sprints in candidate gears to find the right starting gear. For 30-second all-out efforts, starting cadence and gear selection determine whether power is accessible.
- **15-15s / 30-30s pacing:** For these repeating short intervals, starting even one or two reps too hard causes the entire set to collapse in quality. For athletes who make 1,500 W peak on a 270 W FTP, the functional repeatable power for 15-second intervals was ~400–450 W, not 120% of FTP.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- ERG mode is appropriate for some athletes in some contexts; it is not a categorical failure to use it, especially for beginners getting acquainted with effort levels.
- The LT1 range (50–85% of FTP) means some athletes legitimately can set ERG mode for endurance rides without drifting into tempo — but they need to know empirically where their LT1 is, not assume a formula.
- One client with confirmed lab LT1 at ~330 W on a 400 W FTP rides endurance in the mid-200s rather than up at 330, because high volume requires conservative endurance pacing.
- Overreliance on ERG mode can create the pass-fail mental model: "if I got the workout done, it was good." This obscures the more informative signal of how hard the workout felt and whether the power was appropriate.
- The W' (anaerobic capacity) recovery model is very poorly characterized; any pre-programmed multi-interval session that assumes a specific recovery rate will be wrong for many athletes on many days.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- The subjective feeling of ERG mode being "harder" than free-riding at the same watts may reflect a psychological phenomenon: being compelled to do a power (ERG) versus choosing to (free riding). Kolie compares it to being in a group ride where surges are imposed rather than chosen.
- Lactate testing misused as "advanced ERG mode": if you do a lactate test and conclude your LT1 is at 2 mmol, then set ERG mode at that watt, you may be 50–80 W over your actual workable endurance pace and end up training in the "black hole" constantly.
- Athletes who crash frequently returning to racing after trainer-only seasons (noted happening during COVID race restarts) may be partly explained by over-ERG mode use removing cornering and bike-handling skill reinforcement.

### Evolving Views
- None explicitly stated. The episode was framed as adding to existing ERG mode debate rather than reversing any prior position.

## Episode: 1472759278-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-27-programming-over-under-workouts.txt
**Numeric ID:** 1472759278
**Word count:** 10,914
**Series:** Ten Minute Tips (episode 27)

### Core Message
This is a sequel to the previous episode on over-under theory. The episode focuses on practical programming of over-under workouts and honestly deflates their reputation as uniquely superior to standard threshold training. Kolie states that there is no direct study comparing over-unders to 2×20 sweet spot/threshold work that shows over-unders produce meaningfully superior adaptations in anaerobic capacity or "lactate tolerance." They are valued primarily for: (1) boredom relief on the trainer, (2) their flexibility as a workout format with many adjustable variables, and (3) race-specificity when the overs closely match the acceleration demands of target events. The episode provides concrete programming guidance for two main over-under formats and addresses the question of lactate clearance workouts honestly, concluding that long endurance rides are actually the best "lactate clearance" workout.

### Key Training Recommendations
- **Sweet spot over-under (the "bread and butter" format):**
  - Ratio: 1:4 over-to-under
  - Specific durations: 30 seconds over / 2 minutes under, or 1 minute over / 4 minutes under
  - Power targets: overs at roughly 20–50 W above threshold (vibes-based, not a fixed percentage); unders at sweet spot (approximately 88–93% FTP)
  - Set duration: build up to 2–3 sets of 30–40 minutes total
  - Assign without rigid power targets for experienced athletes; give a field test to see where they land and progress from there
  - Start each set with the over (an acceleration), not a rest period — especially race-specific for cross-country MTB and cyclocross athletes (practice clip-in and first effort)
- **Anaerobic capacity / intermittent over-under format:**
  - Use 15-15s or 30-30s for 10–20 minutes total
  - For athletes who struggle with steady threshold work; a way to still recruit large motor units
  - Pacing critical: don't send the first effort — pace for the entire set. If you crush the first one, the set collapses
  - Not a strict "over-under" in the conventional sense, but functionally serves the same role for athletes who cannot sustain any steady-state threshold efforts
- **Lactate clearance under intervals:** If genuinely trying to maximize lactate oxidation, do overs only when you feel recovered from the last one (regardless of elapsed time). This maximizes the total amount of lactate produced and cleared but reduces average power of the unders. Kolie notes this is a very specific approach and most athletes don't need to be this precise.
- **Cadence manipulation on overs:** High cadence on overs is especially useful for track racers and criterium racers (simulates coming out of corners or matching accelerations at high RPM). Low cadence on overs can be used for cross-country MTB and cyclocross riders. Observing how easily an athlete spins up during high-cadence overs is a useful fatigue indicator.
- **Fitting over-unders into a threshold block:** Simply substitute one steady-state threshold day for an over-under day. There is no required ratio or required prerequisite duration to move from steady-state to over-unders.
- **Progression:** Progress over time by increasing total time in the intervals (more sets, longer sets), not just by raising power. If you cannot progress time, you are tired, your FTP is overestimated, or nutrition is insufficient.
- **Using intermittent efforts for FTP assessment (with caution):** Average power from well-paced 15-15s, 30-30s, or 40-20s can give a rough threshold estimate. Use average power, not normalized power, for this. Normalized power is a poor tool for intermittent work. The metabolic load does approximate sustained aerobic stress when well-paced and sustained for threshold-appropriate durations.
- **WKO5 aerobic adaptation / training impact score:** A rough way to evaluate which workouts drive more aerobic molecular signals (AMPK pathway). Endurance rides and threshold work score high aerobically; VO2max work pings high anaerobically but also has good systemic (cardiac) aerobic impact not fully captured by the peripheral score.

### Caveats & Qualifications
- There is no direct randomized study comparing over-unders to 2×20 sweet spot work that shows superior outcomes for over-unders in anaerobic capacity or lactate tolerance. One study Kolie found had a methodology section too vague to be useful; the studies it was based on showed steady-state intervals were slightly superior in terms of adaptations.
- "Lactate tolerance" as a concept is poorly supported — the real adaptations are mitochondrial mass, MCT transporter density, and aerobic capacity; all are improved by any sustained aerobic training, including simple long endurance rides.
- For athletes who naturally tolerate repeated accelerations well (e.g., coach "Katie" who does nothing but steady-state and then wins crits), over-unders add nothing special and are just a variation.
- The overs-at-120%-FTP prescription common in popular training plans is based on a misunderstanding: this conflates the Coggan zone label with the physiological mechanism. 120% FTP may be nowhere near VO2max power for aerobically-dominant riders.
- Assigning overs as a percentage of 5-minute or 1-minute power would likely make the first few intervals fine and then cause collapse — be cautious about anchoring to short-duration maximal power.
- Some athletes simply cannot do any sustained steady-state threshold work at all and need the intermittent format. This is an individual difference, not a failure of programming.

### Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims
- Over-unders do not have meaningfully superior adaptations compared to standard threshold training in the literature. Their value is specificity (acceleration practice), boredom relief, and flexibility — not a magical lactate-system adaptation.
- The best "lactate clearance" workout is a 6-hour endurance ride. Mitochondrial mass from high-volume aerobic riding is the primary driver of lactate oxidation capacity, not over-under intervals specifically.
- Intermittent efforts (15-15s, 30-30s) for 10–20 minutes at sustained threshold-range average power may produce approximately the same aerobic molecular adaptation signals as steady-state threshold work, despite the very different power profile.
- Sprinting all-out on anaerobic capacity efforts (the 2×5 min of 30-30s Kyle describes) until you "can't contract your legs" is actually the indicator that the effort is working correctly for anaerobic capacity — the inability to sustain force is the signal that you've genuinely taxed that system.

### Evolving Views
- Kolie explicitly notes this episode deflates the over-under mystique that even he had contributed to previously. His clearer current view: over-unders are a good format but not categorically superior to other threshold work, and their marketing as "lactate clearance" tools is overstated. This represents a softening of whatever implicit endorsement existed in prior coaching practice.
