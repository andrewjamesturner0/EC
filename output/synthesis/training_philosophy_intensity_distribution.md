# Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

## Easy Rides Must Be Genuinely Easy — Zone 2 Is Defined by Feel, Not Power

The single most common training error identified across the podcast is riding endurance and recovery days too hard. Three coaches independently flag this as their most frequent intervention: athletes riding endurance above LT1 shift the session from a recovery/volume stimulus to a moderate-intensity effort that accumulates fatigue without proportionate benefit. Zone 2 prescribed at 2 mmol/L blood lactate — a common LT1 proxy — frequently places well-trained athletes at low sweet spot intensities, turning every "endurance" ride into a functionally moderate session. Multiple coaches report significant FTP gains — sometimes exceeding 20-30 watts — when athletes finally reduce endurance ride intensity, though these gains typically occur alongside other training adjustments rather than in isolation. Zone 2 has no unique physiological adaptation; the adaptations exist on a continuum with threshold and VO2max training, and "threshold training is endurance training" (Andy Coggan's formulation, endorsed by Kolie). What makes zone 2 genuinely useful is not a unique metabolic pathway but what it *permits* within a training week — large volumes of work that do not compromise recovery for hard sessions. The correct pacing signal is RPE, not a strict wattage window — "the hardest pace that still feels easy," passing the talk test, RPE 4-5/10. Riding 20-30 W below apparent LT1 is fine; no observed adaptation difference exists between 60% and 45-50% of FTP. Pedaling time, not power output, is the key stimulus. The cultural stigma against easy riding — the feeling that riding slowly is "cheating" — is identified as the primary barrier to implementation, and coaches report that persuading athletes to ride easier is their most consistent friction point.

**Recommendations:**
- Use the talk test and perceived effort (RPE 4-5/10) rather than power zones or blood lactate to set endurance pace
- LT1 is the upper limit for endurance rides; calibrate individually (ranges from 45-85% of FTP across athletes)
- Riding 20-30 W below apparent LT1 is fine; do not anchor to a specific power number
- Do not peg endurance pace at the top of the Coggan zone 2 table; the number is a convenience, not a prescription
- If a high-volume easy week feels revelatory (no soreness, feeling great), your previous "endurance" pace was too hard
- Recovery between hard intervals should be genuinely easy, not 70% FTP
- For dieting athletes, easier endurance rides reduce acute energy deficits and improve dietary compliance
- On a trainer, break up endurance rides with cadence variation rather than adding intensity
- Minimize coasting on outdoor endurance rides to maximize pedaling time, which is the actual stimulus
- Track the efficiency factor (normalized power / heart rate) to monitor short-term cardiac stroke volume progression

## Volume Is the Primary Long-Term Driver of Aerobic Improvement, Not Intensity

Across multiple episodes, Kolie argues with increasing conviction that training volume — not interval intensity — is the dominant lever for sustained year-on-year aerobic development. He reports that approximately 14-15 hours per week is the minimum at which he has observed consistent year-on-year FTP improvements in coached athletes. Clients riding 10-12 hours/week rarely see consistent FTP gains regardless of interval quality, while those at 20-30 hours/week almost always do. At low volumes (4-6 hours/week), athletes plateau relatively quickly, and intensity cannot fully substitute for volume over the long term. The mechanism appears to be that higher chronic training load improves the body's capacity to absorb subsequent stress, creating a positive feedback loop. Even inconsistent volume spikes (e.g., a vacation week at 25 hours) produce real aerobic benefits, provided the additional riding is executed at genuinely easy intensity. At very high volumes, easy riding becomes essential because cumulative fatigue limits how much intensity can be stacked on top. Zone 2 is also a powerful maintenance tool: fatigued athletes switching to endurance-only riding see threshold and VO2max numbers recover without interval work. The notion of "junk miles" applied to easy riding is a myth — what is actually junk is semi-hard, unfocused riding that creates fatigue without targeted adaptation. Pandemic data was revealing: athletes who stopped bike commuting lost approximately 10 W FTP, while athletes with no racing to interrupt aerobic development showed unconstrained aerobic progression.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize finding more riding time over adding more intensity if FTP is stalled
- At low volumes (4-6 h/week), adding even one easy endurance ride per week is a significant stimulus increase
- When volume increases are impossible, shift season strategy: stay at 95% fitness and race more frequently rather than chasing peaks
- Bike commuting at low intensity is a legitimate endurance stimulus
- When you have weeks with extra time available, use them for increased endurance volume — the aerobic stimulus is real even if week-to-week consistency is irregular
- If riding only 2-3 times per week for 2 hours, those rides do not need to be zone 2; threshold or VO2max work is a valid use of that time

**Sources:** Episodes 1270352152, 1514086555, 1560449302, 1211797429

## The "Sweet Spot vs. Polarized" Debate Is a False Dichotomy — Let Intensity Distribution Emerge From Diagnosis

The hosts describe the sweet spot vs. polarized debate as "kayfabe" — a manufactured conflict more useful for selling training plans than for guiding individual training decisions. Both approaches are simply tools, and starting from a training modality rather than from an athlete's specific limiters is backwards. Sweet spot training is functionally threshold training, and the large body of lactate threshold literature applies to it directly even though the term "sweet spot" does not appear in peer-reviewed research. Pre-planning a training zone distribution (80/20, all sweet spot, etc.) is discouraged; the distribution should emerge from answering the right questions: what is my limiter, how many hard days per week can I recover from, and what physiological quality do I need to develop? When well-run training programs are inspected retrospectively, they often look pyramidal, but this was not the design target. A "rainbow" of workout types (endurance, sweet spot, FTP, VO2max, sprints) across the intensity spectrum is preferred over reliance on any single method. Whether sweet spot counts as a "high-intensity" workout depends on context — in a building phase it counts against the weekly hard-workout budget, but as maintenance it may not. Elite riders do not uniformly train polarized; distribution varies by event, phase, and individual.

**Recommendations:**
- Ask "what is my limiter?" before choosing training modality — diagnose whether VO2max, threshold, endurance, sprint, or race craft needs work
- Do not pre-plan intensity distributions; instead determine how many hard days per week you can recover from
- Target RPE 7-8 for threshold/sweet spot work; if you are white-knuckling the last 3 minutes, the target is too high
- Limit hard days to 2-3 per week (rarely 4), with genuinely easy recovery between them
- Use a full spectrum of workout types across the training year rather than relying on a single intensity domain
- Count sweet spot as a hard session during build phases but not necessarily during maintenance phases

**Sources:** Episodes 1199788219, 1270352152, 1087518463, 1211797429, 1316155894

## FTP Improvement Requires a Three-Legged Stool: Threshold Work, VO2max Work, and Endurance Volume

Exclusive focus on FTP intervals (e.g., repeating 2x20 sessions indefinitely) will plateau because FTP training is primarily a duration-based stimulus — it extends time-to-exhaustion at threshold rather than raising the ceiling. VO2max sets the ceiling on FTP, and endurance volume supports the aerobic base that allows both to rise. For athletes whose TTE is short (under 35 minutes), extending threshold duration is the priority. For athletes whose FTP is a low fraction of VO2max — when FTP sits at 80-82% of VO2max watts — a dedicated VO2max block raises the ceiling. For athletes whose volume is low, adding endurance riding provides the base for both. Early in a training career, all three modalities raise FTP comparably — largely through plasma volume effects — but as fitness advances, the stimulus must become more targeted. The relationship is cyclical in well-trained athletes: VO2max work raises the ceiling, which shortens TTE, then threshold work extends TTE again at the new, higher FTP. General aerobic riding maintains VO2max; specific VO2max intervals are needed only to push above current capacity.

**Recommendations:**
- Combine FTP training with VO2max work and endurance volume; do not rely on any single modality
- TTE (time-to-exhaustion at FTP) is often a more actionable lever than FTP wattage — target extending TTE from 35 minutes toward 60+ minutes
- Progress threshold intervals by extending time-in-zone: 2x20 to 2x25 to 2x30 to longer single efforts
- When TTE at FTP reaches approximately 60 minutes, shift training emphasis to VO2max intervals to push FTP higher
- Check the FTP-to-VO2max ratio; when FTP is 80-82% of VO2max watts, VO2max work is likely the priority
- Diagnose your race limiter before choosing what to train: dropped on early fresh climbs suggests sprint/anaerobic capacity; blowing up late suggests endurance/FTP

**Sources:** Episodes 1333403119, 1514086555, 1199788219, 918048055, 1087518463, 1211797429, 1301979604

## VO2max Blocks Require Pre-Readiness and Far More Recovery Than Athletes Expect

Before beginning a dedicated VO2max block, athletes should be at or near their previously-established aerobic fitness — ideally achieved via a period of higher-than-normal endurance volume. Jumping into VO2max work immediately after time off accumulates fatigue faster than adaptation. A good winter of low-intensity endurance training can produce TTEs of 65-85 minutes on the first spring test, which is exactly the time to begin a VO2max block. After a block, the recovery window is 2-8 weeks to see full FTP improvements, not 3-4 days. This post-block lag is poorly studied but consistently observed in coaching data. Athletes who test immediately after a block frequently see no improvement and mistakenly conclude the block failed — improvements appear immediately only about 40% of the time. During a VO2max block, the key variable is intensity, not duration: aim for 15-20 total interval minutes per session (12-15 to start), with individual intervals ranging from 2 to 8 minutes. Going beyond approximately 23 total minutes per session shows no additional benefit. Structure blocks as 7-12 workouts across 2-3 weeks. High cadence during VO2max intervals reduces muscular fatigue and is particularly recommended for double-day sessions. A mixed block (1 VO2max + 1 threshold per week) is valid when pure VO2max work stalls.

**Recommendations:**
- Build back to baseline aerobic fitness via endurance riding before starting a VO2max block
- Structure blocks with 2 hard sessions per week; allow 2-3 weeks recovery before expecting full benefits
- After a block, allow 1-2+ weeks of easy riding before testing; expect the full improvement window to extend 2-8 weeks
- Target 15-20 total interval minutes per session; start "not quite so hard" on the first interval when fresh
- Check for improvement signs 2-3 weeks into a VO2max block; if nothing is shifting, the stimulus may not be appropriate
- Do not begin a second VO2max block before full recovery from the first

**Sources:** Episodes 1514086555, 1199788219, 1270352152, 918048055, 1087518463, 1211797429

## Progressive Overload and Stimulus Proxies Differ by Training Domain — Fatigue Is a Byproduct, Not a Goal

A critical insight is that the mechanism of progressive overload varies by training domain, and the proxies for evaluating training quality must be type-specific. For threshold/sweet spot work, progressive overload is achieved by extending time-in-zone within a single session — progressing from 2x20 to 2x25 to 2x30 to longer efforts. Crucially, this time must be accumulated within a single ride; splitting it between morning and afternoon sessions does not count as the same stimulus. For VO2max work, the stimulus is intensity (analogous to load in weight training), not duration — 15-20 minutes of total interval time per session is sufficient. For endurance training, overload comes from extending ride duration until a ceiling is reached, then increasing power at the same duration. The type-specific proxies are: endurance measured by cumulative volume and feeling easy; threshold identified by the combination of significant leg fatigue and labored but controlled breathing; VO2max confirmed when breathing is the primary limiter; anaerobic capacity recognized by concentric leg failure rather than breathing. TSS is specifically unreliable because it conflates different stimulus types: 100 TSS from a 5-hour endurance ride and 100 TSS from threshold intervals represent completely different physiology. Sprint peak watts are primarily a freshness indicator, not a sprint fitness indicator. Fatigue is a byproduct of training stimulus, not itself a driver of adaptation — seeking fatigue as a goal inverts causality and leads to junk volume. Energy neutral intake (not surplus) is sufficient for endurance adaptation; total training time matters more than kilojoules as a metric.

**Recommendations:**
- For threshold work: extend time-in-zone within a single ride; progress asymmetrically if needed (25 + 20 min before 25 + 25)
- For VO2max work: focus on intensity and reaching "gasping like a fish" effort; do not chase longer intervals or more total time
- For endurance: push duration until it challenges you, then increase power at that duration
- Use type-specific proxies: breathing for VO2max, leg failure for anaerobic, cumulative volume for endurance — not a single metric like TSS
- Interpret low sprint peak watts as a fatigue signal, not a sprint training deficit
- Do not pursue fatigue as a training goal; the stimulus drives adaptation, and fatigue is a constraining side effect
- Track total training time rather than kilojoules for endurance development
- Energy neutral intake is sufficient for endurance adaptation; chronic deficit is the primary risk

**Sources:** Episodes 1199788219, 1270352152, 1333403119, 1514086555, 1087518463, 1211797429, 2024446488, 2219611610

## Steady Zone 3 Is Rarely Optimal — Use Stochastic Tempo and Well-Structured Over-Unders Instead

Steady-state zone 3 riding is almost never prescribed as focused training. It occupies an intensity that accumulates meaningful fatigue without providing a stimulus cleanly targeted at either endurance or threshold adaptation. The one exception is during recovery weeks, where 30-90 minutes of zone 3 is used to re-activate the hormonal working-mode before the next hard block. For aerobic development at moderate intensity, stochastic "breakaway-style" tempo rides are preferred — varying effort naturally with terrain rather than holding steady zone 3 power. These rides serve as effective aerobic workouts (60-90 minutes) while also training repeated accelerations, and Kolie attributes substantial cross-fitness gains over 2-3 weeks from this approach. Over-unders are a useful tool but the common symmetrical approach (2 minutes over / 2 minutes under) is wrong. The correct structure is short overs (30 seconds at 20-30W above FTP) followed by long unders (4-5 minutes at tempo watts). If you are "hanging on by the skin of your teeth" during the under portion, the prescription is too hard. Low-cadence overs can obligate recruitment of larger motor units but are appropriate primarily for athletes with a specific motor unit recruitment deficit, not as a default. For highly trained athletes whose LT1 naturally sits at 80-85% FTP, the zone 3 descriptor may not even apply meaningfully — Coggan's training zones were designed to be descriptive rather than prescriptive.

**Recommendations:**
- Replace steady zone 3 rides with stochastic tempo rides that vary effort naturally with terrain
- Reserve steady zone 3 for recovery weeks only (30-90 minutes at a wide power range) as an activation tool
- Structure over-unders as 30s over / 4-5 min under at tempo, not symmetrical durations
- Aim for approximately 20-30 minutes total time over threshold in an over-under session; start with 2 sets and build to 4
- Include short openers (high-intensity efforts) in the days coming out of rest weeks to avoid rest inertia
- Use low-cadence overs only for athletes with a specific large motor unit recruitment deficit

**Sources:** Episodes 918048055, 952643710, 1087518463, 1211797429

## Traditional "Base Training" Is Literally False but Metaphorically True — The Real Benefit Is Recovery

The conventional wisdom that cyclists must spend months doing low-intensity zone 2 riding to "build a base" before starting intervals is reframed as a useful fiction. The actual benefit of a traditional base season is rest and recovery from the prior season, not the development of a new aerobic foundation through zone 2 specificity. A person with a "big base" has simply done a lot of focused aerobic work at all intensities over a career — not a lot of zone 2 miles specifically. The pyramid metaphor ("bigger base, higher peak") is replaced with a "food plate" model where threshold, VO2max, and endurance work all contribute. There is no evidence that athletes must complete months of zone 2 before doing threshold intervals; intervals can begin earlier as long as recovery is managed. The physiological claims behind "you must ride easy to build capillaries" are incorrect, but the practice of easy riding in the off-season still produces real rest and recovery benefits.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not delay interval training for months of mandatory zone 2; begin intervals when recovery allows
- Build your longest ride up to the duration of your longest race (Friel recommendation endorsed)
- Use endurance rides with a negative split: start at lower power and build throughout
- To make endurance rides effective, they must challenge your endurance limit — if 4 hours at 150 W is comfortable, target 4.25-4.5 hours

**Sources:** Episodes 625992387, 1396150891

## Most Cycling Events Are Far More Aerobic Than Assumed — Anaerobic Capacity and Sprint Work Are Polish

Cyclists systematically overestimate the anaerobic demands of their racing. Even criterium racing, punchy hill climbs, and track endurance events (5-20 minutes) are primarily aerobic. A rider who gets dropped on a short climb almost always has an aerobic power problem, not an anaerobic capacity problem. The energy contribution math supports this: at a true 1-minute all-out effort, approximately 50% of energy is anaerobic, but by 4-5 minutes the contribution is overwhelmingly aerobic. Even VLAmax declining from aerobic training is a feature, not a bug, for criterium racers — replenishment of phosphocreatine between surges is nearly entirely aerobic. Anaerobic work capacity should be treated as finishing touches: muscle mass and peak power are the true long-term limiters, not year-round anaerobic intervals. Year-round anaerobic training leads to burnout without gains beyond what a focused 4-6 week pre-race block provides. Natural sprinters often need more aerobic training and less sprint-specific work — "you're not a different species." The minimum effective dose of sprint work is often quite small for natural sprinters; the priority should be finding that minimum dose needed for good legs on race day rather than maximizing sprint volume. Fat oxidation at threshold is more common than assumed — TTE extension is driven in part by improved fat oxidation through training-based adaptation, which does not impair carbohydrate utilization. Dietary fat adaptation (keto/LCHF), by contrast, suppresses carbohydrate use and is detrimental for high-intensity performance.

**Recommendations:**
- If you get dropped in crits or on punchy climbs, raise FTP through aerobic development rather than adding anaerobic interval sessions
- Build anaerobic capacity long-term through off-season strength training (squats, deadlifts) to increase muscle mass, not through year-round intervals
- Limit focused anaerobic capacity work to 4-6 weeks before target races
- If you are a natural sprinter, focus on aerobic development and find the minimum dose of sprint-specific work that keeps your sprint sharp
- Separate sprint sessions from aerobic sessions rather than tacking sprints onto hard aerobic workouts
- For repeatability deficits, use specific intervals (20-30s max efforts with 2-3 min rest, 4-6 reps)
- Do not adopt keto/LCHF diets for performance — training-based fat adaptation supports longer TTE without dietary manipulation

**Sources:** Episodes 918048055, 1087518463, 1211797429, 1316155894, 952643710

## Time-Crunched Athletes Should Prioritize Recovery Quality and Individualization Over Workout Optimization

For athletes with 4-6 hours per week, the binding constraint is not workout selection but recovery capacity and life stress. Training hard on top of high psychological stress produces poor-quality efforts and may not generate the peripheral adaptive signals needed for adaptation. Sleep accounts for the vast majority of recovery; consistently sacrificing sleep for extra training time compounds into a deficit faster than expected. The recommended approach is 2-3 hard sessions per week with full recovery between them, rather than 5 moderate sessions where recovery is never complete. RPE decoupling — when a normally manageable workout feels like grinding through at 9/10 — is a key signal of off-bike stress or inadequate recovery and should trigger a back-off. The "it always depends" refrain is not evasion but reflects genuine individual variability: tapering responses differ (some athletes need hard legs going into races, others respond to classical rest tapers), VO2max block durations vary (2-3 weeks to 8 weeks), and recovery timelines from blocks and races vary enormously. FTP does not change day-to-day under fatigue for most athletes; TTE contracts instead (e.g., from 50 to 30 minutes). If workouts feel like someone else's FTP, the athlete needs rest, not a reduced target. Two-a-day workouts are valid for volume accumulation when single long rides are impossible.

**Recommendations:**
- Manage life stress and protect sleep before optimizing training; 98-99.5% of recovery is eating and sleeping
- Favor 2-3 hard sessions with full recovery days over 5 moderate sessions
- Watch for RPE decoupling as a signal to back off; do not attempt "makeup" workouts
- Two-a-days are valid for volume accumulation when long single rides are impossible
- Experiment with taper strategies early in the season to identify your individual pattern before target races
- When performance feels unusually bad, suspect fatigue-compressed TTE before lowering your FTP target
- Prioritize sleep and nutrition as primary recovery tools; recovery gadgets (ice baths, compression, cupping) are marginal

**Sources:** Episodes 1270352152, 1514086555, 1301979604, 1211797429, 952643710

## Evolution of Views

Kolie's training philosophy has evolved substantially across the podcast's lifespan, with several clear trajectories and explicit course corrections.

**On training volume (progressive hardening).** In the earlier Watts Doc episode (625992387), the emphasis was on reframing "base" away from mandatory zone 2 and toward a mix of all intensities. By the 300K special episode (1270352152), Kolie stated explicitly that "volume is more and more the key driver of continuous aerobic adaptation" with each year of coaching. By the training myths episode (1560449302), his position had solidified further: year-on-year improvement at volumes below approximately 14-15 hours/week is rare for most athletes, and the typical response to stalled FTP should be increased volume rather than more intensity. The 2020 pandemic year (referenced in 1211797429) was pivotal: with no racing to interrupt aerobic development, Kolie observed unconstrained aerobic progression in his athletes, solidifying his conviction that racing-driven taper phases chronically interrupt aerobic momentum.

**On endurance ride pacing (power to RPE).** Earlier episodes suggest zone 2 could be paced by power zones, but by the 2020 and 2021 AMAs (952643710, 1211797429), Kolie had evolved firmly toward RPE-based endurance pacing after observing athletes accumulate excessive fatigue at "the top of Zone 2." The Watts Doc episodes on heart rate (732563563) and the VO2 slow component (814478485) provided the mechanistic justification for this shift. He flagged in episode 1396150891 that his views on zone 2 are still forming: "between now and whenever I've thought about it for a year or two, you're going to see an evolution of thought." He has intentionally used "endurance training" rather than "zone 2" to avoid prescription-by-zone-number. Steady zone 3 was progressively de-emphasized, replaced by stochastic tempo riding (918048055).

**On VO2max block methodology (increasing sophistication).** Earlier practice mixed VO2max and threshold work concurrently. By 2021 (1211797429), Kolie preferred isolated VO2max blocks when the calendar allowed, finding it easier to attribute gains and manage fatigue, though mixed blocks remain common when time is compressed. The post-VO2max-block recovery window was updated based on 2023 coaching data (1514086555): recovery times of 2-8 weeks were observed, a significant shift from more optimistic prior assumptions. Kolie also acknowledged that his earlier approach of setting aggressive FTP targets for athletes was naive.

**On periodization (rigid to adaptive).** The Ten Minute Tips episodes (1301979604, 1316155894) show a clear trajectory away from multi-week advance programming toward shorter 3-4 week planning windows with daily adjustment based on athlete feedback and data. Preset periodization templates are increasingly treated as starting frameworks rather than rigid plans.

**On the sweet spot vs. polarized debate.** Kolie noted in episode 1199788219 that he had "not followed Seiler's polarized model since it was very popular a couple of years ago," suggesting a move away from polarized as a default framework. Low-cadence over-unders, used heavily for several years, fell out of favor as a default prescription by the Instagram/Reddit AMA (1087518463), remaining in the toolbox only for specific motor unit recruitment deficits.

**On measurement tools.** Lab testing moved from "probably useful for most athletes" to "not important for most amateurs" (952643710), as power-meter analysis proved sufficient. The cumulative coaching philosophy shift: early episodes lean on power targets and structured protocols; later episodes increasingly advocate RPE-based prescription, type-specific stimulus proxies, and skepticism toward single-number metrics like TSS.
