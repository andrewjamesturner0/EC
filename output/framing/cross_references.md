- FTP sits at the confluence of multiple molecular adaptation pathways -- AMPK, redox signaling, calcium, and glycogen depletion (Section 1) -- which is why direct threshold work (Section 3) is uniquely potent and cannot be substituted by VO2max intervals or sweet spot alone (Section 4).
- VO2max interval methodology (Section 2) depends on the cardiac preload and stroke volume principles explained in Exercise Physiology (Section 1); high cadence during VO2max work maximizes diastolic filling, which is the mechanism behind eccentric cardiac hypertrophy.
- The phosphocreatine system (Section 1) explains why FTP/threshold training improves repeated sprint ability (Section 5) and criterium performance (Section 12) -- PCr resynthesis is aerobic, linking aerobic fitness to sprint recovery.
- The AMPK and glycogen signaling research (Section 1) directly invalidates fasted training and low-carb protocols discussed in Nutrition (Section 8) -- end-exercise glycogen, not starting glycogen, activates the adaptation signal, so fueling during rides is compatible with adaptation.
- Progressive overload through extending time-in-zone (Section 3, Section 5) is the practical application of the calcium-driven mitochondrial biogenesis pathway (Section 1), where total contraction duration -- not intensity -- drives the calcium signal.
- Recovery and periodization prescriptions (Section 6) are grounded in the sirtuin/NAD+ biochemistry of Section 1: recovery is when ETC proteins are imported into expanding mitochondrial reticulum, and chronic overtraining degrades the sirtuin proteins needed for adaptation.
- The strength training periodization model (Section 7) accounts for the concurrent training interference effect (mTOR vs. PGC-1alpha pathways from Section 1), which is why heavy lifting is concentrated in the off-season and reduced to maintenance during race-specific blocks.
- Nutrition recommendations for aggressive carbohydrate intake (Section 8) are supported by the substrate oxidation research in Section 1 showing that fat and carbohydrate converge on the same aerobic pathway, making substrate burned during exercise irrelevant to adaptation.
- The psychology of rest (Section 9) addresses the behavioral barriers to implementing the recovery prescriptions of Section 6 -- rest must be positively reinforced as a skill, not treated as the absence of training.
- Coaching practice (Section 10) operationalizes the research methodology warnings of Section 13: every training plan is an n=1 experiment, population averages do not predict individual responses, and subjective feedback is primary data alongside power and heart rate.
- The RPE-based intensity prescription advocated across Sections 2, 3, 4, and 5 reflects a cumulative shift away from FTP-percentage zones, supported by the finding (Section 3) that FTP cannot reliably set zones across the full intensity spectrum.
- Equipment and aerodynamic optimization (Section 11) interacts with race tactics (Section 12): Bianchi's road-position aerodynamics and Lockwood's TT position principles show that watts saved through positioning compound with the energy management and tactical skills that determine race outcomes.
- The VLAmax critique (Section 1) directly supports the racing advice in Section 12 that sprint training does not impair FTP -- the proposed trade-off is mechanistically unsound, and sprinter-climbers at the elite level disprove it empirically.
- The diminishing-returns curve for training adaptations (Section 13) explains why intermediate athletes plateau (Section 6) and why VO2max blocks require pre-readiness and extended recovery windows (Section 4) -- short-term study results exaggerate differences that vanish on the long-term logarithmic growth curve.
