# Empirical Cycling Podcast: Training Knowledge Base

## Executive Summary

The most important cross-cutting findings from approximately 180 episodes of the Empirical Cycling Podcast are: (1) Most cyclists ride too hard on easy days and do not rest enough -- reducing endurance ride intensity is the single highest-ROI intervention for the majority of athletes, often producing meaningful FTP improvements when combined with appropriate hard training. (2) FTP improvement requires three concurrent inputs: direct threshold work (extending time-to-exhaustion, not just wattage), VO2max intervals (to raise the ceiling), and sufficient aerobic volume (14-15+ hours/week for consistent year-on-year gains). (3) Fuel aggressively with carbohydrates on the bike (80-120 g/hr when racing) regardless of intensity or substrate being burned; fasted training and ketogenic diets impair performance without improving adaptation. (4) Recovery is biochemically required for adaptation -- plan rest weeks proactively on a 3:1 or 2:1 schedule rather than waiting for breakdown, and treat off-bike stress as real training load. (5) Prescribe VO2max intervals by maximum effort and breathing response, not as a percentage of FTP, and use high cadence (100-120 RPM) to maximize cardiac preload. (6) Training adaptations follow logarithmic curves with diminishing returns; individual variation typically exceeds group-mean effects in studies, making every training plan an n=1 experiment that requires iterative adjustment. (7) Strength training transfers best through single-leg work in the 4-6 rep range, periodized with cycling and maintained at minimal dose in-season.

## 1. Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation

### Lactate Is Continuously Produced at All Intensities and Serves as an Active Fuel

Lactate production is an unavoidable consequence of glycolysis, not something that switches on at a threshold. Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) catalyzes the conversion of pyruvate to lactate with an equilibrium constant of approximately 1.62 x 10^11 in favor of lactate, meaning that whenever pyruvate exists, lactate is produced in overwhelming proportion. At rest the lactate-to-pyruvate ratio is roughly 10:1; during intense exercise it can exceed 500:1. The critical function of this reaction is regenerating NAD+ from NADH so that glycolysis can continue — without it, glycolysis would halt in under a second during high demand. Lactate is also an active fuel: the heart preferentially oxidizes lactate, and working muscles, the brain, and other tissues consume it via mitochondrial oxidation back to pyruvate. Trained cyclists produce more lactate at the same absolute intensity than untrained cyclists but clear and utilize it at dramatically higher rates (34-97% higher clearance depending on intensity). Lower blood lactate in trained athletes reflects superior utilization, not reduced production. The term "anaerobic threshold" (per Wasserman's 1964 hypothesis) implies that lactate accumulation signals the electron transport chain reaching its oxygen limit, but oxygen is nowhere in the lactate production reaction. FTP can occur at 60-90% of VO2max depending on the athlete, further demonstrating that the lactate inflection point is not a fixed oxygen-availability threshold. What actually happens above FTP is that glycolytic rate exceeds mitochondrial capacity to process pyruvate, causing accelerating lactate accumulation — a threshold of enzymatic flux capacity, not oxygen availability.

**Recommendations:**
- Replace the mental model of "anaerobic threshold" with the concept of maximal sustainable oxidative phosphorylation rate (FTP)
- Stop designing training around "clearing lactate" — blood lactate at a single time point reflects production, transport, and clearance simultaneously; power data captures training-relevant information more cleanly
- Recognize that "lactate tolerance" workouts train high-power glycolytic capacity and phosphocreatine resynthesis, not tolerance to a toxin

### Fat and Carbohydrate Oxidation Converge on the Same Aerobic Pathway — The Distinction Is Rate of ATP Delivery

The common framing of "fat = aerobic fuel, carbs = anaerobic fuel" is mechanistically wrong. Beta-oxidation contains no gaseous O2 in any of its steps. Glycolysis contains no O2 in its ten steps. The Krebs cycle contains no O2. Oxygen appears only at complex IV of the electron transport chain, where it accepts electrons to form water. The ETC cannot distinguish whether the NADH and FADH2 it receives came from fat or carbohydrate. When fully oxidized, glucose is approximately 88% aerobic (30 of 34 ATP from oxidative phosphorylation) and palmitate approximately 98% aerobic (100 of 102 ATP). The real difference is rate: fat yields more total ATP per molecule (102 vs. 34) but delivers it far more slowly due to transport across two mitochondrial membranes, the CoA priming requirement, and the sequential nature of beta-oxidation cycles. Fat oxidation is further rate-limited by multi-step transport machinery: hormones trigger lipolysis via HSL in adipose tissue, free fatty acids travel through blood, and the CD36/FAT transporter imports them into muscle cells and mitochondria. A second bottleneck exists at CPT1 and the carnitine shuttle. At high intensities, free carnitine becomes scarce because it is consumed buffering acetyl groups as acetylcarnitine, competitively limiting CPT1 regardless of fat availability. This means fat oxidation declines sharply at high intensities due to a transport bottleneck, not substrate competition within the mitochondria. At moderate intensities (~65% VO2max), fat contribution rises progressively from roughly 30% to 50% of energy over 2 hours as muscle glycogen depletes and signaling shifts.

**Recommendations:**
- Abandon the mental model that fat burning is "aerobic" and carb burning is "anaerobic" — both converge on the same aerobic pathway
- At higher intensities, carbohydrate dominance is about ATP production rate, not a metabolic "switch"
- At FTP, fat can contribute 30-50%+ of total energy; do not assume threshold work is purely carbohydrate-fueled
- Train to increase mitochondrial volume to expand CPT1 activity and CD36 surface area — this is how fat oxidation capacity improves

### Training Adaptation Is Fundamentally About Maintaining ATP Far From Equilibrium Under Greater Demand

The energy available from ATP hydrolysis comes not from the phosphate bond itself but from how far the cell holds the ATP/ADP ratio away from chemical equilibrium. Cells maintain ATP at approximately 10^10 times above equilibrium concentration; the standard free energy of hydrolysis is 30.5 kJ/mol, but under cellular conditions it reaches 50-60 kJ/mol because of this extreme disequilibrium. Every energy system — phosphocreatine, glycolysis, oxidative phosphorylation — exists to defend this ratio. Glycolysis operates in the cytosol (not the mitochondria), enabling near-instantaneous ATP production in response to sudden demand, independent of oxygen delivery. Three irreversible regulatory steps make glycolysis highly responsive to the cell's energy status. This is the mechanistic basis for sustaining power above FTP during surges: cytosolic glycolysis can ramp up nearly instantaneously relative to oxidative phosphorylation. However, the 2-ATP-per-glucose yield of glycolysis is far less efficient than the 34-ATP yield of full oxidation, meaning glycogen is consumed at dramatically higher rates above FTP. Oxygen delivery is the rate-limiting step of the aerobic system: the electron transport chain pumps protons across the inner mitochondrial membrane, creating a gradient that drives ATP synthase (a physical rotary motor spinning at up to 7,800 RPM). Oxygen partial pressure in working muscle remains nearly constant at 3-4% of atmospheric pressure even during intense exercise; what changes is the flux of oxygen through the tissue, which can increase up to 100-fold. This makes oxygen delivery — not oxygen pressure — the bottleneck.

**Recommendations:**
- Frame training adaptations as improvements in the rate of ATP regeneration under load, not simply "producing more energy"
- Recognize that above-FTP capacity depends on glycolytic rate and glycogen availability — fueling strategies should account for the dramatically higher carbohydrate cost of repeated surges
- Long-term aerobic development (capillary density, mitochondrial volume, hemoglobin mass) increases the ceiling of sustainable oxidative phosphorylation — this is what FTP represents physiologically
- Prioritize training adaptations that increase oxygen delivery: sustained aerobic training for capillarization, cardiac output improvements, and hemoglobin mass development

### Phosphocreatine Is an Aerobic Energy System That Links Threshold Fitness to Sprint Recovery

Phosphocreatine (PCr) functions as both a temporal buffer (providing immediate ATP when demand spikes) and a spatial shuttle (transporting energy from mitochondria to myofibrils via a creatine kinase "conveyor belt"). PCr is approximately 20 mM in muscle (4x resting ATP) and diffuses 25% faster than ATP, making it the primary rapid-delivery mechanism during steady-state aerobic exercise, not just during sprints. Mice lacking the creatine kinase system run only 10% as far and compensate with a 3-fold increase in citrate synthase (a mitochondrial mass marker) and a 10-fold increase in cytochrome oxidase (complex IV), illustrating the system's fundamental importance to aerobic function. Aerobic training — particularly FTP/threshold work — significantly improves PCr resynthesis rate, which directly translates to better repeated sprint ability. The resynthesis half-life ranges from 21-57 seconds, with trained athletes trending toward the shorter end. A 30-second sprint depletes PCr by 60-80%, meaning rest intervals need 2-3 minutes for near-full restoration. In a study of 8 x 30 m sprints, groups with 20-second versus 120-second rest intervals showed similar blood lactate accumulation, but only the 120-second rest group maintained sprint times — the performance decrement was attributable to incomplete PCr resynthesis, not lactate. Lactate itself has been shown in isolated rat muscle to restore force production under fatiguing conditions.

**Recommendations:**
- Use FTP and threshold training to improve PCr resynthesis rate, which directly benefits repeated sprint performance in races
- Design sprint interval rest periods around PCr recovery needs (2-3 minutes for near-complete resynthesis), not around lactate clearance
- Keep recovery intervals between hard sprint efforts truly passive — active recovery impairs PCr restoration
- Creatine supplementation (~5g/day) increases total PCr by ~20% but causes ~4-6 lb weight gain — likely not net beneficial for endurance cyclists where power-to-weight matters

### More Mitochondria Is the Primary Driver of Endurance Adaptation — Substrate Selection Is Irrelevant

Mitochondrial volume expansion — not which fuel you burn — determines endurance capacity. A study comparing 0g/hr vs 90g/hr carbohydrate intake during 3 hours at LT1 found identical AMPK and P53 signaling regardless of substrate oxidized, yet the carb-fed group performed dramatically better on a subsequent time trial. Trained athletes utilize roughly 2.5x more fat at comparable intensities than untrained individuals — not because of dietary manipulation but because they have more mitochondria, more CD36 transporters, and better lipolytic capacity. Obese individuals' poor fat oxidation is likewise a mitochondrial quantity problem, not an intrinsic metabolic defect. Untrained subjects show rapid metabolic shifts (60% reduction in glycogen use rate after just 3 days of 2-hour endurance training), and initial VO2max gains are primarily hematological: a study of 16 untrained men over 6 weeks found peak cardiac output correlated R=0.84 with VO2max improvement, while phlebotomy after training returned VO2max essentially to baseline. Mitochondrial volume density increased 40% over the same period but did not predict VO2max improvement. In trained athletes, peripheral muscle adaptations improve fractional utilization — the ability to sustain a high fraction of VO2max — but do not raise the VO2max ceiling. Greater capillary density allows longer oxygen exposure time per red blood cell pass, and these adaptations correlate with higher FTP-to-VO2max ratios.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize training volume at aerobic intensities to drive mitochondrial biogenesis — the substrate burned during training is irrelevant to adaptation magnitude
- Do not manipulate dietary fat intake to improve fat oxidation; increased mitochondrial density handles this automatically
- For untrained athletes, almost any consistent training produces rapid VO2max gains — do not obsess over protocol optimization
- Use sustained moderate-high intensity volume to improve capillary density and fractional utilization; to raise the VO2max ceiling itself, target cardiac output with VO2max intervals

### AMPK Activation Requires Genuinely Hard Work in Trained Athletes and Targets End-Exercise Glycogen

AMPK is a primary signal for mitochondrial biogenesis, activated by three distinct mechanisms: absolute exercise intensity (ATP disequilibrium), low baseline training load (untrained athletes respond more), and end-exercise glycogen depletion. The AMPK beta subunit contains a highly conserved glycogen-binding domain that directly inhibits AMPK when glycogen is bound; when glycogen depletes during exercise, AMPK is released from this structural inhibition. This is a direct molecular interaction, not an indirect effect mediated through energy status. The practical distinction is crucial: starting glycogen level (fed vs. fasted) does not predict AMPK activation; only end-exercise glycogen matters. Well-trained athletes riding 200-400 km/week generate no measurable AMPK signal from 2 hours at 65% VO2max — their mitochondria maintain ATP balance too efficiently. Untrained athletes show roughly 5x greater AMPK and AMP accumulation at the same relative intensity. AMPK activity decreases substantially after 7-10 days of regular training at a given load, meaning the same workout stops producing adaptation signals quickly. Fasted training has zero evidence for increasing AMPK or mitochondrial biogenesis in trained athletes: identical AMPK and P53 signaling occurs between carb-fed and fasted groups at LT1, with the carb-fed group performing dramatically better afterward. The hypothesis sounds plausible but targets the wrong variable (starting vs. ending glycogen), and trained athletes' mitochondria are too efficient for moderate-intensity fasted rides to create meaningful energetic stress.

**Recommendations:**
- Structure hard sessions so glycogen is low by the end — this is the adaptation trigger, not starting in a depleted state
- Fuel early and mid-ride with carbohydrates to maintain performance; the AMPK signal comes from where glycogen ends up, not where it starts
- Well-trained athletes must use genuinely high absolute intensity to activate AMPK — moderate endurance rides alone are insufficient
- Periodize training load and progress volume regularly; repeating the same stimulus beyond 7-10 days produces diminishing AMPK response
- Do not use fasted training as an AMPK strategy — the mechanism does not support it, and performance suffers without benefit to signaling

### ROS and Calcium Drive Parallel Adaptation Pathways — Both Are Self-Limiting in Trained Athletes

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) and calcium signaling represent two parallel but mechanistically distinct pathways to mitochondrial biogenesis, and both exhibit diminishing returns with training status. Approximately 1-2% of electrons leak from the electron transport chain as superoxide at rest, potentially rising to 10% during strenuous exercise. These ROS serve as signaling molecules that activate PGC-1alpha. High-intensity training drives aerobic adaptation in untrained athletes through a specific ROS mechanism: superoxide fragments ryanodine receptors (RyR), causing calcium leak that elevates resting intracellular calcium by ~40%, activating CAMK2 to PGC-1alpha to mitochondrial biogenesis. However, elite trained athletes have approximately 2x higher antioxidant capacity (superoxide dismutase, catalase) compared to recreational athletes, which prevents RyR fragmentation. HIT-driven aerobic signaling plateaus after approximately 7 sessions in trained athletes. The paradox is that the very adaptation HIT produces (improved antioxidant capacity) prevents it from working further through this pathway. Separately, calcium released from the sarcoplasmic reticulum during contraction activates CAMK2 to PGC-1alpha via a duration-dependent mechanism: total duration of calcium exposure — not intensity — is the primary driver. A myotube study showed 5 hours/day of calcium exposure for 5 days produced 2-3x increases in PGC-1alpha. Megadosing antioxidant supplements (1,000 mg vitamin C plus 235 mg vitamin E) can suppress the ROS adaptive signal, while moderate dietary amounts do not blunt adaptations.

**Recommendations:**
- For untrained athletes, HIT is highly effective for aerobic adaptation — the ROS signaling pathway is fully active
- For well-trained athletes, do not rely on HIT as a primary aerobic development tool — the pathway saturates after ~7 sessions
- Prioritize ride duration as a lever for calcium-driven mitochondrial biogenesis — longer single rides may provide qualitatively different stimulus than equivalent total duration split across rides
- Avoid megadosing vitamin C (above ~500 mg) and vitamin E (above ~400 IU) during training blocks focused on aerobic adaptation; obtain antioxidants from dietary sources

### PGC-1alpha Is Not the Master Regulator It Was Believed to Be — HIF-1 Signaling Is Similarly Overstated

Despite its reputation as the "master regulator" of mitochondrial biogenesis, PGC-1alpha is dispensable for exercise-induced adaptation. Skeletal-muscle-specific PGC-1alpha knockout mice show the same exercise adaptation as wild-type controls, and even double PGC-1alpha + beta knockout mice show a ~70% performance deficit (performing at only 30% of control levels) while still adapting normally to training. Alternative pathways (PRC, AKT, p53, p38) provide redundancy. PGC-1alpha protein has a short half-life (~2.5 hours, constitutively ubiquitin-tagged), and mRNA transcript levels are non-linearly related to protein levels, which are themselves non-linearly related to performance — meaning published studies using PGC-1alpha mRNA as a surrogate for "training adaptation quality" draw unreliable conclusions. Similarly, hypoxia-inducible factor 1 (HIF-1) activates when muscle O2 tension drops during intense exercise, upregulating glycolytic enzymes, VEGF for capillary formation, and nitric oxide signaling. But trained athletes accumulate HIF-1 negative regulators: elite athletes had 2.6x more PHD2 (which tags HIF-1 for degradation), 3.5x more FIH, and 5x more SIRT2/SIRT6 compared to controls. The HIF transcriptional signature attenuates rapidly — glycolysis gene expression was absent after just 9 training sessions. A striking HIF-1alpha knockout mouse study produced animals with a seemingly "pre-trained" phenotype (elevated type 1 fibers, higher capillary density, higher citrate synthase) but identical endurance performance to untrained wild-type mice, demonstrating that favorable phenotype does not guarantee performance.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not interpret PGC-1alpha mRNA data from training studies as reliable indicators of adaptation magnitude
- Do not attempt to "hack" HIF signaling (e.g., NIRS-guided desaturation work) expecting aerobic gains in trained athletes — the pathway is already substantially downregulated
- Use performance testing (time trials, power tests) rather than biomarkers or phenotype measurements to gauge training progress
- Progressive overload and recovery remain the practical prescription — no mechanistic shortcut from manipulating any single signaling pathway is supported

### Recovery Is Biochemically Required for Mitochondrial Construction — Apparent Mid-Block "Dysfunction" Is Normal Remodeling

Adaptation signals (SIRT1, PGC-1alpha) peak approximately 3 hours post-exercise and remain elevated up to 48 hours after the final session in a hard training block. The actual construction of new mitochondria — synthesizing membranes, proteins, and mtDNA — requires energy, dietary fat, dietary protein, and time during rest. The NAD+/NADH ratio rises during exercise, activating sirtuins (SIRT1 in cytoplasm/nucleus, SIRT3 in mitochondria) which drive PGC-1alpha and mitochondrial biogenesis. But chronic training without adequate rest causes sirtuin protein levels to drop 20-30%, creating a destructive negative feedback loop: the very signaling proteins needed for adaptation are degraded by overtraining. A key study (Flockhart et al.) that progressively increased HIT load to 5 sessions/week found that mitochondrial maximal respiration per unit mass dropped from ~15 to ~7 pmol/s/ug — yet VO2max continued rising and performance merely stagnated. Proteomics revealed the mechanism: during rapid mitochondrial mass expansion, the reticulum expands in volume first, but electron transport chain proteins are deprioritized during this phase. Cristae formation and ETC protein import machinery peak during recovery. This non-stoichiometric change — volume growing faster than ETC protein density — explains apparent "dysfunction" that resolves during taper. Hawley and Bishop's reanalysis found citrate synthase-normalized respiration was not significantly impaired.

**Recommendations:**
- Build in recovery weeks — taper/recovery is when ETC protein import peaks and functional adaptation consolidates
- Do not interpret mid-block fatigue or stagnation as "overtraining syndrome" requiring dramatic intervention — it is likely normal mitochondrial remodeling
- Eat adequate protein and fat after training to provide substrates for mitochondrial membrane and protein synthesis
- Monitor for signs of chronic overtraining: if adaptation stalls despite consistent training, inadequate recovery may be degrading sirtuin protein content
- Avoid alcohol post-workout as it disrupts sleep and likely disrupts NAD+ metabolism during the critical biosynthetic window

### FTP Is Uniquely Potent Because It Activates Multiple Adaptation Pathways Simultaneously

FTP sits at the confluence of AMPK activation (via ATP disequilibrium), redox signaling (NAD+/NADH ratio shift activating sirtuins), calcium signaling (sustained contraction duration), and glycogen depletion (releasing AMPK from beta-subunit inhibition). "Spicy endurance" efforts between LT1 and FTP combine both AMPK energy-stress signaling and calcium signaling at a sustainable intensity — a dual stimulus that makes threshold work uniquely potent for mitochondrial biogenesis when duration is adequate. Time to exhaustion at FTP (TTE) is often a better training target than chasing higher peak FTP numbers: extending from 30 minutes to 45 minutes at threshold drives more adaptation than raising FTP by a few watts for shorter durations. Well-trained athletes still need dedicated VO2max-intensity intervals to continue improving stroke volume and cardiac output beyond what threshold work achieves — pure zone 2 or low-intensity endurance work does not produce further stroke volume improvements once a high training status is reached. Stroke volume rises linearly from ~130 mL at 90 BPM to ~190 mL at 180 BPM in trained athletes, driven by eccentric cardiac hypertrophy. The World Tour benchmark of LT1 at 80-85% of FTP (vs. 50-60% for well-trained amateurs) illustrates that the primary gain from serious endurance training is raising LT1 relative to FTP, not raising FTP itself.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize extending TTE at FTP over chasing higher peak FTP — duration at threshold drives more adaptation
- Include regular FTP/sweet-spot sessions to activate the convergence of AMPK, redox, calcium, and glycogen-depletion pathways simultaneously
- Track LT1 as a fraction of FTP as a key fitness metric; aim to raise this ratio through combined volume and threshold training
- Well-trained athletes need dedicated VO2max-intensity intervals to continue improving cardiac output beyond what threshold work provides
- Maintain training consistency — blood volume erodes within 10 days of detraining

### VLAmax Is a Flawed Metric and Fiber Type Does Not Reliably Predict Substrate Use

The VLAmax model, popularized by some coaching platforms, is critiqued as fundamentally measuring sprint power rather than glycolytic capacity. The core claim — that high VLAmax "costs" FTP because it floods the system with lactate — is not supported by evidence. Elite sprinter-climbers (e.g., 2,000W sprint + 400W FTP) directly contradict the predicted trade-off. The model assumes fixed carbohydrate/fat oxidation ratios at given intensities, when in reality substrate use is dynamic and governed by mitochondrial volume. The practical harm is that coaches may avoid sprint training in endurance athletes based on a mechanistically unsound fear of "increasing VLAmax." Similarly, the common slow-twitch = aerobic / fast-twitch = anaerobic dichotomy does not hold in trained athletes. In cross-country skiers, arms (40% type 1 fiber) and legs (58% type 1 fiber) showed nearly identical citrate synthase activity, demonstrating that type 2A fibers match type 1 fibers in oxidative capacity when well-trained. A widely cited Coyle study reporting a 0.91 correlation between fiber type and fat oxidation is misleading: the correlation was actually 0.87 against relative intensity rather than fiber type, meaning training history and exercise intensity are stronger predictors of substrate use than intrinsic fiber composition. Ten to seventy percent of fibers in trained athletes are hybrid types expressing multiple myosin heavy chains. Endurance training develops aerobic capacity across all recruited fiber types regardless of classification.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not use VLAmax as a training metric for endurance programming — it conflates sprint power with glycolytic capacity
- Do not avoid sprint or high-power training out of fear it will reduce FTP — the proposed trade-off is not mechanistically supported
- Do not use fiber type composition as a basis for substrate use or training decisions; relative exercise intensity is a better predictor
- Focus on mitochondrial density as the governing variable for substrate use at any intensity
- High-volume endurance training shifts fiber behavior toward greater aerobic capacity regardless of intrinsic type distribution

### Evolution of Views

The podcast's treatment of exercise physiology and molecular adaptation follows a clear arc across approximately 25 Watts Doc episodes, from descriptive biochemistry toward increasingly specific — and sometimes revisionary — training implications.

**Early episodes (WD6, WD13, WD17) laid biochemical foundations with acknowledged uncertainty.** In WD6, Kolie acknowledged he previously accepted the mechanistic rationale for "lactate tolerance" workouts and only later realized the proposed mechanism was incorrect. By WD17, he explicitly updated from the "semi-correct" explanation that lactate is produced when pyruvate dehydrogenase is overloaded to the thermodynamic/equilibrium view — that LDH's equilibrium so overwhelmingly favors lactate that production is proportional to pyruvate concentration at all times. In WD13, Kolie presented his theory that active recovery works via increased blood flow but explicitly flagged that he may hold a different theory in a year.

**The metabolism series (WD29-33) was built incrementally with acknowledged confidence gaps.** At the end of WD29 (fat oxidation), Kolie noted he was only 85% confident in the upcoming content on rate-limiting aspects of fat transport. By WD32, topics like fat adaptation, ketogenic diets, and VLaMax were explicitly deferred as still under research. The Fick equation episodes (WD20-22) established cardiac output as the primary VO2max limiter and peripheral adaptations as secondary contributors to fractional utilization.

**Mid-series episodes (WD38, WD40) began challenging popular frameworks.** WD38 reframed phosphocreatine as an aerobic system, connecting threshold training to sprint recovery in a non-obvious way. WD40 made the strongest claim — that substrate oxidation during training is irrelevant to adaptation — directly contradicting the fat-adaptation movement and the VLAmax model. The "spicy endurance" framing of FTP emerged here.

**The AMPK deep-dive (WD45, with guest Marinus Peterson) marked a significant shift in specificity.** The episode explicitly stated that fasted training has no evidence base for trained athletes, and that the research community has moved away from the fasted-training-for-AMPK hypothesis. Peterson noted that early mechanistic studies in untrained subjects were over-interpreted. This introduced the critical distinction between absolute and relative intensity for AMPK activation.

The historical scientific record is treated with nuance throughout — the hosts emphasize that original researchers were not foolish, and their errors were understandable given the tools available at the time.**

## 2. VO2max & Oxygen Uptake

### There Is No Single "VO2max Power" — The Number Changes With Every Test Protocol

The concept of a fixed power output corresponding to VO2max is an artifact of ramp test design, not a physiological reality. Research reviewed on the podcast shows that ramp tests with step durations of 30, 60, 90, and 120 seconds all produce the same measured VO2max (3.54–3.56 L/min) but yield final power values spanning 84 watts (282–366 W). This means that Garmin VO2max estimates, "zone 5" power targets, and any training prescribed as a percentage of "VO2max power" or Wmax are built on a number that is fundamentally unstable. Athletes with high anaerobic capacity will show inflated Wmax values because their W' covers the final ramp stages anaerobically, while athletes with low anaerobic capacity will appear to have a lower aerobic ceiling than they actually possess. The relationship between power and oxygen uptake is linear only at or below FTP; above FTP, anaerobic contributions break that linearity. The practical consequence is that VO2max intervals should never be prescribed at a specific power target derived from a ramp test — doing so may systematically under- or over-shoot the intended stimulus depending on the athlete's anaerobic profile and the protocol used.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not use ramp test final power as a VO2max training target — this number varies by up to 25% based on protocol alone.
- To track VO2max progression without a lab, compare two maximal 5-minute efforts under matched conditions with no anaerobic capacity work between tests.
- Design ramp tests to last 8–12 minutes from first hard effort to exhaustion; adjust starting wattage and step size to the athlete's fitness level.
- Treat Garmin and similar watch-based VO2max estimates with skepticism — they use power-to-O2 formulas that do not account for anaerobic contributions.

### Maximize Cardiac Preload During VO2max Intervals With High Cadence and Maximum Effort

The primary long-term driver of VO2max improvement is cardiac stroke volume, developed through eccentric hypertrophy stimulated by maximizing diastolic filling volume (preload). The podcast presents five practical principles for achieving this: increase cadence to 100–120 RPM (or 10–20 RPM above natural hard-effort cadence), go maximum effort rather than targeting power, shorten recovery intervals to the minimum that allows quality, assess heart rate range after the session rather than during, and adjust interval duration to the individual. Each 20 RPM increase in cadence raises stroke volume by approximately 15 mL without meaningfully raising power output, producing greater diastolic filling. The high-cadence prescription is specific to VO2max training — race at your naturally preferred cadence. Athletes who cannot generate meaningful power at 110–120 RPM should accept the power drop, because the cardiac adaptation benefit outweighs lower absolute wattage. ERG mode and structured training apps may actually impede VO2max training by enforcing power targets that constrain the pacing pattern needed for maximum cardiac stimulus.

**Recommendations:**
- Use a cadence of 100–120 RPM (or 10–20 RPM above your preferred hard-effort cadence) during VO2max intervals.
- Prescribe VO2max intervals as maximum effort, not at a percentage of FTP — start 20–30% harder than sustainable, then let power fade while maintaining maximal perceived effort.
- Keep total interval work volume to 20–25 minutes per session; expect 12–15 minutes of high-quality cardiac stimulus.
- Review heart rate data after the session looking for a high, relatively stable plateau near maximum heart rate.
- Remain seated during VO2max intervals — standing lowers cadence, recruits larger motor units, and likely reduces diastolic filling.

### Use Breathing Quality, Not Power Targets, to Gauge VO2max Interval Intensity

Because power output does not correspond to a fixed internal metabolic state — especially above FTP — subjective breathing response is a more reliable field indicator of whether a VO2max interval has reached the intended cardiac stress. "Gasping like a fish in a boat" is the target sensation. If an athlete can complete intervals at a prescribed percentage of FTP while breathing through their nose, they have not reached the right stimulus, regardless of what the power meter reads. Heart rate lags behind effort by a minute or more, making it useful for post-session review but unreliable for real-time pacing. This principle extends beyond VO2max work: across the podcast, there is a consistent shift toward RPE-based prescription, with the host noting that the longer he coaches, the more he relies on perceived effort rather than power targets. The key pacing cue is to accelerate to speed in the first 5–10 seconds, then sustain maximum effort, letting power fade naturally. The first 1–1.5 minutes should bring the athlete into the right cardiac zone. If breathing does not reach maximal intensity, go harder.

**Recommendations:**
- Target ragged, forced breathing as the primary indicator of sufficient VO2max interval intensity.
- If intervals at a given power feel manageable and breathing is not maximal, increase effort regardless of the power number.
- Use heart rate and power data for post-session analysis, not real-time pacing of VO2max intervals.
- Develop RPE calibration over time — it is a trainable skill and becomes more precise than power-based prescription for this type of work.

### The VO2 Slow Component Explains Why Oxygen Uptake Rises Even at Constant Power

During sustained efforts above FTP, oxygen uptake progressively rises because fatiguing slow-twitch motor units are replaced by larger, less efficient fast-twitch units that require more oxygen per unit of force. This is the VO2 slow component. At FTP/MLSS, a 30-minute effort can show VO2 rising from 71.3 to 79.3 mL/kg/min — an 11% increase — while lactate remains stable. This is normal, not a sign of exceeding threshold. A milder version occurs even below FTP during long endurance rides, manifesting as heart rate drift at constant power. Critically, this progressive motor unit recruitment is not pathological — it is likely a key mechanism driving fiber type conversion and aerobic adaptation. Starting a VO2max interval with a hard sprint rather than building gradually exploits this mechanism: maximal sprint starts achieve 100% VO2max in approximately 50–70 seconds versus close to 3 minutes for constant work rate efforts, because the sprint immediately recruits a large fraction of available motor units.

**Recommendations:**
- Allow heart rate to drift upward during long endurance rides at constant power — this reflects beneficial progressive motor unit recruitment, not a metabolic problem.
- For VO2max intervals, start with a hard acceleration to recruit motor units quickly and reach 100% VO2max faster.
- Ensure VO2max intervals are long enough (minimum ~2 minutes) to allow the slow component to drive oxygen uptake to its ceiling — short intervals may never get there.
- Do not interpret rising VO2 at constant power as evidence of exceeding FTP — it is a normal physiological response at and above threshold.

### FTP as a Percentage of VO2max Matters More Than Raw VO2max for Endurance Performance

Two athletes with identical VO2max values can have dramatically different endurance performance based on where their FTP sits relative to VO2max. In the Coyle and Coggan (1988) data, FTP ranged from 59% to 86% of VO2max even within a well-trained cycling population. At 88% of VO2max, the athlete at 86% FTP/VO2max lasted 75 minutes; the athlete at 66.7% lasted only 12 minutes with blood lactate of 18.7 mmol/L. At 80% of VO2max for 30 minutes, glycogen use in low-FTP-ratio athletes was more than double that of high-FTP-ratio athletes (65.4 vs. 27.9 mmol/kg). This means VO2max sets the ceiling, but FTP determines how much of that ceiling is usable. For events longer than approximately 5–8 minutes, FTP is the better predictor of performance. This also means that studies assigning exercise intensity as a fixed percentage of VO2max without accounting for individual FTP position produce completely different physiological states across subjects — making their conclusions suspect for individualized application.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize raising FTP as a percentage of VO2max over chasing raw VO2max numbers for events longer than 5–8 minutes.
- Use FTP, not VO2max percentage, as the primary training anchor for endurance work.
- Be skeptical of research that prescribes intensity as a fixed percentage of VO2max without reporting individual lactate threshold positions.
- At FTP, expect roughly 50/50 carbohydrate/fat oxidation (RER ~0.81–0.86), not the pure carbohydrate metabolism many assume at threshold.

### Short Intermittent Intervals (30-15s, Tabata) Do Not Effectively Train VO2max in Well-Trained Athletes

The podcast delivers a sustained critique of short intermittent intervals as VO2max training tools for trained cyclists. The Ronnestad 30-15 study is dissected as a fundamentally flawed comparison: the "long interval" group worked at approximately 72% of VO2max (below FTP for elite cyclists), making the study a comparison of above-threshold intermittent work versus sub-threshold endurance work, not a test of VO2max training methods. In properly designed continuous intervals, athletes accumulate minutes of sustained maximum cardiac stimulus; in 30-15s, only 10–15 seconds per 30-second effort reaches maximum cardiac stress, cutting the effective stimulus roughly in half. Similarly, Tabata intervals are effective for untrained-to-moderately-trained athletes because nearly any hard stimulus improves both VO2max and anaerobic capacity when both systems are underdeveloped. But in well-trained athletes, the ryanodine receptor fragmentation mechanism that drives mitochondrial adaptation from sprints no longer occurs — the stimulus is not novel enough. Short-term VO2max improvements seen in HIT intermittent studies are likely driven by blood volume increases, not the structural cardiac adaptation (eccentric hypertrophy) that produces lasting VO2max gains.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not use 30-15, 40-20, or Tabata-style intervals with the primary goal of improving VO2max in well-trained athletes.
- Use these formats for their actual strengths: anaerobic capacity development, motor unit training, or general fitness in less trained athletes.
- Expect anaerobic capacity improvements from such intervals to plateau after 6–10 weeks.
- For genuine VO2max improvement in trained athletes, use continuous intervals of 2–5+ minutes at maximum effort with short recovery.

### Slow-Twitch Fiber Predominance Improves Efficiency — More Power Per Liter of Oxygen

Muscle fiber type composition directly affects gross efficiency: in the Horowitz study reviewed on the podcast, the athlete with 83% type I (slow-twitch) fibers produced 357 W at the same oxygen cost as an athlete with 46% type I fibers producing only 335 W — a 22 W advantage from efficiency alone. Fiber type explains approximately 70–80% of efficiency differences between individuals. However, absolute oxygen delivery capacity still matters: the highest-power athlete in the study (376 W, 62% type I) succeeded primarily through enormous absolute O2 delivery (5.0 L/min), not through efficiency. The practical implication is that sustained endurance training, which promotes fiber type conversion toward slow-twitch characteristics, simultaneously builds efficiency and reduces the oxygen cost of a given power output. This creates a compound benefit: higher FTP relative to VO2max (because less O2 is wasted as heat per watt), better glycogen economy, and greater endurance at any absolute intensity.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize sustained endurance training to develop slow-twitch fiber characteristics and improve gross efficiency over time.
- Recognize that efficiency gains compound with FTP/VO2max ratio improvements — more efficient athletes burn less glycogen and produce less heat at any given power.
- Do not neglect absolute O2 delivery (VO2max development) in favor of efficiency alone — the highest performers combine both.

**Sources:** Episodes 814478485-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-19-the-vo2max-slow-component-explained.txt, 671509502-empiricalcyclingpodcast-the-watts-doc-11-ftp-vs-vo2max.txt

## 3. Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing

### Threshold Is a Physiological Continuum, Not a Discrete Breakpoint — but the Heavy-to-Severe Boundary Is the Most Useful Fitness Marker

Blood lactate rises exponentially with increasing intensity, not as a piecewise step function; the appearance of a distinct "threshold" is a mental convenience imposed on continuous data. Coggan argues that LT1 and LT2 as discrete points are not supported by underlying physiology, and Petersen's experience at Loughborough confirms this: leading researchers at the same institution disagreed on whether a distinct second threshold even exists as a meaningful construct. Despite the fuzziness of the concept, the power output at the heavy-to-severe domain boundary — whether measured as MLSS, CP, or another threshold metric — is the most sensitive and informative marker of training status in well-trained cyclists, more so than VO2max, which changes little once an athlete is fit. Coggan observes that threshold as a percentage of VO2max rises with training status and age (from approximately 75% in young recreational cyclists to approximately 85% in older trained cyclists), meaning the gap between threshold and ceiling narrows as fitness improves. Podlogar and Kolie agree that consistency of threshold-testing method matters more than which specific method is used, and that all common methods have populations where they work well or poorly. The practical implication: athletes and coaches should think in terms of zones that shade gradually from sustainable to unsustainable, with the rate of fatigue accumulation accelerating as intensity increases, rather than treating threshold as a binary line.

**Recommendations:**
- Treat threshold as a region rather than a single wattage; expect a gradual increase in fatigue accumulation rate as intensity rises through the threshold zone
- Do not assume that training 5 W above or below a calculated threshold value produces categorically different physiological effects
- Track threshold power as the primary marker of training progress in well-trained athletes, rather than VO2max
- Use changes in threshold relative to VO2max as a signal of training effectiveness: a rising ratio indicates improved efficiency and fatigue resistance

**Sources:** Episodes 1345272511 (Perspectives 18), 1309266271 (Perspectives 16), 1219977919 (Perspectives 9), 992187046 (Ten Minute Tips 11)

### Critical Power and FTP Model the Same Inflection Point but Diverge in Practice — and Neither Is More "Real"

FTP (anchored to MLSS) and critical power (CP) attempt to measure the same physiological boundary, but they frequently disagree numerically by 15-40 W. CP is a mathematical model valid for interpolation across approximately 1-20 minute efforts but breaks down when extrapolated to longer durations. In one well-designed study of trained subjects, CP = 278 W vs. FTP = 239 W — a 39 W gap. In coaching practice, Kyle's CP in 2016 was 304 W vs. actual FTP of 285-290 W, a 15-20 W difference that would have made threshold intervals very hard if not impossible. A peer-reviewed comparison found CP overestimated MLSS by an average of +20 W (CP = 253 W vs. MLSS = 233 W). The calculated CP value converges toward 95-99% of the longest test duration used in its derivation; athletes tested only with 3- and 12-minute efforts will receive a systematically inflated CP. Coggan argues that CP is itself an operationalization of maximal metabolic steady state and depends heavily on test durations and mathematical model — making it no more "real" than FTP. He considers FTP the overarching umbrella concept under which CP, MLSS, ventilatory threshold, and other constructs all sit. Petersen offers a useful reframe: CP may function more like VO2max — a performance ceiling representing the highest threshold an athlete could ever achieve — rather than a prescription intensity. A 2020 paper confirmed a "gray zone" between CP and MLSS where fatigue accumulates faster than either model predicts. The widespread adoption of CP in Golden Cheetah is largely accidental — it was easy to program and therefore included, creating a perception of scientific equivalence that is not always warranted.

**Recommendations:**
- Use FTP (MLSS-derived), not critical power, to set threshold training zones
- CP may be useful for prescribing VO2max and anaerobic work above threshold
- Include test efforts of 20 minutes or longer when fitting CP models to avoid systematic overestimation from short-duration-only protocols
- Be cautious with Golden Cheetah CP estimates — they fit to best-ever efforts and assume they are reproducible daily
- If an athlete's CP calculation includes significant W', cross-validate with time-to-fatigue perceptions — CP may overshoot true threshold by 30-40 W
- iLevels in WKO4/5 account for individual variation above FTP and are preferred for assigning all zones

### The Ramp Test and 20-Minute Test Systematically Overestimate FTP for Most Riders

The ramp test is the most problematic FTP test in common use. Two independent datasets — Jim Arnold's 40-minute TT comparison (N~40) and Rory Porteus's coached group (N=16) — both found an average overestimation of +17-18 W, with upper agreement limits reaching +45 W. Approximately two-thirds of ramp test users will have their FTP set too high. The 20-minute test (with the standard 95% correction) performs better but still overestimates for anaerobic riders, whose true correction factor may be 88-92% rather than 95%. The root cause is individual variation in anaerobic capacity: riders with high anaerobic power contribute disproportionate wattage to short-duration tests, inflating the result above their actual aerobic threshold. Even the 5-minute blowout prescribed before the 20-minute test has negligible effect at the population level. The 2x8-minute test is even worse for anaerobic athletes — Kolie's own data showed a result of 310-320 W from 8-minute efforts versus an actual FTP of approximately 270 W, an overestimation of 40-50 W. Ramp tests were used by Team Sky alongside blood lactate in a lab context to find VO2max, not FTP; using one alone on a trainer to set FTP is a misapplication. Aerobic-dominant riders (triathletes, ultra-endurance athletes) are the least affected, sometimes needing only a 95-97% correction. Highly trained road racers above 400 W FTP also tend to see convergence between 20-minute and threshold power. The problem is worst for intermediate riders with significant anaerobic capacity — the largest segment of the cycling population.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not use ramp test results as definitive FTP without knowing your personal correction factor
- If using a 20-minute test, recognize that the 95% correction is an average; your personal factor may range from 88-97% depending on anaerobic profile
- Never use the 2x8-minute test as a primary FTP tool if you have significant anaerobic power
- When uncertain, err low — slightly under-prescribing FTP is far less damaging than over-prescribing
- Cross-validate any test-derived threshold against perceived exertion and time-to-fatigue sensations

**Sources:** Episodes 594860211 (Watts Doc 1), 893177815 (Watts Doc 26), 2168476551 (Ten Minute Tips 63), 1205855287 (Ten Minute Tips 18), 2283684374 (Ten Minute Tips 74)

### An Open-Ended RPE-Guided Test Is the Most Robust Threshold Assessment

The "Kolie Moore test" — an open-ended effort of 40-70 minutes guided by perceived exertion rather than a fixed duration or target power — outperforms shorter tests across rider types. The protocol: start approximately 20 W below estimated FTP for the first 10-15 minutes, then ramp slowly to find the intensity where increasing power by 10 W feels disproportionately harder and decreasing by 10 W feels dramatically easier. This inflection point is FTP. A peer-reviewed study comparing self-selected intensity (RPE-guided 30-min TT) against critical power and MLSS found the RPE test matched MLSS within 10 W in 11 of 13 participants with zero average bias, while CP overestimated by +20 W. The physiological basis is that the FTP inflection point is directly perceptible: breathing becomes ragged above it, effort feels sustainable below it. Kolie's position, reinforced across the entire podcast arc, is that top-down data (perceived exertion, performance outcomes) should override bottom-up data (lactate numbers, model outputs) whenever they conflict. Regardless of which test an athlete chooses, comparing results across different methods is invalid — a 20-minute FTP test in spring cannot be meaningfully compared to a CP test in summer. The three-coach roundtable in Episode 2283684374 confirmed that a well-calibrated athlete can find FTP within approximately 5 W in about 10 minutes of open-ended riding, and that a short 2-minute FTP-effort warmup can provide a daily freshness read before committing to full intervals.

**Recommendations:**
- Use an open-ended test of 40-70 minutes as your primary FTP assessment
- Start 20 W below estimated FTP, hold for 10-15 minutes, then gradually increase until you find the inflection point
- Key RPE cues: +10 W feels disproportionately harder, -10 W feels dramatically easier, breathing goes ragged above threshold
- Choose one threshold-testing method and use it consistently across seasons; do not compare results from different protocols
- Standardize all controllable test conditions: same equipment, environment, pre-test nutrition, hydration, and time of day

### Population-Average Lactate Values Are Unreliable for Individual Prescription

The widely used 4 mmol/L blood lactate concentration as a marker for MLSS is a population average, not a universal constant. Individual athletes may reach MLSS at significantly different lactate concentrations. Riding above true MLSS because a population-average threshold was assumed rather than individually verified causes fatigue to accumulate much faster and makes interval sets nearly impossible. The same problem applies to LT1, commonly assumed to occur at 2 mmol/L. Ramp tests with three-minute stages consistently underestimate MLSS power because lactate has not stabilized; even five-minute stages have individual outliers. Lactate concentration is further confounded by hydration status (dehydration falsely elevates it) and training-induced blood volume expansion (which dilutes it). Kolie considers routine field lactate testing "a waste of time and money" given that performance is easier to measure directly, and the three-coach roundtable confirmed that with at least 10 published methodologies for identifying threshold from lactate data, the multiplicity itself is evidence that none work reliably. In one coaching case, a world-champion track cyclist was told his threshold was far lower than his actual 40-50 minute sustainable power because his lactate profile was atypical. For athletes who want lactate corroboration of their threshold, the best approach is MLSS testing at a power they already believe is near FTP, not a blind graded ramp test. Podlogar's alternative — a 0.5 mmol/L rise from baseline — and field methods such as the talk test or heart rate drift provide practical LT1 detection without the methodological pitfalls of absolute-concentration approaches, though heart rate drift is sensitive to fiber-type distribution and may be less reliable for sprint-trained athletes.

**Recommendations:**
- Use power data and RPE-based feedback to identify FTP rather than paying for lactate testing
- If using lactate testing, prefer MLSS protocols (30-minute constant-power tests) over ramp-test lactate protocols
- Do not assign training zones based on fixed blood lactate concentrations (2 mmol/L for LT1, 4 mmol/L for LT2) without individual verification
- Consider Podlogar's 0.5 mmol/L rise from baseline as an alternative LT1 detection criterion, or use field methods such as the talk test or heart rate drift
- When blood lactate and perception diverge, trust perceived exertion as the primary signal for adjusting intensity

**Sources:** Episodes 992187046 (Ten Minute Tips 11), 1219977919 (Perspectives 9), 2283684374 (Ten Minute Tips 74), 2168476551 (Ten Minute Tips 63), 893177815 (Watts Doc 26)

### FTP Is Not One-Hour Power — Track Both Wattage and Time to Exhaustion

The "FTP equals one-hour power" definition is a historical convention from Training and Racing with a Power Meter, not a physiological truth. Even in that book, Coggan and Allen give slightly different definitions on consecutive pages. The realistic average TTE at FTP for amateur cyclists is 40-45 minutes. In coaching practice, the highest TTE reliably observed was approximately 83 minutes in three exceptional athletes. Coggan clarified that FTP is not fixed at 60 minutes but is approximately 40 km time-trial power or approximately one-hour power, with large individual variation. The WKO4 TTE metric (released 2015) was an implicit acknowledgment that the one-hour definition was wrong — though Coggan deliberately hid TTE from the initial WKO4 release because he distrusted it as a physiological measure; its subsequent display was a product decision by TrainingPeaks, not a scientific endorsement. TTE is strongly influenced by which test durations are in the athlete's power profile — if the athlete only has 20-minute tests, WKO may report TTE just beyond 20 minutes. Attempting a true one-hour TT to test FTP is counterproductive: most athletes' TTE is shorter than 60 minutes, meaning they must ride above FTP for large portions of the effort, which disrupts training for weeks. FTP as trained via intervals has two dimensions — watts and duration (TTE) — and both must be tracked. An athlete can improve TTE without raising FTP, or raise FTP while TTE shortens. Training should focus on extending TTE toward and beyond 60 minutes rather than fixating on a wattage that can supposedly be held for one hour.

**Recommendations:**
- Track TTE alongside FTP wattage; both dimensions matter for training prescription
- Do not attempt a one-hour TT as an FTP test — use a 40-70 minute open-ended effort instead
- Recognize that untrained TTE of 35-45 minutes is normal and not an indication of an incorrect FTP value
- Use TTE to set starting interval volumes for FTP training blocks
- Treat WKO's TTE value with caution; it reflects the data fed into the model more than the athlete's true time-to-exhaustion at threshold
- Once TTE is adequate for the target event, shift emphasis toward raising FTP through VO2max work

### Progressive Overload in FTP Training Means Adding Time, Not Power — With a 10-Minute Minimum Interval Duration

The governing principle of FTP training is progressive overload through extending total time in zone, not increasing wattage. The Billat study (masters runners) demonstrated this: six weeks of FTP-paced intervals, progressing from 30 to 60 total minutes per session at 2x/week, raised TTE from 44 to 63 minutes (+43%) and increased velocity. VO2max also rose from 66.6 to 68.8 mL/kg/min. The template: start with the athlete's current TTE to determine initial interval volume (e.g., TTE of 40-45 min starts at 4x10 min), then progressively extend through 3x15, 2x20, 2x30, 1x40, toward 1x60 over the block. Going over FTP does not improve FTP faster — the aerobic adaptation signals are not enhanced above threshold, only the fatigue rate increases. The first 2-3 minutes of any FTP interval are spent with heart rate and oxygen delivery lagging behind power output, meaning a 20-minute interval yields only 15-17 minutes of true metabolic FTP work. This creates a minimum effective interval duration of approximately 10-12 minutes. The hierarchy is clear: 1x40 > 2x20 > 4x10, even when the longer intervals are performed at slightly lower power. Specifically, 1x40 at 230-235 W is approximately as effective as 2x20 at 245 W for an athlete with 250 W FTP. At the extreme, 60x1-min FTP intervals produce almost none of the adaptation of 4x15. Assign FTP intervals at 95-97% of FTP, not 100%, to reduce fatigue and allow consistent quality across all intervals. Over-unders or 10-second surges every 5 minutes can add metabolic load without requiring a power increase. Fuel threshold sessions adequately — poor fueling causes session failure and may lead to the false conclusion that the FTP block did not work.

**Recommendations:**
- Progress FTP blocks by adding time: 4x10 to 3x15 to 2x20 to 2x30 to 1x40 to 1x60
- Target 30 min total FTP time in week 1, progressing toward 60 min by week 6
- Never go shorter than 10-12 minutes for FTP intervals; preferably use 15-20+ minute intervals
- Assign interval power at 95-97% FTP to reduce fatigue without sacrificing metabolic stimulus
- Start each interval conservatively — 5-10 W below prescription, building from there
- Fuel threshold sessions with adequate carbohydrate both during and after; control indoor temperature aggressively with fans and ice water

**Sources:** Episodes 726050497 (Watts Doc 14), 1324590415 (Watts Doc 39), 601928829 (Watts Doc 3), 769739140 (Watts Doc 16), 2283684374 (Ten Minute Tips 74), 1345272511 (Perspectives 18)

### Direct Threshold Work Is Required — VO2max, Sweet Spot, and Polarized-Only Approaches Are Not Substitutes

The best way to improve FTP is to ride at FTP. Over-unders at threshold are the next best tool. VO2max work does not reliably raise FTP in coached athletes — FTP only rises after returning to dedicated FTP training. VO2max intervals can raise short-duration test scores (ramp test, 8-minute test) without raising actual aerobic threshold, creating the illusion of improvement. Doing two months of anaerobic capacity work can raise ramp test FTP by 30+ W without any aerobic improvement. Sweet spot training is not the low-fatigue alternative it appears to be: if FTP is correctly set, sweet spot fatigue roughly equals FTP fatigue for comparable adaptation, because sweet spot requires approximately 10 additional minutes per interval to match the FTP stimulus. The sport science consensus that all training between LT1 and CP is "junk miles" is challenged by real-world coaching experience: Petersen, after following a strictly polarized distribution (easy below LT1 plus intervals above CP) at Loughborough and plateauing, added threshold and sweet-spot work and found renewed, measurable improvement. The research base for the "junk miles" claim is drawn from studies on poorly trained subjects coming off rest periods, where nearly any stimulus produces improvement, making high-intensity protocols appear artificially superior. However, Kolie has also evolved away from exclusive FTP-block emphasis — over the last 2-3 years he has given athletes a more varied training diet with less dedicated FTP training than listeners might expect, reporting no reduction in FTP gains and possibly larger ones. During VO2max blocks of 3-4 weeks, FTP typically does not decrease and may tick up a few watts, making VO2max blocks a reasonable maintenance period for FTP. When FTP stops rising, shift focus to TTE extension before moving to VO2max work to raise the "roof."

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize direct FTP interval training for FTP improvement — there is no substitute
- Do not use VO2max or anaerobic capacity blocks as a primary FTP-building strategy
- If using sweet spot, increase interval duration by approximately 50% compared to FTP intervals to achieve comparable stimulus
- If progress has stalled on a polarized distribution, trial adding threshold and sweet-spot work and measure the response
- For FTP maintenance during VO2max blocks, one session per week at 90-100% FTP is the minimum dose
- Vary training with VO2max, sweet spot, and race-specific work to maintain engagement and avoid diminishing returns from exclusive FTP focus

**Sources:** Episodes 769739140 (Watts Doc 16), 1205855287 (Ten Minute Tips 18), 601928829 (Watts Doc 3), 1309266271 (Perspectives 16), 1345272511 (Perspectives 18), 2283684374 (Ten Minute Tips 74)

### Train for Aerobic Power, Not Test Scores — Metrics Can Be Gamed and Substrate Manipulation Is Unnecessary

Doing anaerobic capacity work inflates ramp test and 20-minute test FTP scores without improving aerobic function. This is "teaching to the test" — improving the metric while the underlying physiology stagnates. One coached athlete showed a ramp test FTP of 260 W vs. actual FTP of 221 W (+39 W, +18%). A related artifact: when FTP rises, the CP/W' model shows W' (FRC) declining, but this is a mathematical consequence of the model rebalancing, not a real loss of anaerobic capacity. Actual 10-second to 90-second power is often unchanged. Power meter error of plus or minus 2% (worst case) or plus or minus 0.5% (SRM) means worrying about 5-watt differences is largely noise. Coggan extends this principle to substrate manipulation: training for performance and letting substrate utilization sort itself out is superior to attempting to engineer fat oxidation or specific signalling cascades (PGC-1alpha, malonyl-CoA, AMPK). Low lactate at high power in elite athletes like Pogacar reflects extremely high mitochondrial density enabling both high fat and high carbohydrate oxidation simultaneously — not superior fat adaptation achieved through dietary manipulation. Fat max as a training target is mechanistically false and practically useless; fat oxidation rates are a consequence of exercise intensity and mitochondrial capacity, both better addressed by training to raise threshold. Coggan explicitly endorses supplying maximal carbohydrate to maximize training quality, and training quality is the dominant driver of adaptation. His "glycogen budget" thinking suggests approximately 2 hours per day of moderately hard riding is what a high-carbohydrate diet can replenish overnight.

**Recommendations:**
- Train to improve aerobic power, not to improve test scores
- If W' or FRC appears to drop after FTP training, verify actual short-duration power before concluding anaerobic capacity declined
- Never use heart rate as a primary FTP metric; it is too variable within and between sessions
- Accept power meter error margins and train in zones, not to single-watt precision
- Do not attempt to engineer fat oxidation through dietary restriction; train to raise threshold and fat oxidation improves as a by-product
- Supply adequate carbohydrate to support training quality; approximately 2 hours of moderately hard training is the daily glycogen budget on a high-carbohydrate diet

**Sources:** Episodes 1205855287 (Ten Minute Tips 18), 893177815 (Watts Doc 26), 594860211 (Watts Doc 1), 1219977919 (Perspectives 9), 1345272511 (Perspectives 18), 1353913138 (Perspectives 19)

### Retest FTP Every 4-8 Weeks, Use FTP as a Fatigue Barometer, and Calibrate by Discipline

Retest FTP every 4-8 weeks under normal training conditions. Less frequent testing is appropriate for highly trained athletes where 5-10 W/year is the expected gain. Do not simply add 5 W every 4-8 weeks without testing — FTP may not have moved. However, formal retesting every 4-8 weeks is not strictly required when the training block itself functions as continuous testing: a well-calibrated athlete executing intervals near threshold can feel FTP changes without a formal test. A particular risk with prolonged training at one FTP value is "wearing a rut" in perceived effort: athletes become psychologically anchored to their current number and underperform on tests even when fitness has improved. The 2020 pandemic year exacerbated this because the natural "forget-and-retest" cycle from racing was absent. Between formal tests, the WKO5 power-duration model can track FTP if the power curve is well-populated at key durations. FTP should also function as a fatigue barometer: a 20 W apparent drop mid-week almost always reflects accumulated fatigue, underfueling, or heat — not a true fitness decline, since FTP cannot change physiologically that fast. Use this as a recovery signal, not a retest trigger. The TT bike typically produces an FTP 10-15 W lower than the road bike, and terrain specificity is real for some athletes who may show a 10-20 W difference between flat and climbing FTP, requiring separate calibration. All recovery from high-intensity intervals is aerobic — glycogenolysis stops immediately when muscle contraction ceases — so rest interval duration should be set based on how much aerobic recovery the athlete needs, not on lactate-clearance theories.

**Recommendations:**
- Retest FTP every 4-8 weeks; do not auto-increment without testing
- Use an occasional 15-25 minute open-ended test or race effort to break perceived-effort ruts
- Populate the WKO5 power curve at key durations for model-based FTP tracking between formal tests
- Track FTP as a fatigue barometer: unexpectedly low power for a given RPE signals a need for recovery, not retesting
- Calibrate TT bike FTP separately — expect it to be 10-15 W lower than road bike FTP
- Set rest intervals based on ability to complete the next work interval at target power, not on lactate-clearance theories

**Sources:** Episodes 893177815 (Watts Doc 26), 769739140 (Watts Doc 16), 2168476551 (Ten Minute Tips 63), 1353913138 (Perspectives 19), 1309266271 (Perspectives 16), 2283684374 (Ten Minute Tips 74)

### Evolution of Views

The podcast's treatment of threshold, FTP, and testing shows a clear arc from test-centric to perception-centric approaches, with parallel shifts in how the underlying physiology is conceptualized.

**Early episodes (2018-2019):** In Episode 594860211 (Watts Doc 1, the inaugural episode), Kolie introduced the longer open-ended test as an alternative to the 20-minute and 8-minute tests, but still framed it within a structured stepwise progression protocol. FTP was treated as the primary fitness metric. Episode 601928829 (Watts Doc 3) established the Billat-inspired principle of progressive time extension, and Episode 631172070 (Watts Doc 8) laid out the FTP-vs-CP distinction. Episode 992187046 (Ten Minute Tips 11) introduced the critical caveat that population-average lactate thresholds cannot be blindly applied to individuals — but threshold was still discussed as a testable line defined by step tests and lactate values.

**Mid-period (2019-2021):** By Episode 893177815 (Watts Doc 26), Kolie had abandoned the stepwise test protocol for coached athletes entirely, moving them directly to the open-ended RPE-guided version. He shifted toward greater reliance on the WKO5 model for day-to-day FTP tracking, reflecting experience that formal testing can create its own problems (perceived-effort ruts, psychological anchoring). Episode 726050497 (Watts Doc 14) confirmed that the Billat study — chase TTE extension, not wattage — was the inspiration for the podcast itself. Kolie's holistic aerobic training approach (not compartmentalizing FTP vs. VO2max vs. base), noted in Episode 769739140 (Watts Doc 16), produced less FTP detraining compared to his earlier, more compartmentalized periodization.

**Perspectives interviews (2021-2023):** Guest episodes introduced new conceptual frameworks. Petersen (Episode 1309266271) explicitly described changing his own practice after leaving Loughborough — he followed a strictly polarized model, plateaued, then added threshold work and improved. Coggan (Episodes 1345272511 and 1353913138) reframed threshold as a continuum, revealed he deliberately hid TTE from the initial WKO4 release, argued that training for performance beats engineering specific signalling cascades, and demonstrated that fat max is not a useful training target. Podlogar (Episode 1219977919) contributed the individualization critique of population-average lactate values and acknowledged that his earlier promotion of fasted riding was irresponsible.

**Late episodes (2023-2025):** By Episode 2175917922 (Watts Doc 57), the philosophical shift was complete: Kolie stated explicitly that RPE and athlete feedback are primary data, not secondary, and expressed regret about time spent on short-form tests early in his coaching career. The publication of a 2016 paper confirming that self-selected RPE intensity matched MLSS within 10 W for most participants validated what he had independently developed. Episode 2283684374 (Ten Minute Tips 74), a three-coach roundtable, synthesized the matured position: test by feel, fuel properly, be skeptical of lactate testing, use FTP as a fatigue barometer rather than an identity, and recognize when to stop chasing FTP and shift to TTE extension or VO2max work. Kolie noted he has given athletes less dedicated FTP training over the last 2-3 years with no reduction in gains — perhaps the strongest signal that the podcast's earlier FTP-block-centric recommendations have been refined into a more balanced, individualized approach.

## 4. Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

### Easy Rides Must Be Genuinely Easy — Zone 2 Is Defined by Feel, Not Power

The single most common training error identified across the podcast is riding endurance and recovery days too hard. Three coaches independently flag this as their most frequent intervention: athletes riding endurance above LT1 shift the session from a recovery/volume stimulus to a moderate-intensity effort that accumulates fatigue without proportionate benefit. Zone 2 prescribed at 2 mmol/L blood lactate — a common LT1 proxy — frequently places well-trained athletes at low sweet spot intensities, turning every "endurance" ride into a functionally moderate session. Multiple coaches report significant FTP gains — sometimes exceeding 20-30 watts — when athletes finally reduce endurance ride intensity, though these gains typically occur alongside other training adjustments rather than in isolation. Zone 2 has no unique physiological adaptation; the adaptations exist on a continuum with threshold and VO2max training, and "threshold training is endurance training" (Andy Coggan's formulation, endorsed by Kolie). What makes zone 2 genuinely useful is not a unique metabolic pathway but what it *permits* within a training week — large volumes of work that do not compromise recovery for hard sessions. The correct pacing signal is RPE, not a strict wattage window — "the hardest pace that still feels easy," passing the talk test, RPE 4-5/10. Riding 20-30 W below apparent LT1 is fine; no observed adaptation difference exists between 60% and 45-50% of FTP. Pedaling time, not power output, is the key stimulus. The cultural stigma against easy riding — the feeling that riding slowly is "cheating" — is identified as the primary barrier to implementation, and coaches report that persuading athletes to ride easier is their most consistent friction point.

**Recommendations:**
- Use the talk test and perceived effort (RPE 4-5/10) rather than power zones or blood lactate to set endurance pace
- LT1 is the upper limit for endurance rides; calibrate individually (ranges from 45-85% of FTP across athletes)
- Riding 20-30 W below apparent LT1 is fine; do not anchor to a specific power number
- Do not peg endurance pace at the top of the Coggan zone 2 table; the number is a convenience, not a prescription
- If a high-volume easy week feels revelatory (no soreness, feeling great), your previous "endurance" pace was too hard
- Recovery between hard intervals should be genuinely easy, not 70% FTP
- For dieting athletes, easier endurance rides reduce acute energy deficits and improve dietary compliance
- On a trainer, break up endurance rides with cadence variation rather than adding intensity
- Minimize coasting on outdoor endurance rides to maximize pedaling time, which is the actual stimulus
- Track the efficiency factor (normalized power / heart rate) to monitor short-term cardiac stroke volume progression

**Sources:** Episodes 1396150891, 625992387, 1560449302, 1270352152, 952643710, 918048055, 1301979604, 732563563, 814478485, 2239584818, 1783447314, 2219611610

### Volume Is the Primary Long-Term Driver of Aerobic Improvement, Not Intensity

Across multiple episodes, Kolie argues with increasing conviction that training volume — not interval intensity — is the dominant lever for sustained year-on-year aerobic development. He reports that approximately 14-15 hours per week is the minimum at which he has observed consistent year-on-year FTP improvements in coached athletes. Clients riding 10-12 hours/week rarely see consistent FTP gains regardless of interval quality, while those at 20-30 hours/week almost always do. At low volumes (4-6 hours/week), athletes plateau relatively quickly, and intensity cannot fully substitute for volume over the long term. The mechanism appears to be that higher chronic training load improves the body's capacity to absorb subsequent stress, creating a positive feedback loop. Even inconsistent volume spikes (e.g., a vacation week at 25 hours) produce real aerobic benefits, provided the additional riding is executed at genuinely easy intensity. At very high volumes, easy riding becomes essential because cumulative fatigue limits how much intensity can be stacked on top. Zone 2 is also a powerful maintenance tool: fatigued athletes switching to endurance-only riding see threshold and VO2max numbers recover without interval work. The notion of "junk miles" applied to easy riding is a myth — what is actually junk is semi-hard, unfocused riding that creates fatigue without targeted adaptation. Pandemic data was revealing: athletes who stopped bike commuting lost approximately 10 W FTP, while athletes with no racing to interrupt aerobic development showed unconstrained aerobic progression.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize finding more riding time over adding more intensity if FTP is stalled
- At low volumes (4-6 h/week), adding even one easy endurance ride per week is a significant stimulus increase
- When volume increases are impossible, shift season strategy: stay at 95% fitness and race more frequently rather than chasing peaks
- Bike commuting at low intensity is a legitimate endurance stimulus
- When you have weeks with extra time available, use them for increased endurance volume — the aerobic stimulus is real even if week-to-week consistency is irregular
- If riding only 2-3 times per week for 2 hours, those rides do not need to be zone 2; threshold or VO2max work is a valid use of that time

**Sources:** Episodes 1270352152, 1514086555, 1560449302, 1211797429

### The "Sweet Spot vs. Polarized" Debate Is a False Dichotomy — Let Intensity Distribution Emerge From Diagnosis

The hosts describe the sweet spot vs. polarized debate as "kayfabe" — a manufactured conflict more useful for selling training plans than for guiding individual training decisions. Both approaches are simply tools, and starting from a training modality rather than from an athlete's specific limiters is backwards. Sweet spot training is functionally threshold training, and the large body of lactate threshold literature applies to it directly even though the term "sweet spot" does not appear in peer-reviewed research. Pre-planning a training zone distribution (80/20, all sweet spot, etc.) is discouraged; the distribution should emerge from answering the right questions: what is my limiter, how many hard days per week can I recover from, and what physiological quality do I need to develop? When well-run training programs are inspected retrospectively, they often look pyramidal, but this was not the design target. A "rainbow" of workout types (endurance, sweet spot, FTP, VO2max, sprints) across the intensity spectrum is preferred over reliance on any single method. Whether sweet spot counts as a "high-intensity" workout depends on context — in a building phase it counts against the weekly hard-workout budget, but as maintenance it may not. Elite riders do not uniformly train polarized; distribution varies by event, phase, and individual.

**Recommendations:**
- Ask "what is my limiter?" before choosing training modality — diagnose whether VO2max, threshold, endurance, sprint, or race craft needs work
- Do not pre-plan intensity distributions; instead determine how many hard days per week you can recover from
- Target RPE 7-8 for threshold/sweet spot work; if you are white-knuckling the last 3 minutes, the target is too high
- Limit hard days to 2-3 per week (rarely 4), with genuinely easy recovery between them
- Use a full spectrum of workout types across the training year rather than relying on a single intensity domain
- Count sweet spot as a hard session during build phases but not necessarily during maintenance phases

**Sources:** Episodes 1199788219, 1270352152, 1087518463, 1211797429, 1316155894

### FTP Improvement Requires a Three-Legged Stool: Threshold Work, VO2max Work, and Endurance Volume

Exclusive focus on FTP intervals (e.g., repeating 2x20 sessions indefinitely) will plateau because FTP training is primarily a duration-based stimulus — it extends time-to-exhaustion at threshold rather than raising the ceiling. VO2max sets the ceiling on FTP, and endurance volume supports the aerobic base that allows both to rise. For athletes whose TTE is short (under 35 minutes), extending threshold duration is the priority. For athletes whose FTP is a low fraction of VO2max — when FTP sits at 80-82% of VO2max watts — a dedicated VO2max block raises the ceiling. For athletes whose volume is low, adding endurance riding provides the base for both. Early in a training career, all three modalities raise FTP comparably — largely through plasma volume effects — but as fitness advances, the stimulus must become more targeted. The relationship is cyclical in well-trained athletes: VO2max work raises the ceiling, which shortens TTE, then threshold work extends TTE again at the new, higher FTP. General aerobic riding maintains VO2max; specific VO2max intervals are needed only to push above current capacity.

**Recommendations:**
- Combine FTP training with VO2max work and endurance volume; do not rely on any single modality
- TTE (time-to-exhaustion at FTP) is often a more actionable lever than FTP wattage — target extending TTE from 35 minutes toward 60+ minutes
- Progress threshold intervals by extending time-in-zone: 2x20 to 2x25 to 2x30 to longer single efforts
- When TTE at FTP reaches approximately 60 minutes, shift training emphasis to VO2max intervals to push FTP higher
- Check the FTP-to-VO2max ratio; when FTP is 80-82% of VO2max watts, VO2max work is likely the priority
- Diagnose your race limiter before choosing what to train: dropped on early fresh climbs suggests sprint/anaerobic capacity; blowing up late suggests endurance/FTP

**Sources:** Episodes 1333403119, 1514086555, 1199788219, 918048055, 1087518463, 1211797429, 1301979604

### VO2max Blocks Require Pre-Readiness and Far More Recovery Than Athletes Expect

Before beginning a dedicated VO2max block, athletes should be at or near their previously-established aerobic fitness — ideally achieved via a period of higher-than-normal endurance volume. Jumping into VO2max work immediately after time off accumulates fatigue faster than adaptation. A good winter of low-intensity endurance training can produce TTEs of 65-85 minutes on the first spring test, which is exactly the time to begin a VO2max block. After a block, the recovery window is 2-8 weeks to see full FTP improvements, not 3-4 days. This post-block lag is poorly studied but consistently observed in coaching data. Athletes who test immediately after a block frequently see no improvement and mistakenly conclude the block failed — improvements appear immediately only about 40% of the time. During a VO2max block, the key variable is intensity, not duration: aim for 15-20 total interval minutes per session (12-15 to start), with individual intervals ranging from 2 to 8 minutes. Going beyond approximately 23 total minutes per session shows no additional benefit. Structure blocks as 7-12 workouts across 2-3 weeks. High cadence during VO2max intervals reduces muscular fatigue and is particularly recommended for double-day sessions. A mixed block (1 VO2max + 1 threshold per week) is valid when pure VO2max work stalls.

**Recommendations:**
- Build back to baseline aerobic fitness via endurance riding before starting a VO2max block
- Structure blocks with 2 hard sessions per week; allow 2-3 weeks recovery before expecting full benefits
- After a block, allow 1-2+ weeks of easy riding before testing; expect the full improvement window to extend 2-8 weeks
- Target 15-20 total interval minutes per session; start "not quite so hard" on the first interval when fresh
- Check for improvement signs 2-3 weeks into a VO2max block; if nothing is shifting, the stimulus may not be appropriate
- Do not begin a second VO2max block before full recovery from the first

**Sources:** Episodes 1514086555, 1199788219, 1270352152, 918048055, 1087518463, 1211797429

### Progressive Overload and Stimulus Proxies Differ by Training Domain — Fatigue Is a Byproduct, Not a Goal

A critical insight is that the mechanism of progressive overload varies by training domain, and the proxies for evaluating training quality must be type-specific. For threshold/sweet spot work, progressive overload is achieved by extending time-in-zone within a single session — progressing from 2x20 to 2x25 to 2x30 to longer efforts. Crucially, this time must be accumulated within a single ride; splitting it between morning and afternoon sessions does not count as the same stimulus. For VO2max work, the stimulus is intensity (analogous to load in weight training), not duration — 15-20 minutes of total interval time per session is sufficient. For endurance training, overload comes from extending ride duration until a ceiling is reached, then increasing power at the same duration. The type-specific proxies are: endurance measured by cumulative volume and feeling easy; threshold identified by the combination of significant leg fatigue and labored but controlled breathing; VO2max confirmed when breathing is the primary limiter; anaerobic capacity recognized by concentric leg failure rather than breathing. TSS is specifically unreliable because it conflates different stimulus types: 100 TSS from a 5-hour endurance ride and 100 TSS from threshold intervals represent completely different physiology. Sprint peak watts are primarily a freshness indicator, not a sprint fitness indicator. Fatigue is a byproduct of training stimulus, not itself a driver of adaptation — seeking fatigue as a goal inverts causality and leads to junk volume. Energy neutral intake (not surplus) is sufficient for endurance adaptation; total training time matters more than kilojoules as a metric.

**Recommendations:**
- For threshold work: extend time-in-zone within a single ride; progress asymmetrically if needed (25 + 20 min before 25 + 25)
- For VO2max work: focus on intensity and reaching "gasping like a fish" effort; do not chase longer intervals or more total time
- For endurance: push duration until it challenges you, then increase power at that duration
- Use type-specific proxies: breathing for VO2max, leg failure for anaerobic, cumulative volume for endurance — not a single metric like TSS
- Interpret low sprint peak watts as a fatigue signal, not a sprint training deficit
- Do not pursue fatigue as a training goal; the stimulus drives adaptation, and fatigue is a constraining side effect
- Track total training time rather than kilojoules for endurance development
- Energy neutral intake is sufficient for endurance adaptation; chronic deficit is the primary risk

**Sources:** Episodes 1199788219, 1270352152, 1333403119, 1514086555, 1087518463, 1211797429, 2024446488, 2219611610

### Steady Zone 3 Is Rarely Optimal — Use Stochastic Tempo and Well-Structured Over-Unders Instead

Steady-state zone 3 riding is almost never prescribed as focused training. It occupies an intensity that accumulates meaningful fatigue without providing a stimulus cleanly targeted at either endurance or threshold adaptation. The one exception is during recovery weeks, where 30-90 minutes of zone 3 is used to re-activate the hormonal working-mode before the next hard block. For aerobic development at moderate intensity, stochastic "breakaway-style" tempo rides are preferred — varying effort naturally with terrain rather than holding steady zone 3 power. These rides serve as effective aerobic workouts (60-90 minutes) while also training repeated accelerations, and Kolie attributes substantial cross-fitness gains over 2-3 weeks from this approach. Over-unders are a useful tool but the common symmetrical approach (2 minutes over / 2 minutes under) is wrong. The correct structure is short overs (30 seconds at 20-30W above FTP) followed by long unders (4-5 minutes at tempo watts). If you are "hanging on by the skin of your teeth" during the under portion, the prescription is too hard. Low-cadence overs can obligate recruitment of larger motor units but are appropriate primarily for athletes with a specific motor unit recruitment deficit, not as a default. For highly trained athletes whose LT1 naturally sits at 80-85% FTP, the zone 3 descriptor may not even apply meaningfully — Coggan's training zones were designed to be descriptive rather than prescriptive.

**Recommendations:**
- Replace steady zone 3 rides with stochastic tempo rides that vary effort naturally with terrain
- Reserve steady zone 3 for recovery weeks only (30-90 minutes at a wide power range) as an activation tool
- Structure over-unders as 30s over / 4-5 min under at tempo, not symmetrical durations
- Aim for approximately 20-30 minutes total time over threshold in an over-under session; start with 2 sets and build to 4
- Include short openers (high-intensity efforts) in the days coming out of rest weeks to avoid rest inertia
- Use low-cadence overs only for athletes with a specific large motor unit recruitment deficit

**Sources:** Episodes 918048055, 952643710, 1087518463, 1211797429

### Traditional "Base Training" Is Literally False but Metaphorically True — The Real Benefit Is Recovery

The conventional wisdom that cyclists must spend months doing low-intensity zone 2 riding to "build a base" before starting intervals is reframed as a useful fiction. The actual benefit of a traditional base season is rest and recovery from the prior season, not the development of a new aerobic foundation through zone 2 specificity. A person with a "big base" has simply done a lot of focused aerobic work at all intensities over a career — not a lot of zone 2 miles specifically. The pyramid metaphor ("bigger base, higher peak") is replaced with a "food plate" model where threshold, VO2max, and endurance work all contribute. There is no evidence that athletes must complete months of zone 2 before doing threshold intervals; intervals can begin earlier as long as recovery is managed. The physiological claims behind "you must ride easy to build capillaries" are incorrect, but the practice of easy riding in the off-season still produces real rest and recovery benefits.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not delay interval training for months of mandatory zone 2; begin intervals when recovery allows
- Build your longest ride up to the duration of your longest race (Friel recommendation endorsed)
- Use endurance rides with a negative split: start at lower power and build throughout
- To make endurance rides effective, they must challenge your endurance limit — if 4 hours at 150 W is comfortable, target 4.25-4.5 hours

**Sources:** Episodes 625992387, 1396150891

### Most Cycling Events Are Far More Aerobic Than Assumed — Anaerobic Capacity and Sprint Work Are Polish

Cyclists systematically overestimate the anaerobic demands of their racing. Even criterium racing, punchy hill climbs, and track endurance events (5-20 minutes) are primarily aerobic. A rider who gets dropped on a short climb almost always has an aerobic power problem, not an anaerobic capacity problem. The energy contribution math supports this: at a true 1-minute all-out effort, approximately 50% of energy is anaerobic, but by 4-5 minutes the contribution is overwhelmingly aerobic. Even VLAmax declining from aerobic training is a feature, not a bug, for criterium racers — replenishment of phosphocreatine between surges is nearly entirely aerobic. Anaerobic work capacity should be treated as finishing touches: muscle mass and peak power are the true long-term limiters, not year-round anaerobic intervals. Year-round anaerobic training leads to burnout without gains beyond what a focused 4-6 week pre-race block provides. Natural sprinters often need more aerobic training and less sprint-specific work — "you're not a different species." The minimum effective dose of sprint work is often quite small for natural sprinters; the priority should be finding that minimum dose needed for good legs on race day rather than maximizing sprint volume. Fat oxidation at threshold is more common than assumed — TTE extension is driven in part by improved fat oxidation through training-based adaptation, which does not impair carbohydrate utilization. Dietary fat adaptation (keto/LCHF), by contrast, suppresses carbohydrate use and is detrimental for high-intensity performance.

**Recommendations:**
- If you get dropped in crits or on punchy climbs, raise FTP through aerobic development rather than adding anaerobic interval sessions
- Build anaerobic capacity long-term through off-season strength training (squats, deadlifts) to increase muscle mass, not through year-round intervals
- Limit focused anaerobic capacity work to 4-6 weeks before target races
- If you are a natural sprinter, focus on aerobic development and find the minimum dose of sprint-specific work that keeps your sprint sharp
- Separate sprint sessions from aerobic sessions rather than tacking sprints onto hard aerobic workouts
- For repeatability deficits, use specific intervals (20-30s max efforts with 2-3 min rest, 4-6 reps)
- Do not adopt keto/LCHF diets for performance — training-based fat adaptation supports longer TTE without dietary manipulation

**Sources:** Episodes 918048055, 1087518463, 1211797429, 1316155894, 952643710

### Time-Crunched Athletes Should Prioritize Recovery Quality and Individualization Over Workout Optimization

For athletes with 4-6 hours per week, the binding constraint is not workout selection but recovery capacity and life stress. Training hard on top of high psychological stress produces poor-quality efforts and may not generate the peripheral adaptive signals needed for adaptation. Sleep accounts for the vast majority of recovery; consistently sacrificing sleep for extra training time compounds into a deficit faster than expected. The recommended approach is 2-3 hard sessions per week with full recovery between them, rather than 5 moderate sessions where recovery is never complete. RPE decoupling — when a normally manageable workout feels like grinding through at 9/10 — is a key signal of off-bike stress or inadequate recovery and should trigger a back-off. The "it always depends" refrain is not evasion but reflects genuine individual variability: tapering responses differ (some athletes need hard legs going into races, others respond to classical rest tapers), VO2max block durations vary (2-3 weeks to 8 weeks), and recovery timelines from blocks and races vary enormously. FTP does not change day-to-day under fatigue for most athletes; TTE contracts instead (e.g., from 50 to 30 minutes). If workouts feel like someone else's FTP, the athlete needs rest, not a reduced target. Two-a-day workouts are valid for volume accumulation when single long rides are impossible.

**Recommendations:**
- Manage life stress and protect sleep before optimizing training; 98-99.5% of recovery is eating and sleeping
- Favor 2-3 hard sessions with full recovery days over 5 moderate sessions
- Watch for RPE decoupling as a signal to back off; do not attempt "makeup" workouts
- Two-a-days are valid for volume accumulation when long single rides are impossible
- Experiment with taper strategies early in the season to identify your individual pattern before target races
- When performance feels unusually bad, suspect fatigue-compressed TTE before lowering your FTP target
- Prioritize sleep and nutrition as primary recovery tools; recovery gadgets (ice baths, compression, cupping) are marginal

**Sources:** Episodes 1270352152, 1514086555, 1301979604, 1211797429, 952643710

### Evolution of Views

Kolie's training philosophy has evolved substantially across the podcast's lifespan, with several clear trajectories and explicit course corrections.

**On training volume (progressive hardening).** In the earlier Watts Doc episode (625992387), the emphasis was on reframing "base" away from mandatory zone 2 and toward a mix of all intensities. By the 300K special episode (1270352152), Kolie stated explicitly that "volume is more and more the key driver of continuous aerobic adaptation" with each year of coaching. By the training myths episode (1560449302), his position had solidified further: year-on-year improvement at volumes below approximately 14-15 hours/week is rare for most athletes, and the typical response to stalled FTP should be increased volume rather than more intensity. The 2020 pandemic year (referenced in 1211797429) was pivotal: with no racing to interrupt aerobic development, Kolie observed unconstrained aerobic progression in his athletes, solidifying his conviction that racing-driven taper phases chronically interrupt aerobic momentum.

**On endurance ride pacing (power to RPE).** Earlier episodes suggest zone 2 could be paced by power zones, but by the 2020 and 2021 AMAs (952643710, 1211797429), Kolie had evolved firmly toward RPE-based endurance pacing after observing athletes accumulate excessive fatigue at "the top of Zone 2." The Watts Doc episodes on heart rate (732563563) and the VO2 slow component (814478485) provided the mechanistic justification for this shift. He flagged in episode 1396150891 that his views on zone 2 are still forming: "between now and whenever I've thought about it for a year or two, you're going to see an evolution of thought." He has intentionally used "endurance training" rather than "zone 2" to avoid prescription-by-zone-number. Steady zone 3 was progressively de-emphasized, replaced by stochastic tempo riding (918048055).

**On VO2max block methodology (increasing sophistication).** Earlier practice mixed VO2max and threshold work concurrently. By 2021 (1211797429), Kolie preferred isolated VO2max blocks when the calendar allowed, finding it easier to attribute gains and manage fatigue, though mixed blocks remain common when time is compressed. The post-VO2max-block recovery window was updated based on 2023 coaching data (1514086555): recovery times of 2-8 weeks were observed, a significant shift from more optimistic prior assumptions. Kolie also acknowledged that his earlier approach of setting aggressive FTP targets for athletes was naive.

**On periodization (rigid to adaptive).** The Ten Minute Tips episodes (1301979604, 1316155894) show a clear trajectory away from multi-week advance programming toward shorter 3-4 week planning windows with daily adjustment based on athlete feedback and data. Preset periodization templates are increasingly treated as starting frameworks rather than rigid plans.

**On the sweet spot vs. polarized debate.** Kolie noted in episode 1199788219 that he had "not followed Seiler's polarized model since it was very popular a couple of years ago," suggesting a move away from polarized as a default framework. Low-cadence over-unders, used heavily for several years, fell out of favor as a default prescription by the Instagram/Reddit AMA (1087518463), remaining in the toolbox only for specific motor unit recruitment deficits.

**On measurement tools.** Lab testing moved from "probably useful for most athletes" to "not important for most amateurs" (952643710), as power-meter analysis proved sufficient. The cumulative coaching philosophy shift: early episodes lean on power targets and structured protocols; later episodes increasingly advocate RPE-based prescription, type-specific stimulus proxies, and skepticism toward single-number metrics like TSS.

## 5. Interval Workouts

### Over-Unders Are Not Superior to Standard Threshold Work — Their Value Is Variety and Specificity

Over-under workouts have been marketed as uniquely effective "lactate clearance" tools, but Kolie explicitly deflates this claim. There is no direct randomized study comparing over-unders to standard 2x20 sweet spot or threshold work that shows superior adaptations in anaerobic capacity or lactate tolerance. The real drivers of lactate oxidation capacity are mitochondrial mass and MCT transporter density, both of which are improved by any sustained aerobic training — including simple long endurance rides. The best "lactate clearance" workout, per Kolie, is a six-hour endurance ride. What over-unders genuinely offer is threefold: boredom relief on the trainer, flexibility as a format with many adjustable variables (over duration, under duration, power targets, cadence, set length), and race-specificity when the overs match the acceleration demands of target events like criteriums, cyclocross, or mountain bike racing. The bread-and-butter format is a 1:4 over-to-under ratio — 30 seconds over at 20-50 W above threshold, then 2 minutes under at sweet spot. Build to 2-3 sets of 30-40 minutes. For athletes who naturally tolerate repeated accelerations well, over-unders add nothing special and are just a variation.

**Recommendations:**
- Use sweet spot over-unders (30s over / 2 min under) as a substitute for one steady-state threshold session per week for variety, not as a categorically superior workout
- Set overs by feel at roughly 20-50 W above threshold rather than a fixed percentage of FTP
- Progress by increasing total set duration before raising power
- Start each set with an over (acceleration), especially for CX and MTB athletes practicing race starts

**Sources:** Episodes 1472759278-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-27-programming-over-under-workouts.txt, 936171130-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-27-training-repeated-sprint-ability.txt

### Prescribing VO2max and Anaerobic Intervals as a Percentage of FTP Is Fundamentally Flawed

Both hosts independently listed VO2max intervals at 110-120% FTP as one of the worst common workouts. The problem is that the ratio of VO2max power to FTP varies enormously between athletes: five-minute power can be anywhere from 115% to 150% of FTP. An athlete with a high anaerobic capacity doing "4x6 min at 120% FTP" may barely be breathing hard, while an aerobically-dominant athlete at the same prescription is in genuine distress. The original Coggan power zones were intended to be descriptive (categorizing work after the fact), not prescriptive (assigning workout targets). Using fixed percentages like 120% FTP for VO2max is a misapplication of that framework. The same problem extends to shorter efforts: 40-20s at 130% FTP may not become genuinely hard for a well-trained athlete until the final reps of a 10-minute set. The fix is to use either WKO5 iLevels based on an actual power-duration curve, or simply go genuinely maximal — if you are doing VO2max work correctly, you should be breathing extremely hard. For intermittent efforts like 15-15s, self-pacing without a watt target and tracking average power over weeks is more effective than hitting a prescribed number.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not prescribe VO2max intervals as a fixed percentage of FTP; use maximal effort or individualized power-duration targets instead
- For 30-30s and 15-15s, pace by feel for the entire set rather than targeting a specific wattage
- Track progress by observing average power on the "on" portions over weeks, not by hitting a single-session target
- Use WKO5 iLevels or similar individualized models if precise targets are needed

**Sources:** Episodes 1164677890-empiricalcyclingpodcast-200k-the-best-and-worst-workouts.txt, 1440628345-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-22-the-case-against-erg-mode.txt

### Intermittent Efforts (15-15s, 30-30s) Are a Year-Round Staple, Not Just a Race-Prep Tool

Kolie includes intermittent efforts at some level for virtually the entire year — even during FTP blocks. It is rare for him to assign three purely steady-state FTP sessions in a week. The rationale is that these efforts recruit larger motor units that are typically under-trained aerobically in steady-state riding and that making those motor units more aerobically capable is the mechanism behind improved repeated sprint ability. For 15-15s, typical prescription is 3x10 minutes building to 3x20 or 2x30 minutes over weeks. There is no strict power target; the athlete should pace to finish the set feeling worked but not wrecked. Kolie's own numbers — approximately 400-450 W on a 260 W FTP — illustrate how far above FTP-percentage these efforts naturally land. For 30-30s used as anaerobic capacity work, shorter sets (2-3 sets of 3-6 minutes) are preferred to maintain quality; longer sets like 16x1 min on/off cause quality to collapse after the first few reps. The average or normalized power for a well-executed 30-30 set should be in the over-FTP range. These intermittent formats may produce approximately the same aerobic molecular adaptation signals as steady-state threshold work despite the very different power profile.

**Recommendations:**
- Include some form of intermittent effort (15-15s, 30-30s, or over-unders) year-round, even during threshold-focused blocks
- Start 15-15s at 3x10 minutes and progress to 3x20 or 2x30 minutes over weeks
- For anaerobic capacity 30-30s, keep sets short (3-6 minutes) to preserve rep quality
- Pace the first rep conservatively; starting even one or two reps too hard collapses the entire set

**Sources:** Episodes 936171130-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-27-training-repeated-sprint-ability.txt, 1164677890-empiricalcyclingpodcast-200k-the-best-and-worst-workouts.txt, 1472759278-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-27-programming-over-under-workouts.txt

### Stochastic Tempo Rides Are One of the Most Versatile and Underrated Workouts

Both the RSA episode and the best/worst workouts episode highlight stochastic tempo — riding a course aiming for speed rather than a specific power target — as a top-tier workout. The athlete pushes harder on hills, recovers on descents, and accumulates kilojoules while varying power naturally, like an impromptu time trial on rolling terrain. Duration ranges from 1 to 4 hours. This format naturally trains repeated sprint ability by recruiting larger motor units through variable power demands without requiring formal interval structure. It is also described as fun and race-specific, since actual races involve highly variable power, not steady-state efforts. The workout is good for road, cyclocross, and mountain bike athletes alike. The key distinction from simply riding with no structure is intentionality: the rider is actively trying to cover the course as fast as possible, treating every feature of the terrain as an opportunity to push or recover strategically.

**Recommendations:**
- Use stochastic tempo (fartlek) rides of 1-4 hours on rolling terrain as a regular training staple
- Ride for speed over the course rather than targeting a specific power number
- Use a fixed course repeatedly to track fitness progression over time
- Particularly valuable for criterium, cyclocross, and mountain bike racers who need variable-power fitness

**Sources:** Episodes 936171130-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-27-training-repeated-sprint-ability.txt, 1164677890-empiricalcyclingpodcast-200k-the-best-and-worst-workouts.txt

### ERG Mode Undermines the RPE Feedback Loop That Makes Interval Training Effective

ERG mode locks the athlete into a predetermined wattage, which disables the critical skill of self-regulating effort based on how it feels. This creates several problems: athletes develop pass-fail thinking (if the watts were hit, the workout was good), they lose the ability to detect early fatigue signals, and pacing skill atrophies. The damage is worst for VO2max and anaerobic intervals, where full maximal effort is required and pre-programmed targets based on FTP percentages are individually wrong. For endurance rides, ERG mode is also problematic because LT1 ranges from 50-85% of FTP across athletes and shifts 30-40 W day-to-day based on fatigue. Setting ERG at a fixed watt for an endurance ride risks training in the intensity "black hole." Legitimate uses exist — recovery rides at confirmed easy wattage, Zwift racing with simulated hills, fartlek on virtual courses, and remedial pacing education for beginners — but these should be exceptions, not defaults. The goal state is an athlete who can execute any workout by feel alone, with power data reviewed afterward for confirmation.

**Recommendations:**
- Default to free-ride (resistance or slope) mode for all interval training
- Never use ERG mode for VO2max or anaerobic capacity intervals
- If using ERG for endurance rides, do not turn it on for at least the first hour; verify LT1 empirically first
- Develop RPE calibration as a primary training skill — aim to execute workouts by feel with power as confirmation

**Sources:** Episodes 1440628345-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-22-the-case-against-erg-mode.txt, 1164677890-empiricalcyclingpodcast-200k-the-best-and-worst-workouts.txt

### Progression Should Be Time-Based Before It Is Power-Based

Across multiple episodes, the hosts emphasize that adding duration at the same power is safer and more effective than raising watts. The simplest FTP interval progression is adding 5 minutes per rep to the previous session. If an athlete cannot progress time (more sets or longer sets), the likely causes are accumulated fatigue, overestimated FTP, or insufficient nutrition — not a need for more intensity. This principle extends to over-unders and intermittent efforts: build from 3x10 to 3x20 to 2x30 minutes of 15-15s before considering raising the power target. The "boiling frog" approach to threshold training — alternating tempo and threshold segments within a long set (e.g., 2 min tempo / 3 min threshold / 3 min tempo / 2 min threshold as 2-3x30) — is another time-based strategy that reduces psychological stress while accumulating threshold-range volume. Going to true failure in a workout is explicitly listed as a bad practice; workouts should have open-ended structures with RPE-based stop points rather than binary pass-fail outcomes.

**Recommendations:**
- Progress interval training by adding 5 minutes per rep or per session before raising power targets
- Use open-ended interval structures with RPE stop points rather than fixed pass-fail targets
- If unable to progress total duration, investigate fatigue, FTP accuracy, or nutrition before adding intensity
- Consider alternating tempo/threshold segments within long sets to accumulate time at threshold with less mental strain

**Sources:** Episodes 1164677890-empiricalcyclingpodcast-200k-the-best-and-worst-workouts.txt, 1472759278-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-27-programming-over-under-workouts.txt, 1253680522-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-19-ftp-testing-shortcuts.txt

### Repeated Sprint Training Targets a Distinct Physiological Quality From FTP

Repeated sprint ability (RSA) is presented as a separate endurance quality from FTP-based fitness. The mechanism is that large motor units — typically under-trained aerobically because they are seldom recruited during steady-state riding — need targeted work to become aerobically capable. When these motor units have better aerobic capacity, they preserve their own glycogen stores and maintain force production deeper into races. This explains why an athlete can have a high FTP yet perform poorly in criteriums or cyclocross: the large motor units fatigue during repeated accelerations because they lack aerobic development. Specific RST formats include 5-second maximal sprints every 30-60 seconds, FTP with accelerations layered on top every 2-5 minutes, and the stochastic tempo rides described above. A key diagnostic: during FTP-with-sprints sessions, if peak acceleration power falls from approximately 900 W to 450 W by the end, the larger motor units are fatiguing and need more training. RSA adaptation can take months to years and is highly individual; naturally strong sprinters can dig deep holes requiring extra recovery.

**Recommendations:**
- Train repeated sprint ability as a distinct quality from FTP, especially for criterium, CX, and MTB racers
- Use FTP-with-sprints sessions and monitor whether peak acceleration power degrades across the workout
- Set acceleration power at a level sustainable across the full session rather than starting high and fading
- Allow months of consistent RSA work before expecting measurable improvement; adaptation is slow

**Sources:** Episodes 936171130-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-27-training-repeated-sprint-ability.txt

### Normalized Power Is Unreliable for Variable-Power Workouts

Normalized power (NP) is repeatedly flagged as a poor metric for stochastic, intermittent, or race-like training. In criterium-style efforts with 30-40 second surges, NP can read 20-30 W above actual threshold — leading athletes to incorrectly conclude their FTP has risen. NP is course-dependent and can be "busted" by short hard efforts that disproportionately inflate the rolling average. For intermittent work like 15-15s and 30-30s, average power is the appropriate metric for tracking progress and estimating metabolic load. If using intermittent efforts to roughly estimate FTP, use average power of the set, not NP. The same caution applies to stochastic tempo rides: use a fixed test course and compare completion times or average power rather than NP to track fitness changes.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not use normalized power to adjust FTP based on criterium, cyclocross, or stochastic workout data
- Track intermittent interval progress using average power on the "on" portions, not NP
- For stochastic fitness tracking, use fixed courses with time-to-completion or average power as the metric
- Be skeptical of any NP reading from a highly variable-power session

**Sources:** Episodes 936171130-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-27-training-repeated-sprint-ability.txt, 1472759278-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-27-programming-over-under-workouts.txt

The most significant evolution across these episodes concerns the relationship between high-intensity interval work and year-round training. ## 6. Recovery & Periodization

### Rest Is a Training Input, Not the Absence of Training

Recovery is where adaptation actually occurs. The training stimulus breaks the body down; rest rebuilds it stronger. Yet most self-coached athletes treat rest as lost time, leading to chronic under-recovery that masquerades as a training plateau. Adam Pulford (CTS coach) frames this explicitly: periodize recovery with the same intentionality as training. Rory Porteous reinforces this by noting that when athletes plateau, the correct first response is almost always to improve recovery quality before adding volume or intensity. James McKay provides a striking illustration: his lowest-volume client produced measurable performance gains during a vacation week of zero training, high sleep, and low stress — demonstrating that for a chronically under-recovered athlete, rest alone can be more productive than training. The psychological dimension matters too: athletes who are most resistant to taking rest are often those who most need it. Fear of CTL drops, fear of weight gain, and social media comparison pressure all drive under-resting behavior that is physiologically counterproductive. Feeling worse on days 2-3 of a rest week is normal — it reflects the body processing accumulated fatigue, not a sign that rest is failing. Meaningful detraining takes weeks, not the days of a rest week.

**Recommendations:**
- Treat rest weeks as planned training investments, not emergency responses to breakdown
- Default to rest when uncertain whether to train or rest — "when in doubt, sit it out"; this is rarely the wrong choice for recreational athletes
- Accept that feeling worse early in a rest week is normal and expected
- Do not restrict calories during rest weeks — energy restriction impairs the recovery processes rest is designed to support

**Sources:** Episodes `1244788597-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-11-the-best-workout-is-not-working-out-with-adam-pulford.txt`, `1594046289-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-27-common-reasons-for-fitness-plateaus-and-their-fixes-with-rory-porteous.txt`, `2114034129-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-58-why-rest-can-be-so-scary.txt`, `2265497594-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-72-the-roles.txt`, `2291300408-ten-minute-tips-75-the-art-of.txt`

### Planned Rest Weeks Beat Reactive Rest Every Time

By the time most athletes feel they need a rest week, they needed one a week or two ago. The subjective fatigue signal lags the physiological state by days. This makes reactive rest — rest triggered by feeling terrible — a planning failure rather than a success. Planned rest at fixed intervals (every 3rd or 4th week) prevents the deep fatigue hole that reactive rest attempts to climb out of. A standard starting structure is 3 weeks building followed by 1 week recovery, adjustable to 2:1 for athletes who plateau on a 3:1 cadence. Sprint power provides a simple, reliable early warning system: a 5-10 second maximal sprint significantly below your rested baseline signals accumulated fatigue before subjective tiredness is fully apparent. Sprint power tests are more sensitive to CNS fatigue than FTP-based assessments and serve as a low-cost daily readiness check. TTE (time to exhaustion at FTP) is another canary: a declining TTE while FTP holds steady signals eroding capacity. Getting sick frequently is also diagnostic — recurrent illness indicates rest weeks are being skipped or taken too late, and should be treated as a training load problem.

**Recommendations:**
- Schedule rest weeks at fixed intervals (3:1 or 2:1 build-to-rest ratio); do not wait until forced
- Use a 5-10 second sprint power test against a personal rested baseline as a readiness indicator
- Track TTE alongside FTP; declining TTE with stable FTP is an early warning of accumulated fatigue
- If workouts feel terrible for multiple consecutive sessions, swap immediately to rest rather than an easier workout
- Start a rest week early if fatigue signs appear before the scheduled date — do not wait for the calendar

### Recovery Rides Must Be Genuinely Easy or They Are Not Recovery Rides

A recovery ride done at endurance pace is not a recovery ride — it is a low-stimulus training ride that delays recovery. Genuine recovery requires near-zero power output. Coaches prescribe sub-100W for most athletes' recovery rides; Marinus Petersen uses a ceiling of 15 TSS per recovery ride. The "glass cranks" mental model captures the principle: imagine your cranks are made of glass, and any real pedaling force would break them. If you cannot ride that gently, do not ride at all. The belief that active recovery rides "flush lactate" and accelerate recovery has weak evidentiary support, and riding too hard on recovery days likely extends the recovery window. Even clearly sub-threshold easy endurance rides can delay recovery when the nervous system is sufficiently fatigued — HRV data shows continued suppression with moderate sub-threshold riding in fatigued athletes. Coaches who prescribe "active recovery" rides with 30-40 TSS are not prescribing recovery; they are prescribing low-stimulus training. The "100W jail" concept reinforces this: if rest means easy riding, it must be genuinely easy, calibrated to the individual athlete's endurance floor.

**Recommendations:**
- Keep recovery rides below 100W (or your individual sub-endurance floor); use the "glass cranks" test
- If you cannot ride genuinely easy, take a full day off instead
- Target under 15 TSS for any ride labeled "recovery"
- Do not schedule heavy lifting or sprints on recovery days — these are high-stress stimuli
- Recognize that "active recovery" may delay rather than accelerate recovery for a truly fatigued athlete

**Sources:** Episodes `1594046289-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-27-common-reasons-for-fitness-plateaus-and-their-fixes-with-rory-porteous.txt`, `1675129809-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-31-how-to-take-a-rest-day-and-how-to-avoid-screwing-it-up.txt`, `1843418724-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-34-quantifying-training-volume-with-marinus-petersen.txt`, `2114034129-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-58-why-rest-can-be-so-scary.txt`

### Off-Bike Stress Counts as Training Load

Work deadlines, poor sleep, family conflict, and psychological exhaustion are physiologically equivalent to moderate training load. A stressful workday without riding is not "free" recovery time — it accumulates fatigue that must be accounted for in the training load budget. Mental and psychological exhaustion warrants a rest week just as much as physical soreness does. Rory Porteous and the podcast hosts consistently frame off-bike stress as a variable that must be tracked and reported, not as background noise. When leaving workout feedback for a coach or a training journal, off-bike context (sleep quality, nutrition adequacy, life stressors, illness onset) is not complaining — it is essential training data. A 20W power drop in a workout means very different things depending on whether the athlete slept 5 hours or 8. Without that context, training decisions are made blind. Subjective metrics including mood, leg feel, motivation, and RPE are valid data points for adjusting training load. Off-bike stress is inherently difficult to quantify; subjective ratings are more useful than attempting to assign TSS equivalents.

**Recommendations:**
- Log off-bike stressors (sleep, nutrition, work stress, illness) alongside every workout
- Treat high-stress non-training days as a draw on your recovery budget, not as neutral
- Report illness onset early to your coach; do not wait until fully sick
- Use subjective metrics (RPE, mood, motivation) alongside objective data for load decisions
- When life stress spikes, reduce training load proactively rather than waiting for performance to decline

### Endurance Rides Paced at LT1 Are Paced Too Hard

Most athletes over-pace their endurance rides, riding at or near LT1 when they should be well below it. The target for the bulk of a long endurance ride is 60-80% of LT1 power — for a rider with a 350W LT1, that means approximately 200-250W, not 350W. Fatigue accumulation is non-linear above LT1: even 10W over threshold for extended periods carries disproportionate cost. LT1 itself drops approximately 10W after about 2.5 hours of riding, meaning a pace that starts sub-LT1 can drift above it as the ride progresses. The implication is that "feeling easy" is not sufficient to confirm sub-LT1 pacing for trained athletes — perception calibration drifts along with physiology. Properly paced sub-LT1 riding allows extraordinary volumes (20-30 hours per week) without overtraining, whereas poorly paced endurance rides at LT1 create fatigue that constrains total weekly training capacity. Endurance rides are the flexible element of a training week: plan interval days first, then fill remaining days with endurance work at appropriate intensity.

**Recommendations:**
- Target 60-80% of LT1 power for long endurance rides, not "at" LT1
- Account for LT1 drift on rides over 2.5 hours by reducing target power as the ride progresses
- Test and establish your individual LT1 rather than relying on FTP percentage proxies
- Plan interval days first in a training week, then schedule endurance rides around them
- Use heart rate or power to confirm pacing — perceived effort is unreliable for sub-LT1 calibration

### Sweet Spot Has Hidden Fatigue Costs That Undermine Its Time Efficiency

Sweet spot training (~90% FTP) is popular because it produces broad adaptations and structured training platforms can easily prescribe it. However, its perceived time efficiency disappears when the full fatigue-recovery cost is accounted for. A 2-hour sweet spot session may provide stimulus efficiently, but the recovery debt it generates constrains what can be done in subsequent days. Many popular plans built almost entirely on sweet spot produce good initial gains then plateau because sweet spot does not optimally develop either the aerobic floor (LT1) or the aerobic ceiling (VO2max). It creates athletes who are "moderately good at everything and excellent at nothing." Sweet spot is best used as a supplement to a full intensity distribution — a maintenance intensity or an add-on — rather than the primary FTP-building tool. Maximum effective dose per session is approximately 2-3 hours, beyond which fatigue cost outweighs marginal benefit. Over-under intervals (alternating just below and above FTP) are an effective way to structure sweet spot stimulus.

**Recommendations:**
- Use sweet spot as a supplement to a polarized intensity distribution, not as the sole training method
- Cap sweet spot at 2-3 hours per session; account for the multi-day recovery cost
- Consider over-under intervals as a structured alternative to steady sweet spot
- For newer athletes, sweet spot builds a useful base; for advanced athletes, it provides diminishing returns
- Do not confuse sweet spot's ease of prescription with physiological optimality

### A Simple 12-Week Plan Structure Reveals What Matters Most

Rory Porteous's "simplest training plan" thought experiment exposes the core structural decisions that matter: rest week format before workout format, block progression with increasing specificity, and explicit VO2max block design. Block 1 (weeks 1-4) builds sweet spot from 60 to 110 minutes per session. Block 2 (weeks 5-8) develops VO2max with FTP maintenance. Block 3 (weeks 9-12) adds anaerobic work for race preparation. Rest week structure is defined first: day off, recovery ride, endurance ride, openers workout, long endurance ride. VO2max blocks target 15 minutes accumulated at the physiological state associated with pVO2max using 4-minute intervals, accepting up to 20W power decline across the set. When introducing any new training method, a gap analysis should precede adoption: identify the specific limiter the method addresses. If current training is producing gains, do not change it — novelty is not a reason to disrupt a working plan. New methods should be introduced incrementally (once per week for 1-3 weeks before increasing frequency), and rest periods should be planned first, with hard work scheduled around them.

**Recommendations:**
- Structure training in 3-4 week blocks with increasing specificity toward your target event
- Define rest week format before defining workout format for each block
- For VO2max blocks, accumulate 15 minutes at pVO2max using 4-minute intervals; accept up to 20W decline
- Plan 1.5-2 weeks of recovery after a VO2max block, not just one week
- Before adopting a new method, identify the specific gap it fills; if current training works, do not change it

### In-Season Training Should Be Easier Than Off-Season Training, Not Harder

Most cyclists train too hard during race season and wonder why they peak early or fade in the second half. Racing itself provides substantial high-intensity stimulus — adding hard training sessions on top is often redundant or counterproductive. This principle is especially acute for criterium racers: approximately 80% of the energy to recover from a sprint comes from aerobic metabolism, and in-race anaerobic efforts already exceed the optimal dose for adaptation. Adding anaerobic training sessions during race season compounds fatigue without improving race performance. The practical framework is A/B/C race prioritization: taper and recover around A races only, use B races as training, and treat C races as events that require no schedule modification. Easy sub-LT1 volume maintains VO2max through race season even when hard training is reduced. A mid-season break of 1-2 weeks at reduced load is non-negotiable for athletes with dense race calendars. The in-season heuristic: never pedal harder than feels good during training; save maximal efforts for races.

**Recommendations:**
- Assign A/B/C priority to every race before the season begins; structure recovery around A races only
- Limit dedicated anaerobic training to once per week or less during base; eliminate it entirely during race season
- Schedule a 1-2 week mid-season break based on accumulated fatigue, not a fixed calendar date
- Maintain easy sub-LT1 endurance volume during race season to preserve aerobic base
- Do not add VO2max blocks when racing weekly; the recovery demands are additive and lead to fatigue accumulation

### CTL Is Not Fitness and TSS Is Not the Best Load Metric

CTL (Chronic Training Load) measures training stress, not fitness. A rising CTL can coexist with declining performance if recovery is insufficient, and a dropping CTL during a rest week reflects correct training, not fitness loss. Using CTL as a training target leads to maladaptive behavior: chasing the number, conflating fatigue with readiness, and resisting necessary rest. Marinus Petersen advocates replacing TSS as the primary load metric with two complementary tools: hours for sub-LT1 aerobic work and kilojoules for above-threshold work. TSS conflates intensity and duration, making recovery rides look harder than they are and short hard sessions look easier. The practical consequence is that TSS-based planning can systematically underestimate the cost of high-intensity work and overestimate the cost of easy volume. CTL retains value as a retrospective descriptive tool — it describes what you did, not what it did to you. Fitness should be assessed through actual performance tests and subjective readiness.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not set CTL as a training goal; set performance goals and let CTL be a byproduct
- Use hours to quantify sub-LT1 volume and kilojoules to quantify above-threshold work
- Assess fitness through performance tests and subjective readiness, not CTL trajectory
- Accept CTL drops during rest weeks as correct and desirable
- Small daily volume additions compound: an extra 10 minutes per ride equals approximately 50 additional hours per year

**Sources:** Episodes `1363710067-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-24-you-are-not-your-ctl.txt`, `1843418724-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-34-quantifying-training-volume-with-marinus-petersen.txt`

### Intermediate Athletes Plateau From Stale Plans and Poor Recovery, Not Biological Limits

Cyclists with 2-5 years of structured training who gain 10-30W per year in FTP plateau not because they have reached their ceiling but because they repeat the same plan, neglect recovery, or fail to develop training skills. Repeating a plan that worked in year 1 by year 3 is a mistake even if the plan was excellent — the athlete has adapted to it, and the same stimulus no longer drives change. Recovery is the major differentiator at the intermediate level: athletes who prioritize recovery advance faster than those who add volume. Training skills — pacing, readiness assessment, overreaching detection, progressive overload management — are undervalued relative to physical attributes. Improving these skills often produces more gains than improving raw watts. Derailment recovery is another skill to develop: when illness, travel, or life disrupts training, having a protocol for returning to load safely prevents the "catch up" impulse that compounds fatigue. The instinct to interpret slowing gains as needing more training is usually wrong; the correct diagnosis is almost always better structure and more recovery.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not repeat the same annual plan; adaptation requires novel stimulus and progressive overload
- Prioritize recovery quality over volume or intensity increases when gains slow
- Develop training skills: learn to recognize early overreaching signs (performance decline, motivation loss, sleep disruption)
- Build a derailment recovery protocol for returning to training after disruptions
- Use "training age" rather than chronological age or CTL to diagnose plateau issues

**Sources:** Episodes `2061437404-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-52-intermediate-training-mistakes-and-solutions.txt`, `2265497594-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-72-the-roles.txt`, `1889036004-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-41-how-to-try-new-training-methods.txt`

### Auto-Regulation Is a Skill That Complements Structure, Not a Replacement for It

Training by "vibes" — auto-regulation — is most effective when layered onto a foundation of structured training principles, not used as an excuse to abandon structure entirely. Kolie Moore frames this as a spectrum: most athletes should operate somewhere between 25% and 75% vibes-based, depending on experience, schedule predictability, and self-awareness. The key is maintaining a prioritized list of training goals for each week or block, so that when a good-legs day arrives, you know exactly which workout to deploy — primary goal first, secondary if time allows, tertiary optional. Athletes with unpredictable schedules (medical professionals, shift workers, new parents) and those with multi-sport interests benefit most from auto-regulation, but also need the most discipline around recovery. Short FTP efforts (3–5 minutes) on days 3–5 after a hard block serve as an objective readiness check: if threshold power comes easily and you feel you could sustain it for 30+ minutes, you're recovered; if you're counting down seconds, rest more. Short sprints provide a complementary neuromuscular readiness signal. When doing less-structured workouts, still target a specific energy system per session — the "least structured viable workout" concept means giving athletes a time-at-intensity target (e.g., "60 minutes at FTP, 10-minute minimum intervals") rather than rigid interval prescriptions, while avoiding the trap of defaulting to kitchen-sink efforts that train everything shallowly. Importantly, undercooking intensity by 5–20% is far preferable to overcooking by 10% — the recovery hole from excess is disproportionately deep. Tim Cusick's suggestion of hiring a coach for 1–3 years specifically to learn the training rhythm before self-coaching is now endorsed by Kolie as the most effective path for athletes who ultimately want to self-coach.

**Recommendations:**
- Maintain a prioritized list of weekly training goals; on good-legs days, execute the highest-priority session first
- Use 3–5 minute FTP efforts and short sprints as low-cost readiness checks before committing to a hard session
- When auto-regulating workouts, still target a specific energy system — avoid defaulting to unstructured "bit of everything" rides
- Accept ±5–10W of target power outdoors as close enough; do not expect erg-mode precision
- Track mood and motivation as early-warning metrics for overreaching or under-fueling
- When life stress is high, you likely have only one day of truly good legs — deploy them on the highest-priority session
- Consider working with a coach for 1–3 years to learn the rhythm of push-and-pull before self-coaching

### Evolution of Views

The podcast's treatment of recovery and periodization shows a clear trajectory from conventional periodization toward increasingly aggressive rest advocacy and nuanced fatigue management. Early episodes like the Watts Doc criterium episode (599421588) present aerobic base as primarily a performance tool — recovery is discussed mechanistically (creatine phosphate regeneration) rather than as a training philosophy, and in-season training adjustments are framed without a broader periodization framework. By the middle episodes, the framing shifts: Adam Pulford's appearance (1244788597) introduces "the best workout is not working out" as a deliberate provocation, and Rory Porteous (1594046289) begins systematically cataloguing recovery failures as the primary cause of plateaus.

The most notable evolution is in recovery ride prescription. Rory Porteous acknowledges that recovery ride intensity guidance has become "more conservative over time" as coaches recognized how much even easy riding accumulates stress. The sub-100W and sub-15 TSS thresholds (1594046289, 1843418724) represent a significant departure from earlier norms where "easy endurance" pace was considered recovery. By the later episodes (2114034129), the hosts go further, presenting HRV evidence that even clearly sub-threshold riding can delay recovery for truly fatigued athletes.

Sweet spot's role undergoes notable revision. Early in the podcast's history, sweet spot is presented as an efficient default; by the mid-period episodes (1955503299), the hosts are explicitly cautioning against sweet-spot-dominant plans, framing the hidden fatigue cost as the key insight most athletes miss. This parallels a broader move away from TSS-centric thinking: Marinus Petersen's appearance (1843418724) introduces the hours-plus-kilojoules framework as a replacement for TSS, reflecting practitioner skepticism of the Banister impulse-response model that underpins CTL.

The in-season training philosophy shows the clearest periodization arc. The early criterium episode (599421588) argues against excessive anaerobic work during race season on physiological grounds. By the later episodes (1849589265, 2129494140), this has expanded into a comprehensive A/B/C race prioritization system with explicit mid-season break scheduling — a more structured framework than the earlier "just train less hard" guidance. The 12-week plan thought experiment (1960584159) marks a further shift toward explicit structural principles: define rest before defining work, progress from general to specific, and use gap analysis before adopting new methods.

Rest week scheduling evolves from general advice ("take rest weeks regularly") toward specific diagnostic tools. Sprint power as a fatigue canary (2090477523) and TTE tracking alongside FTP (2129494140) represent concrete readiness-assessment methods that replace the earlier reliance on subjective feel alone. Rory Porteous (2129494140) describes auto-regulation by time rather than watt targets as a recent change in his own coaching, marking a shift from prescriptive to adaptive periodization. The endurance pacing guidance similarly tightens: the 60-80% of LT1 target and the LT1 drift correction (1799552662) provide more precise anchoring than the earlier general advice to "ride easy."

Throughout the later Ten Minute Tips series (episodes 55-72), there is an increasing emphasis on the psychological barriers to rest — fear of fitness loss, social media comparison, CTL anxiety — suggesting the hosts recognized that the physiological case for recovery had been made but athletes were still failing to implement it for psychological reasons. James McKay (2265497594) frames diagnosing recovery limitation in low-volume athletes as "a relatively recent conceptual development in the coaching community," marking a departure from the traditional assumption that low-volume athletes simply needed more volume.

The most recent episode on auto-regulation (2291300408) represents a synthesis of the podcast's evolving periodization philosophy. Where earlier episodes prescribed specific rest week schedules and readiness tests, this episode advocates teaching athletes to internalize those principles through a blend of structure and flexibility. Kolie explicitly notes he changed his mind on recommending coaches for self-coaching development — previously he did not suggest it, but after Tim Cusick's appearance he now actively recommends 1–3 years with a coach as an investment in learning training rhythm. The episode also crystallizes a principle that emerged gradually across earlier episodes: undercooking training by a small margin is always preferable to overcooking, because the recovery cost of excess is non-linear. This marks the podcast's clearest statement yet that auto-regulation, properly calibrated, is not a concession to laziness but a sophisticated training skill — one that the hosts now consider essential for long-term athletic development.

## 7. Strength Training

### Cyclists Should Lift Heavy in the 4-6 Rep Range as Their Primary Strength Prescription

The consistent recommendation across the podcast is that 3 sets of 4-6 reps at roughly 80-85% of one-rep max is the "bread and butter" strength prescription for cyclists -- analogous to how 2x20 at FTP is the bread and butter aerobic workout. This rep range builds base strength and neural adaptation without excessive hypertrophy or injury risk. The 1-3 rep range is explicitly discouraged: it is sport-specific to powerlifting and carries disproportionate risk for non-lifters, with the hosts noting that even Olympic weightlifters often skip true 1RM attempts. For beginners or those returning to the gym, an adaptation phase of 8-12 reps at moderate weight should always precede heavy work to embed movement patterns and prepare connective tissue. Importantly, rep ranges exist on a continuum -- sets of 8-12 still build strength, just as sets of 5 still produce some hypertrophy. Cyclists should never attempt true one-rep max testing; instead, track progression by doing more reps or more sets at the same weight. For hypertrophy goals (such as building anaerobic capacity), any load between 30-80% of 1RM taken near failure produces similar muscle growth. The key is getting close to failure without reaching actual failure -- "really hard but I can totally do it."

**Recommendations:**
- Default to 3x5 or 5x5 at approximately 80-85% 1RM for primary strength work
- Begin with 2-3 weeks of sets of 8-12 at moderate weight before progressing to heavy loads
- Never attempt true 1RM testing; progress by adding reps or sets at the same weight, then adding ~10 lbs when you hit your target
- Aim to get close to failure but not to actual failure; if technique breaks down, that counts as failure
- For hypertrophy phases, sets of 8-15 (up to 20 for isolation work) taken near failure are effective

### Single-Leg Strength Transfers to the Bike Far Better Than Bilateral Squats

One of the podcast's most distinctive claims is that bilateral squat strength is a poor predictor of on-bike performance compared to single-leg strength. The hosts cite an anecdote from the Australian national track team where two riders were approximately 100 kg apart in back squat but only 10-20 kg apart in single-leg leg press, with comparable flying 200m times. The "bilateral deficit" -- the gap between single-leg strength and half of bilateral strength -- represents a major opportunity for cyclists. If single-leg strength is only 55-60% of bilateral strength, easy gains are available. The hosts themselves train to approximately 75%. In the periodization model, the single-leg specificity phase is where the biggest bike-transferable strength gains happen: single-leg squats become the first exercise at heavy loads (4-6 reps), and this phase directly precedes race preparation. The leg press is also endorsed as more cycling-specific than barbell squats, since the stability conditions (no balance demands) more closely mirror the pedaling environment.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize single-leg work (single-leg squats, single-leg leg press) as the most cycling-transferable strength exercise
- Assess your bilateral deficit: if single-leg strength is below 65-70% of bilateral, focus on closing that gap
- Use the leg press as a cycling-specific complement to or substitute for barbell squats
- Always start with the weak or injured leg when doing single-leg work
- In periodization, place the single-leg specificity phase after the bilateral strength phase and before race prep

### Keep Gym Sessions Short and Total Working Sets Low

A recurring mistake the hosts identify is cyclists doing too many exercises and too many sets per session. Kolie explicitly admits he used to program excessively long sessions (squat + single-leg press + deadlift + 2-3 other things, taking 3+ hours) and has since moved to much shorter, more focused sessions. The current recommendation is to cap heavy working sets at roughly 6-9 per session, split across 2-3 exercises, with the heaviest compound movement always first. A typical session might be 4 sets of squats, 3 sets of single-leg work, and 2 sets of knee extensions. Guest coaches RJ Boergers and Angelo Gingerelli independently recommend keeping gym sessions to approximately 40-45 minutes. Starting at about 4 heavy working sets and building to about 7 over weeks is the recommended progression. The rationale is simple: connective tissue recovers slower than muscle, cyclists still need to ride, and a single bad weightlifting workout with accumulated fatigue genuinely can ruin a season through injury.

**Recommendations:**
- Cap heavy working sets at 6-9 per session across 2-3 exercises
- Start with approximately 4 heavy working sets and build to 7 over weeks
- Structure workouts with the heaviest compound movement first, then accessory work
- Keep sessions to roughly 40-45 minutes
- Do not add gym sessions on top of an existing full cycling plan without reducing ride volume or intensity

### The Squat Variation You Choose Does Not Matter -- Pick What Fits Your Body

Multiple episodes converge on the message that cyclists waste energy debating high bar vs. low bar vs. front squat when EMG studies show that glute and quad activation is essentially the same across all three variations. The specific squat variation matters far less than people think. The choice should be driven by individual anatomy -- hip socket depth, femur length, ankle mobility, shoulder mobility -- not by internet dogma. If mobility or injury concerns prevent one variation, switching to another (or to split squats, leg press, or belt squat) is completely valid. Front squats use lighter loads (~80-85% of back squat max), are more quad-dominant, and suit people who cannot place weight on their back. Bar position exists on a spectrum, not a binary. The hosts recommend finding your stance by squatting in socks on a smooth floor and wiggling your feet to discover what allows the deepest comfortable range of motion. Lifting shoes with a raised heel can help achieve more depth but are not a cure-all for lacking mobility.

**Recommendations:**
- Choose whichever squat variation you can perform safely and comfortably -- do not switch variations because someone online said to
- If you already know one variation well, stick with it; "squatting is not your sport"
- Experiment with stance width using the socks-on-smooth-floor method
- Consider front squats if you have back issues or cannot place weight on your shoulders
- Lifting shoes with a 15-21mm heel drop can improve depth and upright posture
- If no squat variation works well, split squats, leg press, or belt squat are legitimate substitutes

### Use Proper Equipment Without Ego -- Belts, Straps, and Shoes Make Lifting Safer and More Effective

Across multiple episodes, the hosts repeatedly push back against the gym-culture idea that using equipment is "cheating" or must be "earned." Belts increase intra-abdominal pressure and help you brace harder; they do not make your abs weaker, because you lift heavier loads with the belt and your abs still work at full capacity. Kolie calls the "you didn't earn the weight if you used a belt" attitude "absolute bullshit," distinguishing belts (which help you brace) from powerlifting squat suits (which mechanically assist the lift). Lifting straps should be used for any pulling exercise -- deadlifts, rows, lunges, lat pulldowns -- even at light weights, because thinking about grip "snaps neural drive" and causes unnecessary fatigue in non-target muscles. Hard-soled shoes with a raised heel improve squat depth and reduce lower-back stress. Kyle reports that switching from 15mm to 21mm heel-drop shoes produced an immediate improvement in squat depth. None of this requires expensive gear: basic cotton Harbinger straps, a simple belt, and any hard-soled shoe with a heel will do.

**Recommendations:**
- Use a lifting belt for all heavy compound movements; there is no minimum load threshold
- Use lifting straps for deadlifts, rows, lunges, and pulldowns to remove grip as a limiting factor
- Invest in hard-soled lifting shoes with a raised heel (15-21mm); avoid soft or squishy shoes
- When switching to higher-heeled shoes, reduce weight initially as you adjust to deeper range of motion
- Do not let ego or gym culture prevent you from using equipment that makes training safer and more effective

### Energy Balance -- Not Lifting Strategy -- Is What Prevents Unwanted Weight Gain

Cyclists frequently avoid the gym out of fear of gaining weight. The podcast addresses this head-on with research evidence establishing a clear hierarchy: energy balance (caloric intake) is far and away the most important variable controlling hypertrophy, followed by lifting heavy with low reps, then keeping total volume load low. Skipping the eccentric phase -- a commonly suggested strategy -- has essentially zero effect on lower-body hypertrophy based on meta-analysis evidence. Kolie notes he personally got "pretty big and pretty strong" doing concentric-only deadlifts. The fear of "accidentally getting huge" is unfounded: even subjects fed an extra 2000 calories per day only gained 3 kg in 8 weeks. Initial weight gain of 2-3 lbs when returning to the gym is mostly water retention, glycogen, and inflammation, not muscle tissue. For cyclists who want strength gains with minimal mass gain, the prescription is 2-4 heavy sets per week at low reps, without eating in a caloric surplus. As few as 1 heavy single-to-triple per week is sufficient to maintain strength.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not eat in a caloric surplus while lifting if you want to avoid weight gain; energy balance is the dominant factor
- For strength with minimal hypertrophy: sets of 1-5 reps, minimize total volume, progress weight slowly (~5 lbs every 2-3 weeks)
- Do not worry about skipping eccentrics as a hypertrophy-avoidance strategy; the evidence shows it makes no meaningful difference for lower body
- Expect 2-3 lbs of initial weight gain when returning to the gym; this is water and glycogen, not muscle
- Do not start creatine loading while simultaneously trying to determine maintenance calories

### Periodize Strength Training Across the Season -- Do Not Bolt It On as an Afterthought

The podcast lays out a complete periodization plan that must be coordinated with aerobic training, not treated as a separate program. The phases are: (1) adaptation (weeks 1-2, light loads, technique focus), (2) hypertrophy (~4 weeks at 8-15 reps, paired with low-intensity riding only), (3) maximal strength (~4 weeks at 4-6 reps), (4) single-leg specificity/neural drive (single-leg squats heavy, trap bar deadlifts), and (5) maintenance (in-season, 2 sets of 5-10 reps at 6-7/10 RPE, once per week or even every two weeks). Deload weeks should occur every 3-4 weeks, either cutting reps in half or cutting load in half. Once race prep begins with threshold, sweet spot, or VO2max work, gym work drops to maintenance only. The concurrent training effect (Hickson 1980) means that after roughly 7 weeks of combined training, strength gains begin to plateau due to molecular interference between mTOR and PGC-1a pathways. This is why the off-season is the time for heavy lifting and the in-season is for maintenance only.

**Recommendations:**
- Plan gym periodization alongside aerobic periodization from the start
- Follow the phase sequence: adaptation, hypertrophy, max strength, single-leg specificity, maintenance
- Deload every 3-4 weeks by cutting load in half
- Reduce gym work to maintenance (1-2 sessions/week, low RPE) once race-specific intervals begin
- Do not do sweet spot 3-5x/week plus lifting 3x/week unless you have exceptional recovery
- When tapering endurance volume by 20%, taper gym volume by 20% as well rather than cutting to zero

### The Minimum Effective Dose for Maintaining Strength Is Surprisingly Low

One of the more practically useful findings discussed on the podcast is that maintaining strength requires far less volume than building it. Based on research from Dr. Pack, the minimum effective dose to increase one-rep max in trained lifters is only 3-6 sets of 1-5 reps across 1-3 sessions per week at approximately 7-9/10 RPE. For pure maintenance, even less is needed: 3 heavy singles once a week at roughly 7/10 RPE, or a couple of sets of 10 at 5-6/10 RPE twice per week. For in-season cyclists, one heavy set per week at 6-7/10 RPE is enough to maintain strength skill. This means cyclists can preserve meaningful gym stimulus during the competitive season without materially impacting recovery or ride quality. The hosts frame this through the lens of "strength as a skill" -- losing numbers on your squat after months away from the gym does not mean you have gotten weaker; it means you have lost proficiency at that specific movement pattern. Peak power on the bike is a more relevant measure of whether strength is being maintained.

**Recommendations:**
- For in-season maintenance: 1-3 heavy sets per week at 6-7/10 RPE is sufficient
- Measure strength retention via peak power on the bike rather than squat numbers
- If sprint power is improving, you are maintaining or gaining functional strength even if gym numbers drop
- A couple of sets of 10 at 5-6/10 RPE, twice per week, suffices for general gym maintenance
- Do not assume you need a full gym program to hold onto off-season strength gains

### Proper Breathing and Bracing Technique Prevents Injury Under Load

The Valsalva maneuver -- inhaling a deep belly breath and bracing the core before each rep -- is the correct breathing technique for heavy lifting. This creates intra-abdominal pressure that stabilizes the spine and prevents back injuries during squats, deadlifts, and similar compound movements. The common advice to "never hold your breath while lifting" is characterized as misleading: proper per-rep bracing actually prevents injury. The technique should be performed per-rep (inhale, brace, lift, exhale), not held across multiple reps. Breathing should expand the abdomen, obliques, and low back to create a "cylinder of tension," not raise the shoulders. Anti-rotation core exercises (pushup position shoulder taps, plate drags in plank position, banded Pallof press variations) are recommended over traditional crunches and planks for developing the core stability cyclists need. Having visible abs does not mean you have a strong core for cycling -- you can look strong but be functionally weak. "Neutral spine" during lifting is a range, not a single rigid position; being hyper-concerned about minor back rounding is unnecessary.

**Recommendations:**
- Use the Valsalva maneuver (deep belly breath + full core brace) before each heavy rep; exhale and re-breathe between reps
- Practice the bracing technique unloaded at home before using it under a barbell
- Prioritize anti-rotation core exercises (Pallof press, shoulder taps, plate drags) over crunches and planks
- Include anti-rotation exercises in every gym session
- "Neutral spine" is a range; do not obsess over minor positional variation during the lift
- People with hypertension should consult a medical professional before heavy strength training

### Sleep and Recovery Must Not Be Sacrificed for Gym Time

The hosts make a strong case that sleep is "one of the most anabolic things on the planet" and that consistently trading sleep for gym sessions is counterproductive. If you normally get 7.5-9 hours and are running on 6.5-7 with that groggy feeling, skip the heavy lifting -- poor sleep increases injury risk, and a single bad weightlifting session can ruin a season through injury in a way that a bad bike ride cannot. Recovery from lifting is highly individual, potentially requiring 2-4 days after heavy sessions. Soreness does not map 1:1 to fatigue: you can often ride when sore, but pay attention to when you genuinely cannot perform. Manage soreness with gentle movement -- walking, easy spinning for 10-20 minutes on rollers, light stretching. If zone 2 rides start feeling "soul crushing," you have overdone the combined training load. Lifting depletes glycogen significantly (a few sets of heavy squats can deplete roughly half your muscle glycogen), so nutrition and refueling after gym sessions matter more than most cyclists assume. For double days, ride in the morning, wait 3-4 hours, nap, eat substantially, then lift.

**Recommendations:**
- Never sacrifice sleep for gym sessions; if sleep-deprived, skip heavy lifting
- Allow 2-4 days of recovery after heavy lifting sessions before the next hard effort
- Manage soreness with gentle movement (easy spinning, walking), not complete rest
- If zone 2 rides feel unusually difficult, reduce total training load
- Eat more than you think after lifting sessions; glycogen depletion is substantial
- For double days: ride first, wait 3-4 hours, nap, eat, then lift; do not eat a large meal within 1.5-2 hours of lifting

### Big-Gear Cycling Does Not Substitute for Strength Training -- Use the Gym

Two episodes establish that cycling cannot provide a genuine strength stimulus due to its biomechanics: cycling is a concentric-only movement without the eccentric overload required for meaningful strength adaptation. Force applied at the pedal distributes across hip, knee, and ankle joints, reducing the stimulus at any single muscle group below the threshold for hypertrophy or strength gain. Isometric contraction at high force (as occurs during cycling power peaks) is insufficient compared to full range-of-motion eccentric loading. A 16-week study of elite U23 cyclists found that combining strength training with endurance training raised 45-minute power output by approximately 8% without changing VO2max or FTP -- the gains came from improved neural drive (12-20% MVC improvements) and type 2X to 2A fiber conversion. The mechanism is separate from aerobic adaptation, meaning strength training and endurance training address different limiting factors simultaneously. Over-under intervals and other on-bike formats are not uniquely effective for their commonly claimed mechanisms (lactate shuttling, MCT expression) -- the molecular switches governing these adaptations are the same ones activated by general aerobic training. Over-unders remain effective training but are not special.

**Recommendations:**
- If strength adaptation is a goal, use gym-based resistance training with eccentric loading (squats, deadlifts, lunges); big-gear work on the bike is not a substitute
- Treat strength training and endurance training as separate modalities targeting separate limiting factors
- In-season strength maintenance means low reps at moderate weight with explosive intent (e.g., 3 singles at 60% 1RM), not high-rep sets
- Over-unders are effective threshold-range training but provide no unique lactate clearance benefit beyond what general aerobic training achieves
- If doing over-unders, set the "under" portion at 80-90% FTP to maximize lactate oxidation rate

## 8. Nutrition & Fueling

### Carbohydrate Intake During Rides Should Scale With Intensity and Next-Day Demands, Not Body Size

On-bike carbohydrate recommendations are remarkably consistent across the podcast: 30-60 g/hr for low-intensity endurance rides, 60+ g/hr for tempo through threshold, and 80-120 g/hr for racing or heavy training loads. A critical and perhaps surprising finding is that these targets are not body-weight dependent. Namrita Brooke explains that carb oxidation capacity depends on training status and gut absorption capacity, not on whether the athlete weighs 50 kg or 100 kg. Both she and Kolie Moore reinforce this across multiple episodes. For short, intense efforts (VO2max intervals, time trials, cyclocross) with full glycogen stores, mid-effort fueling is unnecessary — fuel before and after instead. Above 60 g/hr, multiple transportable carbohydrates (glucose/maltodextrin plus fructose) are required because a single glucose transporter maxes out at roughly 1 g/min. The glucose-to-fructose ratio has shifted from the older 2:1 recommendation to approximately 1:0.8, which appears more GI-tolerable. Critically, what you eat today should account for what you are doing tomorrow — Namrita argues athletes should eat more on easy days preceding hard sessions, reversing the common instinct to eat less when training less. Tim Podlogar adds an important nuance: on-bike carbs come out of the total daily carbohydrate budget, so blindly maximizing on-bike intake (e.g., 120 g/hr) can paradoxically harm multi-day performance by leaving insufficient carbs for recovery meals.

**Recommendations:**
- Target 30-60 g/hr carbs on easy rides, 60+ g/hr at moderate intensity, 80-120 g/hr when racing
- Use mixed carbohydrate sources (glucose/maltodextrin + fructose at roughly 1:0.8 ratio) when exceeding 60 g/hr
- Scale fueling up on easy days before hard training days or races
- Do not adjust on-bike carb targets based on body weight — training status and intensity drive absorption capacity
- For multi-day events, balance on-bike intake against the need for sufficient post-ride carbohydrate to replenish glycogen

### On-Bike Carbohydrate Intake Must Be Balanced Against the Total Daily Energy Budget

Tim Podlogar introduces a critical reframing: on-bike carbohydrate intake should be considered as part of a total daily carbohydrate budget rather than maximized in isolation. His research shows that consuming 120 g/hr versus 90 g/hr during a four-hour ride shifts roughly 120 g of carbohydrate from off-bike recovery meals to on-bike intake — and higher on-bike intake primarily changes substrate oxidation (more carbs burned, less fat) rather than meaningfully sparing muscle glycogen. There is a dose-response relationship between post-ride carbohydrate intake and next-day glycogen stores, which means that for multi-stage races or back-to-back training days, having carbohydrate budget remaining for recovery meals is essential. The practical implication is context-dependent: for one-day races where next-day recovery is irrelevant, maximize on-bike intake aggressively. For multi-day events, moderate on-bike intake (80-90 g/hr) and preserve carbohydrate budget for recovery nutrition. Both Podlogar and Moore note that the trend toward ever-higher on-bike carbohydrate rates (with discussion of 200 g/hr emerging) has overcorrected — the field "lost in translation" the importance of distributing carbs across the full day. When riders cannot physically eat enough solid food (common in grand tours), replacing some solid sources with liquid calories like juice or sugary drinks helps meet total intake targets.

**Recommendations:**
- Think of carbohydrate intake as a daily budget: on-bike carbs reduce what is available for off-bike recovery
- For one-day races, maximize on-bike carbs; for multi-day events, moderate on-bike intake to preserve recovery nutrition capacity
- If struggling to eat enough total food, supplement with liquid carbohydrate sources (juice, sugary drinks) that go down more easily
- Consider ramping on-bike intake during single-day events: 80-120 g in the first hour, increasing in final hours
- Do not assume that higher on-bike carb intake always produces better outcomes — context determines the optimal rate

### Gut Training Is a Real Multi-Week Physiological Adaptation, Though Its Limits Are Debated

The ability to absorb high carbohydrate loads during exercise is trainable, but the extent of this trainability is now debated. Namrita Brooke describes three distinct components: upregulating gut sugar transporters through a habitually high-carb daily diet (at least 8 g/kg/day), training gastric emptying by progressively eating larger pre-ride meals and increasing fluid intake during training, and gradually building on-bike intake over weeks. Athletes who eat low-carb diets and then attempt aggressive race-day fueling are fighting their own downregulated gut machinery — the transporters need consistent dietary stimulus to remain active. However, Tim Podlogar introduces a contrasting perspective: his research has not found strong evidence that gut training increases maximal carbohydrate absorption rates, stating "we don't really see the evidence that it does." This tension remains unresolved. Practical tips for maximizing gut tolerance include choosing hypotonic drink solutions over hypertonic ones, using gels with adequate water (nearly half a bottle per gel to dilute the concentrated solution), and nibbling bars over time rather than eating them all at once during high-intensity efforts. For athletes who cannot carry sufficient solid food, carbohydrate drink mixes (e.g., 100 g carbs per serving) offer a practical alternative.

**Recommendations:**
- Maintain a daily carb intake of at least 8 g/kg/day to keep gut transporters upregulated
- Build on-bike carb intake gradually over several weeks; do not jump to 100+ g/hr on race day
- Drink nearly half a bottle of water with each gel to prevent GI distress from hypertonic concentrations
- Choose hypotonic drink solutions; all dissolved particles (sugars, electrolytes, amino acids) contribute to osmolality
- Nibble bars over time at high intensity rather than consuming them in large boluses
- Be aware that the evidence for gut training increasing maximal absorption rates is contested

### A DIY Hydration Mix Using Sodium Citrate and Dual Sugars Outperforms Most Commercial Options

Kolie Moore reverse-engineered science-based hydration drinks and arrived at a DIY formula centered on a few principles: use both dextrose and sucrose to leverage multiple gut sugar transporters (which also aids water absorption), keep the solution hypotonic at roughly 4-5% concentration, and use sodium citrate instead of table salt as the primary sodium source because it is far more palatable during exercise. Most people lose 0.5-1.5 g of sodium per liter of sweat; salty sweaters (identifiable by salt caking on skin or stinging eyes) should nearly double the sodium citrate. Calcium and potassium losses are low enough to address through normal food. Chloride replacement is better handled through solid food like salted boiled potatoes rather than the drink itself. The recipe is adjustable for conditions: more water in hot weather (550-700 mL per serving) and less in cold weather (400-450 mL). Coconut water, despite its health halo, is a poor exercise hydration choice due to excessive potassium and insufficient sodium. Tim Podlogar adds broader context on electrolytes: for rides under four hours, real-time electrolyte replacement is probably unnecessary since daily electrolyte balance matters more. Sweat composition testing is of limited practical utility because results vary day-to-day with acclimatization, diet, and intensity.

**Recommendations:**
- Use sodium citrate rather than table salt for exercise drink palatability
- Combine dextrose and sucrose to exploit dual sugar transporters and enhance water absorption
- Keep drink concentration at approximately 4-5% (4 g per 100 mL water)
- Adjust water volume by weather: 550-700 mL/serving in heat, 400-450 mL/serving in cold
- Salty sweaters should nearly double sodium citrate; skip coconut water entirely
- For rides under 4 hours, do not over-invest in real-time electrolyte replacement; daily balance matters more
- If urinating excessively during rides, reduce fluid intake rather than adding more electrolytes
- Athletes subject to drug testing must source ingredients from NSF/WADA-compliant suppliers

### Weight Loss Should Be Slow, Off-Season, and Never Achieved by Restricting On-Bike Fuel

Multiple episodes converge on a consistent weight management philosophy: lose weight as slowly as possible while eating as many calories as possible, restrict dieting to the off-season or base phase, and never create caloric deficits through on-bike fueling. Namrita Brooke recommends deficits of only 200-250 calories per day, noting that deficits over 500 cal/day caused lean mass loss even in resistance-trained subjects. Kolie Moore's personal approach involves 6-8 week dieting phases losing no more than 6-8 lbs per phase, followed by maintenance periods — a multi-year project, not a single-season crash. Eric Trexler adds the bodybuilding-derived "get in, get out" principle: reach race weight briefly for key events, then return to a sustainable baseline. The common cycling practice of maintaining very low body weight for entire seasons is explicitly criticized as more harmful than the bodybuilding approach. On-bike fueling should remain fully adequate even while dieting — restricting ride calories impairs training quality and represents "such a small part of your day" that it barely moves the deficit needle. Create deficits through slightly less food and slightly more general movement, tracking NEAT via step counts as a guardrail — declining steps signal the deficit is too aggressive. Tim Podlogar reinforces that athletes should first establish a baseline energy balance over 1-2 months before creating any deficit, and warns that rushing into aggressive deficits risks iron problems, menstrual dysfunction, and impaired recovery.

**Recommendations:**
- Limit caloric deficit to 200-250 cal/day; target 0.25-1.0 lb/week weight loss
- Diet only during off-season or base phase; never during build or peak
- Fuel rides fully even while dieting — do not restrict on-bike carbs to create deficits
- Include resistance training during any weight loss phase to preserve lean mass
- Monitor NEAT via daily step counts; declining steps indicate over-restriction
- Use performance on the bike (rising RPE relative to power) as a key signal of excessive restriction
- Establish a 1-2 month energy balance baseline before beginning any deficit

### Chronic Underfueling Causes Irreversible Harm Even at Normal Body Weight

Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) is a recurring concern across multiple episodes, with a key insight being that athletes can be chronically underfueled at any body weight — even while gaining weight — because the body compensates by downregulating metabolism, heart rate, and non-essential functions. Traci Carson emphasizes that even when total daily calories appear adequate, poor distribution (e.g., skipping breakfast before a hard morning ride, then overeating at dinner) can produce hormonal dysfunction equivalent to overall underfueling. A banana before a two-hour session burning 1200 calories creates an 1100-calorie deficit by mid-morning that triggers stress responses, even if dinner later brings the daily total to adequacy. Bone damage from chronic low energy availability is largely irreversible, and one bone stress fracture strongly predicts six more. Warning signs include inconsistent performances, excessive bad training days, brain fog, moodiness, frequent illness, and menstrual irregularity. Kolie Moore estimates at least half his clients have suffered from chronic insufficient caloric intake at some point. The minimum daily intake guideline cited is 45 kcal per kg of fat-free mass. Critically, overtraining syndrome and RED-S have near-complete symptom overlap, and Carson argues underfueling is almost always a component of overtraining — they are not distinct conditions but overlapping circles.

**Recommendations:**
- Aim for no less than 45 kcal/kg of fat-free mass per day as a general minimum
- Distribute calories throughout the day, especially around training — do not backload into a single large meal
- Monitor for RED-S signs: declining performance, brain fog, frequent illness, menstrual irregularity, loss of libido
- Track menstrual cycles in female athletes; loss of period is a late-stage indicator that significant damage may already have occurred
- If weight is stable but symptoms of under-recovery persist, consider that the body may have downregulated expenditure — try increasing intake by 200-400 cal/day and observing whether function improves
- For women on IUDs, use basal body temperature tracking (e.g., Oura Ring) to detect ovulation since hormonal birth control masks cycle status

### Post-Exercise Carbohydrate Timing Matters Most When Sessions Are Back-to-Back

A glycogen replenishment study reviewed in detail on the podcast produced a striking paradox: participants who received 2.4 g/kg carbs immediately after a depleting session performed roughly 30% better the next day than those whose carbs were delayed by 3 hours — even though muscle glycogen levels were essentially identical by the following morning. This suggests that something beyond muscle glycogen (possibly liver glycogen, subcellular glycogen compartmentalization, or within-day energy deficit effects) drives the benefit of immediate refueling. Tim Podlogar independently corroborates this finding, supporting the "delayed recovery process" explanation and emphasizing that consuming carbohydrates as soon as possible after training is critical for next-day performance. The practical implication is that the classical glycogen window matters specifically when training sessions are close together — same-day doubles, back-to-back hard days, or multi-round race days. When the next hard session is 4-5+ days away, total daily intake matters far more than timing. Modern aggressive on-bike fueling (70-90 g/hr) means athletes often return from rides without acute hunger, which can paradoxically lead to poor post-ride nutrition. Including fructose sources off the bike (fruit juice, mixed carbs) may specifically benefit liver glycogen replenishment, which glucose-only sources miss.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize immediate post-exercise carbohydrate intake (within 30-60 minutes) when the next hard session is within 24 hours
- Target approximately 2.4 g/kg carbs distributed over the first 2 hours post-exercise for rapid glycogen recovery
- Include fructose sources (fruit, juice) in post-ride nutrition to support liver glycogen specifically
- When next hard effort is 4-5+ days away, focus on total daily intake rather than precise timing
- Do not let reduced post-ride hunger (from better on-bike fueling) lead to skipping recovery meals

### Energy Expenditure Is Constrained and Adaptive, Making Naive Calorie Math Unreliable

Eric Trexler introduces the constrained energy expenditure model: dramatically increasing exercise volume does not proportionally increase total daily energy expenditure because the body compensates by reducing expenditure in other compartments. This means the common approach of "ride more to lose weight" often backfires — Kolie reports a client example of gaining 3 kg from increased volume because appetite more than compensated. Tim Podlogar reinforces this from the practitioner side, describing energy expenditure calculations as "purely guesswork" due to stacked assumptions: efficiency estimates alone (20% vs 25%) create roughly 900 kcal of error on a 4,000 kJ ride, and nutrition label tolerances (~20%), absorption inefficiency (85-95%), and off-bike activity uncertainty compound further. Even doubly labeled water — the research gold standard — shows 10-15% mismatch with actual energy intake in weight-stable riders. The best practical method for estimating actual energy needs is a 2-4 week empirical tracking approach: hold training load and caloric intake steady, weigh daily under standardized conditions, and calculate the slope of body weight change. Maximum sustainable caloric intake appears to top out at roughly 2.5x basal metabolic rate (some elite athletes reaching approximately 3x BMR), which serves as a ceiling for planning multi-day event fueling.

**Recommendations:**
- Use a 2-4 week energy balance method (stable intake + daily weight tracking) to empirically determine actual energy expenditure
- Weigh daily under standardized conditions (morning, fasted) to smooth out water fluctuations
- Do not trust BMR calculators, wearable calorie estimates, or power-meter kilojoules as precise expenditure measures
- Watch for compensatory symptoms at stable weight: low energy, reduced libido, feeling cold, sleep disruption, amenorrhea
- For ultra-endurance events, plan for maximum feasible intake rather than trying to achieve energy balance
- Expect a 1-2 month calibration period when working with a nutritionist; iterate based on weight trends and recovery quality

### CGMs Are Overrated for Racing but Useful for Off-Bike Dietary Pattern Analysis

A four-person expert panel unanimously agreed that continuous glucose monitors provide no real-time performance advantage during racing. CGMs measure interstitial glucose (not blood glucose) with roughly 10% variance and a 5-10 minute delay — by the time low glucose is detected, the athlete is already in trouble. Proactive fueling plans developed with a coach or dietitian are far more effective than reactive glucose monitoring. The panel identified CGMs' real value as off-bike, 24-hour dietary pattern analysis: observing how different foods, meal timing, and combinations affect glucose stability, energy levels, and sleep quality. However, this requires professional interpretation alongside food diaries, training data, and clinical context — without which CGM data is "meaningless." A significant concern is "glucorexia," where practitioners use CGM data to scare athletes away from healthy foods (like oatmeal) based on glucose spike appearance, potentially triggering disordered eating. Kolie compares CGMs to HRV monitoring: likely to follow a hype cycle where initial enthusiasm gives way to recognition that the data is hard to interpret meaningfully.

**Recommendations:**
- Develop a fueling plan with a coach/dietitian before racing rather than relying on real-time CGM readings
- If using a CGM, focus on off-bike dietary patterns rather than in-ride data
- Always interpret CGM data alongside food diaries, training logs, and professional guidance
- Beware of practitioners using CGM data to discourage healthy high-glycemic foods; a glucose spike from oatmeal is not harmful
- Do not use CGMs as a proxy for diagnosing RED-S or low energy availability

**Sources:** Episodes 1481215921-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-24-continuous-glucose-monitors-kristen-faulkner-and-athlete-health.txt

### Fuel Substrate Burned During Exercise Is Irrelevant to Both Adaptation and Fueling Decisions

Kolie Moore and Kyle Helson argue that which substrate you burn during exercise — fat or carbohydrate — is categorically irrelevant to the muscular adaptations gained from training. This directly challenges the popular notion that Zone 2 training is valuable because it "trains fat burning." The body is an excellent energy accountant that is particularly sensitive to deficits (an evolutionary asymmetry favoring fat storage), and it does not simply replace glycogen in a one-to-one substrate-matched fashion. Trying to eat fats on the bike to "match" fat burning during Zone 2 is not only unnecessary but impractical — carbohydrates are the superior on-bike fuel for reasons of absorption speed, digestibility, affordability, and shelf stability. This position is presented as the conclusion of their extensive Watts Doc metabolism series (10-15 episodes) and is reinforced rather than revised in later episodes. Tim Podlogar's data adds a complementary point: higher on-bike carbohydrate intake primarily shifts substrate oxidation toward carbohydrate and away from fat rather than preserving more glycogen — meaning the substrate mix burned is a consequence of feeding strategy, not an independent training signal.

**Recommendations:**
- Fuel rides with carbohydrates regardless of exercise intensity or which substrate is predominantly being burned
- Do not restrict on-bike carbs during Zone 2 rides because you are "mostly burning fat"
- Use power meter kilojoules as a rough 1:1 estimate of kilocalories burned, recognizing this is an approximation
- Do not pursue substrate-specific fueling strategies based on heart rate zone or perceived fat-burning intensity

### During Illness, Increase Carbohydrates and Eliminate Alcohol Entirely

Dr. Fabiano Araujo recommends raising carbohydrate intake during illness to support immune cells, which require glucose to function. Protein catabolism increases significantly during fever as immune cells demand amino acids, making adequate protein intake important as well. Minimum healthy fat intake should be maintained. The most striking recommendation is that there is no safe amount of alcohol during illness recovery — even a single beer can trigger a full relapse, supported by both the literature and Kolie's personal experience. Training should cease entirely for any below-the-neck symptoms (fever, myalgia, chest pain, resting tachycardia), including gym work, which is not safer than cycling despite common belief. A 6% carbohydrate sports drink consumed during training is associated with lower incidence of upper respiratory illness in studies, providing a preventive measure. Probiotics via yogurt show promising evidence for reducing recurring respiratory illness and are worth incorporating into regular diet.

**Recommendations:**
- Stop all exercise immediately if below-the-neck symptoms appear; do not substitute lighter gym work
- Increase carbohydrate intake during illness to fuel immune cells; do not diet or restrict calories while sick
- Eliminate alcohol entirely during illness and recovery — there is no established safe minimum
- Include probiotics (yogurt) in regular diet to reduce respiratory illness incidence
- Return to training only after all below-the-neck symptoms resolve; start with 30-minute easy rides and add roughly 15 minutes per day

### Supplements and Micronutrients Deserve Attention Only After Macronutrient Fundamentals Are Optimized

Tim Podlogar provides a clear supplement hierarchy for endurance athletes: optimize carbohydrate intake first, then protein, then overall food quality, and only then consider supplements. For micronutrients, the two most important blood panels to monitor are vitamin D and ferritin (iron status). A critical practical insight is that a sports nutritionist may require higher ferritin values than a general physician would flag — doctors aim to prevent clinical anemia, while performance optimization (especially before altitude training camps) demands higher thresholds. Aim for approximately 100% of recommended daily allowance for vitamins rather than megadosing. Cherry juice and similar antioxidant supplements are among the lowest priorities. This pragmatic ordering contrasts with the common amateur approach of spending heavily on supplements while neglecting basic carbohydrate and calorie adequacy.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize carbohydrate adequacy, then protein, then food quality before investing in supplements
- Monitor vitamin D and ferritin via blood panels; ensure ferritin is optimized for performance, not merely above the clinical anemia threshold
- Aim for ~100% RDA for vitamins rather than megadosing
- Do not rely on cherry juice or antioxidant supplements as meaningful performance interventions
- If preparing for altitude training, ensure ferritin levels are checked and optimized well in advance

### Evolution of Views

The podcast's nutrition recommendations have shifted notably over its lifespan, with several clear progressions:

**From restrictive to aggressive on-bike fueling.** Kolie Moore acknowledges that early in his coaching career, norms were roughly 20 g carbs every 30-40 minutes (about one gel per hour). Both he and Kyle Helson describe experimenting with fasted rides and deliberate bonking for training adaptation, which they now consider a "failed experiment with no practical value" (Episode 2223337298). Modern practice has moved to 70-90+ g/hr as standard, with the glucose-to-fructose ratio updating from 2:1 to approximately 1:0.8 (Episode 1258345612).

**From maximizing on-bike carbs to considering total daily distribution.** The most recent evolution, introduced by Tim Podlogar (Episode 2286114404), pushes back on the "more on-bike carbs is always better" trend. Both Moore and Podlogar argue the field has overcorrected — the emphasis on ever-higher on-bike intake rates (with discussion reaching 200 g/hr) has come at the cost of considering full-day carbohydrate budgeting. This represents a maturation from "eat more during rides" to "distribute carbs optimally across the entire day."

**From ignoring to prioritizing off-bike nutrition timing.** The glycogen window concept underwent revision: the early John Ivy literature promoting universal 30-60 minute refueling windows gave way to a more nuanced view that timing matters most for back-to-back sessions, while total daily intake drives outcomes in most scenarios (Episode 2223337298). Kolie admitted that Namrita Brooke's framing of today's nutrition serving tomorrow's performance was a perspective he "had not really considered that much" (Episode 1258345612). Podlogar's work reinforces the back-to-back session finding from a different angle, supporting the "delayed recovery process" explanation.

**Growing awareness of RED-S and chronic underfueling.** Kolie states he did not ask female athletes about menstrual cycles early in his career and now considers cycle tracking essential (Episode 1450793536). Namrita acknowledges she "probably didn't tell some male athletes to eat enough" early in her career (Episode 1450793536). Traci Carson and Kolie grade the sport as B/B+ on carbohydrate awareness but C-minus to D on RED-S recognition, though both note meaningful cultural improvement since the Mary Cain story (Episode 1966298519).

**Incorporation of bodybuilding principles.** Kolie describes relatively recent cross-pollination from bodybuilding literature (MASS Research Review, Iron Culture podcast), adopting concepts like "get in, get out" weight management and resistance training during cuts — principles that were foreign to cycling culture (Episode 2154769800).

**Revised position on overtraining vs. underfueling.** Kolie initially held the view that "there's no such thing as overtraining, there's only under-fueling." He now explicitly rejects this, acknowledging that excessive training volume can outstrip any reasonable fueling strategy, and that overtraining and underfueling are overlapping but distinct phenomena (Episodes 1481215921, 1966298519).

**Energy expenditure estimation growing more humble.** The progression from trusting BMR calculators and power meter kilojoules to recognizing "purely guesswork" (Podlogar, Episode 2286114404) and the constrained expenditure model (Trexler, Episode 2154769800) reflects an increasing humility about the precision of energy math. The practical response — empirical 2-4 week calibration periods — replaces the earlier assumption that calculations could be trusted.

**Gut training consensus fragmenting.** Namrita Brooke (Episode 1258345612) presented gut training as a well-established, trainable adaptation. Podlogar (Episode 2286114404) counters that "we don't really see the evidence that it does" increase maximal absorption rates. This represents an active area of disagreement between expert guests, with the podcast not yet reaching a settled position.

**CGM skepticism refined.** Kolie entered the CGM discussion somewhat dismissive (suspecting marketing hype) and came away with a more nuanced view acknowledging off-bike utility while maintaining that CGMs are not essential and will likely follow an HRV-like hype cycle (Episode 1481215921).

**Pro cycling nutrition infrastructure modernized.** Podlogar (Episode 2286114404) describes the shift from manual Excel-based calculations to team-wide nutrition apps (Hexis, Food Coach) enabling real-time intake logging and verification — a transformation that occurred over just a few years in the professional peloton.

## 9. Psychology & Performance

### Consistency of Training Environment Matters More Than Choice of Training Method

Athletes frequently chase "silver bullet" methods — polarized training, zone 2 blocks, high-intensity programs — often switching after a few months of disappointing results. Dr. Patrick Smith argues, drawing on Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle, that the appeal of a new method is primarily psychological: it resolves the aversive state of uncertainty by offering a fresh explanatory model. The method itself is rarely the issue; inconsistent execution of any reasonable method is. The real performance variable is environmental consistency — sleep, nutrition, schedule stability, and sustained application of a coherent plan over time. Athletes who repeatedly cycle through approaches every few months should treat that pattern as a diagnostic signal: the problem is almost certainly not method selection but behavioral variability and discomfort with uncertainty. Coaches can help by identifying this pattern early and reframing the conversation from "what should I do differently?" to "what is preventing me from doing what I am already doing, consistently?" This does not mean athletes should never change methods — genuine plateaus exist — but premature switching driven by cognitive dissonance rather than evidence is a common failure mode.

**Recommendations:**
- Before switching training methods after disappointing results, ask whether the method is actually failing or whether you are seeking psychological relief from uncertainty.
- Reduce unnecessary sources of lifestyle variability (sleep, nutrition, schedule) to improve training signal-to-noise ratio.
- If you find yourself changing approaches every few months, treat the switching pattern itself as the problem to address.

### Coaches Are Feedback Calibrators, Not Just Workout Designers

Across multiple episodes, the podcast reframes the core function of coaching away from program design and toward behavioral environment management. Smith describes coaches as "bumpers" — like bumper bowling — whose primary job is calibrating an athlete's sensitivity to feedback. Some athletes are hyperreactive: a single bad workout triggers panic, method changes, and spiraling doubt. Others are under-reactive: they ignore consistent warning signs until injury or burnout forces a stop. The correction for each is opposite, and identifying where an athlete falls on this spectrum is a more important coaching skill than writing interval prescriptions. This feedback calibration extends to deciding what information an athlete should see (hiding certain metrics for hyperreactive athletes, introducing new data streams for under-reactive ones) and when to intervene versus when to let an experience do its own teaching. Building a shared language is foundational to this process: coaches and athletes must explicitly define what terms like "hard," "easy," and "8 out of 10 effort" mean in observable behavioral terms before any communication about training is useful.

**Recommendations:**
- Coaches should assess athlete feedback sensitivity as a starting point: determine whether the athlete tends toward hyperreactivity or under-reactivity, and calibrate information flow accordingly.
- Explicitly define subjective terms ("hard," "easy," RPE anchors) in observable, behavioral terms at the start of a coaching relationship.
- Consider hiding or revealing specific metrics strategically based on the athlete's current sensitivity profile.

### Rest Is a Skill That Must Be Deliberately Reinforced

Athletes find rest psychologically difficult not because of weak willpower or excessive motivation, but because their reinforcement history has made training — not rest — the source of positive feedback. Strava kudos, coach praise, performance metrics, and a sense of competence all come from training; rest produces none of these and therefore feels aversive. Coaches inadvertently worsen this by reinforcing toughness and workout PRs while ignoring or passively punishing rest behaviors (no comment, no praise, subtle disapproval). The "high-functioning lunatic" who trains through everything is not exhibiting admirable discipline but a behavioral pattern driven by differential reinforcement and often underpinned by anxiety. Rest must be treated as a trainable skill with its own learning curve: athletes who have never rested properly need time to develop tolerance for the absence of training stimuli. Coaches should explicitly praise rest behaviors and help athletes attach social or sensory rewards to recovery days rather than treating them as empty space.

**Recommendations:**
- Coaches should positively reinforce rest behaviors as explicitly as they reinforce workout performances.
- Deliberately attach social or sensory rewards to rest days — make rest feel like something you do, not a training day you skipped.
- Recognize that athletes who "never rest" are exhibiting a conditioned pattern, often anxiety-driven, not superior discipline.
- Accept that building comfort with rest has a learning curve; initial discomfort is expected and not a sign of failure.

### Treat Emotions as Diagnostic Signals, Not Problems to Eliminate

A consistent theme across the Patrick Smith and Billy Ryan episodes is that strong emotions during training and racing — anxiety, disappointment, frustration — are information systems, not malfunctions. The goal of performance psychology is not to feel better (eliminate aversive states) but to "feel better" (develop improved sensitivity and responsiveness to emotional signals). Pre-race anxiety signals that something valued is at stake. Post-race disappointment indicates a threat to something the athlete cares about. The correct response is to ask "what valued thing does this emotion point to?" rather than reaching for suppression, positive self-talk, or premature reassurance. Ryan's ACT-based approach operationalizes this: when an aversive emotion appears, the athlete asks what it is trying to communicate. Smith adds that post-race disappointment should often be left alone for a day or two before analytical debriefing begins — premature data review or reassurance can short-circuit productive emotional processing. This represents a significant departure from older sport psychology models that emphasized thought-stopping and cognitive restructuring.

**Recommendations:**
- When strong emotions arise before or during performance, ask "what valued thing is at stake?" rather than trying to suppress or override the feeling.
- Treat emotions as messengers: when an aversive emotion appears, ask what it is trying to communicate rather than suppressing it.
- After difficult races or disappointing results, allow 1-2 days of emotional processing before conducting analytical debriefs.
- Evaluate thoughts by workability ("does acting on this help what I am trying to do right now?"), not by positive/negative valence.

### Values-Based Motivation Outlasts Goal-Based Motivation

Smith introduces values clarification as a more durable motivational framework than traditional goal-setting. Goals are finite targets: once achieved or missed, they leave nothing behind. Values are ongoing descriptors of how an athlete wants to act — "I am the kind of rider who prepares thoroughly," "I am the kind of person who shows up when it is hard." Values cannot be failed because they describe a direction of action, not an endpoint. This distinction matters most during setbacks: an athlete who missed a goal has "failed," but an athlete who acted consistently with their values has not, regardless of the outcome. Ryan extends this by noting that when athletes experience low motivation during high-stress life periods, the first diagnostic check should be social connection (are you maintaining contact with people who matter?) and the second should be connection to the meaning of training (why does this matter right now?). If both are intact and motivation remains low, genuine rest rather than motivational intervention may be indicated. Bertine's narrative reinforces this from a different angle: treating rejection as data rather than verdict, and "including yourself as a contender" before evidence supports it, are both values-based commitments to action that precede and enable results.

**Recommendations:**
- Frame training identity using values statements ("I am the kind of athlete who...") rather than relying solely on outcome goals.
- When motivation drops, check social connection first, then connection to training meaning, before assuming a psychological problem.
- Treat setbacks, DNFs, and poor results as information about specific conditions on a specific day — not as verdicts about potential.
- "Include yourself as a contender" by acting before evidence of belonging arrives; the evidence comes from having acted.

### Psychological Flexibility, Not Positive Thinking, Is the Performance Skill

Billy Ryan makes a direct case against the positive psychology approach to sport performance. Positive self-talk, visualization of success, and thought-stopping — the traditional mental skills toolkit — are counterproductive because they treat internal experience as the enemy. Trying to replace "negative" thoughts with "positive" ones expends attention and energy, makes the unwanted thoughts more salient (the beach-ball-underwater effect), and trains athletes to avoid the emotional experiences that carry performance-relevant information. The alternative is psychological flexibility: the ability to carry difficult thoughts and emotions while continuing to pursue valued actions. The "drop the rope" metaphor captures this — the problem with a tug-of-war against difficult thoughts is not who is winning, but that you are holding the rope at all. Letting go costs nothing and frees attention for the task. Critically, the distinction between compartmentalization (keeping difficult experience present while directing attention to the task) and avoidance (suppressing difficult experience) is essential: only compartmentalization works sustainably.

**Recommendations:**
- Stop trying to replace negative thoughts with positive ones; instead, practice noticing thoughts without being directed by them (defusion).
- Use the "drop the rope" cue when caught in a mental tug-of-war during performance.
- Distinguish between compartmentalization (carrying difficulty while acting) and avoidance (suppressing difficulty) — only the former is sustainable.
- Recognize that even "positive" thoughts can be non-workable if they shift attention away from execution.

### Self-Coaching Has a Structural Disadvantage That Knowledge Cannot Fix

Self-coaching is disadvantaged not primarily because of knowledge gaps but because the social reinforcement function of a coaching relationship is absent. The athlete cannot receive external validation or correction of distorted self-perception because the performer and the evaluator are the same person. Behavioral reactivity — the phenomenon where measuring a behavior changes that behavior — compounds this: an athlete tracking their own recovery metrics may create anxiety and performance pressure around rest, paradoxically degrading recovery quality. Smith recommends that self-coached athletes write workout comments as if reporting to another person, creating enough psychological distance to reduce confirmation bias. After 2-3 months of commenting, athletes should review their logs looking specifically for patterns: are rest days described as failures? Are easy workouts described apologetically? That language is diagnostic. Periodically sharing logs with a trusted peer who can reflect back invisible patterns further mitigates the structural disadvantage.

**Recommendations:**
- Write training log comments as if reporting to an external coach, even when self-coached, to reduce self-serving distortions.
- After 2-3 months, review your log language specifically looking for negative framing around rest and easy days.
- Periodically share your training log with a trusted peer for pattern recognition you cannot perform on yourself.
- Be aware that tracking recovery metrics (HRV, sleep scores) can create the very anxiety it is intended to monitor.

### Race Anxiety Habituates With Exposure, Not With Cognitive Intervention

Smith recommends habituation rather than suppression for pre-race nerves. Athletes who race rarely tend to have worse race anxiety than those who race frequently because the novelty signal driving the anxiety has not habituated. The practical implication is that racing more often — even at lower-priority events — is a more effective intervention for race anxiety than any amount of visualization, breathing exercises, or positive self-talk done in isolation. Ryan adds a complementary tool: a sensory contact exercise (deliberately naming the senses you are using — seeing, hearing, feeling, and also thinking and feeling as senses) that works not as distraction but as attentional expansion, moving the athlete from emotional flooding into a broader "wise mind" state where both emotional and rational processing are accessible. For athletes with "rigid flexibility" — those who push through physical pain easily but abandon efforts when specific emotional content appears (e.g., "I am going to fail publicly") — the recommendation is to identify the triggering emotional content and practice tolerating it in lower-stakes contexts before race day.

**Recommendations:**
- Race more frequently, including at lower-priority events, to habituate the novelty signal that drives pre-race anxiety.
- Use sensory contact (deliberately naming each sense you are using) as an attentional expansion exercise, not a distraction technique, when experiencing emotional flooding.
- Identify specific emotional triggers for early quitting and practice tolerating those in lower-stakes training contexts.
- Distinguish between performance-related race anxiety (habituates with exposure) and pervasive anxiety (requires clinical attention).

**Sources:** Episodes 1830891714-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-33-the-psychology-of-excellence.txt, 1971970487-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-37-practical-performance-psychology-with-billy-ryan.txt

### Know When to Refer: Performance Psychology Is Not Clinical Psychology

Smith draws a clear boundary between performance psychology (a coaching and skills domain) and clinical psychology (a treatment domain). Coaches should refer an athlete to a licensed performance psychologist — not just any therapist — when the athlete has been diagnosed with or is suspected of having a clinical mental health condition, when the psychological issue is clearly interfering with life outside sport, or when the coach-athlete relationship itself is part of the problem. The stigma that prevents athletes from seeking performance psychology support is not entirely irrational: it partly reflects a history of sport psychology interventions that treated athletes as broken rather than as skilled performers building capacity. Reframing psychological work as skill-building rather than treatment reduces this barrier. Smith's "brick house" metaphor — physical skills are bricks, psychological and social support skills are the cement — communicates why psychological work is performance work without implying pathology.

**Recommendations:**
- Refer to a licensed performance psychologist (not a general therapist) when clinical conditions are suspected, when issues affect life beyond sport, or when the coaching relationship is part of the problem.
- Frame performance psychology as skill-building, not treatment, to reduce stigma and increase athlete uptake.
- Coaches should not attempt to manage clinical conditions (depression, eating disorders, anxiety disorders) within the coaching relationship.

### Evolution of Views

The performance psychology episodes span from early Perspectives installments through episode 37 and show a clear arc in how the podcast's recurring expert, Dr. Patrick Smith, conceptualizes athlete psychology. In his early appearance (episode 1144665736, Perspectives 4), Smith introduces behavioral analysis as a lens for understanding athlete behavior — shared language, reinforcement of rest, values over goals — but the framework is still relatively focused on individual behavioral patterns. By episode 1435296790 (Perspectives 22), his view has shifted toward environment and relationship as the primary levers: he now frames performance support around feedback calibration and behavioral environment management, drawing on the Free Energy Principle to explain why athletes chase methods rather than consistency. By episode 1830891714 (Perspectives 33), Smith explicitly describes a shift in his own clinical training: he moved from a cognitive model (identify and change dysfunctional thoughts) toward a contextual behavioral model (build flexibility in how athletes relate to internal experience without requiring the experience itself to change). Billy Ryan's appearance in episode 1971970487 (Perspectives 37) reinforces and extends this trajectory, making the ACT framework explicit and directly arguing that the positive psychology movement's emphasis on positive thinking is counterproductive for athletes. The overall progression across the podcast is from cognition-focused interventions (change your thoughts) toward behavior-and-context-focused approaches (change your relationship to your thoughts, and change the environment that shapes your behavior). This mirrors a broader shift in applied performance psychology that Smith references in multiple episodes.

## 10. Coaching, Goal-Setting & Season Planning

### Training Plans Are Written in Sand — Auto-Regulation Is a Core Skill, Not a Weakness

Across multiple episodes, the coaches converge on a single message: rigid compliance with a training plan is a red flag, not a virtue. Kolie Moore has told clients directly that he needs less compliance from them. Gediminas recounts his first coached year where 100% TrainingPeaks compliance led to physical and mental breakdown by season's end, requiring a month off. The coaches now flag all-green calendars as potential over-compliance rather than model behavior. Auto-regulation — the ability to distinguish good-leg days from bad-leg days and adjust accordingly — is identified as the factor that most separates great training from merely adequate training. For athletes with chaotic schedules, the approach is to identify the best day each week for a hard session and be flexible about which day that lands on ("play whack-a-mole with your legs"). One powerful example: a client who repeatedly auto-regulated rest against the prescribed plan went on to UCI podiums — the coach credits learning that athlete's individual rest needs from observing the auto-regulation pattern. The Fernando Alonso analogy captures it: "Driving at 90% is sometimes faster than 100% because the car is unstable at the limit." The caveat is that some athletes genuinely thrive on rigid structure because it reduces decision-making stress; coaches should not universally impose auto-regulation on athletes who find structure intrinsically motivating. For the first year with a coach, the advice is to default to executing the plan, then begin asking questions and customizing.

**Recommendations:**
- Treat training plans as guidelines, not mandates — move interval sessions when fatigue or life stress intervenes
- If an athlete shows perfect compliance over months, investigate for over-compliance and burnout risk
- Develop the skill of reading your legs: push harder on good days, ride easy or skip on bad days
- Log RPE, motivation, soreness, and life stress the same day — subjective data loses precision quickly
- Tell your coach how you actually feel, not what you think they want to hear
- Trust power meter data over subjective feel when assessing whether rest has been adequate

### Life Stress Is Co-Equal with Training Load in Determining Adaptation

Work stress, family obligations, sleep disruption, and travel are not secondary factors — they directly neutralize training adaptations and must be accounted for in the training load budget. Kolie's formative coaching experience (2016-17) involved a client whose 8-10/10 work stress completely negated all training gains, despite a well-designed program. Since then, Empirical Cycling coaches treat stress management as co-equal with training prescription. Mental and psychological exhaustion warrants a rest week just as much as physical soreness does. A stressful workday without riding is not "free" recovery time — it accumulates fatigue that must be accounted for. When leaving workout feedback, off-bike context (sleep quality, nutrition adequacy, life stressors, illness onset) is not complaining — it is essential training data. A 20W power drop in a workout means very different things depending on whether the athlete slept 5 hours or 8. During high work-stress periods, athletes should choose between improving or maintaining fitness — not attempt both. Practical strategies include: dropping FTP targets by 10-20W during stressful periods, giving explicit permission to skip intervals, and using auto-regulation to identify the single best day each week for hard work. Young athletes (16-21) deserve special attention: school stress, poor nutrition access, and social pressure make them at least as susceptible to overtraining as adults — coaches should treat a teenager as if they have a 50-60 hour/week high-stress job.

**Recommendations:**
- During high work-stress periods, reduce FTP targets by 10-20W and choose between improving or maintaining fitness
- Log off-bike stressors (sleep, nutrition, work stress, illness) alongside every workout
- Treat high-stress non-training days as a draw on your recovery budget, not as neutral
- Give athletes explicit permission to skip intervals on high-stress days
- Treat U23/junior athletes as having 50-60 hr/week equivalent stress loads; adjust volume accordingly
- During work travel, prioritize not getting sick — ride easy for fun, add protein, stay hydrated
- Use subjective metrics (RPE, mood, motivation) alongside objective data for load decisions

### Easy Rides Must Be Genuinely Easy — The Most Common Training Error Across All Coaching Sources

A consistent theme across every coaching source is that endurance and recovery rides ridden too hard are the single most common source of chronic fatigue and plateaus. Three coaches independently identify the same pattern as their most frequent intervention. A recovery ride at ~65% FTP is already too hard for true recovery; Coaches prescribe sub-100W for most athletes' recovery rides, and Marinus Petersen uses a ceiling of 15 TSS per recovery ride. The "glass cranks" mental model captures the principle: imagine your cranks are made of glass. If you cannot ride that gently, do not ride at all. The chronic cost is insidious: riding endurance at 70-75% FTP accumulates fatigue over months, not days. GC's coaching cue is illustrative: if an athlete can average 200W for 3 hours but cannot hold that for 5-6 hours, the correct endurance target is closer to 150W for the longer ride. Five to six hours at 150W produces a better endurance stimulus than 3 hours at 200W. Coaches consistently report significant FTP gains when athletes reduce endurance ride intensity, though these gains typically occur alongside broader training adjustments. Warning signs emerge in qualitative training log data: comment tone gradually shifts from "felt great, could have done more" to "glad I survived" over weeks. Recovery between VO2max intervals should also be genuinely easy; athletes riding 70% FTP between hard efforts degrade subsequent interval quality. The cultural stigma against easy riding — the feeling that riding slowly is "cheating" — is the primary barrier to implementation.

**Recommendations:**
- Pace endurance rides by duration, not zone — longer rides require proportionally lower watts
- Keep recovery rides below 100W (or your individual sub-endurance floor); target under 15 TSS
- If you cannot ride genuinely easy, take a full day off instead
- Flag any athlete who never rides below 70-75% FTP on endurance/recovery days as an overreaching risk
- Monitor qualitative training log comments over 2-3 months for tone shifts indicating chronic fatigue
- Commutes count as training load; ride them easy or account for the fatigue they add
- A 2-week reduction in endurance intensity causes no meaningful fitness loss; use this to reassure resistant athletes

### Rest Is a Training Input — Planned Recovery Beats Reactive Recovery Every Time

Recovery is where adaptation actually occurs. The training stimulus breaks the body down; rest rebuilds it stronger. Yet most self-coached athletes treat rest as lost time. Adam Pulford frames this explicitly: periodize recovery with the same intentionality as training. James McKay provides a striking illustration: his lowest-volume client produced measurable performance gains during a vacation week of zero training, high sleep, and low stress. The psychological dimension matters: athletes most resistant to taking rest are often those who most need it. Fear of CTL drops, fear of weight gain, and social media comparison all drive under-resting behavior. Feeling worse on days 2-3 of a rest week is normal — it reflects the body processing accumulated fatigue, not a sign that rest is failing. Meaningful detraining takes weeks, not days. By the time most athletes feel they need a rest week, they needed one a week or two ago — subjective fatigue lags the physiological state by days, making reactive rest a planning failure. Planned rest at fixed intervals (every 3rd or 4th week) prevents the deep fatigue hole that reactive rest attempts to climb out of. Sprint power provides a reliable early warning: a 5-10 second maximal sprint significantly below your rested baseline signals accumulated fatigue before subjective tiredness is apparent. TTE is another canary: declining TTE while FTP holds steady signals eroding capacity. Getting sick frequently is also diagnostic — recurrent illness indicates rest weeks are being skipped or taken too late.

**Recommendations:**
- Treat rest weeks as planned training investments, not emergency responses to breakdown
- Schedule rest weeks at fixed intervals (3:1 or 2:1 build-to-rest ratio); do not wait until forced
- Default to rest when uncertain whether to train or rest; this is rarely the wrong choice for recreational athletes
- Use a 5-10 second sprint power test against your rested baseline as a readiness indicator
- Track TTE alongside FTP; declining TTE with stable FTP is an early warning of accumulated fatigue
- Accept that feeling worse early in a rest week is normal and expected
- Do not restrict calories during rest weeks — energy restriction impairs recovery

### Off-Season and Mid-Season Breaks Should Be Proactive and Guided by Motivation Signals

The coaches converge on a counterintuitive principle: the off-season exists to recharge mentally and physically, not to preserve fitness. Athletes who skip genuine rest accumulate fatigue that compromises the following season. The recommended approach is to wait until you are genuinely burning to train again, then wait "a little bit more" before resuming structured work. Physical fatigue often outlasts mental fatigue — an athlete who feels motivated may still be physically depleted. Actual FTP loss from a proper off-season is typically at most ~10 watts and often zero; fitness frequently increases after rest because pre-rest FTP was suppressed by accumulated fatigue. Mid-season breaks follow the same principle: schedule at least one full week off as an "insurance policy" — do not wait until performance visibly drops. Roughly 50% of athletes feel immediately stronger upon return; the other 50% need 1-2 weeks to regain feel. One example: an athlete who peaked in late April, took a full week off, did two easy weeks, and returned "absolutely flying." The "stairs test" is a useful heuristic for readiness — when you stop dreading stairs and start running up them, you are ready to resume. Coaches who were initially skeptical of mandatory breaks now routinely prescribe them after seeing repeated evidence that athletes without periodic full rest stop adapting. Masters athletes (50s-60s) should maintain roughly 6-8 hours per week during off-season to limit fitness loss, since rebuilding capacity is harder with age.

**Recommendations:**
- Use the "itch to return" as a start signal, not an end signal — wait beyond first motivation to train
- Schedule 7 days fully off mid-season proactively; allow 1-2 weeks of easy riding afterward
- Masters athletes: maintain ~6-8 hours/week at moderate intensity during off-season
- Plan backwards from goal race date to determine available off-season duration
- Do not fear fitness loss; typical off-season FTP decline is minimal (~10W or less)
- Use the "stairs test" and body weight normalization as readiness indicators after breaks
- For athletes 50+, consider shorter breaks with light maintenance riding rather than complete rest

### Consistency at 80-90% Adherence Outperforms Perfect Training Followed by Burnout

Sustainable, moderate adherence to a training plan produces better long-term outcomes than cycles of maximal effort followed by collapse. Athletes who never miss a session but also never genuinely rest (recovery ride IFs at 0.75-0.80 instead of below 0.60) plateau just as surely as those who skip weeks at a time. The practical solution is to build routines that conserve decision-making energy: lay out kit the night before, establish fixed training times, keep simple carb sources always available, and plan routes so interval segments are uninterrupted. For athletes who struggle with all-or-nothing thinking, coaches prescribe achievable minimums rather than aspirational targets — 30-45 minute "momentum days" to maintain the training habit when full sessions are impossible. The "rule of thirds" from running literature applies: roughly one-third of training days will feel good, one-third okay, and one-third bad. Normalizing this prevents athletes from catastrophizing isolated poor sessions into evidence that their plan is failing. Building the assumption of 2-3 weeks of lost training into any season plan provides a realistic buffer. The observation that "even if your FTP doesn't improve, bad days become less frequent and less severe" over years of training represents real performance improvement that never shows up in peak power numbers.

**Recommendations:**
- Establish a fixed daily training time and remove logistical barriers (kit, nutrition, bike prep done in advance)
- If weekly targets are being missed, prescribe 30-45 min momentum rides rather than abandoning training entirely
- Monitor the floor of ride intensity: if nothing in a training file dips below 0.5-0.6 IF, easy days are not easy enough
- Accept the rule of thirds — one-third of days will feel bad, and that is normal
- Build the assumption of 2-3 weeks of lost training into any season plan
- Track multi-year trends in consistency and bad-day frequency, not just peak power numbers

### Realistic Goal-Setting and Panic Training Prevention Require Expectation Management

Unrealistic expectations are the root cause of most coaching failures. Athletes who expect 30+ watts of FTP gain per year, or who set outcome goals like "win the race" without intermediate process milestones, inevitably encounter a gap between expectations and reality that triggers either panic training or burnout. FTP gains follow a log-linear curve: large early gains give way to diminishing returns. Expect roughly 10-15W of FTP gain per two training blocks, with 30W in a single year being exceptional. Process goals (improve positioning every lap, test nutrition protocol, complete one 6+ hour ride per month before an ultra) provide meaningful evaluation criteria regardless of race placement. Panic training — cramming volume and intensity before an event you feel underprepared for — works in roughly one out of ten cases and degrades performance in the other nine. The recommended sequence when panic temptation hits: (1) adjust expectations for the event, (2) consider changing event type, (3) if training is warranted, focus only on highest-ROI modalities. Anaerobic capacity and sprint work are "easy come, easy go" — crit shape can be reached in 2-4 weeks. Endurance/TTE work also responds relatively quickly. FTP does not come back fast. Being "undercooked and motivated" at the start line is often better than being "well-trained and exhausted." The no-makeup-training rule applies: if a session is missed, do not stack it onto the next week.

**Recommendations:**
- Use SMART goals; anchor the "achievable" component in actual recent performance, not aspirational targets
- Expect 10-15W FTP gain per training block; treat 30W/year as exceptional
- Set intermediate process milestones throughout the season
- Never stack missed sessions onto subsequent weeks; shift to the equivalent slot next week or drop it
- If undertrained for an event with less than 2 weeks to go, do no panic training at all — rest and arrive fresh
- With 6+ weeks remaining, focus on fast-ROI modalities: anaerobic repeats for crits, sweet spot extension for endurance events
- Separate best-case performance from most-likely performance when setting race expectations

### Good Coaching Requires Empathy, Daily Feedback, and Years of Calibration

Five episodes spanning coaching philosophy converge on several principles. Daily workout review with comments is identified as the single most important coaching practice — the absence of feedback loops was Kolie's biggest self-identified error in his early coaching years (2015-2016). Knowing an athlete takes approximately 3 months for workout calibration and up to a year for genuine understanding; this timeline is much longer than most athletes expect. The most common coaching mistake is overloading athletes with intensity (more than 2-3 hard days per week). Conservative hard sessions at RPE 7-8, never to absolute limits, produce equivalent or superior adaptation because they allow sustainable training frequency. Self-criticism is identified as the single most important trait for long-term coaching development. Communication medium matters: phone calls over text for sensitive conversations, because tone is lost in text and misinterpretation erodes trust. Mass-platform design (Zwift's Build Me Up) reveals that workout variety and compliance matter more than theoretical optimality — the most effective workout is the one athletes actually complete. Qualitative self-monitoring data is especially important for detecting the "fatigue security blanket" pattern: athletes who have trained heavily develop a sense that accumulated fatigue equals fitness and double down on volume when performance slides instead of pulling back.

**Recommendations:**
- Review athlete workouts daily and provide comments; this feedback loop is more important than the workout itself
- Limit hard sessions to 2-3 per week; more than this is the most common overloading error
- Hard sessions should target RPE 7-8, not absolute limits; leaving reserves enables consistent weekly quality
- Allow 3 months to calibrate workout structure for a new athlete and up to 1 year to truly know them
- Use phone calls rather than text for sensitive coaching conversations
- Treat declining comment tone as an early warning of chronic fatigue before power numbers drop

### Winter Training and Season Planning Should Be Individualized, Not Template-Driven

There is no universal winter training template or canonical periodization sequence. When to start intervals depends on when you need to be in shape, working backward from your first target event. For a July track Nationals, intervals can wait until January-February; for spring road racing, intervals should begin in November. The rigid "zone 2 base" orthodoxy is overrated — if you are detrained, any intensity level improves aerobic fitness. When confined to indoor training, reducing volume and increasing intensity density is reasonable; reverse periodization emerges naturally from practical constraints and is valid. Season planning has shifted from detailed 6-month pre-written plans to monthly sketches with ongoing revision. Kolie found that when disruptions hit early, rewriting 23 weeks of training was impractical; a rough seasonal outline with monthly focus areas requires far less rework. Volume follows a log-linear relationship: below roughly 18-20 hours per week, adding more volume almost always helps aerobic capacity; above it, intensity quality and recovery become the dominant variables. The real benefit of sustained high volume is durability rather than peak FTP. Gym work at 2x per week should not count as "hard training" — keep it at 4-6 working sets per session, avoiding designs exceeding 10-15 total weekly sets for lower body.

**Recommendations:**
- Work backward from your first target event to determine when structured intervals should begin
- Do not default to "zone 2 only" in base; use the minimum effective dose principle
- Prioritize adding volume before adding intensity if currently below 18-20 hr/week
- Keep gym work at 2x per week with 4-6 working sets per session
- Use Zwift racing sparingly (once per week maximum) as a fun hard-session substitute
- Do not reverse-engineer a pro's Strava as your volume prescription; treat survey data as correlational, not causal

### Parent-Athletes Should Be Programmed as Time-Crunched Athletes with Off-Bike Stress Explicitly Accounted For

Off-bike TSS from childcare, sleep disruption, and family stress is real, physiologically meaningful load that should reduce training volume and intensity prescriptions. Split rides (45 minutes morning plus 45 minutes evening) are physiologically equivalent to a single 90-minute ride and more compatible with childcare schedules. For 50/50 custody arrangements, alternating higher-load and lower-load weeks creates a natural periodization structure. Training blocks for parents benefit from shorter durations (2 weeks rather than 4) aligned to natural schedule breaks. Process goals (did I complete the ride? did I fuel well?) outperform outcome goals for sustainability, and starting with a bar "so low you could trip over it" builds consistency that ultimately outperforms ambitious initial targets. Motivation resets — periods of reduced structure or purely enjoyable riding — are legitimate training interventions. Daily saline nasal rinse can meaningfully reduce respiratory infection frequency for parents frequently exposed to childhood illnesses.

**Recommendations:**
- Program parent-athletes for minimum effective dose; account for off-bike stress by reducing training load, not just acknowledging it sympathetically
- Use split rides when schedule demands; they are physiologically equivalent for most endurance and moderate-intensity sessions
- Leverage custody schedules or natural life rhythm as built-in periodization
- Set goals using process measures and values-based frameworks rather than outcome targets
- Daily saline nasal rinse can meaningfully reduce respiratory infection frequency

### Stimulus Proxies Should Be Type-Specific, and Fatigue Is a Byproduct — Not a Goal

Stimulus and adaptation are not the same thing: stimulus is what happens during the workout, adaptation is what the body does afterward, and no proxy directly measures adaptation. Type-specific proxies provide better coaching signals than universal metrics: recovery rides should be forgettable; endurance rides measured by cumulative volume and feeling easy; threshold identified by the combination of significant leg fatigue and labored but controlled breathing; VO2max confirmed when breathing is the primary limiter; anaerobic capacity recognized by concentric leg failure rather than breathing. Sprint peak watts are primarily a freshness indicator, not a sprint fitness indicator — a low sprint peak signals accumulated fatigue. TSS is specifically called out as unreliable because it conflates different stimulus types: 100 TSS from a 5-hour endurance ride and 100 TSS from threshold intervals represent completely different physiology. Fatigue is a byproduct of training stimulus, not itself a driver of adaptation — seeking fatigue as a goal inverts causality and leads to junk volume. Energy neutral intake (not surplus) is sufficient for endurance adaptation. These proxies give coaches and self-coached athletes a practical vocabulary for evaluating whether sessions are achieving their intended purpose.

**Recommendations:**
- Use type-specific proxies: breathing for VO2max, leg failure for anaerobic, cumulative volume for endurance — not a single metric like TSS
- Interpret low sprint peak watts as a fatigue signal, not a sprint training deficit
- Do not pursue fatigue as a training goal; the stimulus drives adaptation, and fatigue is a constraining side effect
- Energy neutral intake is sufficient for endurance adaptation; chronic deficit is the primary risk
- Track total training time rather than kilojoules for endurance development

### Evolution of Views

The coaching philosophy presented across these episodes shows clear progression over the podcast's timeline, from prescriptive training toward individualized, athlete-responsive practice.

**From rigid plans to coached auto-regulation.** The earliest episodes already contain seeds of skepticism toward rigid training compliance, but the shift becomes explicit doctrine by the later episodes. Gediminas's personal account of 100% compliance leading to burnout (episode `2150935608`) becomes a cautionary tale that shapes how all the coaches screen new clients. By the latest episodes, Kolie states directly that he needs "less compliance" from athletes, and flagging all-green TrainingPeaks calendars is standard practice. Kolie's retrospective roast of his 2015-2016 training plans (episode `1493985610`) is a turning point: he identifies the absence of daily feedback, excessive intensity stacking, and poor mechanistic targeting as hallmarks of early-career coaching. By the later episodes, all three coaches emphasize RPE-anchored, sensation-based calibration over rigid power targets. Tim Cusick (episode `1383294643`) and Mikael Eriksson (episode `1338356839`) independently describe the same arc from prescriptive zones to athlete-centered, feedback-driven practice.

**Stress management as core pillar.** Stress management became a core pillar of coaching after Kolie's 2016-17 experience with a client whose extreme work stress negated all training adaptations (episode `2012769243`). Before that case, training dose was assumed to be the primary lever. After it, stress management was elevated to co-equal status with training prescription. The parenting episode (episode `2067161732`) and motivation reset episode (episode `2098202721`) represent a late-podcast expansion of this concept: off-bike stress as real training load, values-based goal-setting, and the explicit hierarchy of family-work-cycling.

The sub-100W and sub-15 TSS thresholds (episodes `1594046289`, `1843418724`) represent a significant departure from earlier norms. By the later episodes (episode `2114034129`), the hosts present HRV evidence that even clearly sub-threshold riding can delay recovery for truly fatigued athletes. Increasing emphasis on the psychological barriers to rest — fear of fitness loss, social media comparison, CTL anxiety — suggests the hosts recognized the physiological case for recovery had been made but athletes were still failing to implement it for psychological reasons.

**Periodization philosophy individualized.** The earlier approach followed canonical sequences (zone 2 base, then tempo, then threshold, then VO2max). By episodes `2215377053` and `2196447155`, coaches explicitly reject this framework in favor of "what does your life allow, and what's your race calendar?" Season planning shifted from detailed 6-month pre-written plans to monthly sketches with ongoing revision (episode `2252506076`). Kolie found that when disruptions hit early, rewriting 23 weeks of training was impractical.

**From power-centric to qualitative coaching.** Kolie identifies a past error in early coaching: not knowing how to use qualitative athlete feedback (episode `2269775624`). By the later episodes (episode `2150935608`), qualitative self-monitoring is positioned as among the most actionable coaching data available — elevated from "nice to have" to core methodology. James McKay (episode `2265497594`) frames diagnosing recovery limitation in low-volume athletes as "a relatively recent conceptual development in the coaching community."

**Fueling revolution.** The coaches describe a collective shift away from fasted training and fat-burning-zone mythology dominant around 2010-2015 (episode `2269775624`). The old standard of ~25g carbohydrate per hour has been replaced by dramatically higher fueling targets.

**Formal testing reduced.** Kolie describes reducing formal FTP testing for athletes (episode `2150935608`) because well-designed training plans already surface fitness trends — a shift from testing as routine practice to testing as optional confirmation. ## 11. Equipment & Technique

### Aerodynamic Position Gains Dwarf Weight Savings for Nearly All Cycling Disciplines

The single most consistent finding across this cluster is that aerodynamic drag reduction produces far larger performance gains than weight reduction at virtually all real-world cycling speeds. Kyle Helson's physics analysis demonstrates that even an extreme factor-of-two difference in rim weight (1,000g vs. 2,000g) yields only 1-2% power savings during a criterium acceleration, and typical consumer wheel upgrades saving 100-300g produce a fraction of a percent. Once air resistance is included, the rotating weight advantage shrinks further. By contrast, Giancarlo Bianchi demonstrates that a rider at 4.1-4.3 w/kg can regularly beat 5+ w/kg riders by maintaining an aerodynamic road position (90-degree elbows on the hoods, flat back, head down) and using deep-section wheels. Corey Lockwood reinforces this principle for time trialing: a rider at 300W in a dialed position can match a rider at 400W with poor aerodynamics. The one notable exception is Everesting, where speeds of 6-8 mph render aerodynamic drag negligible and weight savings legitimately matter -- the hosts explicitly flag this as the inverse of their usual advice.

**Recommendations:**
- Prioritize aerodynamic improvements (position, helmet, deep wheels, skin suit) over weight savings for any event above ~13 mph average speed
- Choose deep-section wheels (50-60mm) over lightweight shallow wheels for most racing; the weight penalty is seconds on a climb while the aero gain applies constantly
- Reserve weight-focused equipment decisions for sustained climbing events (Everesting, hillclimbs) where speed is low enough that drag is minimal
- Spend equipment budget on bike fitting and position optimization before lightweight components

### Time Trial Position Should Optimize Power Output First, Then Add Aerodynamics

Lockwood and Moore argue against the common approach of forcing the most aggressive aero position possible. A TT position that is radically different from the road bike reduces both power output and time to exhaustion. The correct sequence is to find the position where you produce your best sustainable power, then make that position as aerodynamic as possible -- not to sacrifice watts for drag reduction. Lockwood cites velodrome testing where a visually "worse" position measured faster because the power-to-drag tradeoff favored it. Making yourself "really small" does not automatically produce smoother airflow; extremely aggressive positions can create turbulence that negates frontal area reduction. Equipment rigidity matters enormously here: cheap or flexible aero bars that move 0.5cm per bump change hip angle mid-ride, making the position non-repeatable. Carbon one-piece or 3D-printed bars that lock in position were called a "critical component" of Lockwood's national record attempt. Without a wind tunnel, riders can test positions using a consistent flat road segment, changing one variable at a time, comparing average speed at matched RPE -- Lockwood calls this the "rate of perceived exertion wind tunnel."

**Recommendations:**
- Start TT position development from your best sustainable power position, then add aero modifications incrementally
- Invest in rigid, non-flexing aero bars (carbon one-piece or equivalent) -- bar stiffness is a higher priority than bar shape
- Test every position change empirically; intuitive guesses about what "looks fast" are often wrong
- Without a wind tunnel, use consistent flat segments with single-variable changes and matched power/RPE to evaluate positions
- Use auditory and tactile feedback (wind noise, pressure on suit, head load) as drag proxies during field testing

### Gearing Is a Tactical Tool, Not a Set-and-Forget Decision

Multiple episodes treat gear selection as an active pacing and racing instrument rather than a static choice. Lockwood makes the case for a 60T chainring in flat TTs: it avoids spinning out on descents and false flats, produces a straighter chain line with less drivetrain loss, and enables a low-cadence "diesel mode" (70-80 RPM at ~300W) where the rider can recover speed without spiking heart rate or breathing. On hilly TT courses, he reverts to 53/39 or compact -- the 60T becomes a liability on climbs. For track cycling, gear selection is even more consequential: Moore recommends keeping a detailed log of gears used per event, matching gear to expected race dynamics (bigger gear for steady breakaway efforts, smaller gear for surgy field races). At the extreme end, Jeffrey Hoogland reportedly rides the same standing lap time on ~100-inch gear as on ~118-inch gear, illustrating that raw strength can make gear choice less relevant -- but only at the absolute highest level. For Everesting, the advice reverses entirely: choose gearing so you can still climb at 50-100W below target pace, keep 1-2 bail-out gears easier than your preferred gear, and consider mountain bike cranksets for access to smaller chainrings.

**Recommendations:**
- For flat TTs, consider a 60T chainring (1x) to enable low-cadence speed maintenance without breathing spikes
- Revert to standard or compact gearing for hilly TTs and climbing events
- Keep a gearing log for track events documenting what worked under specific race conditions
- For ultra-endurance climbing events, ensure at least 1-2 bail-out gears below your target climbing gear
- Use a gearing calculator to match gear ratio to target speed and preferred cadence before events

### Cadence Should Be Managed Deliberately by Terrain and Objective, Not Fixed at a Single Number

Cadence preferences are highly individual, but the consistent message is that cadence is a tool to manage speed, fatigue, and breathing -- not a number to lock in. Lockwood describes using higher cadence to accelerate and spin up to speed, then dropping to steady-state cadence (wherever breathing rhythm balances with leg pressure, whether 80, 85, or 90 RPM), and further dropping to 70-80 RPM in a big gear on false flats for active recovery while maintaining speed. Spinning out your ceiling elevates heart rate and is difficult to bring back down, so high cadence should be reserved for deliberate acceleration phases. For track cycling, cadence specificity is critical: endurance track racers should modify over-under intervals to take overs at 120-130 RPM to simulate race-pace accelerations, but should not do all FTP work at high cadence. The modern track sprint revolution has moved away from high-cadence small-gear approaches (1990s-era 180 RPM flying 200s on 51x14) toward bigger gears with lower cadence, emphasizing strength and sustained force production. Top-end leg speed is largely a natural attribute -- dedicated high-cadence drills (tiny cranks, 200+ RPM work) "arrived as a trend and departed almost as quickly."

**Recommendations:**
- Use cadence deliberately as a pacing tool: higher to accelerate, steady-state to maintain, lower to recover
- Know your personal steady-state cadence and use it as a baseline; do not chase an arbitrary target
- For track endurance events, incorporate high-cadence (120-130 RPM) intervals in race-specific sessions but keep general FTP work at normal cadence
- Do not invest heavily in leg-speed drills for sprint improvement; gains of 10-15 RPM are possible but top-end cadence is largely innate
- Avoid chronically high cadence in TTs, which elevates heart rate and compromises the ability to recover mid-effort

### Sprinting Is a Technique Problem as Much as a Power Problem

Moore's sprint drill episode makes the case that sprinting is technique-limited, not just power-limited. Newton's third law means the bike pushes back when you push on the pedals, so sprinting requires both force production and force stabilization through the core and arms. Without proper stabilization, force "leaks" through torso rotation instead of going into the pedals. The three-drill progression -- big-gear force stabilization (20-30 RPM to 60-70 RPM), small-gear high-cadence bike rocking (up to 190-200 RPM standing), and full-power race-speed sprints -- builds the neural patterns separately before integrating them. The counterintuitive primary cue for high-cadence sprinting is "relax" -- being too tight locks up muscles and prevents the rapid contraction-relaxation cycle needed for high RPM. After doing small-gear drills at 170 RPM, normal sprint cadence (110-120 RPM) feels slow, making it easier to refine technique at race speed. Bike fit matters: if the saddle bumps your thighs when standing and rocking the bike, the cockpit is too short. Sprint drill work should always be done fresh, in the first 60-90 minutes of a ride -- fatigued sprinting teaches bad motor patterns.

**Recommendations:**
- Incorporate 1-2 sprint drills into interval ride warmups, 2-3 times per week
- Progress through big-gear stabilization, small-gear high-cadence, then full-power race-speed sprints
- Keep total sprint time per session to ~60-90 seconds; quality over quantity
- Do all sprint technique work when fresh; never practice sprints in a fatigued state
- If the saddle contacts your thighs during standing sprints, investigate a longer cockpit
- Focus on relaxation as the primary cue for high-cadence work -- tension is the enemy of speed

**Sources:** Episodes 914859241-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-9-drills-to-hone-your-sprint-technique.txt, 1723883430-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-33-track-training-considerations.txt

### Criterium Cornering Lines Should Match Your Power Profile

Lockwood's motocross-informed approach to criterium cornering rejects a single "correct" line. For non-sprinters who lack explosive acceleration, the optimal strategy is a late-apex line: brake earlier, open the turn up, lay the bike over, and pedal through the turn seated at 400-500W. This preserves momentum and avoids the 800W standing sprint out of every corner that exhausts a diesel rider. For pure sprinters, a cut-and-thrust (square-up) line works: hard brake into the corner, pivot inside, sprint out. This costs more energy per corner but suits riders with high peak power who can absorb the repeated efforts. The key skill underlying both approaches is sensing the limit of grip: feel feedback through handlebars and outside pedal (countersteer loading), listen for tire noise at the limit, and read surface transitions (chip seal to asphalt, concrete to asphalt) before reaching limit conditions. Equipment setup directly affects cornering: tire width, wheel depth, PSI, and contact patch all change cornering traction and spin-up speed, while crank length determines whether you can pedal through tight-radius turns.

**Recommendations:**
- Non-sprinters should practice late-apex lines to maximize exit velocity while staying seated
- Sprinters can use cut-and-thrust lines if their power profile supports repeated hard accelerations
- Learn to feel tire grip feedback through the handlebars and outside pedal
- Adjust tire pressure, width, and wheel depth for specific courses -- these directly affect cornering traction
- Consider shorter cranks if pedal strikes are limiting your ability to pedal through tight corners

**Sources:** Episodes 1169482870-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-5-coaching-the-process-with-cory-lockwood.txt, 1239908659-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-10-a-guide-to-time-trialing-with-cory-lockwood.txt

### Course Reconnaissance Requires a Different Mental Mode Than Training on the Same Roads

Both Lockwood and Bianchi emphasize that riding a training route and reconning it as a race course are fundamentally different activities. Recon means analyzing cracks, sight lines, gradient changes, wind exposure, braking points, and where to position before key sections. Bianchi reconned the Colorado State Road Race championship course even though he trained on it regularly. Lockwood breaks TT courses into 15-20 distinct sections, each with its own pacing strategy, body position, and cadence plan. His first-day record attempt at Moriarty failed not from insufficient fitness but from insufficient course knowledge -- he over-committed on the tailwind-assisted outbound leg and was blown up 10 minutes before the finish. The second day, with conservative outbound pacing informed by the first attempt, he set the record. Wind reconnaissance is particularly important: Bianchi describes winning races by knowing exactly where a tailwind section transitions to a crosswind, pre-positioning in the top 5 before the turn to exploit echelon dynamics that shatter the field.

**Recommendations:**
- Recon every race course in race-mode mindset, even courses you train on regularly
- For TTs, break the course into 15-20 micro-sections with specific power, cadence, and position plans for each
- Study wind patterns and how they interact with course geometry; identify crosswind transition points
- On out-and-back TTs, do not over-commit on the outbound leg when wind conditions may reverse
- Use the lap function to clock gaps at landmarks during the race for real-time tactical information

**Sources:** Episodes 1406970607-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-21-behind-the-new-american-40km-itt-record-with-cory-lockwood.txt, 1658393151-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-31-being-faster-than-your-watts-with-giancarlo-bianchi.txt, 1239908659-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-10-a-guide-to-time-trialing-with-cory-lockwood.txt

### Aero Helmet and Skin Suit Selection Require Individual Testing -- Nothing Is Universally Fastest

Lambie's experience with the hoo-ha/Watt Bike team establishes that no aero helmet is universally fastest -- the optimal helmet depends on the rider's position, head shape, and riding style. Some helmets (e.g., the POC model discussed) are extremely fast but only within a narrow position window; the team tested positions before committing to a helmet choice. Similarly, Lockwood and Moore note that skin suit performance varies dramatically by manufacturer and model: a cheap, slow skin suit may be slower than shaved skin. Smoother surfaces are not always faster aerodynamically (the golf ball effect applies), and body hair aero effects are genuinely uncertain. The underlying principle is that every aerodynamic choice must be tested rather than assumed. Lambie's team used an Alpha Mantis system with a formal testing protocol -- structured methodology for what you test, how you test it, and what the numbers mean is necessary. "You can't just throw something in and call it aero testing." For riders without access to formal testing, Lockwood's field-testing methodology (one variable at a time on a consistent road segment) provides a practical alternative.

**Recommendations:**
- Test helmets in your actual riding position before purchasing; the fastest helmet is rider-specific
- Do not assume expensive skin suits are faster than alternatives without testing
- Use structured testing protocols whether in a tunnel, on a track, or on the road
- Change only one variable at a time during aero testing and return to baseline between changes
- Do not dismiss equipment optimization as irrelevant below a certain ability level -- every detail matters at every level

**Sources:** Episodes 611724549-empiricalcyclingpodcast-talking-to-aston-lambie.txt, 1239908659-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-10-a-guide-to-time-trialing-with-cory-lockwood.txt, 1169482870-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-5-coaching-the-process-with-cory-lockwood.txt

### Ultra-Endurance Equipment Preparation Is About Controlling Variables, Not Chasing Performance

The Everesting episode inverts several principles from the racing-focused episodes. At 6-8 mph on a steep climb, aerodynamics are negligible and weight legitimately matters. But the dominant theme is logistics over performance: do a full mechanical once-over a week before (not the night before), bring spare tubes, tires, wheels, cables, and possibly a whole spare bike, wear your most proven and comfortable kit (not new or untested gear), and never try new nutrition on event day. Bike fit should be checked 1-2 months before the attempt specifically at the gradient of the target climb, because steep gradients close hip angle and strain the lower back. If training on a stationary trainer, set the bike to the average slope of the target hill to simulate position specificity. The same "control variables" principle applies to nutrition: plan for ~93g/hr carbohydrates, include protein to prevent the empty-gut feeling from gel-only nutrition, eat early and eat often, and test everything on recon rides. The central message is that logistics failures end more Everesting attempts than fitness deficits.

**Recommendations:**
- Complete a full bike mechanical check one week before any ultra-endurance event
- Get a gradient-specific bike fit 1-2 months before a climbing event
- Wear proven, comfortable kit; never debut new equipment on event day
- Bring comprehensive spares and have an emergency plan with cell coverage mapped
- Test all nutrition on recon rides; plan for ~90-100g/hr carbohydrate and include protein for satiation

**Sources:** Episodes 890510818-empiricalcyclingpodcast-watts-doc-25-everesting-training-and-preparation.txt

### Evolution of Views

The most prominent shift across this cluster is the evolution in track sprint training philosophy. Moore describes the move from 1990s-era high-cadence, small-gear approaches (flying 200s on 51x14 at 180 RPM) to the modern big-gear revolution driven by the Dutch program and coaches like Metty Cordi, which emphasizes getting "prison big" and pushing massive gears. High-cadence leg-speed drills (tiny cranks, 200+ RPM work) arrived as a trend and departed quickly -- "nobody ever found it to be that effective" (episode 1723883430). Moore himself did "a complete 180" over the preceding 4-5 years on his own flat-vs-climbing power profile.

Lockwood's training philosophy evolved significantly through his work with Moore. In episode 1406970607, he explicitly identifies years of "muddy training" -- riding endurance too hard, doing 3-5 interval sessions per week, and not periodizing gym work -- as mistakes corrected only after working with Moore. He also shifted from 4x8-minute FTP intervals to continuous 60-minute TTE-style threshold work: "if you want to ride your bike for 60 minutes, go ride for 60 minutes."

Bianchi's tactical thinking evolved after his Albuquerque Masters Nationals race (episode 1658393151). He initially attacked a tired breakaway companion too early; post-race debriefing with Moore changed his understanding -- a tired companion still provides draft and occasional pulls, so keeping them until the final kilometer is usually the superior strategy.

Early in the podcast, Moore described being told by a coach to ride 4 hours after heavy gym work "so your body doesn't forget it's a cyclist." He now considers this unnecessary for sprint cyclists (episode 1723883430), reflecting a broader shift toward respecting recovery between distinct training stimuli.

Lambie's interview (episode 611724549, one of the earliest in the cluster) established the "no arbitrary threshold for equipment optimization" principle that persists throughout later episodes. The US national team's adoption of structured aero testing protocols -- imported from the British hoo-ha team via Lambie -- represents an institutional evolution in how American track cycling approached equipment.

## 12. Racing & Team Tactics

### Criterium Success Depends More on Positioning Than Fitness

Across multiple episodes featuring riders at the national and USA Crits level, the single most consistent finding is that positioning in the field trumps raw power output for criterium results. Taylor Warren states that "where you are 10 to go is most likely where you're going to finish plus or minus one or two spots," meaning the race outcome is largely determined by mid-race positioning, not the final sprint alone. Alex Carmona reinforces this: a rider with a 300 W FTP can finish top 5-10 in national-level crits if they can hold top 15 wheels throughout the race. Cole Tamburri's multi-year journey illustrates the inverse — despite reaching ~400 W FTP, he could not finish the GMSR crit until he developed positioning skills separately from fitness. The energy cost of being out of position is enormous: at Athens Twilight, the rider at the front of a single-file corner does 300 W while the 60th-wheel rider does 600 W. Positioning is a logistical skill that starts before the race — staging early, being on the front row behind the moto after the neutral lap, and moving forward immediately when the neutral ends. This skill must be practiced directly in races; group rides and trainers cannot fully replicate it.

**Recommendations:**
- Get to the front during staging (arrive 30+ minutes early) and maintain position from the neutral lap onward
- Practice positioning specifically in pack-riding contexts, not just solo cornering drills
- Recognize that fitness alone plateaus at the Cat 2+ level; race craft must be actively developed as a separate skill

**Sources:** Episodes 1138654516, 985640092, 1874275173, 1921582412

### Cornering at Race Speed Is the Primary Crit-Specific Skill to Train

Cornering ability at 30+ mph in traffic is the rate-limiting skill for criterium racing. Taylor Warren and Alex Carmona both emphasize that cornering well at 20 mph is insufficient — the skill must be practiced at race speed (30+ mph). The recommended drill is finding a quiet downhill and repeatedly practicing corner entry, apex timing, and exit lines at full speed. However, cornering alone is not enough: cornering with riders five-wide and the accordion effect of a pack is a separate skill that solo practice does not develop. At flat USA Crits events, passing someone at 36 mph requires approximately 1,500 W, making cornering efficiency (carrying speed through corners rather than braking and re-accelerating) a massive energy saver. For hilly crits like Athens and Winston-Salem, the demands shift toward anaerobic repeatability (500-600 W every lap) rather than peak sprint power, and lighter riders gain an advantage. Equipment matters too: tire choice and pressure affect corner confidence, and switching tires mid-season (as Taylor did from Vittoria Corsa to Continental GP5000 28mm) can change handling feel significantly. Racing on unfamiliar or neutral equipment requires deliberate reassessment of corner entry speeds.

**Recommendations:**
- Practice cornering at 30+ mph on a quiet descent, not just at training pace
- Supplement solo cornering with group-ride pack cornering to develop the traffic-specific skill
- Test tire setup before race day; switching brands or sizes changes cornering behavior
- For hilly crits, prioritize anaerobic repeatability (500-600 W repeats) over peak sprint power

**Sources:** Episodes 1138654516, 636044361, 1874275173

### Race-Specific Preparation Should Follow a General-to-Specific Progression

The most detailed framework for translating off-season fitness into race readiness comes from Alex Carmona and Kolie Moore. The progression moves through distinct phases: first, a group ride to test both physiology and handling simultaneously (the single best first race-prep session). Then capacity work (30-30s: 30 seconds full gas / 30 seconds rest), followed by power with incomplete rest (2-5 minutes hard / 2-5 minutes rest), then maximum power with full recovery (30 seconds absolute max / 20 minutes recovery), and finally race-specific duration at race-specific effort. Embedded threshold intervals with 10-30 second jumps thrown in (100 W over threshold) mimic the "go hard, settle back in, stay hard" dynamics of actual road racing. Most athletes do too much race-specific work year-round rather than concentrating it in the 4-8 week window before target races. For cyclocross, the progression is similar but skill-weighted: start with one monthly skills session in August (sub-race-pace hot laps with mounts, dismounts, barriers, off-cambers), then add fitness-specific intervals only in the second season when skills are established. The number of practice sessions needed decreases with experience.

**Recommendations:**
- Use a Saturday group ride as the first race-prep workout of the season, not a structured interval session
- Follow the progression: 30-30s (capacity) then incomplete-rest intervals then max sprints then race-specific work
- Concentrate race-specific work in the 4-8 weeks before target races, not year-round
- For cyclocross, prioritize skills sessions over fitness sessions in year one; reduce to maintenance skills sessions by year three

**Sources:** Episodes 1688639271, 1874275173, 1138654516

### Racing-Specific Fitness Requires Discipline-Specific Training Beyond FTP

Three episodes argue that FTP and VO2max are necessary but insufficient for racing success. Between two athletes with the same FTP, the one with higher 30/30s power will almost always have better criterium results. Paris-Roubaix winner Alison Jackson's winning power file looked "ordinary" -- the race was decided by superior energy management, race intelligence, and fatigue resistance, not one outstanding power output. For criteriums: repeated short hard efforts (30/30s, 40/20s, 15/15s) paced as hard as can be sustained consistently across the full set, sprints from race speed (25-30 mph), and pre-fatigued threshold-to-sprint sequences replicate race demands. For cyclocross: VO2max efforts for the hard first 15 minutes, hot laps with short sharp 5-10 second efforts, and skills work under fatigue. For ultra/gravel: TTE extension, consecutive multi-day blocks to reveal nutritional deficits, and nutrition execution practice as an explicit training intervention. Race skill work -- pack riding, cornering, drafting, reading the race -- is framed as having bigger impact on results for many athletes than any fitness improvement. Training mindset and racing mindset are explicitly opposite: training aims to get tired efficiently, while racing aims to conserve energy while competing.

**Recommendations:**
- Build FTP as high as possible in the off-season; arriving at target events with a personal best FTP makes every intensity level feel easier
- For criteriums and road races: train repeated short efforts (30/30s, 40/20s) with flat power across all intervals, not a PR first effort
- For cyclocross: practice skills under fatigue, not just when fresh; technique practice is undertrained relative to its race impact
- For ultra/gravel: train consecutive long days (2-3 days back-to-back) and practice nutrition execution as a separate training intervention
- Invest in racing experience and tactical skills; for aerobically developed athletes, racing more often produces better returns than additional structured training

**Sources:** Episodes 662678975-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-1-training-for-cyclocross-season.txt, 1504009219-empiricalcyclingpodcast-perspectives-25-the-coaching-and-training-behind-a-parix-roubaix-victory-with-adam-pulford.txt, 2243291618-empiricalcyclingpodcast-ten-minute-tips-70-fitness-beyond-ftp.txt

### Stage Racing Rewards Volume-Based Fitness and Day-to-Day Self-Management

Multiple riders who competed at Redlands, Gila, Joe Martin, and Cascade Classic converge on the finding that stage race success depends on aerobic volume more than high-intensity preparation. Megan Eastler's breakthrough came from increasing weekly volume from 13 to 20+ hours and replacing multiple weekly VO2/30-30 sessions with structured endurance plus a single hard session per week. Taylor Warren achieved PRs 13 years into his career by pushing to 20-30 hour weeks at very easy pace. Stage 1 is consistently identified as the worst stage — first-time jitters, positioning errors, altitude adjustment — and riders should not panic. Performance typically improves from stage 2 onward. During dense race calendars (Redlands to Gila to Joe Martin = 14 race days in 25 days), the correct approach is prioritizing recovery over training: threshold sessions only in 2-week gaps, no VO2 work, and letting racing supply the high intensity. Self-management across stages — pacing, eating, sleep quality — matters as much as single-day power. Ingvar Omarsson's coach explicitly recommended racing fewer events: "doing a race just to do a race" sacrifices training quality when the first A-race is months away.

**Recommendations:**
- Build aerobic volume (20+ hours/week) as the primary stage-race preparation, not high-intensity repeats
- Limit hard sessions to one per week when building for stage races; 7-hour long rides are the sweet spot
- During dense race blocks, prioritize recovery; let racing provide high intensity
- Do not panic after a poor Stage 1; expect improvement through Stages 3-5
- Race fewer events — quality over quantity; protect training blocks between A-races

**Sources:** Episodes 1622992359, 1630053015, 1174414534, 659340143

### Fueling at 90-100 g Carbohydrate Per Hour Is a Performance Unlock for Racing

The single most consistently cited nutrition intervention across the cluster is pushing carbohydrate intake to 90-100+ grams per hour during racing and endurance rides. Ingvar Omarsson went from minimal fueling to 100 g/hr and produced his first UCI podium within days. Taylor Warren shifted from 40-60 g/hr to 90-100 g/hr and saw sustained fitness trends upward across an entire season. Emma Edwards received the same coaching intervention (100 g/hr) at Cascade Classic, and Kolie cited it as the single biggest intervention that unlocked a UCI podium for Ingvar. Taylor's practical solution: DIY lemonade in 2-liter bottles with 100-150 g table sugar + lemon juice + 2 g sea salt per bottle. The accumulated recovery and energy effect manifests across a whole training block, not just a single ride. On-bike carbohydrate restriction is explicitly warned against, even when managing body weight off the bike. Gut tolerance is individual — Taylor tolerates high sugar concentrations well, but others may need gradual adaptation.

**Recommendations:**
- Target 90-100 g carbohydrate per hour during racing and endurance rides
- Use simple, cost-effective solutions (table sugar + water + salt + flavor) rather than expensive commercial products
- Never restrict on-bike carbohydrates for weight management; restrict mildly off the bike if needed
- Gradually increase fueling rates to build gut tolerance if currently below 60 g/hr

**Sources:** Episodes 1630053015, 1174414534, 659340143, 1266057934

### Team Time Trial Success Comes From Communication and Institutional Knowledge, Not Just Fitness

MIT's women's TTT victory at collegiate nationals illustrates that the team time trial is won through accumulated protocols, not individual power. Emma Edwards identified communication as the single biggest success factor: "something's going to go wrong; being flexible and communicative was super, super important." MIT's multi-year institutional memory for TTT — standardized commands, calibrated pull durations, aero positioning rules — provided a structural advantage that no single training session could replicate. Tactically, shorter or no pulls should be assigned to the weakest or tallest (most wind-exposed) rider early in the race to conserve energy for finishing climbs. The lightest rider should sit behind the tallest to maximize draft. The host frames this broader principle explicitly: "What is a break? A break is a team time trial, just not with teammates." This insight extends to road racing breakaways, where the same communication and pacing skills apply. Two to three dedicated TTT practice sessions before target events are recommended for calibrating rotation, not fitness.

**Recommendations:**
- Conduct 2-3 TTT-specific practice sessions focused on communication protocols, pull duration calibration, and rotation order — not fitness
- Assign shorter pulls to the weakest or tallest rider; seat the lightest rider behind the tallest for maximum draft
- Conserve energy early so the weakest finisher contributes to the minimum-rider finish requirement
- Document TTT protocols across seasons to build institutional knowledge

**Sources:** Episodes 636044361

### Tactical Intelligence Is a Trainable Skill Separate From Physical Fitness

Alex Carmona's progression from Cat 3 to elite racer illustrates that race craft must be actively developed as a distinct skill. He evolved from thinking that showing up and riding hard would produce improvement to realizing that at Cat 2+ "you need to understand the races better, understand the courses better, understand who you're racing against better." The practical framework: map your winning scenario before a race and work backwards from it, analyzing your own skill set, the course, and your competitors. If you have a modest sprint (~600 W in a men's field), you cannot win from a field sprint — you must engineer a breakaway by following attacks or creating opportunities. Watching YouTube videos of crit sprints and breakaway coverage is legitimate preparation that develops pattern recognition. Erica Clevenger demonstrated that knowing which "cards" you hold changes how you race: her physiology allowed threshold-based volume to substitute for sprint-specific training, but she had to learn this about herself through experience. Cole Tamburri's case shows the opposite trap: riders who progress quickly on fitness alone develop blind spots in race craft that become exposed at higher levels. The athletes who struggled early often build stronger tactical foundations than natural talents.

**Recommendations:**
- Before each race, identify your winning scenario and work backwards: what course features favor you, who are the key competitors, what is your sprint capacity relative to the field
- Study race footage (YouTube, Flobikes) to develop pattern recognition for sprint setups, breakaway timing, and field dynamics
- If your sprint is modest for your category, focus on engineering breakaways rather than contesting field sprints
- Treat race craft as a separate training domain — schedule it deliberately, don't assume it develops passively

**Sources:** Episodes 985640092, 1138654516, 1921582412, 1874275173

### Endurance Pace Should Be Lower Than Most Athletes Default To

A recurring finding across racing-focused episodes is that athletes consistently ride too hard on easy days, accumulating fatigue that compromises race performance. Katie Aman was prescribed 174 W as endurance pace when her actual aerobic ceiling was ~130 W — 44 watts too high, producing chronic fatigue. Taylor Warren settled on 180-185 W for his ~300+ W FTP, explicitly noting that RPE-guided endurance pace naturally stabilizes around the correct value regardless of ride duration. His key insight: "If you're going to get the same adaptation from riding 180 watts as from 220 watts — and most research shows that's probably the case — then the 220-watt ride just burns more kilojoules and you're more tired, with nothing to show for it." The "200 watts" mental target is dismissed as a round-number ego artifact. Taylor Warren achieved PRs riding 30-hour weeks at very easy pace (140 and 180-mile back-to-back days), confirming that volume at genuinely easy intensity, independent of intensity, drives performance. Hard days should be truly hard; easy days must be genuinely easy — Katie's coach prescribed 80 W for easy days, roughly one-third of her threshold.

**Recommendations:**
- Use RPE as the primary guide for endurance pace — it should feel "suspiciously easy"
- For most riders with 250-350 W FTP, genuine endurance pace is 160-185 W, not "200 W"
- Easy days should be at roughly one-third of threshold power
- Do not add intensity to endurance rides; the adaptation is the same at lower power with less fatigue cost

**Sources:** Episodes 1630053015, 1266057934, 1622992359

### Overtraining Can Coexist With Strong Race Results and Must Be Detected Through Subjective Signals

Katie Aman's experience challenges the assumption that overtraining always manifests as poor performance. She won multiple elite races, finished 26th at nationals, and placed 7th at a stage race GC while chronically underfueled and overtrained. The early warning signs are subjective: high RPE at normal wattages, loss of motivation, inability to sustain repeated threshold intervals, soreness when accelerating from a standstill or going upstairs, and for women, loss of menstrual cycle. Coaches should pull back before the athlete self-reports — these signals precede performance decline. Cole Tamburri's experience adds the male perspective: low libido and hormonal suppression are warning signs, not badges of honor, despite cycling culture rhetoric. Both Katie and Cole found that gaining weight from an underweight state produced dramatic performance improvements — Katie gained 200 W of sprint power (600 to ~800 W) and Cole was 9 minutes faster on a 40-minute climb after gaining 20 lbs. Forced rest from injury can paradoxically produce the best fitness in years by finally paying off accumulated recovery debt.

**Recommendations:**
- Monitor subjective fatigue signals (RPE creep, motivation loss, staircase soreness) as leading indicators of overtraining
- Do not use race results alone to assess training load appropriateness — good results can mask chronic underfueling
- For women, menstrual cycle loss requires immediate nutrition and recovery intervention
- If an athlete gains weight after being underweight and performance improves, the previous weight was a limiter, not an asset

**Sources:** Episodes 1266057934, 1921582412, 1174414534

### A Mid-Season Break Followed by a Volume Block Produces Late-Season Breakthroughs

Taylor Warren's season structure demonstrates the value of a deliberate mid-season reset. After an intense early-season race block, he took one full week off (approximately 5 hours of easy mountain biking), then executed a 6-week volume block at 20+ hours per week. This produced PRs in sustained power later in the season. The principle aligns with the broader finding that 80% of fitness gains happen in the off-season or dedicated build blocks — maintaining fitness during race season is relatively easy, but building requires concentrated, uninterrupted volume. Ingvar Omarsson's coach similarly recommended racing less in 2022 after an overloaded 2021 season, with the explicit framing that "more racing does not equal better results" even for a top-50 UCI rider. The signal that an athlete is ready to resume hard training after a break is motivational: wanting to go hard and feeling bored with easy riding. The inverse — refusing to push above threshold — indicates more easy volume is still needed. Season planning should sequence strength work (November-December), aerobic build plus VO2 block (January-February), and race sharpening (March-April) for a May A-race.

**Recommendations:**
- Take a full week off mid-season (easy spinning only), then execute a 4-6 week volume block before the late-season target races
- Use motivation to go hard as the readiness signal, not a calendar date
- Structure the annual plan: off-season strength, then aerobic build, then VO2 block, then race-specific sharpening
- Race fewer events to protect training blocks; every race-weekend costs training quality

**Sources:** Episodes 1630053015, 1174414534, 1688639271

## 13. Research & Methodology

### Single Studies Are Unreliable — Demand Replication and Prefer Meta-Analyses Before Changing Your Training

Exercise physiology operates under a p-value threshold of 0.05, meaning up to 1 in 20 published findings is a false positive by design. This is orders of magnitude less rigorous than the 5-sigma standard used in particle physics. Compounding this, an estimated 60-70% of published papers across science are not reproducible, and sports physiology is not exempt — partly because living human subjects introduce uncontrollable variability. The podcast hosts repeatedly warn against treating any single paper as settled truth. "The science says" is almost always an overstatement when referencing one or a handful of studies. Meta-analyses, which pool 5 to 40 studies, provide a far stronger statistical basis for claims. Kolie's own evolution on creatine illustrates this: after reviewing meta-analyses rather than individual studies, he became more confident that aerobic performance effects are truly null, whereas earlier single-study evidence left room for ambiguity.

**Recommendations:**
- Never change your training based on a single study; require replication across multiple studies with different populations before acting.
- When a meta-analysis exists on a topic, use it as the primary evidence source rather than cherry-picking individual papers.
- Be aware that "publish or perish" incentives lead to p-hacking — researchers running many regressions and reporting the one that crosses significance — which inflates false-positive rates beyond the nominal 5%.
- Treat industry-funded studies (especially supplement research) with extra skepticism due to built-in conflicts of interest.

### Population Averages Do Not Predict Your Individual Response — Every Athlete Is an N=1 Experiment

The "fallacy of division" is endemic to how training research is communicated: a group average tells you nothing reliable about any given individual's response. A meta-analysis on training intensity distributions found average VO2max improvement of approximately 2% — but individual responses ranged from -20% to +30%. Standard deviations reported in papers are not just statistical noise; they prove that individual variation exists and is often larger than the group effect. Papers that show individual-level data (such as Bent Ronnestad's work) are more useful for coaching than those reporting only group means, precisely because the spread is the coaching-relevant finding. The hosts argue that most apparent "non-responders" are not fundamentally different physiologically — they are experiencing dosing problems, recovery deficits, or inappropriate outcome measures. Coaching is therefore always n=1 science: every training plan is an experiment, and evidence-based practice means acting on the individual's response data, adjusting iteratively, not defaulting to population-level recommendations.

**Recommendations:**
- Use the scientific literature as a starting point for a protocol, then track individual athlete response and adjust; do not lock in because "the science supports it" if the athlete is not responding.
- Pre-establish what metric you expect to improve and what a reliable, meaningful change looks like (accounting for measurement error) before concluding an intervention worked.
- If progress stalls, diagnose by exclusion: assess training dose sufficiency, recovery quality, and off-bike stressors before concluding the training plan is ineffective.
- Assign 1-2 months as the minimum trial period before evaluating whether an experimental training change has worked.

### Study Populations Rarely Match You — Scrutinize Who Was Actually Tested

The applicability of any study hinges on the participant population. Untrained, recreationally trained, highly trained, and elite athletes produce wildly different effect sizes, and results from one group should not be assumed to transfer to another. A particularly insidious problem is studies recruiting athletes "early season" or after deliberate detraining wash-in periods: these subjects are hypersensitive to any stimulus, producing inflated effect sizes that will not replicate in genuinely in-season, well-trained athletes. Women are substantially underrepresented in exercise physiology research (with the exception of RED-S / LEA literature), limiting the applicability of most findings to female athletes. Even a study labeled "well-trained" may use recreationally active adults training fewer than 3 hours per week, making its findings irrelevant to competitive cyclists. The meta-analysis on training intensity distributions included approximately 350 participants, roughly 300 of whom were male — the authors acknowledged this limits generalizability, though they argued within-individual variation exceeds between-group variation.

**Recommendations:**
- When evaluating a paper, look at the participant list first: check training status, sex, age, sport specificity, and where in their season the athletes were tested.
- Be especially skeptical of "highly trained" populations tested early in their return from off-season — they are hypersensitive to stimuli and will show improvements from almost any intervention.
- Do not assume results from running, rowing, or swimming studies transfer directly to cycling without sport-specific validation.
- Recognize that findings from male-dominated studies may not apply equally to female athletes.

### Training Gains Follow a Logarithmic Curve — Short-Term Study Results Exaggerate Long-Term Differences

A landmark strength-training study (Steel et al., N=14,690, up to 6.8 years of data) demonstrated that training improvements follow a logarithmic growth curve with a mode change point around 26-31 weeks where gains slow dramatically. This pattern is visible in cycling FTP data as well. The critical implication is that short-duration intervention studies (3-18 weeks) capture the steepest part of the improvement curve, especially for less-trained starting points. A study showing one method is "twice as effective" as another over 8 weeks is almost certainly describing different positions on the early growth curve, not meaningfully different long-term ceilings. Coaches should calibrate annual improvement expectations accordingly — typically 10-15 watts per year for athletes well into their training careers. After a mid-season break (illness, vacation), athletes can expect to return to previous season bests within roughly one training block (about 3 months), not to regress permanently.

**Recommendations:**
- Plot your own FTP year-on-year; if the log-transformed time axis goes linear, you are on the expected diminishing-returns curve. Use this to set realistic goals.
- When large gains have occurred in consecutive years, shift emphasis toward race preparation and other weaknesses rather than continuing to chase aerobic ceiling gains.
- When reading study results, check where subjects were on their personal growth curve; rapid early-season improvements do not validate one method over another long-term.
- For early-career athletes, do not over-optimize training type — consistency and progressive overload matter more than method selection.

### Measurement Error Is Larger Than Most Training Effects — Use the "Meaningful, Noticeable, Measurable" Framework

A recurring theme across the podcast is that the effect sizes reported in exercise physiology studies often fall within or near measurement noise. Day-to-day time trial variability is approximately 1-3%, VO2max measurement error is 1-5%, and metabolic cart error is larger than a well-calibrated power meter's approximately 2% error. A 2-watt FTP increase is statistically indistinguishable from noise. A 5-watt FTP change is within normal measurement uncertainty. Even a "medium" effect size by conventional standards (SMD ~0.6) can translate to only 27-30 watts on a 1,000-watt sprint — barely outside power-meter error. The hosts advocate a practical framework: before acting on any finding, ask whether the effect is meaningful (large enough to matter for your goal), noticeable (detectable in real riding), and measurable (distinguishable from instrument and biological noise). A statistically significant effect that falls inside measurement error or is offset by body mass change is not actionable.

**Recommendations:**
- Do not celebrate or change training based on FTP changes smaller than 5-10 watts; these are within normal measurement uncertainty.
- When evaluating any supplement or intervention, apply the "meaningful / noticeable / measurable" filter before acting.
- Prefer convergent indicators (heart rate trends at fixed power, RPE trends, race results) over single-metric point-in-time tests for assessing training response.
- Monitor longer-term trends (monthly or across a full block) rather than reacting to individual test results.

### How to Read a Scientific Paper: Methods First, Conclusions Last

The methods section, not the conclusion, is the most important part of a paper for assessing trustworthiness. A flawed measurement tool invalidates otherwise interesting results — for example, a 1-minute all-out test is a poor proxy for FTP, and a 20-minute test does not assess 1-minute power physiology. Reading papers is a learnable, practiced skill requiring an iterative approach: read the abstract and introduction to gauge your background knowledge, evaluate the methods critically, examine figures by first identifying axis variables and units before interpreting patterns, and only then engage with the discussion. Small sample sizes alone are sufficient reason to substantially discount conclusions regardless of statistical significance. Authors can manipulate data presentation — best-fit lines can be applied to scattered data, and data can be massaged to support a desired conclusion. Published does not mean correct or final; papers are meant to be questioned and investigated further.

**Recommendations:**
- Start with the methods section to assess whether the measurement tools and protocol are appropriate for the stated research question.
- When reading figures, identify axis variables and units first, then predict what you expect before assessing whether the data supports the authors' claims.
- Note sample size and population characteristics before accepting conclusions; a study of six recreationally active men warrants much less confidence than a larger, more diverse study.
- Practice explaining papers to non-experts as a comprehension check — if you cannot explain the key finding simply, you may not fully understand it.

### Data Without Context Is Meaningless — Subjective Feedback and Off-Bike Factors Are Primary Data

Power files, heart rate traces, and lab metrics are only interpretable when paired with contextual information. Sleep quality, nutrition, life stress, menstrual cycle phase, equipment changes, and environmental conditions (heat, altitude, illness) all substantially affect performance data and must be captured alongside objective metrics. The podcast hosts argue that athlete communication — RPE reports, workout comments, and even the absence of communication (radio silence as a data signal) — is primary data on par with watts and heart rate. Failed AI coaching systems are cited as evidence that even comprehensive objective data cannot substitute for qualitative athlete feedback. A bike position change of 5-10mm in saddle height can shift RPE substantially, masking the true training stimulus and leading to misattribution of fatigue. Coaches' ability to detect when recovery is inadequate may exceed what any current sensor technology can provide, particularly for non-training stressors like seasonal depression or life stress.

**Recommendations:**
- Log workout feedback (RPE, context notes) in the same system as power data so subjective and objective data are paired for later analysis.
- Use the Borg CR10 scale (not the linear 6-20 scale) for above-threshold intensities, as lactate and pain responses are exponential.
- Report both global session RPE and interval-specific RPE; for interval sessions, note RPE for the first and last interval to capture fatigue across the session.
- Lead every data review with a question, not with data — open analysis software only after formulating what you want to know.

### The Literature Is Most Useful for Ruling Things Out, Not Prescribing Protocols

A counterintuitive but consistent position across the podcast is that scientific literature is most reliably useful for understanding what does not work or what mechanisms are implausible, rather than for prescribing specific protocols. Null results and mechanistic understanding narrow the intervention space usefully. The creatine episode exemplifies this: meta-analytic evidence convincingly rules out aerobic performance benefits, which is more actionable than any positive finding about marginal sprint gains. Coaching routinely leads scientific research because coaches run individualized experiments continuously in real time; the purpose of research is to follow and explain why things work, not to lead practice. The hosts consistently frame the literature as informing a starting point for experimentation — providing physiological understanding that combines with coaching experience and individual athlete characteristics — not as dictating specific workouts.

**Recommendations:**
- Use the literature primarily to eliminate interventions that probably do not work, narrowing your experimental space.
- When a client asks why they are doing a specific workout, explain that the literature informs physiological understanding, which combines with coaching experience and individual characteristics — no single paper dictates the plan.
- Treat any training framework championed in the literature (polarized, threshold, pyramidal) as a hypothesis to test on the individual, not a universal prescription; abandon or modify it if the athlete shows no improvement after 6-12 months.
- Recognize that coaching experience, not published research, is typically the leading edge of practical training knowledge.

### Beware Overclaimed Headlines — Science Communication Distorts More Than Bad Science

The hosts identify science communicators outside academia as causing more harm than bad actors inside academia — not through malice but through overconfident extrapolation that original authors would not endorse. Clickbait titles and science-influencer content often use accurate "weasel words" in the body while implying certainty in the headline that does not exist. The "durability" concept is cited as a case study: coaches have tracked fatigue resistance informally for decades, but the media explosion around the term circa 2024-2025 reflects marketing and trend-following more than new science, with conclusion-drawing outpacing what the small, young literature actually supports. Correlations of R=0.4 and small effect sizes in emerging concepts get amplified into strong heuristics. World Tour teams jumping on training bandwagons may be rational at that level (any small edge matters for potential hyper-responders), but this does not mean interventions will transfer to amateur athletes.

**Recommendations:**
- Be skeptical of any training claim using absolute language ("always," "you must," "the only way") — these are hallmarks of oversimplification.
- When encountering a bold claim, trace it back to the original paper and check the actual effect size, confidence interval, and population studied.
- Recognize that trending training concepts may reflect media cycles more than new scientific discoveries; ask whether the underlying training prescription actually differs from what good coaches were already doing.
- Do not assume that what works for World Tour teams applies to amateur athletes operating under fundamentally different constraints.

### Evolution of Views

The podcast's approach to research methodology has undergone a clear and self-documented maturation arc. In the earliest episodes (Tip 3, circa episode 708990247), the treatment of bro science and research skepticism was straightforward — be wary of absolute claims, require replication. By Tip 10 (episode 949117027), the hosts provided practical paper-reading skills but did not yet engage deeply with statistical concepts like effect sizes or reproducibility rates.

The middle period saw the hosts become more prescriptive and confident. Kolie's VO2max series (Watts Doc 23-24, referenced in episode 2135972586) delivered specific interval recommendations that were taken more literally than intended. He later described these episodes as embarrassing and walked back the prescriptive framing, acknowledging he should have included more guardrails and caveats. He also stopped trying to "rip apart" studies as a format, viewing it as sounding smarter than it was.

By the later episodes (Tip 46, episode 1977186143), the vocabulary had matured substantially: Kolie explicitly cited the "fallacy of division," engaged with reproducibility crisis data (60-70% non-reproducible), and acknowledged that he himself had occasionally over-interpreted findings by "five or ten percent" and been "more bullish" than warranted in earlier episodes.

The most recent episodes show a further shift. Kolie now communicates statistics openly (effect sizes, confidence intervals, forest plots) — influenced by the Mass Podcast / Iron Culture approach — where earlier episodes avoided detailed statistics. He expresses increased comfort with meta-analytic methods (episode 2191047787) having previously avoided them due to complexity. His coaching philosophy has shifted from maximizing single variables to asking "what is the lowest fatigue intervention that will produce the needed improvement?" Jem Arnold (episode 2040860164) mirrors this arc, noting his 2019-2020 blog posts on optimizing interval protocols no longer keep him up at night — the literature shows session-to-session differences produce only 1-3% effects. Both hosts have converged on a position that the literature is better for ruling things out and calibrating expectations than for dictating specific protocols, and that explicitly flagging weak evidence as weak — even when coaching intuition aligns with the direction — is a professional obligation.
