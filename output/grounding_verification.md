### Claim 4: "Burke race walker studies -- involving 22 Olympic-level athletes over 3 weeks -- show that the ketogenic group experienced... 40-70 watts worse on time trials"
- **Theme:** Nutrition & Fueling
- **Cited source:** 654554156 (Watts Doc 10) and 1160146492 (Watts Doc 34)
- **Transcript evidence:** The Burke race walker study is discussed in episode 1160146492 (WD34). At lines 306-307, the transcript confirms "22 of the 29 participants from this study... either went to the 2016 Olympics or the race walking team championships." The study design is confirmed as 3 weeks. However, the Burke study results are reported as race walking TIME differences (the keto group's times stayed the same while the carb groups got 2-3 minutes faster on a 10K race walk), NOT watt differences. The "40-70 watts" figure comes from a DIFFERENT study discussed in episode 654554156 (WD10) -- the Havemann cycling study, where "the fat-adapted group lost 70 watts on each of the middle one-kilometer time trials" and "did about 40 or 50 watts less average over the four-kilometer time trials."
- **Verdict:** NOT SUPPORTED
- **Notes:** This is a conflation of two separate studies. The Burke race walker study showed impaired economy and stagnant race times (while carb groups improved), but the performance deficit was expressed as time (minutes), not watts. The "40-70 watts" figure comes from the Havemann cycling study (discussed in a different episode). The final document incorrectly attributes the watt-based performance deficit to the Burke study. The Burke study involved 29 total participants (22 of whom were Olympic-level), not "22 Olympic-level athletes" -- the document slightly misrepresents the sample as well. This claim needs correction.

### Error Taxonomy
1. **Factual conflation** (2 cases): Merging details from different studies or contexts into a single claim (Burke/Havemann watts, 30-40W case study).
2. **Fabricated structure** (2 cases): Inventing terminology or frameworks not used in the source (TEAM acronym, 5-4-3-2-1 grounding exercise format).
3. **Numerical inversion** (1 case): Reporting "~30% deficit" when the actual deficit was ~70% (PGC-1alpha knockout mice).
4. **Fabricated detail** (1 case): NRF1 never mentioned in transcript; actual compensatory markers were citrate synthase and cytochrome oxidase.
5. **Attribution error** (1 case): Sub-100W prescription attributed to Rory Porteous without transcript support.

All 5 errors (plus the 2 from the initial 10-claim verification: Burke/Havemann conflation and TEAM acronym) have been corrected in both the synthesis files and `final_output.md`.

## Overall Assessment

The final document demonstrates strong fidelity to source material. 70% of sampled claims are fully supported with exact or near-exact matches to transcript content. The 22% partially supported claims involve minor issues: imprecise attribution, approximate numbers, or missing nuance — none materially misrepresent the source. The 8% error rate (5 of 60) reveals systematic patterns: the LLM synthesis process is most prone to (a) conflating details across studies discussed in the same episode, (b) importing well-known concepts from general knowledge and attributing them to podcast speakers, and (c) inverting or fabricating specific numbers during multi-step paraphrasing. All identified errors have been corrected.
