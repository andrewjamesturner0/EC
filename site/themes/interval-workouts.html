Over-Unders Are Not Superior to Standard Threshold Work — Their Value Is Variety and Specificity

Over-under workouts have been marketed as uniquely effective "lactate clearance" tools, but Kolie explicitly deflates this claim. There is no direct randomized study comparing over-unders to standard 2x20 sweet spot or threshold work that shows superior adaptations in anaerobic capacity or lactate tolerance. The real drivers of lactate oxidation capacity are mitochondrial mass and MCT transporter density, both of which are improved by any sustained aerobic training — including simple long endurance rides. The best "lactate clearance" workout, per Kolie, is a six-hour endurance ride. What over-unders genuinely offer is threefold: boredom relief on the trainer, flexibility as a format with many adjustable variables (over duration, under duration, power targets, cadence, set length), and race-specificity when the overs match the acceleration demands of target events like criteriums, cyclocross, or mountain bike racing. The bread-and-butter format is a 1:4 over-to-under ratio — 30 seconds over at 20-50 W above threshold, then 2 minutes under at sweet spot. Build to 2-3 sets of 30-40 minutes. For athletes who naturally tolerate repeated accelerations well, over-unders add nothing special and are just a variation.

Recommendations:

  • Use sweet spot over-unders (30s over / 2 min under) as a substitute for one steady-state threshold session per week for variety, not as a categorically superior workout
  • Set overs by feel at roughly 20-50 W above threshold rather than a fixed percentage of FTP
  • Progress by increasing total set duration before raising power
  • Start each set with an over (acceleration), especially for CX and MTB athletes practicing race starts

Sources: Episodes Ten Minute Tips 27 Programming Over Under Workouts, Watts Doc 27 Training Repeated Sprint Ability

Prescribing VO2max and Anaerobic Intervals as a Percentage of FTP Is Fundamentally Flawed

Both hosts independently listed VO2max intervals at 110-120% FTP as one of the worst common workouts. The problem is that the ratio of VO2max power to FTP varies enormously between athletes: five-minute power can be anywhere from 115% to 150% of FTP. An athlete with a high anaerobic capacity doing "4x6 min at 120% FTP" may barely be breathing hard, while an aerobically-dominant athlete at the same prescription is in genuine distress. The original Coggan power zones were intended to be descriptive (categorizing work after the fact), not prescriptive (assigning workout targets). Using fixed percentages like 120% FTP for VO2max is a misapplication of that framework. The same problem extends to shorter efforts: 40-20s at 130% FTP may not become genuinely hard for a well-trained athlete until the final reps of a 10-minute set. The fix is to use either WKO5 iLevels based on an actual power-duration curve, or simply go genuinely maximal — if you are doing VO2max work correctly, you should be breathing extremely hard. For intermittent efforts like 15-15s, self-pacing without a watt target and tracking average power over weeks is more effective than hitting a prescribed number.

Recommendations:

  • Do not prescribe VO2max intervals as a fixed percentage of FTP; use maximal effort or individualized power-duration targets instead
  • For 30-30s and 15-15s, pace by feel for the entire set rather than targeting a specific wattage
  • Track progress by observing average power on the "on" portions over weeks, not by hitting a single-session target
  • Use WKO5 iLevels or similar individualized models if precise targets are needed

Sources: Episodes 200k the Best and Worst Workouts, Ten Minute Tips 22 the Case Against Erg Mode

Intermittent Efforts (15-15s, 30-30s) Are a Year-Round Staple, Not Just a Race-Prep Tool

Kolie includes intermittent efforts at some level for virtually the entire year — even during FTP blocks. It is rare for him to assign three purely steady-state FTP sessions in a week. The rationale is that these efforts recruit larger motor units that are typically under-trained aerobically in steady-state riding and that making those motor units more aerobically capable is the mechanism behind improved repeated sprint ability. For 15-15s, typical prescription is 3x10 minutes building to 3x20 or 2x30 minutes over weeks. There is no strict power target; the athlete should pace to finish the set feeling worked but not wrecked. Kolie's own numbers — approximately 400-450 W on a 260 W FTP — illustrate how far above FTP-percentage these efforts naturally land. For 30-30s used as anaerobic capacity work, shorter sets (2-3 sets of 3-6 minutes) are preferred to maintain quality; longer sets like 16x1 min on/off cause quality to collapse after the first few reps. The average or normalized power for a well-executed 30-30 set should be in the over-FTP range. These intermittent formats may produce approximately the same aerobic molecular adaptation signals as steady-state threshold work despite the very different power profile.

Recommendations:

  • Include some form of intermittent effort (15-15s, 30-30s, or over-unders) year-round, even during threshold-focused blocks
  • Start 15-15s at 3x10 minutes and progress to 3x20 or 2x30 minutes over weeks
  • For anaerobic capacity 30-30s, keep sets short (3-6 minutes) to preserve rep quality
  • Pace the first rep conservatively; starting even one or two reps too hard collapses the entire set

Sources: Episodes Watts Doc 27 Training Repeated Sprint Ability, 200k the Best and Worst Workouts, Ten Minute Tips 27 Programming Over Under Workouts

Stochastic Tempo Rides Are One of the Most Versatile and Underrated Workouts

Both the RSA episode and the best/worst workouts episode highlight stochastic tempo — riding a course aiming for speed rather than a specific power target — as a top-tier workout. The athlete pushes harder on hills, recovers on descents, and accumulates kilojoules while varying power naturally, like an impromptu time trial on rolling terrain. Duration ranges from 1 to 4 hours. This format naturally trains repeated sprint ability by recruiting larger motor units through variable power demands without requiring formal interval structure. It is also described as fun and race-specific, since actual races involve highly variable power, not steady-state efforts. The workout is good for road, cyclocross, and mountain bike athletes alike. The key distinction from simply riding with no structure is intentionality: the rider is actively trying to cover the course as fast as possible, treating every feature of the terrain as an opportunity to push or recover strategically.

Recommendations:

  • Use stochastic tempo (fartlek) rides of 1-4 hours on rolling terrain as a regular training staple
  • Ride for speed over the course rather than targeting a specific power number
  • Use a fixed course repeatedly to track fitness progression over time
  • Particularly valuable for criterium, cyclocross, and mountain bike racers who need variable-power fitness

Sources: Episodes Watts Doc 27 Training Repeated Sprint Ability, 200k the Best and Worst Workouts

ERG Mode Undermines the RPE Feedback Loop That Makes Interval Training Effective

ERG mode locks the athlete into a predetermined wattage, which disables the critical skill of self-regulating effort based on how it feels. This creates several problems: athletes develop pass-fail thinking (if the watts were hit, the workout was good), they lose the ability to detect early fatigue signals, and pacing skill atrophies. The damage is worst for VO2max and anaerobic intervals, where full maximal effort is required and pre-programmed targets based on FTP percentages are individually wrong. For endurance rides, ERG mode is also problematic because LT1 ranges from 50-85% of FTP across athletes and shifts 30-40 W day-to-day based on fatigue. Setting ERG at a fixed watt for an endurance ride risks training in the intensity "black hole." Legitimate uses exist — recovery rides at confirmed easy wattage, Zwift racing with simulated hills, fartlek on virtual courses, and remedial pacing education for beginners — but these should be exceptions, not defaults. The goal state is an athlete who can execute any workout by feel alone, with power data reviewed afterward for confirmation.

Recommendations:

  • Default to free-ride (resistance or slope) mode for all interval training
  • Never use ERG mode for VO2max or anaerobic capacity intervals
  • If using ERG for endurance rides, do not turn it on for at least the first hour; verify LT1 empirically first
  • Develop RPE calibration as a primary training skill — aim to execute workouts by feel with power as confirmation

Sources: Episodes Ten Minute Tips 22 the Case Against Erg Mode, 200k the Best and Worst Workouts

Progression Should Be Time-Based Before It Is Power-Based

Across multiple episodes, the hosts emphasize that adding duration at the same power is safer and more effective than raising watts. The simplest FTP interval progression is adding 5 minutes per rep to the previous session. If an athlete cannot progress time (more sets or longer sets), the likely causes are accumulated fatigue, overestimated FTP, or insufficient nutrition — not a need for more intensity. This principle extends to over-unders and intermittent efforts: build from 3x10 to 3x20 to 2x30 minutes of 15-15s before considering raising the power target. The "boiling frog" approach to threshold training — alternating tempo and threshold segments within a long set (e.g., 2 min tempo / 3 min threshold / 3 min tempo / 2 min threshold as 2-3x30) — is another time-based strategy that reduces psychological stress while accumulating threshold-range volume. Going to true failure in a workout is explicitly listed as a bad practice; workouts should have open-ended structures with RPE-based stop points rather than binary pass-fail outcomes.

Recommendations:

  • Progress interval training by adding 5 minutes per rep or per session before raising power targets
  • Use open-ended interval structures with RPE stop points rather than fixed pass-fail targets
  • If unable to progress total duration, investigate fatigue, FTP accuracy, or nutrition before adding intensity
  • Consider alternating tempo/threshold segments within long sets to accumulate time at threshold with less mental strain

Sources: Episodes 200k the Best and Worst Workouts, Ten Minute Tips 27 Programming Over Under Workouts, Ten Minute Tips 19 Ftp Testing Shortcuts

Repeated Sprint Training Targets a Distinct Physiological Quality From FTP

Repeated sprint ability (RSA) is presented as a separate endurance quality from FTP-based fitness. The mechanism is that large motor units — typically under-trained aerobically because they are seldom recruited during steady-state riding — need targeted work to become aerobically capable. When these motor units have better aerobic capacity, they preserve their own glycogen stores and maintain force production deeper into races. This explains why an athlete can have a high FTP yet perform poorly in criteriums or cyclocross: the large motor units fatigue during repeated accelerations because they lack aerobic development. Specific RST formats include 5-second maximal sprints every 30-60 seconds, FTP with accelerations layered on top every 2-5 minutes, and the stochastic tempo rides described above. A key diagnostic: during FTP-with-sprints sessions, if peak acceleration power falls from approximately 900 W to 450 W by the end, the larger motor units are fatiguing and need more training. RSA adaptation can take months to years and is highly individual; naturally strong sprinters can dig deep holes requiring extra recovery.

Recommendations:

  • Train repeated sprint ability as a distinct quality from FTP, especially for criterium, CX, and MTB racers
  • Use FTP-with-sprints sessions and monitor whether peak acceleration power degrades across the workout
  • Set acceleration power at a level sustainable across the full session rather than starting high and fading
  • Allow months of consistent RSA work before expecting measurable improvement; adaptation is slow

Sources: Episodes Watts Doc 27 Training Repeated Sprint Ability

Normalized Power Is Unreliable for Variable-Power Workouts

Normalized power (NP) is repeatedly flagged as a poor metric for stochastic, intermittent, or race-like training. In criterium-style efforts with 30-40 second surges, NP can read 20-30 W above actual threshold — leading athletes to incorrectly conclude their FTP has risen. NP is course-dependent and can be "busted" by short hard efforts that disproportionately inflate the rolling average. For intermittent work like 15-15s and 30-30s, average power is the appropriate metric for tracking progress and estimating metabolic load. If using intermittent efforts to roughly estimate FTP, use average power of the set, not NP. The same caution applies to stochastic tempo rides: use a fixed test course and compare completion times or average power rather than NP to track fitness changes.

Recommendations:

  • Do not use normalized power to adjust FTP based on criterium, cyclocross, or stochastic workout data
  • Track intermittent interval progress using average power on the "on" portions, not NP
  • For stochastic fitness tracking, use fixed courses with time-to-completion or average power as the metric
  • Be skeptical of any NP reading from a highly variable-power session

Sources: Episodes Watts Doc 27 Training Repeated Sprint Ability, Ten Minute Tips 27 Programming Over Under Workouts

Evolution of Views

The most significant evolution across these episodes concerns the relationship between high-intensity interval work and year-round training. An earlier episode (Wattstock #2, "Why You're Probably Training Too Hard for Crits") cautioned against short, high-intensity work done year-round. In episode 936171130 (Watts Doc 27), Kolie explicitly revisits this, clarifying that the earlier caution was specifically against very short VO2max and anaerobic capacity work done continuously without periodization — not against the medium-hard intermittent and over-under work that he now recommends including year-round. This represents a refinement rather than a reversal: the types of intensity matter more than the presence of intensity.

On over-unders, Kolie acknowledges in episode 1472759278 (Ten Minute Tips 27) that he had previously contributed to the "over-under mystique" — the idea that they produce uniquely superior lactate-system adaptations. His updated position is that over-unders are a good format but not categorically better than standard threshold work, and their marketing as "lactate clearance" tools is overstated. This is a notable softening from implicit prior endorsement.

On FTP precision, episode 1253680522 (Ten Minute Tips 19) notes Kolie now assigns threshold targets within approximately 10 W bands rather than the 5 W precision he used previously, reflecting growing recognition that power meter error and day-to-day variation make finer precision meaningless. This aligns with episode 1164677890's criticism of overly precise workout prescriptions (e.g., "738 W for 28 seconds") copied directly from modeling software.
