Cross-References

Connections between themes — how findings in one area inform or constrain recommendations in another.

  • FTP sits at the confluence of multiple molecular adaptation pathways -- AMPK, redox signaling, calcium, and glycogen depletion (Section 1 (Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation)) -- which is why direct threshold work (Section 3 (Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing)) is uniquely potent and cannot be substituted by VO2max intervals or sweet spot alone (Section 4 (Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution)).
  • VO2max interval methodology (Section 2 (VO2max & Oxygen Uptake)) depends on the cardiac preload and stroke volume principles explained in Exercise Physiology (Section 1 (Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation)); high cadence during VO2max work maximizes diastolic filling, which is the mechanism behind eccentric cardiac hypertrophy.
  • The phosphocreatine system (Section 1 (Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation)) explains why FTP/threshold training improves repeated sprint ability (Section 5 (Interval Workouts)) and criterium performance (Section 12 (Racing & Team Tactics)) -- PCr resynthesis is aerobic, linking aerobic fitness to sprint recovery.
  • The AMPK and glycogen signaling research (Section 1 (Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation)) directly invalidates fasted training and low-carb protocols discussed in Nutrition (Section 8 (Nutrition & Fueling)) -- end-exercise glycogen, not starting glycogen, activates the adaptation signal, so fueling during rides is compatible with adaptation.
  • Progressive overload through extending time-in-zone (Section 3 (Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing), Section 5 (Interval Workouts)) is the practical application of the calcium-driven mitochondrial biogenesis pathway (Section 1 (Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation)), where total contraction duration -- not intensity -- drives the calcium signal.
  • Recovery and periodization prescriptions (Section 6 (Recovery & Periodization)) are grounded in the sirtuin/NAD+ biochemistry of Section 1 (Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation): recovery is when ETC proteins are imported into expanding mitochondrial reticulum, and chronic overtraining degrades the sirtuin proteins needed for adaptation.
  • The strength training periodization model (Section 7 (Strength Training)) accounts for the concurrent training interference effect (mTOR vs. PGC-1alpha pathways from Section 1 (Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation)), which is why heavy lifting is concentrated in the off-season and reduced to maintenance during race-specific blocks.
  • Nutrition recommendations for aggressive carbohydrate intake (Section 8 (Nutrition & Fueling)) are supported by the substrate oxidation research in Section 1 (Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation) showing that fat and carbohydrate converge on the same aerobic pathway, making substrate burned during exercise irrelevant to adaptation.
  • The psychology of rest (Section 9 (Psychology & Performance)) addresses the behavioral barriers to implementing the recovery prescriptions of Section 6 (Recovery & Periodization) -- rest must be positively reinforced as a skill, not treated as the absence of training.
  • Coaching practice (Section 10 (Coaching, Goal-Setting & Season Planning)) operationalizes the research methodology warnings of Section 13 (Research & Methodology): every training plan is an n=1 experiment, population averages do not predict individual responses, and subjective feedback is primary data alongside power and heart rate.
  • The RPE-based intensity prescription advocated across Section 2 (VO2max & Oxygen Uptake), Section 3 (Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing), Section 4 (Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution), Section 5 (Interval Workouts) reflects a cumulative shift away from FTP-percentage zones, supported by the finding (Section 3 (Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing)) that FTP cannot reliably set zones across the full intensity spectrum.
  • Equipment and aerodynamic optimization (Section 11 (Equipment & Technique)) interacts with race tactics (Section 12 (Racing & Team Tactics)): Bianchi's road-position aerodynamics and Lockwood's TT position principles show that watts saved through positioning compound with the energy management and tactical skills that determine race outcomes.
  • The VLAmax critique (Section 1 (Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation)) directly supports the racing advice in Section 12 (Racing & Team Tactics) that sprint training does not impair FTP -- the proposed trade-off is mechanistically unsound, and sprinter-climbers at the elite level disprove it empirically.
  • The diminishing-returns curve for training adaptations (Section 13 (Research & Methodology)) explains why intermediate athletes plateau (Section 6 (Recovery & Periodization)) and why VO2max blocks require pre-readiness and extended recovery windows (Section 4 (Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution)) -- short-term study results exaggerate differences that vanish on the long-term logarithmic growth curve.
