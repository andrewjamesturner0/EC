13. Research & Methodology

Core Message

Host Korey Moore and Empirical Cycling coach Fabiano (MD, PhD in molecular medicine/genomics) discuss RPE scales, athlete feedback, and muscle adaptation in the context of three Training Peaks blog articles Fabiano authored. The central thesis is that RPE — properly understood and calibrated via the Borg CR10 scale — is a powerful subjective metric that must be cross-referenced against objective testing anchors, recovery patterns, and contextual feedback to be genuinely useful. They argue that athlete communication (i.e., workout feedback metadata in TrainingPeaks) is undervalued relative to the raw power/HR data, and that the most successful athletes are those who actively communicate context to their coaches. A systematic review of muscle adaptations is used to conclude that all training modalities (endurance, high-intensity intervals, sprint intervals) produce similar mitochondrial gains regardless of age, sex, or disease status — but that as athletes become more trained, they must train progressively harder to continue breaking homeostasis and achieving allostatic adaptation.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Use the Borg CR10 scale (not the linear 6–20 RPE scale) for intensities above threshold, because lactate and pain responses are exponential, not linear; the CR10 scale's non-linear spacing better reflects this.
  • Calibrate your RPE by anchoring it to identifiable pain levels at known intensities (e.g., the pain of a 30-second Wingate vs. a 5-minute VO2max interval) and use the vocabulary labels on the CR10 scale ("very strong," "extremely strong/almost max") to ensure consistency.
  • Re-test FTP (or perform a comprehensive performance testing battery) every 8–12 weeks to maintain accurate training anchors; do not rely solely on subjective RPE without objective cross-referencing.
  • Perform separate indoor and outdoor testing rounds when switching between environments; do not assume RPE will transfer directly from outdoor to indoor (or vice versa), especially across seasons.
  • Log workout feedback in TrainingPeaks — not via a separate messaging channel — so that subjective metadata is paired with objective data in the same historical record for later analysis.
  • After completing a workout, immediately note the RPE for the overall session and, for interval sessions, record RPE for the first and last interval to capture fatigue effects across the session.
  • For sessions with intervals embedded in a longer ride, report both a global session RPE and interval-specific RPE in the comments; for continuous rides (endurance, tempo), report a single average RPE.
  • Log any equipment changes, nutritional anomalies, sleep quality, or environmental conditions (heat, altitude, illness) alongside workout data, as these are the contextual factors that make raw data interpretable.
  • If training load is high and recovery uncertain, track HRV trends over 3–7 day windows (not single-day readings) to detect cumulative fatigue in well-trained athletes doing intensive blocks.
  • Prioritize training consistency over intensity during winter or low-motivation periods; keep sessions fun (entertainment during trainer rides, Zwift with restraint) rather than forcing high-intensity work at the cost of long-term adherence.
  • For health-oriented training (not performance), target at least 300 minutes/week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity, or 150 minutes/week of vigorous intensity, plus at least 2 strength sessions per week — this positions you near the bottom of the J-curve for lowest morbidity risk.
  • Obtain medical clearance at least once per year before undertaking high-intensity training, especially if there is any family history of cardiovascular events; this is relevant even for apparently healthy, active individuals.
  • Specify expected CR10 values in workout prescriptions so athletes have a concrete target to match and report against, making RPE a two-way communication tool rather than a retrospective report alone.
  • To distinguish whether poor performance is due to training load, nutrition, sleep, equipment, or illness, require athletes to flag which contextual factors changed that day rather than guessing from power/HR data alone.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • RPE calibration is highly individual: sprinters (e.g., Korey) may perceive their LT1 as a 5/10 while high-volume endurance riders perceive it as a 1/10; coaches should meet athletes where they are rather than expecting a universal scale mapping.
  • Pain tolerance changes with age (Fabiano notes his own decreasing pain tolerance over time) and personality type (Type A athletes may systematically suppress RPE reporting); both affect the reliability of self-reported CR10 scores.
  • HRV is most useful for well-trained athletes under very high training loads or life-stress loads; it is noisy for untrained/moderately trained athletes and can produce inverse responses in some individuals (HRV improves with training, drops on recovery days). Consumer wearable HRV (wrist-based infrared pulse) is significantly noisier than ECG-derived HRV.
  • Do not modify high-intensity key workouts on poor sleep or unrecovered states; reschedule them rather than attempting them with degraded capacity and then attributing performance failure to training variables.
  • The systematic review discussed (on mitochondrial adaptations across modalities) confirms all training types work, but does not answer optimal long-term combination/periodization for a given athlete — that remains empirically determined over years of athlete-coach history.
  • Training above 300 minutes/week (typical for competitive cyclists) has widening confidence intervals in the health literature; evidence for harm is limited but long-term data on very high training volumes across decades is sparse since this population is historically novel.
  • Bike position changes of even 5–10mm in saddle height can shift RPE substantially, masking true training stimulus and leading to misattribution of fatigue to training load rather than biomechanical inefficiency.
  • AI and algorithmic coaching attempts have repeatedly failed partly because subjective/contextual metadata is hard to capture and is required for any data-driven model to produce valid outputs.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The original Borg CR10 scale is explicitly non-linear (exponential spacing), but most online reproductions and app implementations render it as linear, which is incorrect and degrades its validity for above-threshold intensities.
  • Mitochondrial content adaptations to training are not significantly influenced by age, sex, menopausal status, or disease (per the systematic review discussed) — the same training stimulus produces similar physiological adaptations across all these subgroups, which the hosts note challenges assumptions that older or sicker populations require fundamentally different training stimuli.
  • Coaches' ability to know when recovery is inadequate — based on RPE, feedback, and experience — may exceed what any current sensor technology (HRV, power meters, HR) can provide, particularly the detection of non-training stressors (seasonal depression, life stress) that suppress readiness.
  • HRV is proposed as more sensitive to stress than to recovery: it reliably signals accumulating fatigue under high load but is less diagnostically useful during easy/recovery phases. One client case showed an indoor VO2 block produced blunted HRV recovery signals compared to an identical outdoor block, suggesting indoor training demands higher allostatic load even when power output is matched — the plausible mechanism is greater thermoregulatory demand indoors.
  • Increasing absolute VO2max (liters/min) through exercise confers health benefits even without body weight reduction; overweight and obese individuals who begin training improve both absolute and relative VO2max, reducing cardiovascular and metabolic disease risk before reaching normal body weight.
  • Fabiano argues that "data without context is meaningless" for coaching, citing failed AI coaching systems as evidence that even comprehensive objective data cannot substitute for qualitative athlete feedback.

Evolving Views

  • Korey explicitly acknowledges correcting a previous public recommendation: in an earlier episode on endurance ride pacing, he told listeners to pace to a 4–5/10 RPE. He has since learned his own RPE scale is atypically skewed (as a sprinter, he is highly sensitive to neural drive output and perceives LT1 as a 5), meaning his advice would cause other riders to overtrain their endurance rides. He has addressed this on multiple subsequent episodes.
