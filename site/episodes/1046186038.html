Perspectives 2 How to Manage Sickness with Dr Fabiano Araujo

Perspectives 10,003 words View transcript

Referenced in

8. Nutrition & Fueling

Core Message

Host Kolie Moore interviews Dr. Fabiano Araujo (medical doctor, twice-master, PhD, and Empirical Cycling coach) on managing illness for cyclists. The central message is that stopping training as soon as below-the-neck symptoms appear preserves immune system energy and shortens recovery time, whereas pushing through makes illness worse and can cost months of training. Nutrition during illness should prioritize carbohydrates, adequate protein, and healthy fats over dietary restrictions; supplements like vitamin C and D have conflicting evidence and offer little beyond a well-balanced diet. Alcohol has no scientifically established safe amount and can dramatically impair immune recovery even in small quantities.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Neck check rule: Stop ALL exercise (including gym work) if any below-the-neck symptoms are present: fever, myalgia (muscle pain), chest pain, or resting tachycardia — not just reduced-intensity alternatives
  • Being sick causes up to a 25% reduction in endurance capacity (e.g., a 100-minute TTE at 75% VO2max becomes ~75 minutes); RPE at any given power will be substantially elevated
  • Return to training after illness: Begin only once all below-the-neck symptoms are gone; start with easy rides of ~30 minutes, progress by roughly 15 minutes per day based on athlete feedback, not a fixed timetable
  • Carbohydrate intake during illness: Raise carbohydrate intake to support immune cells (which need glucose); avoid weight-loss diets or caloric deficits while sick
  • Drinking 6% carbohydrate solutions (e.g., sports drink) during training is associated with lower incidence of upper respiratory illness in studies — likely via combined hydration and carbohydrate effects
  • Protein: No specific supplement (including glutamine) is consistently supported; the recommendation is a well-balanced diet, not extremes of very high or very low protein
  • Fats: Ensure adequate healthy fats (mono/polyunsaturated — olive oil, avocado, wild fish) during illness; very low-fat dietary periods should be suspended until recovery
  • Avoid alcohol entirely during illness recovery: There is no established safe minimum; even a single beer can trigger a full relapse (host's personal experience corroborated by Fabiano); alcohol impairs immune function for several hours
  • Probiotics (most accessible form: yogurt) show promising evidence for reducing recurring respiratory illness; worth incorporating into regular diet
  • Hydration: Illness increases risk of dehydration via reduced ADH (antidiuretic hormone) production and elevated protein catabolism; prioritize fluids alongside carbohydrates
  • Prevention: Frequent hand washing, mask use indoors, avoid touching eyes/nose/mouth, do not share water bottles or towels, avoid binge drinking after competitions
  • Write down subjective workout feel (beginning and end) to help coach/athlete identify when to reduce training load, especially since immune response varies person-to-person

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Most illness data in athletes comes from elite Olympic athletes, not amateurs or masters — amateur recovery typically takes longer due to life stress, less access to nutritional support, and less recovery resources
  • The J-curve of training load vs. susceptibility to illness applies mainly to amateurs; elite athletes may follow an S-curve (more training = more protection at the highest levels)
  • Evidence for vitamins C and D is equivocal in the literature (50/50 results); no consistent benefit established beyond a well-balanced diet
  • Individual variation in immune response is high; there is no technology readily available to amateurs to directly measure immune function markers daily
  • COVID-19 return-to-sport data is largely from non-athletic or older populations, making guidance for young trained athletes uncertain
  • "Above the neck" symptoms (runny nose, sore throat, mild cough) are generally considered safe to train through lightly — this was not explicitly contradicted but context suggests caution even then

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • There is no safe amount of alcohol according to the literature Fabiano reviewed — not even moderate wine consumption — contrary to popular belief about moderate drinking being benign
  • Gym work during below-the-neck illness is not safer than cycling — the common advice to "just do a shorter gym session instead" is not supported; full rest is recommended
  • Protein catabolism increases significantly during fever because immune cells require amino acids and glucose to function; this is an underappreciated nutritional demand during illness
  • The protective J-curve benefit of moderate training is largely for amateurs; elite athletes appear to have different (possibly better) immune regulation than amateurs training at high loads
  • Weight loss during injury recovery (Remco Evenepoel's cited example) counterintuitively delays recovery because the body needs energy to repair tissue

Evolving Views

  • None explicitly identified in this episode (the episode does not discuss past recommendations that have changed). The literature on inflammation, probiotics, and the elite vs. amateur distinction is described as still emerging and "work in progress."
