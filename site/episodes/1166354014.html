7. Strength Training

Core Message

This episode lays out a complete periodization plan for strength training across a cycling season, moving through distinct phases: adaptation, hypertrophy, maximal strength, single-leg specificity/neural drive, and finally maintenance. The gym program must be planned alongside aerobic periodization, not bolted on as an afterthought. The later phases focus heavily on single-leg strength because bilateral squat strength transfers poorly to the bike compared to single-leg strength. The episode also covers deloading, in-season maintenance, and why plyometrics are generally not worth the squeeze for cyclists.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Adaptation phase (weeks 1-2): Ease back into lifting, focus on technique, do not push intensity
  • Hypertrophy phase (~4 weeks: 3 hard + 1 deload): 3-5 sets of 8-15 reps (up to 20 reps for isolation exercises). Pair with low-intensity riding only
  • Strength phase (~4 weeks: 3 hard + 1 deload): Move into the 4-6 rep range for at least 1-2 main exercises; a third exercise can use lighter weight with higher reps
  • Deloading: Either cut reps in half or cut load in half. Kolie recommends cutting load in half for most people
  • Single-leg specificity / neural drive phase: Switch to single-leg squats as the first exercise (heavy, 4-6 rep range), then trap bar deadlift on another day. This is where the biggest bike-transferable strength gains happen
  • Bilateral deficit target: If single-leg strength is only 55-60% of bilateral strength, there are easy gains to be made. Kyle and Kolie sit at about 75%
  • Maintenance (in-season): 2 sets of 5-10 reps at 6-7/10 RPE, roughly 60-70% of 1RM. Once a week or even every two weeks is probably fine
  • Hamstring accessory work: Keep it light; do not try to hammer hamstrings with heavy good mornings or stiff-leg deadlifts
  • Protein: Eat enough to maintain muscle mass in-season; roughly half of athletes Kolie sees trying to lose weight are not eating enough protein
  • Trap bar deadlift: Use the high handles, especially for anyone taller than ~5'2"
  • Sprint testing during lifting blocks: Throw in a couple of 5-10 second all-out sprints on the bike once a week or every other week to see strength gains transferring
  • When to back off weights: Once you are in race prep doing threshold, sweet spot, or VO2max work, reduce gym work to maintenance
  • Do not do sweet spot 3-5x/week plus lifting 3x/week unless you have exceptional recovery ability
  • Always start with your weak/injured leg when doing single-leg work
  • Plyometrics and box jumps are generally not worth it for cyclists beyond initial noob gains. Kolie has never assigned box jumps to any athlete

Caveats & Qualifications

  • This periodization scheme is based on coaching experience, not published literature: "I've never seen any literature on periodization for this kind of stuff" (Kolie)
  • Recovery from lifting is highly individual; it may take 2-4 days to recover from heavy lifting sessions
  • Gaining meaningful muscle mass as an endurance athlete is hard because energy availability is limited; fear of weight gain from a hypertrophy phase is largely unfounded
  • If you are naturally strong, you may not need much gym work at all

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • "There's more technique involved in lifting well than there is in riding a bike well" (Kolie)
  • Single-leg strength is a far better predictor of on-bike sprint performance than bilateral squat strength (anecdote from Australian national track team: two riders ~100 kg apart in back squat but only 10-20 kg apart in single-leg leg press, with comparable flying 200m times)
  • Olympic weightlifters produce peak power at ~90% 1RM, not 30-50% as commonly cited
  • "Anybody who says that all the power in the cycling stroke comes from the glutes -- that's demonstrably incorrect"

Evolving Views

  • Kolie explicitly states he changes his programming year to year: "I'll change my mind again next year with this stuff too. A lot of my athletes are like, how come the program is different from last year? It's like, well, I learned stuff."
