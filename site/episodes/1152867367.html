7. Strength Training

Core Message

Cyclists should identify their specific goals before selecting strength exercises, working backwards from race demands rather than blindly copying lifting programs from other sports. The squat variation you choose (high bar, low bar, front squat) largely does not matter for cycling -- pick whichever you can do safely and comfortably given your anatomy and mobility. Front squats are more quad-dominant and use lighter loads (~80-85% of back squat max), making them a good option for those with back concerns or who cannot put weight on their back. If you have any mobility or injury concerns, see a professional (PT or qualified trainer) before loading up.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Start by identifying your specific goal for lifting (e.g., sprint power, injury prevention) and work backward from there -- do not just default to "I should squat"
  • Squat variation (high bar, low bar, front squat) does not matter much for cyclists -- pick the one you can do safely and comfortably; there is a gradient of bar positions, not a dichotomy
  • If you already know one squat variation, do not waste time learning a different one just because someone online said so -- "squatting is not your sport"
  • Front squat is more quad-dominant (closed chain) and automatically uses lighter loads (~80-85% of back squat max); good for people who cannot put weight on their back
  • Front squat requires significant ab strength, upper back endurance, and shoulder mobility; straps looped over the bar can reduce wrist/elbow stress if front rack position is difficult
  • Lifting shoes with a heel-to-toe drop can help achieve more depth and improve stability, but are not a cure-all for lacking mobility
  • Use the Valsalva maneuver (big breath, brace core hard) for spinal stability under load
  • Belt use is personal preference with no minimum load threshold -- it increases intra-abdominal pressure and stabilizes the spine but does not replace core strength
  • Belts do not make your abs weaker; the consensus is abs work equally hard because you lift heavier loads with the belt
  • Aim for a "neutral spine" but understand that neutral is a range, not a perfectly rigid position throughout the entire lift
  • If you have any issues with movement, see a professional PT or trainer before progressing
  • Learning complex movements (snatches, cleans) requires a large time investment that is probably not worthwhile for cyclists
  • Often when a cyclist thinks they "need sprint work," the real solution is more aerobic training so they arrive at the sprint with a fuller tank

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Squat variation selection depends heavily on individual anatomy (femur length, ankle mobility, shoulder mobility) -- there is no universal best choice
  • If you have back problems, family history of back problems, or joint issues, equipment decisions (belt, shoes) and exercise selection become more important
  • Kyle (co-host) is missing a PCL, which means he cannot do open chain exercises like knee extensions or sissy squats -- individual injury history dictates exercise selection
  • The episode is explicitly focused on exercise selection, not programming (sets/reps covered in the next episode)

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The specific squat variation you choose "kind of doesn't matter" for cyclists -- people obsess over high bar vs. low bar unnecessarily
  • Belts do not make your abs weaker, contrary to common gym belief; they allow you to lift more while your abs still work at 100%
  • "Neutral spine" is a range, not a single rigid position -- being hyper-concerned about minor back rounding is unnecessary
  • The most common cyclist complaint of "I can't sprint at the end of a race" is usually solved by more aerobic training, not sprint or strength work
  • Learning Olympic lifts (cleans, snatches) is probably not worth the time investment for cyclists due to the months needed to reach useful loads
