Watts Doc 38 Phosphocreatine is a Critical Aerobic Energy System

Watts Doc 10,533 words View transcript

Referenced in

1. Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation

Core Message

Phosphocreatine (PCr) is not merely an anaerobic buffer — it is a critical aerobic energy system that functions both temporally (providing immediate ATP when demand spikes) and spatially (transporting energy from mitochondria to myofibrils via a creatine kinase "conveyor belt"). PCr is ~20 mM in muscle (about 4× resting ATP) and diffuses ~25% faster than ATP, making it the primary rapid-delivery mechanism. The creatine kinase system is so integral to aerobic function that mice without it run only 10% as far and compensate with 10× more NRF1 and 2× mitochondrial density. Aerobic training — particularly FTP/threshold work — significantly improves PCr resynthesis rate, which is the key to repeated sprint and high-intensity interval performance.

Key Training Recommendations

  • FTP and threshold training (TTE at FTP) directly improves PCr resynthesis rate, which translates to better repeated sprint ability and repeated high-intensity interval performance.
  • PCr resynthesis half-life is 21–57 seconds; passive recovery is superior to active recovery for PCr restoration — keep recovery intervals between sets truly passive when PCr restoration is the goal.
  • In a soccer study, sprint time + OBLA velocity together predicted repeated sprint ability with R²=0.89 — both aerobic capacity and sprint capacity matter for repeated-sprint sports.
  • Creatine supplementation (~20g loading, then ~5g/day) increases total PCr by ~20%, but causes ~4–6 lb weight gain; host suggests this is likely not net beneficial for endurance athletes where power-to-weight matters.
  • A 30-second sprint depletes PCr by 60–80%; understanding this informs why rest intervals need to be at least 2–3 minutes for full or near-full PCr restoration between hard efforts.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • PCr resynthesis rate is highly aerobically dependent — the faster someone can deliver oxygen post-sprint, the faster PCr recovers; this is why aerobically fit athletes recover faster between sprints even if their sprint power is similar.
  • Creatine supplementation benefits are likely sport-specific: probably beneficial for team sport athletes where weight penalty is acceptable; less likely beneficial for endurance cyclists where every lb matters on climbs.
  • The 21–57 second half-life range reflects individual and fitness variation; trained athletes trend toward the shorter end.
  • Double-CK knockout mouse data is mechanistic/animal evidence — the quantitative findings (10% distance, 10× NRF1) may not translate linearly to humans but illustrate the dependency.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • PCr is an aerobic system, not just an anaerobic one — the spatial shuttle function (PCr carrying energy from mitochondria to fibrils) is active during steady-state aerobic exercise, not just during sprints.
  • Improving FTP/TTE improves sprint recovery — the mechanism is PCr resynthesis rate, which is aerobically driven; this means "sprint-focused" athletes who neglect aerobic base lose repeated sprint capacity.
  • The CK knockout mouse experiment is framed as strong evidence that aerobic energy delivery fundamentally depends on the creatine kinase system, not just ATP diffusion.
