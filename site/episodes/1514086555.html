Ten Minute Tips 28 Ftp and Vo2max Thoughts and Listener Questions

4. Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

Core Message

Following up on the VO2max podcast series, this episode clarifies programming details for VO2max and FTP blocks, addresses common mistakes, and answers listener questions. The key messages are: (1) before beginning a VO2max block, an athlete should be at or near their previously-established aerobic fitness (best achieved via a period of endurance training, not by immediately jumping into hard intervals after time off); (2) recovery after a VO2max block takes far longer than most athletes expect — 2–8 weeks to see full FTP improvements — and beginning a new training phase after only 3–4 days of rest is a common error; (3) FTP training does not necessarily raise FTP, especially in trained athletes, because it is primarily a duration-based stimulus, not an intensity-based one; and (4) volume is the primary long-term driver of FTP development, with most athletes being volume-limited rather than intensity-limited in their FTP improvements.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Before a dedicated VO2max block, bring aerobic fitness to a prior baseline first — ideally via a period of higher-than-normal endurance volume. Getting off the couch and doing VO2max work immediately accumulates fatigue faster than adaptation can occur.
  • VO2max block structure: 2 hard workouts per week is the right starting point; a "mixed block" (1 VO2max + 1 threshold workout per week) is a valid alternative when pure VO2max work stalls.
  • One possible block stacking: 2–3 consecutive hard days (Thu/Fri/Sat), then easy Sunday, then recovery Mon–Wed. Not the only valid approach.
  • After a VO2max block, allow full recovery until legs feel good (often 1–2+ weeks of easy riding), then test with a 3–5 minute maximal effort to assess FTP changes. Do not test after 3–4 days.
  • Post-block FTP check: expect 50/50 chance of seeing the improvement immediately vs. still being fatigued. If fatigued, take more easy days. Minimum expected gain after a good block for high-volume athletes: 15 W immediately plus another 10–15 W over the following 2 weeks. Lower-volume athletes will see smaller gains.
  • For VO2max intervals: minimum interval duration ~2 minutes; a range of 2–8 minutes is useful. Total interval time per session 15–20 minutes; no benefit observed from going beyond ~23 minutes. Starting intervals "not quite so hard" when fresh prevents blowing up the first effort.
  • High cadence during VO2max intervals reduces muscular fatigue and is particularly recommended for double-day sessions.
  • FTP training: do not summon the "pass/fail at a specific wattage" mindset. If FTP workouts feel very hard, your FTP target may be overestimated. Use RPE and heart rate alongside power.
  • Volume is the primary driver for most athletes stuck on FTP plateaus: clients riding 10–12 h/week rarely see consistent FTP improvements; those at 20–30 h/week almost always do.
  • When volume plateaus are unavoidable, shift focus: improve sprint, pack-handling skills, repeated-effort capacity, or race craft rather than continuing to chase FTP in a context where volume limits it.
  • Consider an alternative season structure for volume-limited athletes: stay at 95% fitness all season and go race more, rather than trying to peak 2–3 times.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • There is no single "correct" way to periodize VO2max and FTP work — mixing modalities in the same block can work; dedicated sequential blocks can work. The key is recovery and measuring output.
  • The post-block FTP improvement lag is real but poorly studied; it may relate to the mitochondrial biogenesis lag described in the dysfunctional mitochondria episode (referenced but not numbered in this transcript).
  • Dedicated VO2max blocks are not always appropriate — skip them if there is insufficient runway for recovery before a target race.
  • "Noob gains" from FTP training are primarily plasma volume effects and apply to all aerobic training modalities equally — the real mechanism is not threshold-specific.
  • FTP test anxiety can deflate results; informal threshold discovery (ride 10 minutes at a spot, build to threshold feel, stop at any point) is often a better approach than formal testing for anxious athletes.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • FTP training does not necessarily raise FTP in trained athletes — it is primarily a duration-based stimulus, more like extending TTE than lifting a ceiling. The ceiling is VO2max.
  • Early FTP improvements from just about any aerobic training are largely plasma volume gains, not threshold-specific adaptations.
  • Most athletes are volume-limited in their FTP development, not intensity-limited — adding more hard intervals when volume is already low is the wrong prescription.
  • When FTP improvements stall, the correct response is often to change the season strategy (race fresher, more often) rather than add more training stress.

Evolving Views

  • The 2–8 week post-VO2max-block recovery window was updated from the 2023 season of coaching data: "for a lot of them, it was not the average full recovery time…anywhere from two to six to eight weeks." This represents an updating of expectations compared to a more optimistic prior assumption.
  • Kolie comments he "used to" give athletes their old coach's advice to aim for 400 W FTP quickly, and acknowledges this was naive.
