Perspectives 5 Coaching the Process with Cory Lockwood

Perspectives 14,964 words View transcript

Referenced in

11. Equipment & Technique

Core Message

This episode introduces Corey Lockwood as a new Empirical Cycling coach. The conversation primarily covers Lockwood's background in motocross racing and road cycling, his experience with a pseudoscientific coach (the "Semper Poro" system), and his philosophy on what makes good coaching. The most technically dense section covers criterium cornering technique and how Lockwood's motocross background informs line selection and power management in bunch races. There is substantial material on how bike setup (tire pressure, wheel depth, stem length, crank length) affects cornering and power application.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Criterium cornering — line selection (exit-speed focus): Riders who lack explosive sprint acceleration should prioritize late-apex lines to maximize exit velocity. Remove all riders from the mental picture first, find every opportunity to pedal through turns, then add road furniture, then add rider dynamics. The goal: stay seated and spin up, never having to sprint out of the saddle at 800W from a corner.
  • Late apex technique for non-sprinters: Brake a little earlier, open the turn up, lay the bike over, pedal into and out of the turn. This preserves seated power output (400–500W seated vs. 800W standing) and allows momentum maintenance.
  • Cut-and-thrust (square-up) lines for sprinters: Hard brake into a corner, pivot inside, sprint out. This costs energy but suits riders with high peak power. Not appropriate for riders who must rely on sustained power.
  • Sensing the limit of grip: Feel feedback through handlebars and outside pedal (countersteer loading); you can hear the tire at the limit. Read road surface changes (chip seal to asphalt, concrete to asphalt) before reaching limit conditions — surface transitions change traction coefficients mid-corner.
  • Bike setup variables that matter for racing: Tire width, wheel depth, PSI, and contact patch all affect cornering traction and spin-up speed. Crank length matters for pedaling through tight-radius turns. Stem length and bar width affect sprint power generation. These details matter as much on a road bike as on a motocross bike.
  • Wheel depth for light riders on flat TTs: Choose deepest wheels you can handle given your weight and expected crosswind. A lighter rider with lower CdA can achieve the same velocity as a heavier rider at higher watts if the aerodynamic advantage is real. Focus on position quality — a rider at 300W in a dialed position can beat a rider at 400W with poor aero.
  • TT gearing: A 60T chainring (1x) was described as a legitimate TT option to avoid spinning out on descents and false flats, allowing a lower cadence (70–80 RPM) "diesel" mode where the rider can reduce power output, lower breathing, and still carry speed. On hilly courses, revert to a 53/39 or compact.
  • Fabric vs. skin in TTs: Not universal — depends heavily on the specific suit. A cheap, slow skin suit may be slower than shaved skin. Only testing reveals the answer. Importantly: smoother surfaces are not always faster aerodynamically (golf ball effect), and body hair aero effects are genuinely uncertain.
  • FTP workout discipline: Never add power during threshold workouts based on feel. Threshold wattage should stay constant; re-test to confirm gains. Most athletes who "feel good" and add power mid-threshold-workout are either fatiguing themselves unnecessarily or are wrong about the gain.
  • Athlete understanding of workouts: Athletes who understand why a workout is prescribed execute it better — they operate from a performance mindset rather than a fear/survival mindset. This appears to produce better physiological outcomes from the same workout.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Lockwood is explicit that aerodynamics is outside his area of deep expertise — he defers to aerodynamicists and notes he has limits to his knowledge on fabric vs. skin questions.
  • Cornering line choice is engine-dependent: the optimal line for a diesel non-sprinter is completely different from the optimal line for a sprinter. There is no universally correct criterium line.
  • Threshold FTP development: early in training it tends to go up; later in training it typically doesn't. Understanding this prevents athletes from adding too much power prematurely.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Motorcycle racing (motocross) provides a highly transferable fitness base to road cycling — it produces aerobic/anaerobic fitness, muscular strength, and the mental discipline of sustained high-intensity efforts (160–180 BPM with brief recovery windows), which translates directly to TT fitness. Lockwood's FTP was never below ~370W when he started cycling.
  • Conventional "ride with people faster than you" advice is often misapplied — it only works if the pace differential is manageable. If you're hanging on for dear life every ride, you're not learning, you're just accumulating fatigue.
  • "One size fits all" coaching programs have fundamental holes because physiological and mental individuality is too large to accommodate with generic programming. What worked for the coach may not work for the athlete, and using the coach's own racing success to validate the methodology is a logical trap.
