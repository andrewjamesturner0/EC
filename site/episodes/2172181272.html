Watts Doc 56 Strength Training Strategies to Avoid Weight Gain

Watts Doc 18,066 words View transcript

Referenced in

7. Strength Training

Core Message

This episode examines four research papers to determine how cyclists can strength train while minimizing muscle mass gain. The hosts establish a clear hierarchy of factors: energy balance (caloric intake) is far and away the most important variable, followed by lifting heavy with low reps, and then keeping total volume load low. Skipping the eccentric phase of lifts, a commonly suggested strategy, is shown to have essentially zero effect on lower-body hypertrophy based on meta-analysis evidence. The overall message is that cyclists should not be afraid of the gym -- accidentally getting huge is extremely unlikely.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Energy balance is the number one factor for avoiding weight gain while strength training -- do not eat in a caloric surplus while lifting
  • Hierarchy of influence on hypertrophy avoidance: 1) Energy balance (by a large margin), 2) Lifting heavy/low reps, 3) Keeping total volume load low; skipping the eccentric is negligible
  • For strength gains with minimal hypertrophy: do 2-4 heavy sets per week; as few as 1 heavy single-to-triple per week is sufficient to maintain strength
  • Rep ranges: for strength-focused work, sets of 1-5 reps; if not comfortable lifting that heavy, keep to 1-2 sets in the 5-8 rep range
  • Progress weights slowly if trying to minimize hypertrophy -- add roughly 5 pounds every 2-3 weeks
  • Adaptation phase first: start with higher reps (sets of 10-20) at light weight to build movement skill before progressing to heavy singles/doubles/triples
  • In-season maintenance: 1 heavy set per week at 6-7/10 RPE is enough to maintain strength skill; once per week to once every 10 days
  • Accessory work for health: hamstrings and glute medius should be trained progressively; upper body for stability especially for mountain bikers
  • Pre-gym nutrition: 100-200 calories of carbs shortly before the session
  • Creatine timing caveat: do not start creatine loading while simultaneously trying to determine maintenance calories, as 2-3 lbs of water weight gain will confuse the picture
  • For home training with limited weight: slow the eccentric to 6-8 seconds per rep to increase time under tension
  • Get coaching on technique; always use safety pins when squatting

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Initial 2-3 lbs of weight gain when returning to the gym is mostly water retention, glycogen, and acute inflammation -- not true muscle tissue
  • Most hypertrophy studies use untrained or moderately trained subjects, which is actually representative of most cyclists returning to the gym after 8-10 months away
  • If you are already very lean (~8% body fat), you are "probably not going to see a recomp effect" -- you will likely gain a small amount of weight
  • Strength training response is highly individual -- some people are "super responders" and many are not
  • There is a fuzzy short-term relationship between strength and muscle size; strength athletes can lose weight while getting stronger due to the skill component

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Skipping the eccentric does essentially nothing for lower-body hypertrophy avoidance -- the meta-analysis shows the lower-body effect is centered at zero; Kolie notes he personally got "pretty big and pretty strong" doing concentric-only deadlifts
  • Volume load (sets x reps x weight) is a very good predictor of hypertrophy stimulus regardless of rep range -- 3x10 and 7x3 produced roughly equivalent hypertrophy when volume load was equated, though 7x3 produced better maximal strength
  • You do not need extra protein or calories to gain strength -- control group (normal diet, no supplement) still gained strength and even recomped
  • One heavy single per week is enough to maintain strength in a lift, far less than most programs prescribe
  • The fear of "accidentally getting huge" is unfounded -- even with an extra 2000 calories/day, subjects only gained 3 kg in 8 weeks

Evolving Views

  • Kolie references updating his view on tempo and hypertrophy: "I remember a couple episodes ago, I wasn't really sure at one point if slowing a rep down and getting less reps would lead to about the same growth. And it turns out it does." He now endorses slow eccentrics (6-8 seconds) as a valid strategy for home training with limited weights.
  • Kolie mentions personally experiencing recomposition he found surprising: he started upper body training in January, dropped 4-5% body fat and gained ~6 lbs while dieting and lifting heavy
