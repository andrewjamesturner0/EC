3. Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing

Core Message

This is the inaugural episode of the podcast, focused on how to test FTP accurately and why most common testing methods systematically overestimate FTP for anaerobic riders. Kolie argues that the standard Hunter Allen 20-minute test (take 95% of 20-min power) is too inflated for anaerobic or sprint-oriented riders, whose 20-min power may be significantly above their actual aerobic threshold. The "correct" FTP is the inflection point on the power curve above which fatigue accumulates faster and below which it accumulates slower. The preferred approach is a longer, open-ended test (40–70 minutes) performed at a sustainable pace, letting the athlete feel out threshold by RPE rather than chasing a number.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Use a longer FTP test: ride 40–70 minutes at what feels sustainable. Start deliberately below perceived FTP for the first 10–20 minutes, then ramp gently to the edge of "I can hold this for a long time."
  • Do NOT use the 2×8-minute test or ramp test as your primary FTP tool if you have significant anaerobic power. For anaerobic riders, the 8-minute test can produce FTPs that are 15–50 watts above actual threshold (host's own data: ~270 W FTP from an 8-min test that yielded 310–320 W).
  • The 20-minute test without the 5-minute blowout is particularly problematic for anaerobic riders, who may get an FTP that is 88–90% of 20-minute power rather than the assumed 95%.
  • FTP intervals should be assigned at ~10 watts below FTP to reduce fatigue and allow more total training volume.
  • If your FTP is correctly set, a 2×20-minute FTP workout should be very accomplishable, not soul-crushing. If it feels near-impossible, your FTP is likely set too high.
  • TTE (time to exhaustion at FTP) ranges from ~30 minutes in early training to 70+ minutes in well-trained athletes; this variability makes "FTP = one-hour power" an oversimplification.
  • Look for the inflection point in the power curve when assessing FTP, not a fixed percentage of a short test.
  • Ramp tests are even more unreliable than 8-minute tests for FTP; they require blood lactate data to be valid, as used by Team Sky (who were not just "doing a ramp test on their trainer").
  • If someone sets a new long effort in a race or long ride, that effort can serve as an FTP data point when examined in context of the full power curve in WKO4/5.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The 95% factor for the 20-minute test only applies on average; for aerobic-dominant riders (ultra-endurance, triathletes who rarely go over threshold), the correction factor may actually be smaller (20-min power closer to real FTP). For highly anaerobic riders, the correction may need to be 90–92%.
  • FTP is not always one hour; it is "approximately one hour" in the Coggan definition, and can range 30–80+ minutes depending on training state.
  • Power meter error of ±2% (worst case) or ±0.5% (SRM) means that worrying about 5-watt differences in FTP is largely noise; train in zones, not to single-watt precision.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Many athletes who think they "prefer sweet spot because FTP feels too hard" are actually riding at their real FTP — because their FTP has been overestimated and sweet spot (90–95% of inflated FTP) equals their true threshold.
  • The ramp test — despite being used by Team Sky — was used alongside blood lactate in a lab context to find VO2max, not FTP. Using it alone on a trainer to set FTP is a misapplication.
  • A well-executed longer FTP test (45–60+ min) should not feel like a pro TT rider collapsing at the line. It should feel like a sustained hard effort that only becomes truly difficult in the last several minutes.
