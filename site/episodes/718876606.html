Referenced in

2. VO2max & Oxygen Uptake

Core Message

This short episode introduces the distinction between external stress (power output) and internal strain (metabolic state), arguing that they do not have a one-to-one relationship — particularly as duration, fatigue, and recovery interact. The central practical point is that when you begin a VO2max-intensity interval, you are almost certainly not at VO2max in the first minute, because oxygen uptake kinetics lag behind power output and cardiovascular response. The episode previews the series of Watts Doc episodes to come on this theme, and introduces perceived exertion as an underappreciated coaching tool.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Do not assume you are at VO2max simply because your power is in the "VO2max zone" — it takes time for oxygen uptake to reach its ceiling, and anaerobic systems cover the shortfall early in any interval.
  • For sprint and anaerobic capacity intervals, go all-out or at maximal perceived effort; the key coaching variables are duration of effort and sufficient rest between repetitions, not a specific power target.
  • After 6–10 weeks of anaerobic capacity training, expect improvements to plateau — at that point, consider addressing other limiters like muscle mass.
  • Prefer prescribing intervals by perceived effort (RPE) over rigid power targets where appropriate, especially for VO2max work — RPE-based prescription can produce better adaptations with less unnecessary fatigue.
  • For endurance-type intervals and work around or below FTP, accumulated fatigue matters less than for sprint/anaerobic work, but avoid high-intensity, max-power intervals when fatigued.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • If your time to exhaustion at FTP is 45 minutes, doing 2×20-minute sweet spot intervals will very likely not improve your FTP — the stimulus is insufficient.
  • Sweet spot work at 90% FTP for 40 minutes is maintenance work if you can hold that power for 80–90 minutes uninterrupted.
  • Heart rate lags behind effort by a minute or more, making it only an approximation of oxygen demand — you can be at VO2max without being at maximum heart rate, and vice versa.
  • Anaerobic capacity improvements plateau after a finite training period; further improvements require addressing structural limiters.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The longer you coach, the more you should prescribe intervals by perceived effort rather than power — understanding the discrepancy between power output and internal metabolic state makes RPE a more precise tool than it appears.
  • "Time in zone X equals adaptation Y" is an oversimplification — metabolic state is dynamic even within a single interval, meaning the adaptation target changes continuously.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie notes that his coaching philosophy has shifted increasingly toward RPE prescription over power targets as his experience has grown.
