3. Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing

Core Message

FTP (anchored to maximal lactate steady state, MLSS) and critical power (CP) are attempting to measure the same physiological inflection point but via different models, and they frequently do not agree numerically. CP is a mathematical model valid for interpolation across ~1–20-minute efforts but breaks down when extrapolated to longer durations. FTP is superior for setting training zones below threshold because it directly reflects a physiological state (constant maximum blood lactate). Using CP in place of FTP to assign threshold intervals will systematically over-prescribe intensity for athletes with high anaerobic capacity, making workouts incomplete or impossible.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Use FTP (MLSS-derived), not critical power, to set threshold training zones. If CP overestimates, FTP-based intervals at 3×20 or 2×30 watts will become unsustainable.
  • A real-world example: Kyle's CP in 2016 was 304 W vs. his actual FTP of 285–290 W — a ~15–20 W gap. At 300 W (CP), his threshold intervals would have been "very hard if not impossible."
  • CP can be useful above FTP (e.g., to interpolate 3-minute to 10-minute powers for VO2max work), but should not be used to anchor zone-based training.
  • FTP as trained via intervals updates continuously as TTE extends — it is a "living model" requiring periodic retesting, not a static number.
  • iLevels (WKO4/5) account for individual variation above FTP where CP has historically failed, and are preferred for assigning all zones.
  • A 5–15 W difference between CP and FTP may be statistically insignificant in a research sense but practically significant for training: 7% error causes cascading training zone problems.
  • FTP tests that rely on accurate long-duration steady-state efforts (40–70 min) are more valid than CP model estimates for training zone purposes.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Some studies find CP and FTP statistically equivalent; others find CP significantly higher. The results depend on the subjects' anaerobic profiles. On average, one well-designed study (trained subjects) found CP = 278 W vs. FTP = 239 W — a 39 W gap.
  • CP is valid for interpolation within its tested duration range (e.g., 1–20 min); only extrapolation beyond that range is problematic.
  • Using CP from Golden Cheetah (which fits it automatically to all ride data) is particularly risky because it uses only the athlete's best efforts and overlays them on the mean-max curve as if they are achievable every day.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The widespread adoption of CP in Golden Cheetah is largely accidental — it's easy to program and therefore was included. This created a perception of scientific equivalence with FTP that is not always warranted.
  • CP was designed as a laboratory measurement tool for physiological research, not as a field tool for coaches to prescribe workouts. FTP was specifically designed for that purpose.
  • The literature on CP vs. MLSS is cherry-pickable — you can find papers supporting either equivalence or large differences. The robust studies with trained athletes tend to find CP higher.
