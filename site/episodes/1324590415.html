Watts Doc 39 Why You Probably Cant Hold Your Ftp for an Hour

Watts Doc 19,480 words View transcript

Referenced in

3. Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing

Core Message

This episode builds on a deep reading of the Billat et al. paper (masters endurance runners doing FTP interval blocks) to argue that (1) most cyclists cannot hold their FTP for one hour — the realistic average is ~40–45 minutes TTE; (2) the "FTP = one hour power" definition is historically motivated but physiologically arbitrary; and (3) the correct training response is extensive FTP work (progressively extending time in zone) not intensive FTP work (raising power). The episode makes a metabolic case for why extending TTE is mechanistically linked to improved lactate clearance, carnitine transport, and mitochondrial density.

Key Training Recommendations

  • The practical average TTE for amateur cyclists is ~40–45 minutes, not 60. Training to extend TTE toward 60 minutes (and beyond) is a primary goal of FTP blocks.
  • The Billat study protocol (mimicking what Kolie prescribes): 2 sessions/week, starting at 3×10 and 2×15 in week 1, progressing to 3×20 and 2×30 in week 6. Total time in zone went from 30 to 60 minutes over 6 weeks. TTE went from 45 to 63 minutes (+40%).
  • This protocol (FTP intervals twice weekly, progressive time) is the template for all FTP blocks. There is no "wrong" way to increment — adding one interval, adding 2 minutes to each interval, or mixing interval lengths are all valid as long as total time in zone increases.
  • High-volume athletes (long slow distance base): adding FTP work on top of maintained aerobic volume produces the largest TTE gains. The Billat subjects had 10 years of 5×/week LSD running. The FTP intervention was added on top of that, not in place of it.
  • Metabolic basis for extending TTE: lower blood lactate at minute 10 of a TTE test predicts longer TTE (R² = 0.69). Lower lactate means better mitochondrial oxidation of lactate (keeping it inside the cell and converting it to energy before it spills into the blood). This is what extensive FTP training develops.
  • Fat oxidation increases with FTP training (not by reducing carb use at FTP, but by enabling more fat use at sub-FTP intensities once FTP rises, because old FTP intensities are now lower % of the new FTP).

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Study population: masters runners (43–51 yrs, VO2max ~55, half-marathon 83 min). Carbohydrate use at FTP averaged ~80–83% (RER ~0.93) pre- and post-training — did not change at the same absolute intensity. This appears to contradict the "FTP training increases fat burning at FTP" belief; the benefit is at intensities that were previously FTP but are now sub-FTP.
  • Highest TTE ever reliably tested in Kolie's coaching practice: ~83 minutes in three athletes simultaneously. This is exceptional.
  • Highest FTP relative to VO2max typically seen: ~90%. Average across the scientific literature: ~75–85%. FTP at 85% of VO2max in this study is high but within published ranges.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • "FTP equals one hour power" is an arbitrary convention from the original Training and Racing with a Power Meter book — and even there the two authors (Coggan vs. Allen) give slightly different definitions on consecutive pages. There is nothing physiologically special about 60 minutes.
  • Doing an honest one-hour TT to test FTP will "fuck up your training for weeks" (as an early coach warned Kolie) because most athletes' TTE is shorter than 60 minutes — meaning a true one-hour TT effort would require riding above FTP for large portions of the test.
  • The TTE metric in WKO4 (released 2015) was an implicit acknowledgment that the one-hour definition was wrong, but Coggan had to field years of "one hour" questions defending it.

Evolving Views

  • The coach's view on FTP training philosophy was largely formed by this paper, which he credits with inspiring the podcast itself. The key insight — chase TTE extension, not wattage — has remained consistent throughout the series.
