Watts Doc 54 Glycogens Effects on Ampk

Watts Doc 14,306 words View transcript

Referenced in

1. Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation

Core Message

This episode provides the mechanistic link between low end-exercise glycogen and elevated AMPK: the AMPK beta subunit contains a highly conserved glycogen-binding domain that directly inhibits AMPK activity when bound to glycogen. When glycogen is depleted, AMPK is released from this inhibition and becomes more responsive to AMP/ADP accumulation. This means low glycogen acts as an independent activator of AMPK — separate from the energetic stress (high AMP/ADP) that also activates it. The implication for training is that finishing hard training sessions in a glycogen-depleted state provides a potent and mechanistically validated AMPK signal, whereas starting training in a low-glycogen state (fasted) does not replicate the same effect.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Design training sessions that result in low end-exercise glycogen (e.g., long hard rides without excessive mid-ride carbohydrate, or back-to-back hard sessions) to maximize AMPK signaling via the glycogen-binding domain mechanism.
  • Starting glycogen level (fed vs. fasted) does not predict AMPK activation — it is end-exercise glycogen that matters; fuel early in sessions to perform better and still deplete glycogen by the end.
  • Well-trained men (VO2max ~65, riding 2–3 hours, 5–8×/week) showed substantially higher AMPK activation when riding at 70% VO2max in a glycogen-depleted state vs. glycogen-loaded state — this confirms the mechanism in trained athletes specifically.
  • The beta-GPA compound (creatine analog used in rat experiments) mimics chronic energy depletion and chronically activates AMPK — the practical analog is training loads that chronically challenge glycogen storage, not single-session fasting tricks.
  • Progressive overload of training volume (more km/week) naturally increases chronic energy stress and glycogen turnover, maintaining AMPK sensitivity that otherwise declines with adaptation to a fixed training load.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The glycogen-binding domain mechanism was characterized in a 2009 paper — this is foundational biochemistry, not recent clinical trial data, but the evolutionary conservation (across species) suggests high biological importance.
  • Beta-GPA rat experiments create an extreme, chronic energy deficit not replicable by practical training; the mechanistic insights are valid but the quantitative magnitudes don't translate directly to humans.
  • The glycogen-depleted vs. loaded AMPK comparison in well-trained men was conducted at 70% VO2max; results at higher intensities (where AMPK activates regardless via ATP disequilibrium) may show less differential.
  • "Low end-exercise glycogen" is not the same as "no carbohydrates during training" — the strategy is to structure sessions so glycogen is low by the end, not to avoid carbohydrates entirely and impair performance throughout.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Glycogen directly inhibits AMPK via a structural binding domain — this is not an indirect effect mediated through energy status but a direct molecular interaction, making glycogen a genuine AMPK gatekeeper.
  • The implication is that carbohydrate intake strategy during training matters most at the end of the session (do not top up glycogen post-exercise before the adaptation window closes), not just for performance optimization.
  • Fasted training conflates "low starting glycogen" with "low ending glycogen" — the mechanism shows these are different, and only the latter activates AMPK via the beta-subunit pathway.
  • The high evolutionary conservation of the glycogen-binding domain across species (noted in the 2009 paper) is used as evidence that this mechanism is fundamental rather than incidental, strengthening its relevance to human training physiology.

Evolving Views

  • This episode is framed as mechanistically confirming and refining the AMPK/glycogen relationship explored in Watts Doc 45 — the earlier episode showed the correlation (low glycogen → high AMPK), and this episode provides the structural biochemical explanation for why.
