Ten Minute Tips 14 Sets and Reps for Strength Training

7. Strength Training

Core Message

For cyclists, the "bread and butter" strength rep range is 3 sets of 4-6 reps at roughly 80-85% of one rep max -- analogous to how 2x20 at FTP is the bread and butter aerobic workout. Most cyclists should skip the 1-3 rep max range entirely (it is sport-specific to powerlifting/weightlifting) and should never attempt a true one rep max due to the risk-reward ratio. An adaptation phase using sets of 8-12 at moderate weight should precede heavy strength work. Total heavy working sets in a leg session should probably cap at around 8-10, and workouts should be structured with the heaviest compound movement first.

Key Training Recommendations

  • The "bread and butter" strength prescription for cyclists is 3 sets of 4-6 reps (analogous to 2x20 for FTP work); 3x5 and 5x5 are classic starting points
  • Sets of 5 typically land at approximately 80-85% of one rep max
  • 1-3 rep range: sport-specific to powerlifting/weightlifting/strongman; not recommended for endurance athletes due to high risk, high mental/neural taxation, and joint stress
  • 4-6 rep range: best for building base strength and neural adaptation; good if you already have some technique and strength
  • 8-12 (up to 15) rep range: best for adaptation phase (returning to gym), technique learning, and hypertrophy; also good for building anaerobic capacity/W' without increasing Pmax
  • 15-30 rep range: useful only for small range-of-motion muscles (calf raises, delt raises); as low as 30-40% of 1RM can provide hypertrophy stimulus if taken close to failure
  • Do NOT attempt true one rep max testing -- even experienced Olympic weightlifters often skip true 1RM attempts because the risk is not worth it
  • Track strength progression by doing more reps or more sets at the same weight rather than testing 1RM
  • Aim to get close to failure but NOT to actual failure; think "really hard but I can totally do it"
  • Structure workouts: heaviest compound movement first, then accessory work; do not start with knee extensions and work up to squats
  • Cap heavy working sets (not warmup sets) at roughly 8-10 per session for legs
  • Kolie typically does about 4 sets of squats, 3 sets of single leg work, and 2 sets of knee extensions per session
  • For progression: if you hit your target (e.g., 3x5), add ~10 pounds next session; if that drops you to 3x4, work back up to 3x5 at the new weight
  • For hypertrophy progression: add sets (e.g., go from 3x5 to 4x5 or 5x5) rather than adding weight
  • For in-season maintenance: sets of ~8-12 reps NOT close to failure, once every week or two
  • A bad workout where you can ruin your season is a real concern with weightlifting -- injury risk from poor form under heavy load is real

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Rep ranges are a continuum of stimulus, not rigid categories -- sets of 8-12 still build strength, just as sweet spot still affects VO2max
  • If technique breaks down, that counts as "failure" even if you could muscularly complete the rep
  • Recovery ability improves with training, allowing you to eventually handle more sets
  • Renaissance Periodization (Mike Israetel) videos recommended for guidance on recoverable set volumes per muscle group per week

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Cyclists should probably never test their one rep max -- even Olympic weightlifters skip true 1RM attempts because "it's just not worth the risk"
  • The 1-3 rep range is "not even that good at building strength" -- it is better for building heavy-weight specificity, which is sport-specific to strength sports, not cycling
  • Building muscle mass (hypertrophy) is a valid approach to increasing anaerobic capacity (W') without increasing Pmax
  • A single bad weightlifting workout genuinely can ruin your season through injury, unlike cycling where going too hard just means extra rest
  • 20-rep squat sets are described as a "gimmick" -- "the closest thing you can do in the gym that's going to feel like a kilo" (1km track effort)
