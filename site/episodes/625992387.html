4. Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

Core Message

The episode challenges the conventional understanding of "base training" as months of low-intensity zone 2 miles. Kolie and Kyle argue that many of the adaptations attributed to zone 2 base miles can also be obtained from threshold or VO2max work, and that the actual benefit of traditional base seasons is often rest and recovery from the prior season rather than the development of a new aerobic foundation. "Base" is reframed as the cumulative effect of all focused aerobic work across a season and a career, not specifically low-intensity mileage. The "bigger base, higher peak" pyramid metaphor is replaced with a "food plate" model where threshold, VO2max, and endurance work all contribute.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Build your longest ride up to the duration of your longest race (Friel recommendation endorsed as "pretty solid advice").
  • Endurance rides should use a negative split — start at lower power (e.g., 150–170 W for a planned 200 W average) and build throughout. One Reddit user reported smashing all multi-hour power records by simply starting easier.
  • To make endurance rides effective, they must challenge your endurance limit: if you can comfortably sustain 4 hours at 150 W, target 4.25–4.5 hours to push the adaptation.
  • For athletes who are time-limited and have a training camp or long event approaching, VO2max intervals are an effective preparation tool: they raise the power you can sustain on subsequent long rides.
  • A 90–120 minute steady endurance ride is excellent as a maintenance/between-race day workout (e.g., between a Friday and Sunday criterium).
  • Friel recommends a minimum ~2-hour ride for zone 2 adaptations, but this is only useful if 2 hours is actually challenging for the athlete.
  • If time-crunched, increase ride intensity rather than doing very short zone 2 rides. Zone 2 rides shorter than what you can easily handle provide minimal adaptation.
  • Once you hit a duration ceiling, shift progressive overload to increasing power output on endurance rides, not just extending duration.
  • Intervals can be started earlier than tradition suggests — there is no evidence that athletes must complete months of zone 2 before doing threshold intervals.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The advice about riding long applies to athletes racing long events. Time-Crunched Cyclist author Chris Carmichael is quoted: if your longest ride is 1–1.5 hours, race races that aren't longer than that.
  • Elite professional cyclists are "genetic freaks" — their training is hard to screw up regardless, and their practices should not be blindly copied by amateurs.
  • Scientific studies on base training typically use recreationally active or average amateur populations; results may not transfer to well-trained athletes.
  • The physiological claims behind "you must ride easy to build capillaries" are incorrect, but the practice of easy riding in the off-season still produces real rest and recovery benefits.
  • Endurance pace (zone 2) and VO2max training do produce subtly different adaptations even though both cause aerobic improvements. They cannot be exactly equated.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Months of zone 2 riding work "literally false, metaphorically true" — the real benefit is recovery from the previous season, not building an aerobic base.
  • Zones 1–5 produce roughly the same tick-marks on the Coggan physiological adaptation chart; "aerobic is aerobic" and different intensities yield similar adaptations.
  • A person with a "big base" has simply done a lot of focused aerobic work at all intensities over time — not a lot of zone 2 miles specifically.
  • "Going slow makes you slow" is probably not true in a strict sense, but "you need to go slow to go fast" is only true if interpreted as "you need adequate rest."
  • Performing extensive zone 2 before starting intervals is not a universal prerequisite; it's fine to begin intervals earlier as long as recovery is managed.

Evolving Views

  • None identified in this episode beyond the general thesis reframing what "base" means — this appears to be a settled position for the hosts rather than an update to a prior view.
