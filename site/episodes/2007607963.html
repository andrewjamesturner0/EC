Ten Minute Tips 47 Strength Training with Limited Equipment

Ten Minute Tips 23,262 words View transcript

Referenced in

7. Strength Training

Core Message

This episode provides a practical tiered approach to strength training for cyclists who lack gym access, organized by equipment level: body weight only, resistance bands, loadable dumbbells, and home squat rack. The earlier you are in your strength training journey, the more utility you will get from minimal equipment. The hosts emphasize safety repeatedly, as home training without proper equipment carries real injury risk. They also cover intensifiers (techniques to get more stimulus from limited weight) and discuss cycling-specific isometric training based on the Cordy study.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Body weight quad exercises: Sissy squats and reverse Nordic curls are the best cycling-specific options; start with 1-2 sets per week at about 6/10 RPE
  • Reverse Nordics caution: Use thick padding, control depth carefully; can cause extreme muscle damage at long muscle lengths
  • Body weight glute exercises: Modified step-ups on a high, stable box (pushed against wall for safety)
  • Isometrics (planks): Front, side, and reverse planks for trunk and shoulder stability; modify plank position to mimic your bike position
  • Equipment hierarchy for investment: Resistance bands first, then loadable dumbbells, then squat rack with safeties
  • Intensifiers to get more from limited weight:
  • Heel elevation (1.5-2 inches): shifts emphasis to quads, enables deeper knee flexion
  • Slow eccentrics: control the descent, pause at bottom; reduces reps substantially
  • Unilateral training: single leg versions of any exercise to increase challenge without adding weight
  • Rest-pause sets: heavy set of 10-15, rest 20-30 seconds, repeat; do NOT use for heavy compounds (squats, deadlifts) -- only for exercises where you can fail safely
  • Lengthened partials: go to full depth, come only halfway up, immediately go back down; research shows similar hypertrophy to full ROM
  • Cycling-specific isometric protocol (Cordy study): On a locked-off track bike/trainer, push maximally at 3 pedal positions (45, 90, 135 degrees from TDC), each leg; 3 seconds per rep with 60 seconds rest, 2 minutes between sets; progressed over 6 weeks from 1x3 to 4x3. Control group did 3-5 sets of 3-5 reps at 85-95% 1RM back squat; experimental group did only 3 sets of 5 at 70-75% plus the isometrics. Experimental group (elite British sprint cyclists, multiple Olympians) showed significant sprint power improvement
  • Bone density: Home strength training loads are typically insufficient; instead do jump rope, light plyometrics (start with 3 sets of 10 per leg), short jogs, hiking, or pickup soccer
  • Pistol squats: "A party trick and nothing more" -- balance demands outweigh strength stimulus; prefer heel-elevated or front-foot-elevated split squats instead
  • Smith machines: Better than nothing; actually decent for many movements and safer for failing squats
  • Lifting straps: $20 Harbinger cotton straps last for years; use when grip limits the target exercise

Caveats & Qualifications

  • None of this is medical advice; if you have knee, hip, or ankle issues, work with a PT
  • Safety is paramount: do not do step-ups on unstable surfaces; do not rest-pause heavy compound lifts; practice failing before going heavy
  • The stronger you already are, the less useful body weight and minimal equipment training will be -- diminishing returns are real
  • Distinguish between "my muscles are tired" (keep working) and "my bones/joints don't like this" (modify or stop)

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Pistol squats are dismissed as a party trick with no real strength training value
  • Smith machines are endorsed as legitimate training tools despite their poor reputation among free-weight purists
  • Isolated quad exercises (sissy squats, belt squats) can be humbling for people who think they have strong quads based on back squat numbers; people back squatting 400+ lbs may belt squat only 185-225
  • The cycling-specific isometric protocol produced significant sprint power gains in already-elite Olympic-level sprinters
  • Rest-pause can deliver approximately the same stimulus as traditional straight sets in roughly half the time
  • Lengthened partials produce "just about as much muscle growth as full range of motion" based on recent research

Evolving Views

  • Kolie states he previously dismissed bands ("I've shit on bands before") but now recognizes their substantial value for beginners and those with limited equipment
  • Kolie was "totally wrong" in his prior belief that elite track sprinters could not further improve neural motor patterns for on-bike force production, based on the Cordy study results
