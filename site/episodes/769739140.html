3. Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing

Core Message

This listener Q&A episode covers practical questions about improving FTP, including: whether sweet spot or VO2max work can substitute for direct FTP training (answer: generally no); how much FTP error is tolerable (5–10 W); minimum FTP maintenance dose; training by terrain; the FTP/VO2max relationship; and why FTP work benefits even riders who never race near threshold. The central theme is that FTP is the aerobic foundation for all cycling performance, and that nothing replaces direct threshold training for raising it.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Best way to improve FTP: ride at FTP. Over-unders at threshold are the next best tool. VO2max work does NOT reliably raise FTP — in coached athletes, FTP only rises after returning to dedicated FTP training.
  • VO2max work can raise short-duration test scores (ramp test, 8-minute test) without raising actual FTP, especially if the test protocol has significant anaerobic contribution.
  • FTP maintenance (minimum dose): one interval session per week at 90–100% FTP. For TTE of ~60 min, 3×20 is sufficient. If doing VO2max blocks (3–4 weeks), FTP typically does not decrease and may tick up a few watts — this is the best scenario for FTP maintenance without dedicated threshold sessions.
  • Tolerable FTP error: ±5–10 W. At high FTPs (350+ W), 5–10 W is a small percentage; at lower FTPs (100–150 W), it matters more. Always set training targets 5–10 W below FTP to avoid inadvertent over-prescription.
  • TT bike FTP is typically 10–15 W lower than road bike FTP; this is a real difference that requires its own calibration.
  • Standing vs. seated FTP: does not warrant two separate FTPs unless you race exclusively in one position (e.g., Contador-style standing climbing intervals). For most athletes, train as you race.
  • Continuous intervals (4×15 with 5-min rests) are better than the same total volume fragmented more (e.g., 60×1 min). Minimum effective continuous interval: ~10 minutes. 60×1-min FTP intervals would produce "almost none" of the adaptation of 4×15.
  • FTP work benefits sprinters and criterium racers even though they "never use FTP watts in races." Higher FTP means surges to 130–140% FTP (the race intensity) are now a smaller fraction of what you can sustain aerobically, meaning faster recovery between attacks. A sprinter with 280 W FTP spending race time at 330–350 W is working largely anaerobically; raising FTP to 300 W nearly halves the distance to those watts aerobically.
  • For beginners: getting a coach accelerates FTP gains dramatically. Example cited: athlete went from 180 W to 230–240 W FTP within a couple of months.
  • For flatland vs. mountain terrain: prescribe free-form targets (e.g., "accumulate 80 minutes at FTP across the ride") for mountainous terrain rather than structured intervals.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Garmin's VO2max estimate is off by a minimum of 5–10%; ignore it as a training guide. WKO4/5 VO2max estimate is more accurate but still imperfect.
  • FTP does not change meaningfully week to week. A perceived drop of 20 W in interval performance is typically accumulated fatigue, not a true FTP decline. FTP cannot change physiologically that fast.
  • One-minute work (anaerobic capacity) reaches adaptation ceiling within 6–8 weeks; FTP work can be trained more continuously and cumulatively.
  • Time-in-zone over FTP is not a reliable FTP estimator. Well-trained racers can spend enormous time over FTP in a race without that indicating their FTP is underestimated.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Doing a lot of one-minute intervals will make your ramp test FTP score go up without actually raising FTP — this is "gaming the test." You're studying for the test (anaerobic contribution to the test), not improving the quality (aerobic capacity) it's supposed to measure.
  • Sweet spot is not really "lower fatigue" training — if set correctly, sweet spot fatigue ≈ FTP fatigue for comparable time. The real fatigue difference disappears when you account for needing ~10 minutes more per interval at sweet spot to match the FTP stimulus.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie notes that his holistic aerobic training philosophy (not compartmentalizing FTP vs. VO2max vs. base) has led to much less FTP detraining in his athletes over time compared to his earlier, more compartmentalized approach.
