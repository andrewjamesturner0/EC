Ten Minute Tips 16 Can Functional Training Get Too Functional

Ten Minute Tips 9,551 words View transcript

Core Message

This episode examines whether "functional training" can replace traditional strength training. Kolie and Kyle agree that functional training is a valid concept but cannot fully replace foundational strength movements like squats, deadlifts, and presses. Even CrossFit athletes and military/firefighter populations still rely on traditional strength exercises as a base. The most "functional" approach for cyclists is to build a traditional strength base and then add sport-specific work on top, not the reverse.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Functional training should supplement, not replace, main strength exercises (squats, deadlifts, overhead press)
  • Even CrossFit Games athletes like Mat Fraser had deep backgrounds in traditional strength training (Olympic weightlifting)
  • For cyclists, build a traditional strength base first and then add sport-specific work on top
  • CrossFit-style training includes substantial traditional strength work (often an hour of squats before the metabolic conditioning portion)
  • A barbell squat is itself quite functional -- it involves the whole body, requires core strength, and improves daily-life activities

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The public/social-media understanding of "functional training" may differ significantly from the formal definition
  • Strength is specific to the kinetic chain, joint angles, and movement pattern being tested; a strong squat does not automatically mean you can pick up an Atlas stone

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Functional training is better understood as a supplement to traditional strength work, not a replacement -- the best functional athletes all still squat, deadlift, and press heavy
  • A barbell squat is already quite "functional" despite being dismissed by functional-training advocates as too rigid
