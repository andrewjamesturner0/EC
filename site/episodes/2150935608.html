Core Message

A coach roundtable (Kolie, Megan, Gediminas, Alex, Erica) tackles listener questions about limiters, big volume weeks, under-optimized training practices, and the pitfalls of sporadic intervals. The overarching theme is that most recreational athletes under-optimize the fundamentals — nutrition, sleep, qualitative self-monitoring, and mental engagement with hard efforts — while over-optimizing arbitrary metrics. The episode is one of the most comprehensive discussions of recovery micro-practices and the "macro vs. micro" training mindset in the podcast.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Big volume weeks — safe ranges:
  • Athletes doing 10 hours/week can safely stretch to ~15 hours (150% of normal) for a camp week; going to 20–25 hours is likely too much without a coach directing recovery.
  • Athletes doing 15 hours/week can stretch to ~22–25 hours.
  • If a climbing destination forces higher terrain intensity, reduce total hours target further.
  • Practical preparation: do all grocery shopping before the week begins; buy lots of energy-dense food (explicit recommendation: stock ice cream and high-calorie snacks). Coming home from a 6-hour ride to an empty fridge causes athletes to skip eating and crash.
  • Sporadic vs. structured intervals: Sporadic "ride hard when I feel like it" intervals that accumulate the same time-in-zone as structured intervals can produce similar acute adaptations, BUT: (1) they cannot be progressively overloaded in a systematic way, (2) coaches cannot identify the cause when they stop working, (3) athletes cannot assess whether they are improving their TTE at a given power. Recommendation: structured intervals allow a logical framework for progression and diagnosis.
  • Under-optimized practice #1 — nutrition:
  • Do not let >5–6 hours elapse without eating on a training day. Athletes who skip lunch and train in the evening will not catch up on fueling by workout time.
  • Keep non-perishable snacks (e.g., Larabars, granola bars) at your desk or in your bag for days when food access is unpredictable.
  • Endurance athletes doing intermittent fasting: the literature may show similar strength/hypertrophy gains for intermittent fasting in sedentary populations, but fasting with high-energy training (thousands of additional kCal/day) is genuinely harmful for recovery and long-term adaptation.
  • Under-optimized practice #2 — sleep environment: During heat waves, actively cool the bedroom. Sleeping too warm impairs recovery and causes workout quality to drop noticeably within days. Use AC at night, even if culturally it feels unusual. For racing travel: bring earplugs, an eye mask, and a neck pillow.
  • Under-optimized practice #3 — qualitative self-monitoring: Self-coached athletes almost never formally track qualitative signals (mood, motivation, perceived effort, sleep quality, general stress). Coaches ask for this information from athletes 20–50% of the time in consultations. Recommendation: log a brief daily comment on how you feel, not just power numbers. Trends in comment tone over 2–3 months are among the most actionable coaching data available.
  • Under-optimized practice #4 — mental engagement during hard efforts: During VO2max, sprint, and threshold efforts, actively engage with the effort — cadence, power production, breathing — rather than just trying to survive it. This engagement may produce meaningfully higher watts and better race-readiness for suffering. For strength training: the final reps closest to failure are the most valuable; slow down and lean into those reps rather than rushing through.
  • Under-optimized practice #5 — macro-level perspective: Individual bad workouts (especially in heat or high-stress weeks) should not trigger doubt about the entire season. The relevant signal is the trend over weeks and the whole training block, not any single session.
  • Interval progression rule: To prevent plateau from sporadic training, intervals must be progressively lengthened over time. Example: if FTP intervals start at 8 minutes, advance to 10 → 12 → 15 minutes. Stagnating at the same time-in-zone at the same power is the most common reason sporadic training stops working.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Doubling volume for a camp week works for athletes who are otherwise consistent. An inconsistent athlete suddenly doing 20 hours may not recover quickly enough to resume training.
  • The "all roads lead to PGC-1α" principle (any training stress drives aerobic adaptation) is described as "roughly true but one-dimensional" — useful as a mindset anchor but not a complete framework.
  • VO2max zone time-in-zone from a group ride or cyclocross race does not equal focused VO2max intervals; heart rate high without sustained progressive effort does not drive VO2max adaptation in well-trained athletes.
  • Sporadic intervals can work as a limited method, especially for advanced athletes who use them within a logical framework (e.g., timed hot-lap circuits with tracked normalized power and consistent route).
  • Strength and hypertrophy gains are roughly equivalent between intermittent fasting and normal eating in strength/hypertrophy literature — but this does not transfer to endurance athletes with large daily energy expenditures.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Coach Kolie states he has reduced formal FTP testing for athletes because a well-designed training plan already surfaces fitness trends — testing burns energy that is better saved for race day. For athletes with consistently structured training, direct testing may be unnecessary.
  • When a client has a history of performing better after more rest than planned, Kolie's strategy is to deliberately program less and see benefits. One example: a client who repeatedly auto-regulated rest against the plan and went on to UCI podiums — the coach credits learning the athlete's individual rest needs from observing that auto-regulation.
  • Nutrition is framed as the highest-leverage under-optimization for most recreational cyclists, above gadgets, training plans, or interval design.

Evolving Views

  • Gediminas describes a personal lesson from his first coached year: 100% training plan compliance led to end-of-season physical and mental breakdown, requiring a month off and a loss of enthusiasm for the sport. He now immediately flags clients who show all-green TrainingPeaks calendars as potential over-compliers, not model athletes.
  • Alex notes that off-the-shelf training plans often consist of "random hard riding every week" with no progressive focus or coherent framework — a pattern he identifies as one of the most common traps athletes fall into before seeking professional coaching.
