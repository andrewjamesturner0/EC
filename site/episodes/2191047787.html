Watts Doc 58 Creatines Actual Effects on Cycling Performance

Watts Doc 20,936 words View transcript

Referenced in

13. Research & Methodology

Core Message

Creatine supplementation's reputation has outpaced the actual evidence, particularly for cyclists. A 2023 meta-analysis of 277 well-trained athletes found essentially zero effect of creatine on aerobic performance (effect size: −0.07, 95% CI −0.27 to +0.13). A 2022 meta-analysis on repeated sprint performance found a statistically significant but practically modest improvement in mean sprint power (~27 W) alongside a large and reliable increase in body mass (~0.8 kg). A race-simulation cycling study (2010, n=12) found no difference in time-to-exhaustion sprint at the end of a 2-hour ride after 28 days of supplementation, while the creatine group gained ~2 kg. The host's framework of "is it meaningful, noticeable, or measurable?" leads to a cautious conclusion: creatine is probably not worth taking for road cyclists, may be mildly useful for track sprinters who are already doing serious gym work, and carries a small but non-zero contamination risk for competitive athletes.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Do not supplement creatine if your primary goal is aerobic cycling performance (road racing, mountain bike XC, gravel): the best available evidence shows no effect on aerobic capacity or race-simulated sprint performance after weight gain is accounted for.
  • Consider creatine supplementation only if you are a track or criterium sprinter who spends significant time in the weight room and are not weight-sensitive; the small (~27 W) improvement in mean sprint power may be meaningful in a bike-throw finish, but only if body mass gain is minimal (~1 kg rather than the more typical 2–5 kg).
  • If supplementing, do not use a loading protocol unless you are on a strict study timeline; simply take 3–5 g/day continuously — muscle saturation will occur without the gastrointestinal side effects of 20 g/day loading doses.
  • If loading, spread doses across the day (e.g., 4 × 5 g), never take 20 g in a single dose.
  • Always use third-party-tested creatine monohydrate (e.g., NSF Certified for Sport or Informed Sport) and keep lot numbers and bottle photos in case of a WADA challenge.
  • Vegetarians and vegans may be lower-responders in terms of performance benefit but higher-responders in terms of weight gain because their baseline muscle creatine is lower; factor this into the cost-benefit assessment.
  • For ultra-endurance events involving severe sleep deprivation, creatine at ~0.35 g/kg taken in the hours before an overnight effort may modestly improve cognitive task speed (roughly 2 seconds per question at 4 AM) — worth considering given the low risk at that time frame and mass concerns likely being irrelevant after days of racing.
  • When assessing body composition (DEXA, Bod Pod, bioimpedance) while taking creatine, standardize by either always measuring on-supplement or stopping supplementation for 2–3 weeks before the scan; do not mix measurement contexts.
  • When evaluating any supplement or intervention, use the "meaningful / noticeable / measurable" framework before acting: a statistically significant effect that falls inside power-meter measurement error or is offset by body mass change is not actionable.
  • Never overhaul your training or supplementation practice based on a single study; wait for the body of literature (preferably a meta-analysis of well-trained, sport-specific athletes) to converge before drawing strong conclusions.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The meta-analyses reviewed used mixed sport populations (rowers, swimmers, runners) with only 3–4 cycling-specific studies inside the sprint meta; the true effect in trained cyclists specifically is still poorly characterized.
  • Individual response to creatine weight gain varies widely: the host gains ~2 lb (low responder), co-host Kyle gains ~5 lb (more typical); effect on Watts/kg therefore varies considerably.
  • The 27 W mean sprint power improvement (SMD 0.61) in the sprint meta came with wide confidence intervals and somewhat non-normally distributed study results, raising the possibility of residual file-drawer bias; Kolie explicitly flags low confidence in this result.
  • Cognitive benefits are preliminary: the main sleep-deprivation study (n=15, crossover RCT) found ~1–2 seconds faster response time per question at 4 AM and ~0.5 more correct answers out of 8 — real but small; effect appears larger in older adults (>60) per one meta (effect size ~0.88), though based on pooled outcomes from only two studies.
  • Creatine supplementation does not increase creatine kinase in the blood in a way that indicates overtraining; elevated serum creatine kinase is a marker of muscle rupture, not of creatine intake.
  • The claim circulating online that women have 70–80% less endogenous creatine than men is a misreference: the original source states women's daily creatine synthesis rate is 70–80% of men's (likely explained by lower average muscle mass and activity), not that their muscle stores are depleted. No studies in the reviewed metas found sex-based differences in the effectiveness of supplementation.
  • Creatine does not appear to impair heat tolerance based on the limited data Kolie reviewed; the water retained is intramuscular, not plasma volume expansion.
  • Stopping creatine does not cause loss of strength or muscle gains earned during supplementation; those gains come from training adaptations, not creatine retention.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Aerobic training — not sprint-specific training alone — is what primarily drives phosphocreatine recovery between repeated sprints; therefore, improving aerobic fitness is a more powerful lever for repeated sprint performance than creatine supplementation.
  • The weight gain from creatine is entirely osmotic water — not contractile tissue — in the short term; strength gains enabled by creatine accumulate slowly over months of training and are far larger contributors to sprint performance than the direct phosphocreatine top-up.
  • Even a "medium" effect size by conventional exercise physiology standards (SMD ~0.6) translates to only 27–30 W on a ~1,000 W sprint, which is barely outside typical power-meter measurement error; the statistical label "medium" is therefore misleading in practical terms.
  • A study simulating a bike race (2-hour ride at 60% VO2 peak + sprints every 15 minutes at 110% VO2max, final sprint to exhaustion) found identical time-to-exhaustion in creatine vs. placebo groups, while the creatine group gained 2 kg — the most race-relevant cycling study available returned a null result.
  • Creatine's well-established gym benefit (~1–2 extra reps per set, ~5–10 lb on major lifts) requires months of accumulated training before it might translate to measurable sprint power on the bike; the chain of causation is several steps long and attenuates considerably.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie notes this is his second dedicated creatine episode (the first was Watts Doc 38), and having now reviewed meta-analyses rather than single studies, he is more confident that aerobic performance effects are truly null — whereas earlier he may have left more room for ambiguity given the mixed single-study literature.
  • He also expresses increased willingness to engage with meta-analytic methods (having previously avoided them due to complexity), calling the approach "excellent" for resolving the wide variance in single small-sample exercise physiology studies.
