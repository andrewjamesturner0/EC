Perspectives 6 2021 Season Review and 2022 Planning with Ingvar Omarsson

Perspectives 16,004 words View transcript

Referenced in

12. Racing & Team Tactics

Core Message

Season review and 2022 planning call with Ingvar Omarsson, an elite Icelandic privateer cyclist competing across road, gravel, and MTB (UCI top-50 ranking at time of recording). Main themes: how to structure a season around peak races rather than maximizing race count; how a mid-season nutrition intervention (eating 100 g/hr instead of 3 gels for a long stage) directly produced a first UCI podium; how early-season fatigue from racing depresses training volume and delays late-season fitness; and the value of an intentional off-season with gym work for athletes who normally ride year-round.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Do not race in January if your first A-race is May — use that time for VO2 max blocks and strength work instead; "doing a race just to do a race" sacrifices training quality
  • Race fewer events: quality over quantity — Ingvar's coach explicitly recommended racing less in 2022 after overloading 2021
  • Eat 100 g carbohydrate/hr during long race stages and endurance rides; for Ingvar, going from minimal fueling to this standard produced his first UCI podium within days
  • After a multi-week VO2 max block (which Ingvar described as 3 weeks, same workouts each week, ~20–25 hrs/week): plan follow-on aerobic volume (17–20 hr weeks) to maintain fitness; the adaptations persist through easy maintenance riding
  • An intentional off-season (novel for a career-long year-round rider) allows targeted strength/gym work that is incompatible with a VO2 max training block
  • Sequencing: strength phase in Nov–Dec → aerobic build + VO2 block Jan–Feb → sharpen for first A-race (May); threshold work in March–April before target peaks
  • For bunch sprint situations (tactical): have patience, "reduce your odds" by staying in the pack until the final laps — don't attack if you have the aerobic engine to win from a small group sprint; but do practice sprinting even if you're strong enough to solo, to develop the skill

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Ingvar notes that his best form came in spring (after the VO2 block) but dropped in June due to training volume reduction from racing and then having a newborn — fitness is load-dependent and can fade mid-season without volume maintenance
  • Carrying fatigue from late-season racing into November compressed the off-season; starting the prep cycle 6 weeks later than ideal is a real cost
  • Being a privateer gives schedule freedom but requires self-management of race selection, logistics, and sponsor obligations that team riders don't face
  • Early-season stage races (Lanzarote, Casablanca, Andalusia) in January are training races; factoring them as A-races creates a peak-too-early problem

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Kolie: "I hope next season you can race a little less. Because I know that we could get your form a little better." — More racing does not equal better results; this is stated explicitly as a structural recommendation for a top-50 UCI rider
  • Ingvar: Getting second at the Rift (24 seconds behind Pete Stetna) felt better than every gold medal in Iceland — perspective on the importance of racing at the right level, not just winning
  • A VO2 max block that leaves you unable to eat pizza (Ingvar's anecdote about staring at pizza unable to motivate yourself to eat it) is at the appropriate intensity; stopping too early is a waste

Evolving Views

  • Ingvar evolved from never having an off-season (one off-season in his career, from a motorbike accident in 2015) to deliberately planning one; he acknowledges deep discomfort with losing fitness intentionally but recognizes the long-term benefit
  • Ingvar changed from winning races predominantly solo (attacking off the front) to practicing bunch sprint tactics — won the Icelandic road national championship in a bunch sprint for the first time in years, realizing he could do it when he committed to the tactic instead of defaulting to soloing
