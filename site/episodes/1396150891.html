4. Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

Core Message

Responding to a client consultation question and forum discussions, this episode argues that zone 2 has no unique physiological adaptation that cannot be stimulated by riding at higher intensities — it's a continuum, and "threshold training is endurance training" (Andy Coggan's words, endorsed by Kolie). What is special about zone 2 is what it allows within a training plan: because it accumulates relatively little fatigue, large volumes can be stacked without compromising recovery for quality interval sessions. At very high volumes (20–30 h/week), the "pro zone 2 approach" makes sense because the fatigue from that volume itself limits how much high-intensity work can be stacked on top. For most athletes riding 10–15 hours/week, there is no obligation to include dedicated zone 2 if other work is being done well.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Endurance riding (zone 2 / LSD) is best defined as "the hardest pace that still feels easy" and should pass the talk test, regardless of power number. Do not peg it at the top of the zone 2 range by power table.
  • Riding 20–30 W below your apparent LT1 is fine; Kolie reports no observed adaptation difference between riding at 60% of FTP versus 45–50% of FTP for athletes whose LT1 is around 60–65% of FTP.
  • For pro athletes (20–30 h/week) under high volume, maintain endurance riding during a VO2max block but ride easier than usual — doing so maintains FTP and VO2max even when no hard intervals are happening.
  • Endurance riding is a highly effective maintenance tool: when fatigued pro athletes switch to easy endurance-only training, their threshold and VO2max numbers recover over time without any interval work.
  • On a trainer, break up endurance rides with cadence variation (e.g., 10 minutes at 10 rpm above normal, then 10 rpm below, repeat) rather than adding intensity.
  • When transitioning from 3-hour to 6-hour long rides, add 30 minutes per week conservatively or 60 minutes per week aggressively — either is fine for most athletes.
  • Include preemptive rest weeks when increasing volume dramatically. A coach's job is often to pull the leash (reduce training) rather than crack the whip.
  • If only doing 2–3 rides of 2 hours per week, those rides do not need to be zone 2. Threshold or VO2max work in that slot still provides endurance-continuum adaptation. Zone 2 is not mandatory for short schedules.
  • Consistency (80% effort every session) outperforms intermittent crushing (100%) followed by rest (0%).

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The lower limit of zone 2 adaptation is unknown; there is no established minimum wattage. What seems clear is that riding "too easy" (literally chainless or zero resistance) would not adapt, but once pedaling meaningfully, very low intensities still provide adaptation.
  • Very high volume (15–30 h/week at zone 2) does accumulate real fatigue and limits quality interval capacity. The "zone 2 is free/costless fatigue" narrative is only true at moderate volumes.
  • Zone 2 defined by 2 mmol/L blood lactate puts many trained athletes at low sweet spot — this is a common misapplication that turns endurance rides into tempo rides with associated fatigue accumulation.
  • There is no strict correlation between LT1 power (relative to FTP) and fiber type; training volume appears to be the larger predictor of LT1 position.
  • Zone 2 pace varies greatly between athletes; the number in the Coggan zone table is a convenience, not a physiological fingerprint.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Zone 2 is not special in terms of adaptations; it is special in terms of what it permits in a training week — low fatigue, high stackability.
  • Copying Pogacar's zone 2 training plan would not make an amateur competitive; his genetics are the differentiating factor, not the zone 2 volume.
  • A retired pro or someone with many years of high-volume training can maintain very high fitness on surprisingly low volume because many aerobic adaptations become semi-permanent.
  • Zone 2 physiologically is best defined as "a training intensity that does not accumulate much undue fatigue" — its defining feature is what it doesn't do (accumulate fatigue), not a specific fuel-usage or fiber-recruitment pattern.
  • "Junk miles" is a myth when applied to easy riding; truly junk effort is moderate-but-not-structured riding (sprinting from stoplights, tempo fartlek commutes) that creates fatigue without targeted adaptation.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie explicitly flags that his views on zone 2 physiology are still forming: "between now and whenever I've thought about it for a year or two, you're going to see an evolution of thought" on what zone 2 means physiologically.
  • He has historically referred to endurance riding as "endurance training" rather than "zone 2" to avoid prescription-by-zone-number; this naming preference is confirmed here as intentional.
