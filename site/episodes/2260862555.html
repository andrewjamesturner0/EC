13. Research & Methodology

Core Message

Training improvements follow a logarithmic (diminishing returns) growth curve, not a linear one — this is demonstrated with high confidence by a landmark study on strength training (Steel et al., N=14,690, up to 6.8 years of data from a Dutch gym chain) and is visible in Cole's own multi-year cycling athlete data. The mode change point where gains slow dramatically occurs around 26–31 weeks for most people in strength training; equivalent patterns appear year-on-year in cycling FTP data. The critical practical implication is that studies showing one training intervention is "twice as good" as another in a short block are almost certainly describing different positions on the early part of the growth curve, not meaningfully different long-term asymptotes. Coaches and athletes should calibrate annual FTP improvement expectations accordingly — typically 10–15 W/year for athletes well into their training careers — and find this clarifying rather than discouraging.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Plot your own FTP (or key performance metric) year-on-year as a case study. If you log-transform the time axis and it goes linear, you are seeing the expected growth curve; use this to set realistic annual improvement goals rather than comparing yourself to others.
  • When you've had large FTP gains in consecutive years (e.g., 30W two years in a row), recognize that a third identical gain is unlikely; shift training emphasis toward race preparation and other weaknesses rather than continuing to pursue aerobic ceiling gains.
  • After a mid-season break (illness, vacation), expect to return to your previous season's best within approximately one training block (roughly 3 months based on Cole's athlete data), not to regress permanently. Use this to reduce anxiety about missed training.
  • If you are early in your training history (left side of growth curve), almost any training stimulus — threshold, sprint intervals, VO2max intervals — will raise VO2max and FTP. Do not over-optimize training type at this stage; consistency and progressive overload matter more.
  • For well-trained athletes (far right on the growth curve), identify the specific physiologic bottleneck (e.g., VO2max has plateaued despite FTP intervals) and add targeted stimulus for that variable (e.g., genuine VO2max intervals at high oxygen uptake for sufficient duration). Simply hammering the same FTP intervals yields diminishing returns — example: one athlete doing only threshold work for a year gained approximately 5W.
  • When evaluating study results, read the methods for where subjects are on their personal growth curve. If endurance athletes come in after an off-season and improve rapidly on any intervention, the study is describing early-season re-sensitization, not a long-term superiority of one method over another.
  • Treat training as a dynamic equilibrium between stimulus and recovery. More stimulus digs a deeper fatigue hole; if you increase training load you must also ensure adequate recovery capacity. Most amateurs are simultaneously limited by insufficient stimulus AND insufficient recovery — both matter.
  • Voluntarily participate in university training studies if approached as an amateur; well-designed in-season data from trained athletes is extremely rare and advances practical knowledge.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The Steel et al. data uses 1 set / 1 session per week — a very low dose. Change points (where gains slow) would likely arrive sooner with higher weekly volume (e.g., 2 sessions/week might compress 31 weeks of leg press change point to ~15 weeks, though this is speculative).
  • Cycling has more physiologic variables than strength training (central: stroke volume, blood volume, capillary density; peripheral: fiber type, muscular endurance, anaerobic capacity), so the growth curve concept becomes more complex with multiple interacting sub-curves. A single training intervention may move some variables without others.
  • R-squared values in cycling physiology studies explaining performance outcomes via single measured variables (e.g., VO2max or MLSS) typically explain only 15–40% of variance — the same ~0.4 R-value that appeared in the prior durability episode. This is a structural feature of the field, not a bug in individual studies.
  • Diagnosing that an athlete has truly reached their genetic ceiling requires years of ruled-out alternatives (insufficient stimulus, insufficient recovery, off-bike stress). Cole notes this is rare in amateur athletes; most improvement limitations are training-environment problems, not genetics.
  • Individual growth curves differ in both starting point (baseline fitness) and rate of improvement. These two parameters are not correlated in Cole's experience — a high baseline does not imply fast improvement, and a low baseline does not imply fast improvement.
  • Life stress (graduate school, high-pressure jobs, family events) can create multi-year plateaus that look like genetic ceilings but resolve when circumstances change. Cole recommends "shepherding people through" and striking when the iron is hot.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Short-term study results showing one training intervention is "twice as effective" as another almost certainly reflect position on the early growth curve, not a meaningful difference in long-term asymptotic potential. Cole's position (clearly flagged as supposition lacking direct evidence): different interventions change the rate of early gains, not the ultimate ceiling.
  • Sprint interval training raises VO2max in untrained or moderately trained people — but that improvement is a crossover effect from being on the left side of multiple growth curves simultaneously. At some point, sprint intervals alone cannot provide sufficient sustained stimulus at high oxygen uptakes to continue moving VO2max.
  • Studies claiming superiority of one endurance training method over another are structurally very difficult to conduct validly in trained athletes: you need large samples (to detect <1.2% changes over 12 weeks), athletes who are genuinely post-change-point for the variable of interest, and in-season participation that most competitive athletes will decline.
  • A coach telling an athlete "I think we're done with aerobic gains for this season, time to race" is delivering good news: it means the athlete achieved their expected improvement ceiling and can now focus on performance application rather than fitness building.

Evolving Views

  • Cole states he was previously skeptical that real-world data could ever demonstrate diminishing returns at population scale. The Steel et al. dataset changed his view: he now considers the logarithmic growth model a well-established principle applicable to cycling, validated by his own multi-year athlete case series even if N is small.
  • He flags that the reference back to the durability episode R-value of ~0.4 is not coincidental: he now views this ceiling as a systemic feature of physiological research, not a problem with specific durability studies, and has updated how he weights individual studies versus meta-analyses accordingly.
