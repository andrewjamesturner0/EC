Watts Doc 18 Why Everything You Know About Vo2max Power is Probably Wrong

Watts Doc 9,308 words View transcript

Referenced in

2. VO2max & Oxygen Uptake

Core Message

This episode systematically dismantles the concept of a fixed "VO2max power" or "maximal aerobic power" (MAP/Wmax), showing it is an artifact of ramp test protocol design rather than a physiologically meaningful quantity. Using two papers examining ramp test step durations (Adami study: 15–180 second steps; second study: 1–10 minute stages), the episode demonstrates that the power value associated with true VO2max varies by up to 84 watts depending solely on step duration — all while producing the same measured oxygen uptake. The practical takeaway is that Garmin VO2max estimates, zone 5 power targets, and "VO2max power" figures should be treated with profound skepticism, and that meaningful VO2max tracking requires either direct O2 measurement or carefully controlled comparative field tests.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Do not use your ramp test final power value as a "VO2max power" training target — this number changes by up to 25% depending purely on how long the ramp test steps are, while true VO2max (in liters/minute) remains constant.
  • To track VO2max progression without lab equipment, compare two 5-minute maximal efforts under matched conditions (same freshness, same day of week, same conditions) with only VO2max training — no anaerobic capacity work — between tests, to control for W' (anaerobic capacity) confounding the result.
  • For ramp tests to be valid for VO2max measurement, they should last 8–12 minutes total from first hard effort to exhaustion — design the starting wattage and step size with the athlete's fitness level in mind.
  • The gold-standard criterion for true VO2max is a plateau in O2 uptake despite a meaningful increase in power output — not blood lactate cutoffs, not RER thresholds, not maximum heart rate.
  • A VO2max test requires large muscle mass involvement (whole-body leg cycling) — arm cycling or single-limb work will not elicit true VO2max.
  • VO2max peak (highest O2 value seen without a confirmed plateau) can be established as VO2max through repeated verification bouts at progressively higher intensities, but requires multiple tests.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Ramp test step durations of 30, 60, 90, and 120 seconds produced the same VO2max (3.54–3.56 L/min) but final power values of 366, 320, 297, and 282 W respectively — an 84 W spread from protocol alone.
  • Stage durations longer than 2 minutes can cause subjects to fatigue before reaching true VO2max, underestimating it.
  • Athletes with high anaerobic capacity ("sprinters") will show inflated Wmax values in ramp tests because W' covers the anaerobic contribution to the final stage power — their measured "VO2max power" will be substantially above their true aerobic ceiling.
  • Athletes with low anaerobic capacity may test with a Wmax that underrepresents their VO2max relative to peers.
  • Garmin VO2max estimates derived from power data use formulas that do not account for anaerobic contributions to power and should not be treated as direct O2 measurements.
  • VO2max is sport-mode dependent: cyclists will typically show lower VO2max on a bike than during running; cross-country skiers show the highest values because they involve the most total muscle mass.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • There is no such thing as a single "VO2max power" — the power output at which any given individual reaches VO2max is not a fixed quantity; it changes depending on pacing, freshness, muscle mass recruitment, and how much of the effort is covered anaerobically.
  • Andy Coggan's method of looking for a plateau in a pursuit-style effort to find VO2max power has a fundamental flaw: as the effort progresses, progressively larger motor units with more anaerobic capacity are recruited, meaning power never fully represents a purely aerobic output.
  • VO2max is not exercise-mode specific in a problematic way for cyclists — a cyclist's bike VO2max is the relevant number for cycling performance, even if it is 5–10 mL/kg/min lower than their running VO2max.
  • The assumption of a linear relationship between power output and O2 uptake only holds at and below FTP; above FTP, multiple metabolic systems contribute to power in ways that break the linear relationship.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie explicitly states that after investigating this topic in preparation for the episode, he updated his own understanding and no longer treats Wmax or ramp-test final power as a valid VO2max training target.
