Ten Minute Tips 39 Individualizing Training for Crits Cx and Mtb

Ten Minute Tips 19,029 words View transcript

Referenced in

12. Racing & Team Tactics

Core Message

Kolie Moore interviews Erica Clevenger (former pro MTB rider, then Silence WorldTour road team, cyclocross national team member) about individualizing training for criteriums, cyclocross, and mountain biking. A key finding: Erica's physiology required almost no specific high-intensity training for criteriums — consistent hours + threshold/sweet spot work + racing was sufficient for top 5-10 national crit finishes, while most athletes need more sprint-specific prep. The episode stresses that race skills (cornering, positioning, reading the field) can substitute for fitness, and that knowing which "cards" you have changes how you race.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Know your ceiling for effort: if your training prescribes "120% FTP" for 3-min efforts but you can do 150% repeatedly, you're artificially limiting yourself in races — practice maximal, open-ended efforts to learn your true limit
  • For criteriums with a hill: if you're doing 140–150% FTP on every lap and finishing in the front group, your repeatability is high enough that you don't need much extra sprint work — just race
  • Cyclocross prep: start 1 monthly cross practice in August (sub-race-pace hot laps + specific skills: mounts, dismounts, remounts, barriers, off-cambers); race-pace laps start in September
  • Beginners in CX: need more practice sessions to build skills base; experienced riders can reduce to a skills-only session once a week (easy pace, technical focus) and save fitness for races
  • CX number of practices needed: "it depends on how many years you've been racing cross"; 1 season: weekly practice + racing; 3+ seasons: skill maintenance session only
  • For national-level crits: sprint to the front at the start and maintain position; "if you're 60th wheel at Athens coming into a one-line corner, the guy at the front is doing 300 W and you're doing 600 W — you can only do that for so long"
  • After the neutral lap in a crit: immediately begin moving toward the front; "neutral doesn't mean slow, it means together"
  • Pre-nationals crit tip: commit to starting from the front row (stage through all staging areas); "we were second row half an hour early" — front positioning is a trainable logistical skill

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Erica's approach (no sprint work, just volume + threshold + racing) is individual — most riders need sprint-specific sessions; "there are a couple of clients where they can do just steady state riding and be fine to race"
  • MTB racing in the US suffered from lack of development programs; being based in Europe dramatically changes the availability of racing development relative to US-based riders
  • CX tire choice (file treads vs. mid treads) and pressure are race-day tactical decisions; skill sessions need to include the tires you'll race on

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • "There are a couple of clients — and they are rare — where they can do just steady state riding and that helps them the most. And they need like one or two workouts [race-specific]. And then they are ready to race." — Flat rebuttal to the idea that crit racing always requires anaerobic-specific training.
  • Erica went 19th at nationals (elite women's road) on a fitness built primarily from volume and threshold, in a race with all the top pros — the ceiling of threshold-based training is much higher than most assume

Evolving Views

  • Erica evolved from using power zone targets for efforts (wanting to know "what power target do you want me to hit?") to open-ended maximal efforts prescribed by Kolie; realized she was limiting herself by capping her power at prescription numbers — "I can do way more power than I thought"
  • Erica shifted from performing best at her lightest weight (145 lbs at 6'2") to performing best 20 lbs heavier — 9 minutes faster up Mount Greylock, consistent with Cole Tamburri's experience in ep 35 of the same series
