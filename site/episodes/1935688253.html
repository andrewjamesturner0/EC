Ten Minute Tips 42 the Training Implications of Strength As a Skill

Ten Minute Tips 19,199 words View transcript

Referenced in

7. Strength Training

Core Message

Strength is a skill, not just a measure of raw force production, and losing numbers on your squat does not necessarily mean you have gotten weaker. How you define and measure strength determines whether you are "maintaining" it. For cyclists, peak power on the bike is a more relevant measure of strength than barbell squat numbers. The minimum effective dose for maintaining or even increasing strength-as-skill is surprisingly low -- as few as 3-6 sets of 1-5 reps per week -- which means cyclists can maintain meaningful heavy lifting during the season without sacrificing bike training.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Minimum effective dose to increase one-rep max in trained lifters (per Dr. Pack's research): 3-6 sets of 1-5 reps, over 1-3 sessions per week, at approximately 7-9 out of 10 RPE (~80% 1RM)
  • For cyclists, measure strength via peak power on the bike rather than squat numbers; if sprint power is improving, you are maintaining/gaining strength even if squat numbers drop
  • To maintain strength during the season: 3 heavy singles once a week at roughly 7/10 RPE is sufficient
  • For general gym maintenance: a couple sets of 10 at 5-6/10 RPE, a couple times per week
  • Unilateral strength (single leg press, single leg squat) is more cycling-specific than bilateral squats and should probably be prioritized
  • Leg press is more specific to cycling than barbell squats because stability conditions are similar (no balance demands)
  • If your goal is to maximize strength while minimizing hypertrophy: do sets of 1-5, minimize total volume, eliminate eccentric components (e.g., deadlift and drop the bar)
  • Core foundational lifting program for any cyclist: heavy squats or deadlifts plus single leg press
  • Weighted glute bridges are not a substitute for hamstring curls -- they are different movements targeting different muscles (hip extensors vs. knee flexors)
  • Most cyclists should train hamstrings at a minimum level to prevent quad-hamstring imbalance; start with a couple sets per week
  • Body weight exercises for quads: reverse Nordic curls (use padding, start with 1-2 sets per week, build up cautiously)

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Everyone starts at different strength levels due to anthropometrics, neuromuscular recruitment ability, and training history
  • Some people naturally respond better to strength training than others
  • Cyclists should not be maxing out on lifts -- RPE-based loading is preferred over percentage-based one-rep max testing
  • Reverse Nordic curls carry injury risk (broken kneecaps reported); use padding, control depth
  • When combining strength and endurance training, watch for overreaching: if zone 2 rides feel "soul crushing," you have probably overdone it

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Losing kilos on your squat does not mean you are getting weaker -- it means you have lost skill at the squat movement pattern, not necessarily force production capacity
  • Strength training research has individualization figured out far better than endurance sports research does (Kolie)
  • Initial rapid "strength gains" new lifters see are almost entirely neurological/skill-based, not muscular
  • Maintaining body weight is a good proxy for maintaining strength (Kyle, personal experience)
  • Soreness after strength training is most likely explained by the inflammation hypothesis; weight fluctuation of a few pounds post-strength-session is water weight, not muscle gain

Evolving Views

  • Kolie states that reading Dr. Pack's paper on minimum effective training volume "really changed my view of what does maintenance look like for strength"
  • Kolie was "totally wrong" in his prior belief that elite track sprinters could not further improve their neural motor patterns for on-bike force production
  • Kolie notes that on a previous podcast, they "didn't do a good enough job delineating" that the strength-hypertrophy rep range boundary is not a hard line but a continuum
  • Kolie says he is "getting more and more convinced by the day" that the inflammation hypothesis for soreness is the most likely explanation
