Perspectives 3 Usa Crits 2021 with Alex Carmona and Taylor Warren

12. Racing & Team Tactics

Core Message

Alex Carmona and Taylor Warren (CS Velo road captain) debrief a full USA Crits series season, their first extended foray into high-level crit racing after careers focused on road and stage racing. Core message: criterium success is dominated by cornering skill and positioning; fitness matters but is secondary to being able to float in the top 10–15 wheels. At the USA Crit level, teams like Legion control the race speed from lap 5, creating a peloton train system that requires full team coordination to penetrate. Season management across a crit calendar requires building fitness before the season, not during it.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Cornering practice is the #1 crit-specific skill: find a quiet downhill at race speed (30+ mph) and practice corner entry, apex timing, and exit lines repeatedly — "it doesn't matter if you can corner well at 20, you've got to do it at 30"
  • A rider with a 300 W FTP can finish top 5–10 in national-level crits if they can hold top 15 wheels all race — positioning saves more energy than fitness
  • In USA Crits on flat courses, passing someone at 36 mph requires ~1,500 W; you need a substantial sprint to contest pure flat finishes
  • For hilly crits (Athens, Winston-Salem): anaerobic repeatability to do 500–600 W every lap is more important than peak sprint power; smaller/lighter riders favored
  • Intensity distribution across a crit season: ~60–65% of training should be aerobic/endurance riding; let the races supply the high intensity (VO2, anaerobic) — "let the races really be the intensity and kind of fill it in around"
  • Build the "reservoir" of fitness in the off-season: "about 80% of the gains you're gonna make is probably gonna be in the off-season when you're not racing where you can actually do a proper build"
  • Openers for evening crits: morning opener (tempo, some low-threshold efforts, a few high-cadence sprints) works well, especially after travel days; day-before easy spin also effective
  • Tire setup for crits: Taylor ran Vittoria Corsa G2 25mm/latex at 80 front/85 rear (65–67 kg); mid-season switched to Continental GP5000 28mm at ~75 front/80 rear and felt better; Alex ran Pirelli P Zeros 24mm (measured 26–27mm on wide rim) tubeless at 70 front/75 rear (~150 lb body weight)
  • Recovery during crit season: prioritize sleep and nutrition above all; compression/massage are secondary; incorporate 1–2 mid-season break weeks; don't skip entire race weekends but be selective about which ones

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Cornering with people around you (5-wide, accordion effect) is a separate skill from cornering alone; without pack-riding context the skill doesn't fully transfer
  • Radio/earpiece communication in crits is often impractical at high speed; half the time riders pull the earpiece out because they "can barely hear anything"
  • Travel stress and logistics fatigue accumulate significantly; working full-time during a crit series requires treating travel recovery as a training variable
  • Warmup for crits: highly individual; staging for 45 min after a 20-min warmup is counterproductive; some riders (Alex) found warmups mostly arbitrary

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Taylor: "Where you are 10 to go is most likely where you're going to finish plus or minus one or two spots" in UHC/Legion-controlled races; position at lap 10-to-go is the race within the race
  • The "crit beef" / social media drama is primarily theater — in person, the same riders are "pretty chill," respectful of each other, and safety-conscious because they race together every weekend
  • At Athens Twilight (150 starters, 25 finishers), finishing the race is itself a meaningful accomplishment; the attrition separates the result from the preparation

Evolving Views

  • Both Alex and Taylor shifted from thinking road racers can "just pivot" to crit racing toward recognizing that crit racing requires years of accumulated skill building; they each got their teeth kicked in early in the season and only felt competent by the final events.
  • Taylor revised his view on fueling: previously did 40–60 g carb/hr on endurance rides, often starting with no food; moved to 90–100 g/hr deliberately and saw sustained fitness trends upward across the whole season (discussed again in the Perspectives 29 episode).
