4. Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

Core Message

This 100K listens AMA covers a wide range of training questions submitted via Reddit, centering on the relationship between aerobic and anaerobic systems. The core argument is that most cyclists systematically underestimate how aerobic their performance requirements are — even criterium racing, short climbs, and repeated sprint work primarily come down to aerobic capacity. Kolie argues that anaerobic capacity is best thought of as "polish" that comes quickly and goes quickly, while aerobic development takes years and drives long-term performance gains. Several Q&A exchanges develop the idea that TTE extension at FTP is driven by improved fat oxidation, not just glycogen storage, and that VO2 max work (not FTP work) is often the key lever for well-trained athletes.

Key Training Recommendations

  • For athletes who get dropped in criteriums or on punchy climbs: the limiter is usually aerobic power, not anaerobic capacity. The fix is raising FTP (aerobic base), not adding more sprint or anaerobic interval sessions. A 400W FTP rider would not have been taxed anaerobically in the same situation.
  • Stochastic "breakaway-style" tempo rides — varying effort naturally with terrain rather than steady Zone 3 — are preferred over prescribed steady-state Zone 3. These serve as good aerobic workouts (60–90 min) while training repeated accelerations. Kolie attributes substantial cross fitness gains over "2–3 weeks" from this approach.
  • Steady-state Zone 3 is rarely prescribed as focused training; it is assigned only in rest weeks for ~1 hour or less. A pro doing 6 × 90-minute Zone 3 blocks was identified as training sub-optimally.
  • FTP maintenance in non-cycling off-season: do as much riding as possible without compromising the priority sport. Without specific FTP training but with activity, FTP can likely be maintained within 5–10W for roughly 1–2 months before significant decline.
  • For athletes with a high proportion of Type II muscle fibers: watch fatigue more carefully from sprint efforts — they can dig a much deeper hole than the power output suggests. Also, carbohydrate burn from hard efforts is not 1:1 with kilojoules on the power meter; actual carbohydrate cost is higher for high-intensity work.
  • Anaerobic work capacity (AWC): treat it like polish — focus on it only for a few weeks before racing. Doing repeated anaerobic intervals all year without a long aerobic base does not build AWC in the long term; muscle mass and peak power are the real long-term limiters of AWC.
  • TTE extension at FTP: driven in large part by improved fat oxidation at FTP (not purely glycogen storage). Athletes after a good winter base can achieve TTEs of 65–85 minutes. When TTE reaches ~60+ minutes, that is a good time to begin VO2 max work, which will then raise FTP and shorten TTE, beginning the cycle again.
  • FTP training does not increase FTP in well-trained athletes; VO2 max work does. This was offered as the "most controversial training opinion."
  • VO2 max block recovery: expect improvements to appear 40% of the time immediately, 25% of the time within 2–3 weeks of finishing the block; for most athletes full benefits take 1–2 months to materialize. Do not begin a second VO2 max block before full recovery from the first.
  • For combined cycling + marathon training: maintain as much cycling volume as possible without compromising run training; it will not fully preserve FTP but is better than nothing.
  • FTP interval progression: track TTE separately from FTP; progress by adding interval time (e.g., 4×10 → 4×12 → 3×15). Also track sweet spot TTE and over-under progressions as separate parallel tracking variables.
  • For Type II fiber-dominant athletes seeking to maintain endurance during weight-training off-season: ride 6 hours/day on weekends if possible alongside twice-weekly lifting. Separate riding from lifting by time (avoid cycling immediately after lifting to protect hypertrophy via mTOR interference).
  • Sprint development (snap): 10–15 second standing sprints from ~39×16–17, starting at 60–70 rpm, targeting a cadence range of 70–130+ rpm. This trains neuromuscular coordination and can produce a small (~0.1–0.2 m/s) bump in Msmax within 1–2 weeks for untrained sprinters.
  • Pedaling time, not power, is the key stimulus for Zone 2 adaptation. Indoor vs outdoor equivalency is approximately 1:1 when pedaling time is matched. Athletes should minimize coasting on outdoor endurance rides.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • FTP maintenance during time off depends heavily on the individual and age; young athletes sometimes return from 2-week breaks and lose essentially nothing.
  • Zone 3 training may not be inefficient for highly trained athletes whose LT1 is naturally at 80–85% FTP; the zone description becomes inapplicable to them.
  • The "50% more time on the trainer" rule is dismissed as unfounded; the real comparison factor is pedaling time.
  • Coggin's training zones were designed to be descriptive (after the fact) rather than prescriptive (during training).
  • Blood lactate at MLSS (OBLA standard ~4.0 mmol/L) is a population average; individual ranges are roughly 2–8+ mmol/L. Prescribing training from a population-average lactate number is unreliable.
  • Machine learning / auto-coach systems are limited by inability to account for non-training stressors, individual variation in biomarkers, and the fundamental problem that even exhaustive power datasets converge toward Gaussian distributions that miss outliers.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Doing anaerobic intervals all year without aerobic base will not improve you at anaerobic events; "if you do aerobic work really well for 10 years, you'll be a really well-trained endurance athlete. If you do anaerobic work for 10 years without anything else, you're not going to be that good at anything."
  • FTP training alone, for well-trained athletes, does not increase FTP; VO2 max work is the lever.
  • A good winter period of low-intensity endurance training can produce TTEs of 65–85 minutes in the first spring FTP test, which then allows a VO2 max block to push FTP to a new ceiling.
  • Sprint (anaerobic) efforts are not purely anaerobic: a 1300W sprinter still needs to sit down and recover aerobically after 15-second efforts; a 900W "climber" type doing the same sprint workout shows far less aerobic distress, demonstrating that the hole dug is less about fiber type and more about the absolute cellular disturbance from maximum motor unit recruitment.
  • Burning fat at FTP is more common than often assumed; data from ketogenic athletes shows 50–67% of energy at FTP from fat oxidation. The mechanism of FTP does not preclude fat burning.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie evolved away from prescribing steady Zone 3 rides in favor of stochastic tempo and cleaner aerobic/hard efforts.
  • Previous approach emphasized posterior chain strength for cyclists; he now considers this largely bro-science given the limited role of hamstrings in knee-flexed cycling mechanics.
