Core Message

Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) are neither necessary nor sufficient for endurance sport performance, but they can be a useful supplementary tool when data is interpreted by qualified professionals in context. The panel (Namrita Brooke, RD; Tracy Carson, epidemiologist; Fabiano Araujo, MD/PhD) unanimously agrees CGMs do not provide a competitive advantage in racing, that the UCI ban is inconsistently reasoned (heart rate and temperature monitors are permitted), and that the real value of CGMs lies in off-bike 24-hour dietary pattern analysis rather than in-race decision-making. The episode uses Kristen Faulkner's Strade Bianche disqualification as a jumping-off point to discuss broader issues of athlete health, RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport), women's health in sport, and the risks of data fixation without professional guidance.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Do not rely on CGM data alone to guide fueling during races. Interstitial glucose (what CGMs measure) is not blood glucose -- it is an estimate with ~10% variance and a 5-10 minute delay. It does not reflect muscle glycogen stores, gastric emptying rate, or glucose uptake into working muscle (Namrita).
  • Develop a fueling plan with a coach/dietitian before racing rather than reacting to real-time glucose readings. A gel every 15 minutes (as Faulkner reported doing) addresses on-bike fueling, but off-bike nutrition is where most athletes' deficits accumulate (all panelists agree).
  • CGMs are most useful for off-bike, 24-hour dietary pattern analysis: observing how different foods, combinations, and timing affect glucose stability, energy levels, sleep quality, and subjective well-being (Namrita, Fabiano).
  • If using a CGM, work with a qualified professional (sports dietitian, physician) to interpret the data. Without context from food diaries, training data, blood tests, and clinical history, CGM data is "meaningless" (Fabiano).
  • Beware of "glucorexia" -- an obsessive focus on minimizing glucose spikes that leads to disordered eating patterns. Some practitioners are using CGM data to discourage healthy foods like oatmeal based on glucose spike appearance, which is misleading and potentially harmful (Namrita).
  • Highly trained athletes have fewer glucose excursions (deviations from baseline) over 24 hours compared to recreational athletes. This means the same CGM readings may have different implications at different fitness levels (Tracy).
  • A few days of low energy availability can decrease fasting blood glucose, but CGMs have not been validated as a proxy for low energy availability or RED-S. Low glucose is just one of many consequences of under-fueling, and fixing it is not as simple as eating more sugar (Tracy).
  • Under-fueling is extremely common in endurance athletes. Kolie estimates at least half his clients have suffered from chronic insufficient caloric intake at some point, ranging from days to years.
  • Multiple stressors beyond nutrition can disrupt the menstrual cycle: psychological stress, physical training load, and sleep deprivation. CGMs cannot capture or address these factors (Tracy).
  • There is no substitute for good coaching. CGM race data typically just confirms what the athlete already felt (e.g., missed a feed, dropped a bottle). The fueling plan, training plan, and pacing plan should all be developed together beforehand (Namrita).

Caveats & Qualifications

  • CGMs measure interstitial glucose, not blood glucose. There is a ~10% margin and 5-10 minute time delay. During exercise, additional factors (dehydration, heat stress, gastric emptying rate, absorption rate, individual GLUT transporter variability) further distort the relationship between what you eat and what the sensor reads (Namrita).
  • Research on CGMs in endurance sport is extremely limited: only 16 papers on PubMed for "continuous glucose monitoring and endurance training" and only 26 citations for "glycemic control and menstrual cycle" as of recording (Fabiano).
  • Super Sapiens (the CGM brand Faulkner used) was not FDA-approved at time of recording; athletes using it had to be part of a research trial (Tracy).
  • CGM data is siloed in separate platforms (e.g., Super Sapiens app) and does not integrate well with training platforms like Training Peaks, making contextual analysis impractical without significant manual effort (Fabiano).
  • The sensors are expensive and not universally available (some brands only in Europe), creating equity concerns if racing use were permitted (Fabiano).
  • The UCI rule banning metabolic sensors in competition (Article 1.3.006) prohibits the device itself, not just data recording. It allows heart rate, temperature, and sweat rate monitors, which is inconsistent (all panelists note this).
  • Kolie compares CGMs to HRV monitoring: likely to follow a hype cycle where initial enthusiasm gives way to the realization that the data is hard to interpret meaningfully, especially since in well-trained athletes HRV seems to mostly reflect sleep quality rather than training readiness.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • CGMs provide no real-time performance advantage in racing because by the time low glucose is detected (with the interstitial delay), the athlete is already in trouble. Proactive fueling plans are far more effective than reactive glucose monitoring (Namrita, Kolie).
  • The framing of CGMs as essential for women's health in sport is "way oversimplifying a complicated issue." Low energy availability involves far more than blood glucose -- if it were just about glucose, "we would just tell people to eat frosted flakes all day" (Namrita).
  • Power meters provide more physiological insight than CGMs or most other wearable monitors for trained athletes (Kolie).
  • The biggest value of a CGM may be off-bike dietary analysis rather than anything exercise-related. Fabiano found the most useful data came from observing post-workout meal choices and 24-hour glucose patterns, not from in-ride monitoring.
  • Some dietitians are misusing CGM data to scare people away from healthy foods (e.g., "don't eat oatmeal, look at the glucose spike"), which is nutritionally irresponsible and potentially triggers disordered eating (Namrita).
  • The research needed to validate CGMs for athlete health cannot be conducted if the devices are banned in competition -- creating a catch-22 where UCI demands evidence that cannot be gathered under their rules (Tracy).

Evolving Views

  • Kolie states he learned a lot from this conversation and that his initial hot takes became "it depends" after hearing from the panel. He entered the episode somewhat skeptical of CGMs (suspecting marketing hype from Super Sapiens) and came away with a more nuanced view that they have some off-bike utility but are far from essential.
  • Kolie draws an explicit comparison to his earlier position that "there's no such thing as overtraining, there's only under-fueling" -- he now recognizes (informed by the previous episode with Namrita) that both can independently be true, and excessive training volume can outstrip any fueling strategy.
