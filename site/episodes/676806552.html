Tmt2 Homemade Hydration Mix

Referenced in

8. Nutrition & Fueling

Core Message

Kolie Moore reverse-engineered popular science-based hydration drink mixes and provides a DIY recipe using basic chemistry. The key principle is leveraging dual sugar transporters (glucose and fructose) in the gut to maximize water absorption, while keeping the solution hypotonic (around 4-5%). Sodium citrate is used instead of sodium chloride (table salt) because it is far more palatable during exercise. The recipe is customizable based on individual sweat rate, salt loss, taste preferences, and weather conditions.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Keep the drink solution at approximately 4% concentration (4 grams per 100 mL of water); the recipe ends up around 5% depending on salt content
  • Use both dextrose and sucrose to take advantage of multiple gut sugar transporters, which also helps water absorption piggyback on sugar absorption
  • Use sodium citrate instead of sodium chloride (table salt) as the primary sodium source -- it is "absolutely delicious" compared to unpalatable table salt during rides
  • Most people lose 0.5 to 1.5 grams of sodium per liter of sweat
  • Calcium loss is low priority at only 50-100 mg per liter of sweat; potassium can be skipped entirely if desired
  • If you are a salty sweater (caked in salt after rides, sweat burns eyes), consider nearly doubling the sodium citrate amount
  • Replace chloride through solid food (e.g., salted boiled potatoes) rather than trying to put it in the drink
  • In hot weather, increase water to 550-700 mL per serving (vs. the baseline 500 mL) to keep the solution dilute
  • In cold weather, reduce water to 400-450 mL per serving
  • Grind freeze-dried fruit in a food processor while keeping it cold to prevent coating; store dry at room temperature
  • Citric acid should be started at lower amounts and increased gradually; it is easy to overboard on
  • Ascorbic acid (vitamin C) controls the "dryness" of the drink; skip it if you do not like that quality
  • Skip coconut water -- it has too much potassium and not enough sodium for exercise hydration

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Individual sweat salt content varies based on heat adaptation and genetics; salty sweaters will "probably always be pretty salty sweaters"
  • The ingredient source (Modernist Pantry) is explicitly noted as NOT a reputable NSF-tested facility; athletes subject to drug testing should source from WADA-compliant tested suppliers
  • The body "craving what it needs" is presented as a useful but imperfect indicator ("we can't directly rely on this, but it's a pretty good indicator")

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Sodium chloride (table salt) is actually fairly unpalatable during exercise; sodium citrate is a superior form for drink mixes despite being less commonly used in commercial products
  • Coconut water is not a good exercise hydration choice despite its health halo -- it has too much potassium and insufficient sodium
  • Replacing chloride is better done through solid food than through the drink
