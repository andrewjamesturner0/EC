Perspectives 13 Overtraining Sucks with Katie Aman

Perspectives 19,045 words View transcript

Referenced in

12. Racing & Team Tactics

Core Message

Katie Aman (Empirical Cycling coach and former elite road racer) discusses her overtraining experiences in swimming and cycling, providing a first-person account of how relative energy deficiency (RED-S / underfueling) can coexist with strong race results, and how to recognize and recover from overtraining. The episode connects nutrition, weight loss, chronic fatigue, and injury risk as an interconnected system. Coaching tips focus on communication, reading athletes' subjective fatigue signals, and the "less is more" philosophy of interval work.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Endurance pace should be suspiciously easy: Katie was prescribed 174 W as endurance pace when her actual aerobic ceiling was ~130 W — 44 W too high, leading to chronic fatigue and post-ride exhaustion; Kolie revised downward based on direct assessment
  • Do 2–3 hard interval sessions per week maximum; 5–6 per week leads to cumulative fatigue without additional adaptation — "less is more"
  • Hard days hard, easy days truly easy (80 W for Katie on easy days = ~1/3 of threshold); polarized distribution reduces overtraining risk
  • Do not restrict carbohydrates on the bike even when attempting body weight management off the bike; off-bike minor caloric reduction is compatible with performance, on-bike restriction is not
  • During injury recovery: eat freely to support healing; attempting to lose weight during a broken collarbone repair delayed recovery in Remco's reported case — reversed here as a cautionary example
  • When athletes show signs of deep fatigue (high RPE at normal watts, loss of motivation, inability to sustain repeated threshold intervals, soreness/weakness when accelerating from a standstill or going upstairs), pull back before the athlete reports it — these are early-warning signs
  • For women: losing menstrual cycle is a significant RED-S warning sign requiring immediate nutrition and recovery attention, not normalizing

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Katie's overtraining was invisible to external observers and generated positive race results (national podium, stage race GC contention) simultaneously — overtraining does not always mean bad performances immediately
  • Gaining back 10–15 lbs after injury and returning to racing showed 200 W improvement in sprint power (600 → ~800 W) — the weight was suppressing top-end output
  • Swimming training culture (high volume, no power metrics, doubles culture) predisposed her to ignoring fatigue signals; cyclists from swimming backgrounds may carry similar habits

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Performing well while overtrained (Katie won multiple elite races, was 26th at nationals, got 7th at Green Mountain stage race while at a dangerously low bodyweight) — "if you're performing well, you may still be chronically underfueled and overtraining"
  • Forced rest from injury can paradoxically produce the best fitness in years afterward (Kate Courtney 2018, Vanderpoel post-Olympics cited); the recovery debt from prior overtraining is finally paid
  • "Do you really have to go to every local race? Racing every Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday, Wednesday — that's a recipe for fatigue." Voluntary rest from racing is underutilized as a recovery tool

Evolving Views

  • Katie evolved from a "heroic effort" mindset (missing friends' birthdays for swimming, treating every rest as failure) to explicitly recommending "less is more" as a coach — the hardest shift for athletes is giving themselves permission to rest
  • Kolie updated his coaching process to "ask athletes everything — warm-up, food, water, leave no stone unturned" after missing a key underfueling pattern in Ingvar's stage race; this came from coaching feedback in this episode's context
