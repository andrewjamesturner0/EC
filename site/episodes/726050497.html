3. Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing

Core Message

The single governing principle of FTP training is progressive overload, and the most practical and safe form of overload is adding time to intervals, not increasing power. FTP has two dimensions — watts and duration (TTE) — and chasing TTE extension is more reliable than weekly power increments that may not exceed power meter noise. A key study (Billat et al., masters runners) showed that six weeks of FTP-paced intervals, progressively increasing total time from 30 to 60 minutes per session at 2×/week, raised TTE from 44 to 63 minutes and velocity (proxy for watts) — supporting both the approach and the training structure.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Start each FTP training block with an FTP test that gives both watts and TTE. Use TTE to determine starting interval volume.
  • TTE 40–45 min (very common): start at 4×10 min with 3-min rests. Progression: 3×15 → 2×20 → 2×30 → 1×40 → 1×60.
  • TTE 55–65 min: start at 6×10 or 3×15. Progression: 5×12 → 6×12 → reduce rest periods over weeks.
  • Progress session volume from ~30 min total FTP time (week 1) toward 60 min total over a 6-week block, mirroring the Billat study protocol.
  • Going over FTP does NOT improve FTP faster — the aerobic adaptation signals are not enhanced by going over threshold, only by accumulating time at or below it. 3×10 at 105% FTP ≈ 3×10 at 100% FTP, but you hold 100% FTP much longer, so 100% produces more total aerobic stimulus.
  • "Over-unders" or FTP with bursts (10-second surges every 5 minutes) can provide additional metabolic load without requiring a power retest or exceeding TTE. Vary surge frequency or duration to increase load progressively.
  • FTP training raises both FTP wattage and TTE. In the Billat study, TTE went from 44 to 63 minutes (+43%) at a higher absolute pace. VO2max also rose from 66.6 to 68.8 mL/kg/min (+3.3%).
  • Sweet spot training (if used) requires more interval time than FTP intervals to match the adaptation stimulus. If 2×20 at FTP is the baseline, sweet spot might need 2×30 or 2×35 to produce equivalent fatigue and adaptation.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • FTP training does not always raise VO2max, especially in already well-trained athletes. Expect it mainly in those new to structured threshold work.
  • At FTP, carbohydrate use averages ~83% (RER ~0.93) in this population and does not change after training at the same absolute intensity. What changes is that old FTP now feels easier (lower % of new FTP), shifting fat use upward.
  • Programs that stagnate (e.g., standard TrainerRoad plans) typically stall because they never increase total time in zone — they repeat the same intervals indefinitely, providing no progressive overload.
  • TTE improvements can be dramatic in athletes who have never done FTP work; gains become smaller and slower in already-trained athletes.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • "One hour FTP" is not achievable for most amateur cyclists. The coach's observation: most amateur cyclists can hold their FTP for 35–45 minutes before TTE is specifically trained. "One hour power" is therefore not FTP for most people — it is below FTP.
  • Staying at the same power and duration indefinitely is one of the most common reasons FTP progress stalls (same dose, no overload — like staying on the same weight in the gym indefinitely).
