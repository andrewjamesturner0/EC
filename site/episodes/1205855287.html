Ten Minute Tips 18 Metrics Are Not Fitness

Ten Minute Tips 8,852 words View transcript

Referenced in

3. Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing

Core Message

This episode warns against confusing the measurement of fitness (a metric) with actual fitness or performance. Using the 20-minute FTP test or ramp test as a training target rather than a diagnostic tool creates "teaching to the test" — improving the metric without improving the underlying aerobic physiology. A specific example: doing anaerobic capacity work raises ramp test and 20-minute test FTP scores without raising actual aerobic threshold. Similarly, the CP/W' model shows a decline in W' (FRC) when FTP rises — but this does not mean anaerobic power dropped; it is a mathematical artifact of the model.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Train to improve aerobic power (FTP, VO2max), not to improve test scores. Anaerobic capacity work will inflate ramp test results without improving aerobic function.
  • If a W' or FRC metric appears to drop after FTP training, verify actual short-duration power (1–90-sec) before concluding anaerobic capacity declined. In most cases, the drop is a model artifact.
  • To make all parameters go up simultaneously (FTP and anaerobic power), you need both aerobic and anaerobic training — but not at the same time. Periodize them appropriately for the goal event.
  • Prioritize training focus in the planning calendar: aerobic training and heavy strength/sprint work compete for recovery and cannot both be maximized simultaneously.
  • Avoid burning the candle at both ends: 3–4 aerobic sessions/week (sweet spot, FTP, long rides) cannot be effectively combined with 2–3 gym sessions/week without compromising either.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • An athlete whose FTP rose from 330 to 350 W and who also gained an immediate 20 W by going to sea level showed their FRC (W') drop from 17.5 to 13 kJ on the model — but actual 10-second to 90-second power was unchanged. The model's FRC drop was a mathematical consequence of the FTP rising, not a real loss.
  • Pmax and VLA Max are correlated: higher sprint power = higher maximal glycolytic rate. Aerobically trained athletes with moderate Pmax will have lower FRC/VLA Max metrics than sprint-trained athletes, but this reflects muscle fiber composition differences, not a training deficit.
  • Aerobic power gains at sea level after altitude training can inflate FTP test metrics by 20+ W without any training-induced adaptation — altitude return effect.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • High FRC (W') or high W prime is not inherently desirable for criterium racers if it comes at the expense of FTP. A sprinter with FTP 80 W higher than the competition will find that most surges in a criterium are aerobically manageable, making the sprint more powerful.
  • It is possible to "game" the ramp test or 20-minute test significantly (by doing anaerobic capacity work) without any improvement in actual cycling performance.
