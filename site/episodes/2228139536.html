Ten Minute Tips 68 Using Data in Coaching

Ten Minute Tips 14,170 words View transcript

Referenced in

13. Research & Methodology

Core Message

This episode, featuring host Kolie Moore and coach Gediminas (Gideon) Minas, examines how data and scientific literature are actually used in a coaching practice versus how athletes and media often assume they are used. The central thesis is that data is not self-interpreting: you must first have a conceptual question, then use metrics as shortcuts to answer that question faster — never as autonomous decision-makers. Coaching's "logical framework" is surprisingly simple (threshold → VO2max → threshold, plus managing recovery and life stressors), and pressure to appear more scientific than necessary is a widespread problem in training media. The episode also emphasizes that athlete communication and off-bike context (sleep, nutrition, life stress, menstrual cycle) are irreplaceable data that no software metric can substitute.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Lead every data review with a question, not with data; open WKO5 or TrainingPeaks only after formulating what you want to know — "is this person training too hard?" or "is there a minimum dose maintaining threshold fitness?" — then use the relevant metric to answer it.
  • When onboarding a new athlete, check intensity factor (IF) over time as one of the first graphs: a floor of 0.70–0.75 across easy rides signals that easy rides are too hard and easy aerobic gains are available by simply slowing down.
  • Treat athlete feedback (text messages, comments, RPE, menstrual cycle data, illness reports) as primary data on par with power and heart rate; radio silence (no workout uploads, no communication) is itself a data signal warranting check-in.
  • Use power, heart rate, and RPE as the core "holy trinity" of session metrics; more exotic analyses (DFA-alpha1, SMO2, compound score) add little decision-making value for most athletes and require deep understanding of how they are calculated before use.
  • Interpret TSB/form correctly: a positive TSB does not guarantee readiness or mean you can push harder — the fastest way to make TSB positive is to get sick, which clearly does not improve performance.
  • Do not call CTL "fitness" as a label; it is a proxy for training load and can mislead decision-making if conflated with actual fitness.
  • When a client appears to be rebuilding slower (or faster) than expected, look at off-bike fundamentals first before adjusting training: changes to nutrition timing, total caloric intake, and sleep quality typically have larger effects on training response than fine-tuning interval structure.
  • Assign 1–2 months as the minimum trial period before evaluating whether an experimental training change has worked; avoid scrapping a new approach after only a few weeks.
  • If an athlete's FTP stagnates, look at race results and multi-faceted fitness (durability, repeatability, tactical skill) before concluding performance has plateaued; FTP alone is a single metric and can be unchanged while actual race performance improves substantially.
  • Do not create busy work in training plans — adding sprints, complexity, or novel interval formats during recovery or base phases primarily serves the coach's need to appear active rather than the athlete's development.
  • Before introducing advanced modalities (heat training, altitude, durability-specific blocks), confirm that elite-lifestyle fundamentals — consistent sleep, fueling, and stress management — are already in place.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The logical framework for endurance training is very simple (FTP block → VO2max block → FTP block, repeat), and most athletes are disappointed to hear this; coaches should resist pressure to make plans appear more complex or "scientific" than necessary.
  • Scientific literature conclusions are routinely over-interpreted in training media; correlations of r = 0.4 and small effect sizes in emerging concepts like durability mean that media heuristics drawn from those papers often overstate applicability to individual athletes.
  • What works for elite athletes (heat training, altitude, etc.) requires all other aspects of training and lifestyle to already be optimized before the marginal gains are worth pursuing; for most amateurs, the basics remain the largest untapped lever.
  • An athlete who trains purely on "vibes" — no structure, no periodization — can still see substantial improvement, especially if gifted or early in their training career; science-based training is not magic.
  • WKO5's power model requires consistent, high-quality data input to remain valid ("you've got to keep it fed"); metrics produced from sparse or inconsistent data can mislead.
  • Menstrual cycle tracking is valuable for detecting RED-S risk and explaining training variability, but only if there is no hormonal contraceptive confounding the signal; coaches should establish this context early and normalize the conversation.
  • Compound score has limited staying power as a metric because it oversimplifies athlete profiling; FTP, for all its flaws, persists because it is simple and practically useful at scale.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Athletes expect coaching to involve deep biochemical rationale; in practice, what they actually need is a coherent logical framework explaining why this, why now, why not something else — and that framework requires almost no biochemistry to communicate effectively.
  • Most new coaching clients are overtrained when they arrive; the first intervention is often telling them to do nothing (season break), which requires more coaching confidence than any complex interval prescription.
  • Fat-max training (training at the intensity that maximizes fat oxidation) does not improve training stimulus because substrate choice is irrelevant to the mitochondrial biogenesis signaling cascade (PGC-1α pathway); burning more fat during a session is a byproduct, not a driver.
  • "Durability" as a training concept has been investigated for decades under other names (fatigue resistance, etc.); the media explosion around the word "durability" circa 2024–2025 reflects marketing and trend-following more than new science, and much of the media conclusion-drawing outpaces what the small, young literature actually supports.
  • A client switching from single-sided to dual-sided power measurement mid-season may see all their numbers stay the same or appear to drop slightly — not because fitness stalled, but because they had been measuring at half-power; race results are therefore often a more reliable performance indicator than any lab-derived metric.
  • Being science-based is not "cope" for lacking talent; the process of optimization is intrinsically motivating for competitive amateurs hitting time or genetic ceilings, and the training science framework helps channel that motivation productively even when FTP gains stall.
  • Training plans should occasionally include workouts with shaky theoretical foundations if they keep the athlete engaged and riding, especially during base or off-season phases; motivation and consistency dwarf the marginal difference between interval formats.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie references that his early podcast episodes (pre-2016 training plans) were full of "busy work" — sprints during recovery periods, unnecessary complexity — and that he now actively avoids this. He frames it as a maturation of coaching philosophy: putting the client first over appearing busy or smart.
  • He notes that his willingness to communicate statistics (effect sizes, confidence intervals, forest plots) has increased substantially in recent years, partly influenced by the Mass Podcast / Iron Culture's approach to statistical communication; earlier episodes avoided detailed stats as too daunting.
  • He acknowledges that 5–6 years ago he would have been more willing to make strong heuristic recommendations from weak literature ("here's what you should do based on coaching experience, the evidence isn't great but…"); he now more explicitly flags weak evidence as weak even when his coaching intuition aligns with the direction.
