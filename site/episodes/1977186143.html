Ten Minute Tips 46 Pitfalls of Science Interpretation and Communication

13. Research & Methodology

Core Message

Kolie Moore hosts a roundtable with Kyle Helson and coach Rory Porteus examining how sports science is misread, overcommunicated, and misapplied. The core problem is that confident, definitive statements like "the science says" ignore the inherent uncertainty, narrow population scope, and statistical limitations of individual studies. Meta-analyses are far more reliable than single papers, and even well-replicated findings may not apply to any given individual due to high inter-individual variability. The practical upshot for coaches is that the literature should inform a starting point for experimentation, not a mandate — every athlete is an n=1 study, and evidence-based coaching means tracking individual response and adjusting accordingly.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Never apply the phrase "the science says" as though a single or small number of papers constitute settled truth; always reference specific papers and acknowledge the boundaries of their findings.
  • Prefer meta-analyses over individual papers when making training decisions — pooling 5, 10, 20, or 40 studies provides a far stronger statistical basis for a claim than any single result.
  • When evaluating a paper's applicability, look at the participant list first: untrained vs. recreationally trained vs. highly trained vs. elite populations produce wildly different effect sizes, and results from one group should not be assumed to transfer to another.
  • Be especially skeptical of "highly trained" study populations that were actually tested early in their return from off-season — athletes returning from rest are hypersensitive to stimuli, producing effect sizes that will not replicate in truly in-season trained athletes.
  • Use the scientific literature as a starting point for a protocol, then track individual athlete response and adjust; do not lock in because "the science supports it" if the individual is not responding.
  • When introducing a new training method, pre-establish what metric you expect to improve and what a reliable, meaningful change in that metric looks like (accounting for power meter error) before concluding the intervention worked. A 2-watt FTP increase is within measurement error and should not be interpreted as progress.
  • Do not use ramp test results as universal FTP estimates — because FTP as a percentage of VO2max ranges from ~55% to ~90% across individuals, and anaerobic capacity strongly influences ramp test endpoint power, the "75% of peak ramp power" heuristic will be wrong for many athletes. Use athlete-specific data to determine the appropriate percentage.
  • Do not prescribe VO2max intervals at a fixed percentage of FTP (e.g., 115% FTP) without considering individual physiology — athletes with high anaerobic capacity relative to aerobic capacity may need to work at 150–160% FTP to actually stress the VO2max system.
  • Treat polarized training (or any other framework championed in the literature) as a hypothesis to test on the individual, not a universal prescription; if an athlete shows no improvement after 6–12 months of polarized work, abandon or modify it rather than waiting for theoretical gains to appear.
  • When a client asks why they are doing a specific workout, explain that the literature informs your physiological understanding, which combines with coaching experience and individual athlete characteristics to produce the plan — not that any single paper dictates a specific workout.
  • Use the scientific literature primarily to identify what probably does not work, in addition to what might work — null results and mechanistic understanding both narrow the intervention space usefully.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Biological sciences have a p-value publication threshold of ≤0.05 (1-in-20 false positive rate), far below the 5-sigma standard (~1-in-a-million) used in particle physics; exercise physiology results are therefore intrinsically less certain than headlines suggest.
  • An estimated 60–70% of published papers across science are not reproducible; sports/exercise physiology is not exempt, partly due to inherent measurement error from studying living humans.
  • Standard deviations reported in papers assume normal (Gaussian) distributions, which may not hold; linear regressions may fit poorly once data from extreme ends of the population is included.
  • Average results from a study's intervention can be heavily skewed by a small number of hyper-responders, making the mean misleading for predicting individual response.
  • Supplement research funded by or testing products against nothing (rather than an active control) is essentially worthless as evidence; even caffeine — the best-studied ergogenic aid — does not work for everyone and can be actively harmful at high doses.
  • Published intervention studies often use early-season populations returning from off-season rest; results from these populations are not directly applicable to in-season well-trained athletes.
  • "Publish or perish" pressure creates incentives to find something statistically significant; researchers sometimes run many regressions on a dataset and report the one that crosses the p-value threshold — a form of p-hacking that inflates false-positive rates.
  • Clickbait titles and science-influencer content often accurately use "weasel words" in the video itself, but the thumbnail/title implies a certainty not present in the content; viewers who only absorb the headline receive an overclaimed message.
  • World Tour teams and elite coaches jumping on training bandwagons may actually be rational — at that level, any small edge matters and the individual being coached may be a hyper-responder — but this does not mean the intervention will transfer to amateur athletes.
  • Women are substantially underrepresented in most exercise physiology research (the noted exception being RED-S / LEA literature), limiting applicability of findings to female athletes.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The "fallacy of division" is endemic to how training research is communicated: just because Mrs. Smith's class on average likes cake does not mean every student likes cake. A population average tells you nothing reliable about any given individual's response.
  • Showing individual-level data in papers (participant-by-participant responses) would weaken the apparent significance of many published findings — which is precisely why authors often do not include it. Papers that do show individual responses (e.g., Bent Rønnestad's work) are more useful for coaching than those that only report group means.
  • Standard deviations prove there is individual variation in training response; the existence of spread in the data is itself the key coaching finding, not the group mean.
  • The ramp test has no universal conversion factor: because VO2max-to-FTP ratio and anaerobic capacity vary enormously across individuals, any fixed percentage (e.g., 75%) of peak ramp power will systematically over- or under-estimate FTP for large proportions of athletes.
  • Coaching is always n=1 science; every training plan is an experiment, and evidence-based practice means acting on the individual's response data, not defaulting to population-level recommendations.
  • A 2-watt FTP PR is statistically indistinguishable from measurement noise; coaches should tell athletes this rather than celebrating it as confirmed progress.
  • The scientific literature is most reliably useful for understanding what doesn't work or what mechanisms are implausible, rather than for prescribing specific protocols.
  • Science communicators outside academia likely cause more harm than bad actors inside academia — not through malice but through overconfident extrapolation that the original authors would not endorse.
  • Early VO2max off the couch does not predict training response; starting high does not guarantee large gains, and starting low does not preclude large gains.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie notes that in this episode (Tip 46) he is returning to themes first addressed in much earlier tips (Tip 3 on bro science, Tip 10 on reading papers), indicating that these themes have persisted as central concerns throughout the show's run. His framing has matured: he now explicitly cites "the fallacy of division" and engages with reproducibility crisis data (~60–70% non-reproducible), which were not part of the earlier episodes' vocabulary.
  • Kolie acknowledges that he has occasionally over-interpreted study findings himself ("by maybe five or ten percent occasionally") and that in past episodes he was "more bullish" in his interpretations than he now believes was warranted — suggesting a general shift toward more epistemic caution over the podcast's lifespan.
  • Rory (coach, former academic) states he no longer works in academia (~"two thirds of people would tell you, I'm not going to academia") and frames his scientific background as past-tense; his current view is that evidence-based coaching is fundamentally experimental and individual-focused, not literature-driven.
