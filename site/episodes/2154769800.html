Perspectives 40 Energy Expenditure and Compensation with Eric Trexler

Perspectives 20,944 words View transcript

Referenced in

8. Nutrition & Fueling

Core Message

Human energy expenditure is dynamic and adaptable, not a simple fixed equation. The body compensates for both increased exercise and caloric restriction through adaptive mechanisms that shrink energy deficits or surpluses over time — a concept formalized as the "constrained energy expenditure model." For endurance athletes, this means that naive calorie math (BMR + exercise kilojoules = total expenditure) is unreliable, and the best practical method for estimating energy needs is a 2-4 week empirical tracking approach using daily weight and intake data. Weight manipulation in cycling should follow a "get in, get out" philosophy similar to bodybuilding contest prep, rather than the common cycling practice of staying excessively lean for extended periods.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Use a 2-4 week "energy balance method" to estimate total daily energy expenditure: hold training load and caloric intake steady, weigh yourself every morning in standardized conditions, and calculate the slope of body weight change to infer actual expenditure. This is far more reliable than BMR calculators plus power meter kilojoules.
  • Weigh yourself daily (not just start/end) to smooth out water weight fluctuations and get a reliable body weight trend line.
  • If weight is stable at a given caloric intake, that intake approximates your total daily energy expenditure — but check for compensatory symptoms (low energy, low libido, feeling cold, sleep disturbance, amenorrhea, low T3) which indicate you may be weight-stable only because your body has downregulated expenditure.
  • When dieting, expect weight loss to plateau as energy expenditure adapts downward. Plan to periodically recalculate your expenditure using the energy balance method and adjust caloric intake accordingly.
  • Include resistance training (barbell, dumbbell, machine, or resistance band) during any weight loss phase. The best predictor of weight regain after a diet is how much fat-free mass was lost (Trexler, guest).
  • For amateur cyclists, do the bulk of weight loss in the off-season when training intensity demands are lower, not during high-intensity in-season training.
  • For professional cyclists, adopt a "get in, get out" approach to competition weight: reach race weight briefly for key events, then return to a higher sustainable baseline. Do not try to hold race weight for extended periods.
  • When refeeding after a period of under-eating, expect the body to preferentially regain fat first, then fat-free mass. Ravenous appetite typically does not subside until fat-free mass is fully restored. Allow this process rather than fighting it.
  • For ultra-endurance events (multi-day races), accept that you cannot fully fuel the effort in real time — you are "borrowing against future energy stores" and everyone loses weight. Focus on maximizing intake rather than trying to achieve energy balance during the event.
  • Maximum sustainable caloric intake appears to top out at roughly 2.5x basal metabolic rate (some elite athletes may reach ~3x BMR). Use this as a planning ceiling for long-duration fueling strategies.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The 2.5x BMR "ceiling" on sustainable energy expenditure/intake is a population-level observation with biological variation; some elite athletes may push to ~3x BMR, possibly because this capacity is part of what makes them elite (Trexler).
  • Non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT) is essentially impossible to measure directly — it is always calculated as the residual after subtracting other components, making it the "dark matter" of energy expenditure research with large error bars (Trexler).
  • Power meter-based calorie estimates have inherent error because exercise energy expenditure is a metabolic process that cannot be perfectly inferred from external work output (Trexler).
  • BMR prediction equations introduce error; if BMR is measured shortly after exercise, it may be inflated by residual metabolic effects lasting into the next day (Trexler).
  • Wearable devices and consumer gadgets are not accurate enough for energy expenditure estimation — "none of them are where they need to be in terms of precision and accuracy" (Trexler).
  • Low thyroid hormones, disrupted menstrual cycles, or other metabolic adaptations during heavy training are "not necessarily a problem until it's a problem" — it is a matter of magnitude, clinical relevance, and whether performance goals are being met (Trexler). Do not automatically pathologize these adaptations.
  • The energy balance method requires honest, diligent food tracking for 2-4 weeks, which many athletes resist because they want immediate answers.
  • Weight regain dynamics differ dramatically depending on starting context (e.g., obese individual losing weight vs. already-lean athlete cutting further). The Minnesota starvation experiment results should not be directly extrapolated to typical athletic weight cycling.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • You can be weight-stable and still experiencing significant energy compensation — weight stability does not mean you are adequately fueled. A cyclist maintaining weight on 3,400 kcal/day may actually need 3,700-3,800 kcal/day to function optimally if their body has downregulated via compensatory mechanisms (Kolie/Trexler).
  • The day after an ultra-endurance event, even while highly sedentary, energy expenditure can reach 3x BMR — the metabolic cost of repair is far higher than most people estimate (Kolie, citing recent paper).
  • The common cycling practice of maintaining very low body weight for entire seasons is more problematic than the bodybuilding approach of brief, targeted cuts — cycling culture lacks the "get in, get out" concept that bodybuilding has developed (Kolie).
  • Dramatically increasing exercise volume does not proportionally increase total daily energy expenditure — the body compensates by reducing expenditure in other compartments, shrinking the expected energy deficit (Trexler, constrained energy expenditure model).
  • Changes in mechanical/exercise efficiency at very low power outputs have been observed but largely disappear at training-relevant intensities — do not assume your body has become "more efficient" at hard efforts just because you have been training a lot (Trexler).
  • Some athletes who are "stuck" at a given weight may simply need to eat more, not less. Increasing calories from 3,400 to 3,600 kcal/day can result in no weight gain but restoration of thyroid function, return of menstrual cycle, and improved well-being (Trexler/Kolie).

Evolving Views

  • Kolie mentions that his fascination with bodybuilding literature (MASS Research Review, Iron Culture podcast) has been "incredibly helpful" to him as a cycling coach, suggesting he has recently incorporated bodybuilding-style weight management principles (get in/get out, resistance training during cuts) into his cycling coaching approach — this represents a cross-pollination of ideas he appears to have adopted relatively recently.
