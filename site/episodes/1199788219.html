4. Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

Core Message

The episode argues that the "sweet spot vs. polarized" debate is a false commercial dichotomy (kayfabe) that obscures more useful thinking about training. Both are merely tools, and starting from a training modality rather than from an athlete's specific limiters and desired adaptations is backwards. The hosts advocate for thinking first about what physiological quality needs development (VO2max, threshold, endurance, anaerobic capacity), then selecting the methods that develop it. Sweet spot training's primary benefit is its favorable stimulus-to-fatigue ratio and ease of measuring progress via FTP. The episode also argues that sweet spot is essentially threshold training, meaning the large body of lactate threshold literature fully applies to it even though it's not labeled "sweet spot" in research.

Key Training Recommendations

  • For time-crunched athletes (8 hours/week or less), increasing intensity (up to sweet spot / threshold) is necessary to elicit sufficient adaptation, since easy volume alone will be insufficient.
  • Sweet spot training becomes increasingly demanding at higher fitness levels: once you can do 3x20 comfortably, you must progress to 3x30, 3x40, or 2x40+ to continue gaining.
  • For threshold/sweet spot work, target an RPE of 7–8: if you're "gritting your teeth and really digging deep," you're at a 9 and that's too hard for this intensity domain. Do not white-knuckle the last 3 minutes.
  • Progress sweet spot intervals asymmetrically if needed: a 25-minute + 20-minute session is a valid step before 25+25, then 30+25, etc.
  • Time-in-zone must be accumulated within a single session or at minimum within a single ride; splitting it between a morning session and an afternoon session does not count as the same stimulus.
  • For VO2max work, the stimulus is the intensity, not the duration. Aim for 15–20 total interval minutes per session (12–15 to start); going beyond ~23 total minutes is unusual. Individual intervals can range from 2 to 8 minutes depending on athlete.
  • Athletes with VO2max in the mid-high 90s ml/kg/min may need more endurance training, not more VO2max training.
  • Hard workouts: target 7–8 RPE for threshold/sweet spot, 9–9.5 for VO2max-type efforts, essentially never a true 10. Limit hard days to 2–3 per week (rarely 4), with genuinely easy recovery between.
  • Do not pre-plan training zone distributions (e.g., "80/20" or "all sweet spot"). Ask instead: how many hard days per week can I recover from, and how hard should they be?
  • Polarized approaches are most relevant for athletes who can sustain very high training volumes with proper easy riding between hard sessions.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • "80/20" polarized distribution may mean only 4 hard sessions in a 20-session block, which is not much for advanced athletes who may need more specific stimulus.
  • Sweet spot training does produce real metabolic fitness: it increases the sustainable metabolic load (threshold) and extends TTE, with some athletes able to hold threshold power for 80+ minutes after long-term training combining sweet spot and endurance.
  • Both sweet spot and polarized training approaches are subject to confirmation bias. If someone burns out on sweet spot and rests, the perceived improvement from "switching to polarized" may be mostly the rest.
  • Testing frequently (e.g., every 4–8 weeks) is already over-testing by most standards; rely on modeled FTP data (e.g., WKO5) and RPE cues rather than constant testing.
  • Many people set their sweet spot power too high because their FTP is overestimated, making what they call "sweet spot" actually threshold.
  • Measurement bias: 5-minute power includes a significant anaerobic contribution and is a poor proxy for VO2max gains; FTP/TTE are more reliable progress metrics for threshold-focused training.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • "Sweet spot vs. polarized" is a commercial construct ("kayfabe") — the debate is more useful for selling training plans than for guiding individual training.
  • Sweet spot training is basically threshold training; the absence of a "sweet spot" label in peer-reviewed literature does not mean there is no scientific evidence for it — it's all the LT/LT2/MLSS literature.
  • The mechanism of progressive overload differs by training domain: for VO2max work it's intensity (like weight training stimulus = load), not time-in-zone; for sweet spot/threshold it's extending time-in-zone.
  • An individual workout rarely matters; what matters is the mechanism of progressive overload and whether you are consistently embracing it.
