13. Research & Methodology

Core Message

This episode examines the scientific philosophy behind N=1 self-experimentation in training, using two research papers as a framework: one crossover study on sprint vs. endurance interval training variability, and one conceptual paper on intra-individual variation. The hosts argue that what looks like "individual response differences" to training is largely explained by three confounding noise sources: measurement error, biological error (off-bike life factors like stress and sleep), and true biological variation. They conclude that most apparent "non-responders" are actually experiencing dose standardization problems, recovery deficits, or inappropriate outcome measures — not a fundamentally different physiology. The episode then provides actionable guidance on how to run a more rigorous personal training experiment by controlling dose, measuring performance (not proxies), tracking progress during training, and contextualizing results against one's own history.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Control your training dose precisely: understand whether intensity or duration is the primary driver of the stimulus you are targeting, and keep that variable stable across sessions so you can detect progress.
  • Conduct ongoing within-training performance checks instead of relying solely on infrequent formal tests — "testing is training and training is testing." Rather than flying blind for 4–8 weeks and then testing, watch your capacity trend during the block itself.
  • For threshold-type training, track time-to-exhaustion (TTE) or duration at a fixed power as the primary progress metric; for VO2max and sprint work, track the intensity (power output) achievable over the prescribed duration.
  • Measure actual performance, not performance proxies. TSS, normalized power, critical power model estimates, and lab tests are only correlated with performance — they are not performance. Use outputs that directly reflect your goal (e.g., time up a target climb, power in a target race-specific effort).
  • If you use lab tests, know the protocol details and how they may over- or underestimate your performance; recognize that typical metabolic cart measurement error is larger than a well-calibrated power meter's ~2% error.
  • Pick performance benchmarks appropriate to your actual goal: fresh 5-minute power is only a moderate proxy for end-of-race 5-minute efforts; if racing off-road, test off-road, because road power does not directly predict dirt power.
  • Understand your own day-to-day variation by observing how much your performance fluctuates under consistent conditions; if FTP varies by more than ~5–10 W across comparable sessions, chronic fatigue or under-recovery is the more likely explanation, not a shifting FTP.
  • When comparing sprint/high-intensity efforts in training, compare them to your recent training baseline rather than your all-time best — a best-ever 1-minute power of 600 W recorded fresh after taper is not the right reference for a training day number.
  • Contextualize any apparent non-response against where you are in the long-term training arc: if you already responded well earlier in the season or in prior years, you may simply be further along a diminishing-returns curve, not a non-responder.
  • If progress stalls, diagnose by exclusion: systematically assess training dose sufficiency, recovery quality, and off-bike stressors before concluding the training plan is ineffective.
  • When in doubt about whether you are recovering adequately, err on the side of more rest rather than more stimulus, especially for highly trained athletes.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The crossover study discussed (Bonafiglia et al.) used recreationally active adults (<3 hours/week of activity), so its individual variability findings may not translate directly to trained cyclists.
  • The sprint interval protocol (8×20 s at 170% VO2 peak, 10 s rest) is a fixed absolute percentage of VO2 peak. For athletes with high anaerobic capacity, this is not genuinely hard sprint-interval training; the RPE and resulting adaptation will differ, confounding individual response conclusions.
  • Kolie notes that the RPE during SIT was significantly related to VO2 peak change (r=0.5, p<0.05), suggesting the standardized protocol did not deliver equivalent stimulus across participants — a key limitation when inferring individual response.
  • The Robinson et al. strength training preprint (cited as under peer review at time of recording) used a novel replicated within-participant unilateral leg-press design; despite cleaner methodology, the authors themselves concluded they could not find irrefutable evidence of true inter-individual response variability, only that higher volume generally produced greater gains.
  • The Robinson design (left leg vs. right leg randomized independently) is difficult to replicate in cycling since cardiovascular adaptations are central and you cannot use two separate legs as independent "subjects."
  • Measurement error from lab metabolic carts is larger than from a power meter; small reported VO2max changes of 1.5–3 mL/kg/min in published studies may fall within the noise floor, especially with small n.
  • Kolie's argument that "all differences are dose in disguise" (quoting Robinson on resistance training) is offered as his coaching opinion and inference, not a formally tested claim.
  • Off-bike stress that is chronic and accumulating (not just acute day-to-day variation) can chronically suppress training response and be misread as a non-response to the training program itself.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Kolie argues there is effectively no such thing as a true unique responder who responds only to sprint training but not endurance training (or vice versa). He believes the physiological signals are universal enough across humans that apparent individual response differences are almost always explained by dosing problems, recovery deficits, or inappropriate outcome measures rather than fundamentally different physiology.
  • Metabolic cart VO2max tests have larger measurement error than a calibrated power meter (~2%), which undermines reliance on small VO2max changes as evidence of training superiority between protocols.
  • Breaking out individual participant data from a group study is mathematically equivalent to reducing statistical power to N=1 — that is, it makes each person's data as statistically unreliable as a single case study or anecdote.
  • "Biological error" (off-bike life stressors) can be thought of as a chronic confounder, not just day-to-day noise: sustained work stress during a training block can suppress training response even if the program would have worked under normal circumstances, and this will not appear in published papers.
  • Kolie suggests that some amateurs confuse gifted physiology (in teammates or training partners) for greater work ethic, and that personal response magnitude to training is partially genetically constrained regardless of effort — though everyone does respond to training.

Evolving Views

  • None identified in this episode, though Kolie frames the episode as putting formal scientific language around principles he says he has coached by "subconsciously" for years, suggesting refinement rather than a reversal of prior views.
