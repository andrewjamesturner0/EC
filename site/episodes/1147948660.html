7. Strength Training

Core Message

Kolie Moore and Kyle Helson provide a comprehensive beginner's guide to strength training for cyclists. The episode covers why cyclists should lift (on-bike comfort, bone density, longevity, muscle mass preservation with aging, mobility), how to get started (find a coach or use video resources, record yourself, start light), nutrition considerations (prioritize real food over supplements, BCAAs are unnecessary), load progression, managing soreness, rest between sets, deload weeks, integrating lifting with cycling, and the role of functional exercises. The overarching message is that strength training supplements cycling — it's not the sport itself — so ego-driven heavy lifting and chasing soreness are counterproductive.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Start with 1-2x/week with very light weights; increase load only after soreness subsides and movement patterns feel solid (may take 2-3 sessions)
  • Record your last set of each exercise — that's when technique is most likely to break down
  • Focus on one technique cue per set, not a laundry list of corrections
  • If you can't hit full depth with good form, stop the set — a poorly executed rep doesn't count
  • Going deeper with less weight provides approximately the same stimulus as going shallower with more weight, with less injury risk
  • Rest between sets as long as needed to feel ready — often 5-15 minutes for heavy sets; do not use arbitrary rest timers
  • For hypertrophy (e.g., to improve anaerobic capacity), any load between ~30-80% 1RM taken near failure produces similar muscle growth
  • Initial weeks: higher reps (8-12+), lower load to embed movement patterns before progressing to heavier weights
  • Take deload weeks: reduce to ~half the working weight, 2-3 sets of 2-5 reps — enough to maintain movement patterns without accumulating fatigue
  • Manage soreness with gentle movement: walking, easy spinning (10-20 min on rollers), light stretching — soreness ≠ fatigue
  • For double days (ride + lift): ride in the morning, wait 3-4 hours, take a 30-min nap, eat substantially, then lift. Don't eat a large meal right before lifting (1.5-2 hours gap).
  • Don't just add lifting on top of an existing full training plan — something has to give; reduce ride volume/intensity when lifting 2-3x/week
  • Keep rides at or below tempo intensity when lifting heavy 2-3x/week
  • Lifting depletes glycogen significantly — eat more than you think you need for recovery
  • Heavy compound lifts first in the session, functional/accessory work last
  • Recommended resources: Squat University (Aaron Horshig), Mike Israetel's form videos, Mike Boyle's "New Functional Training for Sports", Ryan Flaherty's "Seven Way Hips" exercise

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Standard 1RM estimation tables (from Soviet weightlifting data) are unreliable for endurance athletes — endurance athletes can typically do more reps at a given % of 1RM than the tables predict
  • Soreness does not map 1:1 to fatigue — you can often ride when sore, but pay attention to when you genuinely can't perform
  • Functional exercises (pistol squats, etc.) are fine for general health/enjoyment but shouldn't be expected to directly improve cycling-specific power
  • Connective tissue (tendons, ligaments, cartilage) recovers slower than muscle due to lower blood supply — account for this in programming

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • BCAAs (branched chain amino acids) are unnecessary supplements for most athletes — leucine, isoleucine, and valine are abundant in whey protein and yogurt; just eat food first
  • Protein alone does not build muscle — you need the training stimulus first, then the protein to support adaptation (analogy: B vitamins don't improve VO2max)
  • Cyclists should rarely if ever attempt true 1RM testing — it's unnecessary neural stress for a non-lifting sport
  • Endurance athletes often can do their "1RM" for 2 reps but fail with just 5 more pounds added — the rep-max tables don't apply to them
  • A few sets of heavy squats can deplete roughly half your muscle glycogen — strength sessions are more metabolically costly than most cyclists assume
  • The Golgi tendon organ acts as a safety cutoff — if you feel your muscles suddenly "shut off" under load, back off immediately; this is a protective mechanism against tendon/connective tissue damage
  • Adding muscle mass via strength training may be the key to improving 1-minute anaerobic power when on-bike training has stalled

Evolving Views

  • Kolie mentions that the idea of doing low-intensity base training in the off-season being "for rest" started as a joke on an earlier episode ("What is Base Really?") but has become about 50% serious in hindsight — suggesting their views on off-season intensity have softened over time.
