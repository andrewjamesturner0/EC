Ten Minute Tips 34 Intermediate and Advanced Strength Training Mistakes and Solutions

Referenced in

7. Strength Training

Core Message

This is part two of a series on common strength training mistakes cyclists make. Due to a severe transcription error, only the first topic is preserved: the mistake of thinking you are not "strong enough" or "serious enough" to use proper lifting equipment (straps, belts, lifting shoes). The hosts emphasize that these accessories improve training quality and safety for lifters at all levels, including cyclists who only lift part of the year.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Use lifting straps for exercises like deadlifts, rows, lunges, and lat pulldowns -- even at light weights -- to remove grip as a limiting factor and improve neural drive to the target muscles
  • If you want to train grip, do dedicated grip work rather than letting grip limit your back or leg exercises
  • Use a lifting belt to increase intra-abdominal pressure and help stabilize your spine; it does not do the work for you but helps you brace harder
  • Consider lifting shoes with a raised heel (e.g., 15-21mm heel-to-toe drop) to increase squat depth and range of motion
  • Higher-heeled lifting shoes also help you stay more upright during squats, reducing stress on the lower back (Kyle went from 15mm to 21mm heel drop and immediately achieved deeper squats)
  • Hard-soled shoes are important for lifting -- avoid soft/squishy shoes

Caveats & Qualifications

  • You do not need to spend hundreds of dollars on premium equipment; basic functional versions of straps, belts, and hard-soled shoes are sufficient
  • When switching to higher-heeled shoes, you may need to reduce weight initially as you adjust to deeper range of motion

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Thinking about grip during pulling exercises "snaps neural drive" and causes extra fatigue -- a reason to use straps even at light weights
  • The idea that "you didn't earn the weight if you used a belt" is called "absolute bullshit" (Kolie); a belt simply helps you brace harder, unlike a powerlifting squat suit which is "the equivalent of riding an e-bike in a race"

Evolving Views
