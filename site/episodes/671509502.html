2. VO2max & Oxygen Uptake

Core Message

This episode analyzes the 1988 Coyle & Coggan paper "Determinants of Endurance in Well-Trained Cyclists" to establish the relationship between FTP (expressed as a percentage of VO2max) and endurance performance. The key insight is that two athletes with identical VO2max values can have dramatically different endurance performance based solely on where their FTP sits relative to VO2max. Athletes whose FTP is a higher percentage of VO2max use far less glycogen at any given absolute intensity and can sustain effort much longer. The episode also argues that studies assigning training or testing intensities as fixed percentages of VO2max are deeply flawed if they don't account for where each individual's FTP sits relative to that percentage.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Prioritize raising FTP as a percentage of VO2max rather than chasing raw VO2max numbers for endurance performance improvement — an athlete with 85% FTP/VO2max will typically outperform an athlete with a higher VO2max but lower percentage.
  • When designing racing strategy, account for the fact that surges above FTP rapidly deplete glycogen; an athlete whose FTP is close to race-relevant power demands will be far more economical than one who spends significant time above their threshold.
  • Use FTP (not VO2max percentage) as your primary training anchor for endurance work — assigning training at a fixed percentage of VO2max will produce very different metabolic states across athletes, making it an unreliable prescription method.
  • Treat VO2max as a ceiling that sets the upper limit for FTP — it is necessary but not sufficient for endurance performance.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The Coyle study used 14 male well-trained cyclists matched for absolute VO2max (in liters/minute), so body-weight-relative VO2max was also similar between groups — findings may not fully generalize to mixed populations.
  • FTP as a percentage of VO2max ranged from 59% to 86% even within an endurance-trained population, showing wide individual variation.
  • The study's test at 88% VO2max produced wildly different experiences: the athlete at 86% FTP/VO2max lasted 75 minutes; the athlete at 66.7% FTP/VO2max lasted only 12 minutes with a blood lactate of 18.7 mmol/L.
  • At a 30-minute test at 80% VO2max, glycogen use in the low-FTP group (60–67% FTP/VO2max) was 65.4 mmol/kg, more than double the high group's 27.9 mmol/kg — a practically enormous difference in fueling demands.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • VO2max is not the best indicator of endurance performance beyond ~5–8 minutes — FTP is a better predictor because it defines what fraction of aerobic capacity can be maintained without progressive fatigue accumulation.
  • Studies that assign exercise at fixed percentages of VO2max without accounting for individual lactate threshold position can produce completely different physiological states in different subjects — making their conclusions suspect.
  • At FTP, athletes were burning approximately 50% carbohydrate and 50% fat (RER ~0.81–0.86), not the pure-carbohydrate metabolism many assume at threshold.
  • VO2max is best understood as a ceiling for FTP, not as a direct performance metric.
