4. Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

Core Message

This myths episode addresses several common training volume misconceptions. Myth 1: you can overtrain on volume but not intensity — false; you can overtrain on intensity at surprisingly low volumes if riding hard on every session. Myth 2: women cannot do as much training volume as men — false; Kolie has never coached a woman who couldn't match or exceed men's volume capacity, and the apparent gap is due to shorter race distances and second jobs, not physiology. Myth 3: you can replace high volume with high intensity — 90% myth, 10% true; works to a limited degree at sub-threshold intensities but definitely not at or above threshold. Myth 4: just spinning easy is junk miles — false; all easy aerobic riding provides central adaptations (plasma volume, cardiac output) regardless of intensity; what's actually "junk" is semi-hard, unfocused riding that creates fatigue without targeted adaptation. Myth 5: you don't need to train a ton of hours to be fast — has the largest grain of truth (genetics and training history are huge), but for most athletes, volume is the primary long-term driver of aerobic performance.

Key Training Recommendations

  • If prone to overtraining on volume, the most common cause is riding endurance too hard (e.g., because LT1 was assigned at 2 mmol/L, placing it at low sweet spot). Fix: ride endurance pace genuinely easy, passing the talk test. Athletes should feel like they're "pedaling but not really doing any work."
  • A new client's first camp/block experience should be confirmatory: if an athlete does a 20-hour easy week and reports no soreness and feeling great, the diagnosis is that they had been riding their endurance too hard all along — the solution is to ride genuinely easier.
  • For high-volume blocks or camps: keep intensity very low ("suspiciously easy"). Eating and recovering adequately allows volume accumulation without overtraining.
  • Commuting by bike at low intensity (try-not-to-sweat intensity) is a legitimate endurance stimulus and can meaningfully add to weekly training volume. Pandemic data: athletes who stopped bike commuting lost ~10 W FTP.
  • Two-a-days are a valid volume-accumulation strategy; if an athlete can ride AM and PM, the accumulated contractions produce aerobic adaptation.
  • Hiking, walking, and other low-impact aerobic cross-training provides central (cardiovascular) adaptations that transfer to cycling; not an exact replacement but meaningfully complementary.
  • For athletes where FTP is plateaued: the solution may not be more training. Consider whether race experience, pack skills, repeated-effort capacity, or sprint improvement would produce better outcomes.
  • If doing a VO2max block with high volume simultaneously, keep endurance intensity very low and manage nutrition carefully (high energy demands at 20–30 h/week + VO2max intervals).

Caveats & Qualifications

  • "You can overtrain on volume but not intensity" as a myth has a grain of truth: many people overtrain on volume because they accidentally ride their endurance too hard, making every session moderate-intensity; they don't experience this with pure HIT because they'd never sustain 5 days/week of maximal VO2max work.
  • Intensity substitution for volume: can work for sub-threshold zones (e.g., 4-hour endurance ride → 2-hour slightly higher endurance ride is an imperfect but acceptable substitution). Definitively does not work for converting long endurance rides into over-threshold interval sessions — different stimuli, disproportionately high fatigue.
  • Scientific literature on HIT vs. volume training typically uses subjects who are in a state of readiness for noob gains, which inflates the apparent efficacy of HIT and makes results hard to generalize to well-trained athletes.
  • The "genetics" caveat for low-volume fast athletes: some are just gifted. Some are coasting on past training history. Some are highly experienced tactically. Don't assume their training is the differentiating factor.
  • Even with very favorable genetics, training adaptation rate and magnitude still vary significantly — some gifted athletes respond explosively to any stimulus; coaching these athletes requires care not to overload them.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • "Going kind of hard all the time" — not committing to easy or hard — is the closest thing to actual junk miles: enough fatigue to accumulate, not enough stimulus to adapt meaningfully.
  • Sprinting from stoplights during commutes is close to junk in the purest sense: stimulus-to-fatigue ratio of near zero.
  • Women's apparent training volume limitations are almost entirely explained by shorter race distances and second-job demands rather than physiological capacity — the physiology appears equivalent.
  • The "high volume" training done by elite women (e.g., 30–35 h/week off-season for Van Vluten) is consistent with physiological parity, not an outlier.
  • A sedentary VO2max in the high 60s (vs. population average ~40 for men) means some athletes "start halfway to home plate" — their training load is not the primary differentiator in their performance.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie's position on the necessity of volume has hardened over time: he is more confident than in earlier episodes that year-on-year improvement at training volumes below ~14–15 h/week is rare for most athletes, and that the typical response to stalled FTP should be increased volume (when feasible), not more intensity.
  • He flags that the question of whether women can do as much volume as men is one he has answered empirically through coaching (they can and often do more) rather than from the literature, and that historical race calendar differences are the confounding variable.
