Watts Doc 55 the Right Vo2max Training and 3015s Epilogue

Watts Doc 22,549 words View transcript

Core Message

This episode is an explicit retrospective and correction of Kolie's earlier Watts Doc VO2max series (episodes 23-24), where he argues his recommendations were taken too literally and too prescriptively. There is no single "right" way to do VO2max training; the optimal approach depends on training status, time in season, goals, and what system (central aerobic vs. anaerobic capacity) you are targeting. High-cadence steady intervals, intermittent efforts (30-30s, 30-15s), hill climb repeats, sprint intervals, and even threshold and endurance training all improve VO2max under the right circumstances. The episode also re-analyzes the 30-15s paper (Rønnestad et al.) and a low-cadence training study, concluding both showed no statistically significant VO2max advantage over steady efforts, while the apparent "superior performance" in the 30-15s group is most parsimoniously explained by anaerobic capacity gains rather than VO2max gains. Recovery is emphasized as the primary constraint before increasing training dose.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Use high-cadence VO2max intervals (approximately 110 RPM or higher) to reduce leg fatigue and allow more hard efforts — either double days or multi-day blocks — while achieving equivalent or greater aerobic stimulus; this is particularly valuable when you want to cluster stimulus with less muscular damage.
  • Do not anchor VO2max intervals to a specific "VO2max power" (e.g., a fixed % of FTP); instead, go hard enough that you are breathing hard and your heart rate is elevated — the target is the physiological state, not the watt number.
  • Start VO2max efforts at approximately 10-20% harder than you think you can sustain (not a true sprint start), then let power fade while holding the same perceived effort; this teaches pacing and ensures a genuine max effort without the extreme fatigue of a kilo-style sprint start.
  • Rory's preferred pacing approach: start 10-20W below target, ramp through the interval, and finish strong — reaching target watts by mid-effort and pushing harder in the final portion; he finds this particularly helpful mentally for high-cadence work.
  • For your first VO2max workout of the season, begin the first interval at only ~10-20W above FTP, assess, and work up from there; avoid pinning maximum intensity on the first session back.
  • Perform high-cadence VO2max intervals on steady terrain (flat, consistent grade, or trainer) to maintain rhythm; undulating gradients cause cadence spikes and crashes that destroy interval quality.
  • Use longer total interval time — up to 30 minutes of interval work per session — when specifically targeting aerobic capacity improvements, recognizing this is harder to sustain; 20 minutes is a practical coaching target, not an upper ceiling.
  • Match the VO2max tool to the training context: (a) early season or short timeline → intermittent efforts (30-30s, 30-15s, 40-20s) to simultaneously build VO2max and anaerobic capacity; (b) well-trained athletes with high anaerobic capacity and a need for central aerobic improvements → steady high-cadence efforts; (c) climbers or riders with a hill-specific weakness → steep hill repeats (6x5 min) to combine VO2max work with specificity.
  • Before increasing training dose (moving from 4x5 to 6x5, or from 3-4 week block), first ensure off-bike recovery is in order: nutrition, sleep, stress, ferritin stores. Address recovery before adjusting interval structure.
  • If not seeing improvements, troubleshoot recovery before increasing interval volume or intensity; athletes with poor serum ferritin, immunosuppression, or poor sleep will not respond to high-intensity blocks.
  • For a VO2max block, give athletes explicit pre-block permission to bail in week 2-3 if they are truly crashing; do not push through if the cord needs to be pulled.
  • Use a VO2max workout at the end of the preceding training block as a baseline comparison for the start of the upcoming VO2max block — this gives an early signal about how the block is likely to go.
  • Sprint interval training and short anaerobic capacity work (30s efforts) also improve VO2max in the literature, especially early season — these are legitimate tools even if they are not "VO2max intervals" in the traditional sense.
  • Assess effort quality by checking that heart rate reached approximately the same relative level across all intervals in a set — if watts declined but heart rate stayed high and stable, those were equally hard efforts.
  • Do not use caffeine to artificially raise heart rate to hit a heart rate target during VO2max efforts; if heart rate is low, it may signal fitness or fatigue — neither is fixed by stimulants.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • High-cadence block training induces documented side effects in the literature: immunosuppression, reduced serum ferritin (at both 3 and 6 weeks), and mood disturbance — these are real risks in real training blocks.
  • VO2max blocks typically produce ~15W improvement on average from a coaching standpoint; 20-30W is considered an excellent response at ~300W FTP. Literature reports 6-8% average improvement in well-trained pursuit cyclists.
  • Approximately 1 in 10 VO2max blocks gets pulled early due to athlete not recovering or tolerating the load; the rate of blocks that need modification or termination is non-trivial.
  • "Block training" in this context means a concentrated focus on one type of stimulus (e.g., all VO2max for 2-4 weeks), not the more common usage of a 3-week build followed by a recovery week.
  • Studies showing VO2max improvements are often conducted on athletes coming in from off-season, where almost any stimulus will produce improvement; this limits the applicability of findings to already in-season well-trained athletes.
  • VO2max improvements plateau; once you have made early-season gains from intermittent efforts or any other method, you will need to change the stimulus or increase dose to continue seeing adaptation.
  • Anaerobic capacity improvements and VO2max improvements are difficult to separate from standard testing (ramp test, 20-minute power); improvements in ramp test power or 20-minute power may reflect W prime gains, not VO2max gains.
  • Short recovery intervals between sets (e.g., 2 minutes for 6x5min) increase fatigue and make it harder to do subsequent training — may not be worth the tradeoff for most athletes.
  • Athletes with inadequate ferritin, who are immunocompromised, or who are anemic will not respond well to high-intensity blocks and may see no improvement.
  • Heart rate max declines as fitness improves and across a season; do not chase the highest heart rate you have ever recorded — VO2max intervals don't need to hit all-time max heart rate to be effective.
  • 90% of athletes Kolie consults with probably do not need a full VO2max block; many will respond adequately to lower doses integrated across training.
  • High-cadence preload theory (the mechanism by which high cadence reduces fatigue while maintaining aerobic stimulus) remains plausible but unconfirmed in the literature; the practical effect is real, but the mechanism may involve multiple interacting factors.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The 30-15s paper (Rønnestad et al.) does not show superior VO2max improvements in the 30-15s group: the p-value for VO2max change between groups was 0.49 (liters/min) — approximately a coin flip — and reporting effect sizes on a result with p=0.49 is misleading. Both groups showed statistically the same VO2max improvement.
  • The apparent "superior performance" in the 30-15s group (better ramp test power, better 20-minute power) is most parsimoniously explained by anaerobic capacity (W prime) improvements, not VO2max gains — and the word "anaerobic" appears only once in the paper (in a Tabata citation), suggesting the authors did not consider or test this possibility.
  • Tabata himself was originally targeting anaerobic capacity improvements, not VO2max; his VO2max finding was incidental, because he used VO2 as a proxy for anaerobic capacity.
  • The low-cadence training study claiming "superior aerobic capacity improvements" from low-cadence intervals has an r=0.51 correlation between W max and VO2max change (r²=0.30), meaning 70% of ramp test power improvement was not explained by VO2max change — the improvement likely reflects anaerobic capacity or motor unit recruitment gains, not aerobic capacity per se.
  • Doing cycling intervals to "reach muscular failure" does not create a meaningful hypertrophy signal because — despite fatigue — there is insufficient mechanical tension on the muscles; this is a common misapplication of strength training principles to endurance training.
  • Power zones (e.g., Coggan's zones) were designed to be descriptive of what athletes were already doing, not prescriptive targets — just as Kolie's VO2max recommendations were intended as modifications to existing training, not a replacement protocol.
  • Starting VO2max intervals at 1,000W sprint intensity then fading to 200W produces roughly the same total kilojoule output and heart rate as an evenly paced effort, but creates far more fatigue and jeopardizes subsequent training quality; the acute VO2max elevation from EPOC priming is marginal (possibly 5-10 seconds of elevated kinetics) and not worth the cost in a multi-effort set.
  • Grip strength is not worth specifically training for longevity — it is a surrogate for muscle mass and neural drive, not a causal mechanism; this is offered as a parallel example of how coaching/training information gets distorted through "telephone" effects.
  • Even cyclocross racing — which pins heart rate at maximum for the entire race duration and is structurally identical to long-duration on-off intervals at very high heart rates — does not reliably improve VO2max in well-trained athletes, suggesting that intermittent high-intensity efforts have limited additional benefit for those already trained.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie explicitly walks back the presentation of his earlier VO2max series (Watts Doc 23-24), stating he is "embarrassed" by those episodes and that his recommendations were taken too prescriptively. He intended the advice (start hard, raise cadence, shorten rest, go max) as modifications to existing interval protocols — not a complete overhaul or mandatory block structure. He now wishes he had included more guardrails and caveats.
  • He no longer recommends shortening recovery intervals as a primary strategy and says he "doesn't reach for this one anymore" after previously suggesting it.
  • He stopped trying to "rip apart" studies as a format after his earlier episodes, believing it sounds smarter than it is and does not reflect his actual values as a coach; he also felt his critique of the 30-15s paper at the time, while largely standing up methodologically, was delivered unfairly and he partially takes responsibility for overreacting to the media interpretation rather than the paper itself.
  • He now views the 30-15s paper more charitably than at the time of his earlier episode: the finding that 30-15s produced equivalent VO2max but possibly superior anaerobic capacity is exactly what he would now expect and find interesting — rather than dismissible. The paper's title ("superior performance improvements") is now seen as more defensible than he previously gave it credit for, since "performance" does not have to mean "VO2max."
  • His coaching philosophy has shifted away from maximizing a single variable toward asking: "What is the lowest fatigue intervention that will produce the needed improvement?" — starting there rather than jumping to blocks.
