2. VO2max & Oxygen Uptake

Core Message

This listener Q&A follow-up to Watts Doc 23 clarifies and extends the practical VO2max training advice, with particular focus on the high-cadence prescription, interval pacing, rest period management, and individual variation. It confirms that the high cadence is a training device only (not a race strategy), that perceived effort and breathing quality are more reliable feedback during intervals than power targets, and that the five-by-five is a reasonable starting framework that can be improved by applying the principles from the previous episode. Additional discussion addresses juniors, TT bike position, and standing during intervals.

Key Training Recommendations

  • During VO2max intervals, use high cadence (100–120 RPM or ~10–20 RPM above your hard-effort preferred cadence) — but race at your preferred cadence, as economy is maximized at the cadence your body selects naturally.
  • If you cannot generate meaningful power at 110–120 RPM, accept the power drop — the stroke volume and cardiac adaptation benefits outweigh the cost of lower absolute wattage during these sessions.
  • For recovery between VO2max intervals, take the shortest rest that allows you to immediately begin the next interval at a maximum effort — not a defined time period, but a readiness criterion. Avoid the common trap of 30+ minute rests before short intervals.
  • Pacing cue for interval start: accelerate to speed in the first 5–10 seconds (not a full sprint), then sustain maximum effort — let power fade naturally while keeping effort at maximum. The first ~1–1.5 minutes should bring you to the right cardiac zone.
  • If you consistently find breathing does not reach maximal intensity during intervals assigned at a percentage of FTP, go harder — the breathing response (ragged, forced, "gasping") is a reliable field indicator that the effort reached the intended cardiac stress.
  • VO2max intervals can be done unstructured (on available terrain) as long as the principles — high cadence, maximum effort, adequate duration, attention to breathing — are maintained.
  • For athletes moving directly from a base phase to VO2max work: build in a preparation phase with high-cadence sub-maximal intervals before full-intensity VO2max blocks — the transition requires preparation.
  • Avoid standing during VO2max intervals — standing lowers cadence, recruits larger motor units that take longer to recover, and likely reduces diastolic filling volume relative to seated high-cadence pedaling.
  • Five by five minutes is a reasonable default VO2max structure ("the two-by-20 of VO2max work") if you cannot individualize — the key improvement is applying high cadence even within that structure.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The high-cadence prescription is specific to the VO2max training goal of maximizing preload — for FTP work, track racing, or motor unit training, different cadence prescriptions apply.
  • There is a cadence ceiling beyond which diminishing returns set in — if you are doing 120 RPM but power is so low that you cannot reach the correct breathing response, reduce cadence slightly.
  • Athletes whose preferred cadence is 70 RPM should target approximately 85–90 RPM for VO2max intervals, not 120 — individualize the cadence target based on your natural hard-effort cadence, adding 10–20 RPM.
  • Standing during intervals is not necessarily disqualifying if the athlete finds a genuinely equivalent or better physiological stimulus, but the default should be seated high-cadence pedaling.
  • TT position may reduce diastolic filling slightly relative to road bike position, but the specificity benefit of TT training probably outweighs this for dedicated time trialists — train in TT position as much as possible.
  • VO2max training should generally not follow immediately after a pure off-season or long low-intensity block without a transitional period.
  • Juniors: no evidence that high-intensity VO2max work is harmful — gear restrictions exist for power/fairness reasons, not physiological harm concerns.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • A 10–20 W power reduction from using high cadence is a worthwhile trade-off for the substantial increase in stroke volume and diastolic filling — the adaptation target (cardiac preload) is better served by the lower-power, higher-cadence approach.
  • The minimal structure for effective VO2max training is not a formal prescribed session — it is any effort that produces maximal breathing and high heart rate at high cadence, even on variable terrain.
  • The reason athletes fresh out of base training struggle to "get into" VO2max intervals (legs give out before breathing maxes) is a transition issue, not a sign of inadequate aerobic fitness — it requires a preparatory mini-phase.
  • Popular training platforms (ERG mode, structured apps) may actually impede VO2max training by enforcing power targets that constrain the pacing pattern that produces maximum cardiac stimulus.
