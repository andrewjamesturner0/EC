Ten Minute Tips 43 Off Season Breaks and Massive Listener Qa

Ten Minute Tips 22,610 words View transcript

Core Message

This roundtable of Empirical Cycling coaches (Kolie Moore, James, Megan, Erica, GC, Corey) discusses how to approach the off-season break, emphasizing that effective rest is the goal — not just time off. The panel stresses that the purpose of the off-season is mental and physical recharge, not the preservation of fitness, and that athletes (especially masters) who skip genuine rest often pay for it later in the season. The episode also covers a wide listener Q&A on FTP testing, strength training RPE limits, sprint and anaerobic capacity timing, volume vs. intensity, and coaching philosophy.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Off-season activity level: Masters athletes (50s–60s) should aim for ~6–8 hours/week at moderate intensity during their off-season to minimize fitness loss; fitness is harder to rebuild with age.
  • Off-season gym: You can go straight into strength training after the cycling season ends without waiting for a bike-specific rest period; gym work does not appear to impede recovery from accumulated cycling fatigue.
  • Strength training RPE cap: Keep heavy strength sessions at RPE 7 (out of 10) routinely. Going to RPE 9–10 every session requires a rest week every third week and leads to a required month of rest after 3–4 months. The minimum effective dose cited is 3–6 sets at ~RPE 7 at 1–6 reps. Coach Kolie's personal maintenance dose: 3 sets of 1 rep per week of single-leg squat at RPE 6–7 is sufficient to maintain strength.
  • FTP indoors vs. outdoors: Expect indoor FTP to run ~10–15 watts lower than outdoor for many athletes (one client example: 305W outdoor / 290W a struggle indoors). Substitute sweet spot for FTP work indoors rather than trying to force target outdoor FTP watts.
  • Sprint training timing: Sprint efforts (keep them ≤10 seconds for true peak power development) can be trained year-round if it is a weakness; from a periodization standpoint, ~6 weeks before racing is sufficient if it is not a primary limiter.
  • Anaerobic capacity timing: Anaerobic capacity peaks quickly — expect 4–6 workouts to near-max it if you have been doing some intensity, 6–10 from scratch. It also decays quickly, requiring ~1 session/week or every 1.5 weeks to maintain. Recommendation: build it close to target races rather than early in the season when you'd burn a lot of sessions just maintaining it.
  • Volume vs. intensity: Extra time should almost always be filled with volume, not intensity. The intensity any individual can handle is relatively capped; volume capacity can be substantially grown. The more aerobic volume you carry, the larger the response to any subsequent interval work.
  • Recovery ride pacing: Recovery rides done at ~65% FTP (e.g., 200W for a ~300W FTP athlete) are already too hard for true recovery. A 200W recovery ride on a 300W FTP is flagged as a top-tier coaching mistake.
  • Monitoring fatigue via good days: Watch whether your good days are rare. If they are, that is a sign of accumulated fatigue; good days should not be few and far between.
  • Post-off-season ramp-up cue: Wait until you are burning to train again, then wait "a little bit more." Use the itch-to-return as the start signal, not the end of a fixed calendar period.
  • Training backwards from goal race: For self-coached athletes, start from the goal race date and work backwards to determine how much off-season you can actually afford.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Off-season rest duration varies enormously by individual: a low-volume rider might need only 1–2 weeks; a high-volume athlete who raced heavily may need a month or more.
  • Physical fatigue may take longer to shed than mental fatigue — an athlete who feels motivated may still be physically depleted.
  • The ~10-watt indoor vs. outdoor FTP gap is athlete-specific; heat, inertia differences in the trainer, and pedaling pattern all contribute.
  • Anaerobic capacity as a limiter may be structural (muscle mass, rate-of-force development) rather than purely trainable; skinny climbers have a hard ceiling here.
  • The "ride at the pace you could do for 8 hours" endurance cue is not appropriate for athletes already doing 6+ hour rides, who may actually be able to self-destruct at "8-hour pace."
  • Whether 10 days off counts as an off-season depends entirely on what training looked like before it and what follows it. If training looks identical before and after, it was just a rest week, not an off-season.
  • Altitude training: ~100 hours at altitude yield approximately 1% hematocrit improvement (rule of thumb, acknowledged as approximate). Adaptation disappears quickly after returning to sea level.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • You can likely begin heavy-ish strength training immediately after your race season ends without needing a bike-specific recovery window first — gym work seems well-tolerated even on accumulated cycling fatigue.
  • Typical FTP loss over the off-season is at most ~10 watts, and often zero. Significant loss from off-season rest is rare. Coaches often see athletes increase FTP after rest, partly because pre-rest FTP was suppressed by fatigue.
  • A client with a very short training age had a 5-minute power only 2–3 watts off his PR after a two-week rest — the fitness security blanket fear is largely unjustified.
  • Sprints for peak power development should be ≤10 seconds; 30-second "sprints" in the scientific literature are not sprints in the peak-power sense and do not develop peak power meaningfully.
  • Well-trained athletes see progressively smaller adaptations to high-intensity and sprint-intensity training; endurance training adaptations extend beyond what high-intensity training provides at advanced training ages (citing a meta-analysis discussed in the episode).

Evolving Views

  • Coach Kolie notes that for athletes who lose fitness easily, a small amount (~2 rides/week) of low-intensity activity during the off-season is preferred over complete rest — this represents a nuanced departure from a blanket "take full time off" prescription.
  • James describes an ongoing n=1 self-experiment: after two years building from ~6–7 hours/week to ~17–18 hours/week, he found that day-to-day and within-ride intensity tolerance improved substantially — suggesting that the relationship between volume and intensity capacity is bidirectional and trainable.
