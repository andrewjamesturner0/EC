Ten Minute Tips 30 Strength Training Mistakes Every Cyclist Makes

Ten Minute Tips 21,168 words View transcript

Referenced in

7. Strength Training

Core Message

Kolie and Kyle discuss common mistakes cyclists make when incorporating strength training. The core theme is that cyclists sabotage themselves by sacrificing sleep for gym time, using poor technique without feedback, doing too many exercises per session, using outdated training approaches from high school, and avoiding useful equipment like belts and straps out of misplaced ego. Strength training for cyclists should be about health, injury prevention, and improving cycling -- not chasing weight room numbers.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Do not sacrifice sleep for gym sessions; consistently cutting sleep impairs recovery
  • If you normally get 7.5-9 hours of sleep and get only 6.5-7 hours with that groggy feeling, skip the heavy lifting -- poor sleep increases injury risk
  • Get technique checked by a personal trainer, especially for squat and deadlift
  • Record every set or every other set on your phone; watch the video while resting between sets
  • Knees should go over toes during squats -- the old advice to keep knees behind toes causes poor mechanics
  • Use greater range of motion in squats: same or more stimulus using less weight, which decreases injury risk
  • Do not sacrifice technique for depth -- if your back rolls, butt wink, or you "good morning" the weight up, the weight is too heavy
  • Limit heavy leg work to a maximum of about 6-9 working sets per session, split across 2-3 exercises
  • Starting recommendation: about 4 heavy working sets, building up to about 7
  • Program rep ranges of 8-15 for most cyclists, maybe up to 20, especially early on
  • Do not just add gym sessions on top of existing cycling plan without adjusting total training load
  • Use equipment: belts, straps, weightlifting shoes all make lifting better, safer, and more effective
  • Machines (Smith machine, hack squat, belt squat, leg press) are useful when stabilizers are fatigued
  • Do not borrow bodybuilding programs or use high school weight room routines

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Sleep is "one of the most anabolic things on the planet" -- if you are impacting sleep, you are hurting gains both aerobically and strength-wise
  • The "first year effect" applies: whatever plan worked your first year probably worked because everything works for beginners, not because it was optimal
  • Individual differences matter: not everyone can squat deep enough for hamstrings to meet calves, and that is fine

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Sleep may be more valuable than the gym session it would replace -- consistently trading sleep for workouts is counterproductive
  • Knees going over toes in squats is not only safe but desirable; Olympic lifters routinely have knees "miles in front of toes"
  • Greater range of motion with lighter weight is preferable to heavier weight with limited range
  • Machines and isolation exercises are underrated for cyclists -- not "cheating" but smart when stabilizers are fatigued
  • Your high school strength training was probably neither well-designed nor effective; you got better because you were young and a beginner

Evolving Views

  • Kolie mentions he used to program too many exercises per session (squat + single leg press + deadlift + 2-3 other things, taking 3+ hours) and has since moved to much shorter, more focused sessions of about 4-7 working sets
