3. Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing

Core Message

This three-coach roundtable (Kolie, Erica, Fabiano) enumerates common FTP-building mistakes, organized as a practical checklist. The central themes are: test FTP by feel rather than relying on short-effort proxies; ensure sessions are well-fueled and properly rested before you execute threshold work; be skeptical of lactate testing as a training tool; do not assume FTP is changing when fatigue or underfueling may be the real variable; and understand when to stop chasing FTP gains and shift toward TTE extension or VO2max work. A recurring emphasis is that FTP is a stable physiological marker that should function as a fatigue barometer, not a day-to-day fluctuating number.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Test FTP by feel using the open-ended RPE-guided approach: start conservatively, gradually ramp to the sustainable edge, and trust that a well-calibrated athlete can find their FTP within ~5 W in about 10 minutes. Do not predetermine the target number before the effort.
  • Do not extrapolate FTP from non-FTP efforts. Building sweet spot to 90 minutes or completing long sweet spot work does not confirm FTP has risen. Only riding at FTP tests FTP.
  • Come into FTP sessions with appropriate rest. Physical fatigue or high life stress will skew RPE and produce an unreliable threshold calibration.
  • Use minimum interval durations of approximately 12 minutes for threshold work; shorter intervals risk drifting into VO2max territory and inflate perceived FTP. Progress interval duration over the block.
  • Fuel threshold sessions adequately. Two-by-20 at threshold is a large kilojoule day and requires carbohydrate both during and after the session. Poor fueling will cause sessions to fail and may lead to the false conclusion that the FTP block "didn't work."
  • Hydrate aggressively before morning sessions — eight hours of fasting and overnight breathing causes significant fluid loss. Consider evening carbohydrate loading as a compensatory strategy.
  • Control indoor heat buildup. 65–75°F (18–24°C) indoor temperature without aggressive cooling will degrade interval quality significantly. Use fans and ice water actively; expect 10–15 W reduction in sustainable power if cooling is inadequate.
  • When transitioning from indoor to outdoor training in spring, recalibrate your RPE/threshold perception for the changed thermal environment and physics. Terrain type (flat vs. climbing) can shift FTP expression by 10–20 W for some athletes.
  • Do not attempt to build FTP continuously through a busy race schedule. Racing disrupts the controlled training dose and recovery predictability required for progressive FTP overload.
  • Start each threshold interval conservatively — 5–10 W below the prescription. Build from there. The last interval (or an extension of the final interval) is the appropriate place to test the ceiling, not the first.
  • When FTP stops rising, shift focus to TTE extension (longer intervals at the same watts) before moving to VO2max work. Then, if needed, raise the "roof" with VO2 work to open space for a higher FTP later.
  • Track FTP as a fatigue barometer. A 20 W apparent drop mid-week almost always reflects accumulated fatigue, underfueling, or heat — not a true fitness decline. Use this as a recovery signal, not a retest trigger.
  • For extensive vs. intensive FTP work: prefer extensive (time-in-zone) for building TTE. Reserve intensive work for situations where a new higher FTP needs to be consolidated or validated.
  • To prevent RPE rut when FTP improves, be aware that 15–20 W higher watts will feel unfamiliar. Push through this brief adjustment period; TTE may temporarily drop and then rebuild at the new level.
  • Do a short FTP test effort in the warmup (~2 minutes) to get a daily read on freshness before committing to full interval intensity. Adjust the session accordingly.
  • Vary training with VO2max, sweet spot, and other efforts to maintain mental engagement and avoid the diminishing returns and opportunity cost of exclusive FTP focus.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Lactate testing uses population averages (e.g., 4 mmol/L as threshold, 2 mmol/L as LT1) that do not apply reliably to individuals. In coaching practice, the fixed-concentration method has underestimated threshold by 30–40 W in individual athletes. A world-champion track cyclist was told his threshold was far lower than his actual 40–50 minute sustainable power because his lactate profile was atypical. Kolie considers routine field lactate testing "a waste of time and money" given that performance is easier to measure directly.
  • CPEX (cardiopulmonary exercise testing) and other lab-based threshold protocols also commonly overestimate threshold in practice. When athletes attempt 2×20 at lab-derived numbers and fail the second interval, that is a clear sign of overestimation.
  • Metabolomics testing (discussed as an emerging option) requires ~4 tests per year at ~$250 each; the coaches find this cost-prohibitive and not yet proven for practical training zone prescription.
  • Seasonal factors (sleep quality worsening in heat, accumulated fatigue near season apex, increased outdoor volume) all affect the rate and nature of FTP adaptation and should be accounted for when evaluating a block's results.
  • At very low fitness levels (e.g., after months of easy riding), FTP expression is compressed: the range between sustainable and maximal short-duration power is narrow, making RPE-based threshold identification genuinely difficult.
  • For athletes on an energy deficit or morning training in a fasted/dehydrated state, RPE is unreliable and FTP intervals may fail for nutritional reasons unrelated to true fitness.
  • Terrain specificity is real for some athletes: individuals may show a 10–20 W difference between flat and climbing FTP, requiring separate calibration if their event demands are terrain-specific.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Lactate testing has too many valid methodologies (at least 10 published approaches) to be considered a reliable gold standard. The multiplicity of methods is itself evidence that none of them work reliably. (Kolie's position, shared by Fabiano.)
  • The 2×8-minute test at 90% correction — popular at the time Erica began coaching — can overestimate FTP by up to 60 W for highly anaerobic riders (Kolie's own data: ~330 W 2×8 result vs. ~270 W actual FTP).
  • An athlete can feel their FTP going up during a training block without testing. The block itself — when intervals are executed near threshold — functions as continuous testing. Formal retesting every 4–8 weeks is not required to detect progress.
  • Doing FTP training three times per week is not necessary and may be counterproductive. Kolie reports that over the last 2–3 years he has given athletes less FTP training than listeners would expect, with no reduction in FTP gains or TTE improvements — and possibly larger gains.
  • The notion that FTP intervals must be "erg mode perfect" or "clean" to count is incorrect. Normalized power within ~1 W of average power on an outdoor interval is a high-quality effort; natural stochasticity of outdoor riding is not a reason to dismiss the session.
  • FTP is not the endpoint of a training career. A well-chosen variety of efforts — including VO2max, sweet spot, and race-specific work — is almost certainly more effective than single-minded FTP block accumulation for most cyclists.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie notes that in the last 2–3 years he has moved away from heavy FTP block emphasis and has given athletes a more varied training diet. He reports this has not reduced FTP progress and may have improved it — representing a practical evolution from the podcast's earlier, more FTP-focused recommendations.
  • The panel collectively notes the recurring pattern in exercise physiology of new testing methods (lactate, CPEX, metabolomics) cycling through popularity before returning to the same conclusion: direct performance measurement is more reliable. Fabiano: "Physiology is not changing, so you don't need to reinvent the wheel."
