Ten Minute Tips 3 Bro Science

13. Research & Methodology

Core Message

This episode, hosted by Kyle Helson, introduces listeners to "bro science" — dubious claims that circulate in fitness and sports culture, often carrying a small kernel of truth wrapped in overgeneralization. The central warning is that confident, absolute claims ("you always have to do X") are a reliable red flag, whereas genuine expertise is characterized by nuanced, context-dependent answers. Scientific backing does not automatically legitimize a claim, particularly when studies are industry-funded, poorly replicated, or interpreted out of context. The p-value standard in biological sciences (≤0.05) means up to a 1-in-20 chance of a false positive, so single-study findings should be treated with caution until replicated.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Be skeptical of any training or supplement claim that uses absolute language ("always," "you must," "the only way") — these are hallmarks of bro science rather than evidence-based advice.
  • Do not accept training methodology or supplement efficacy based on a single study; require replication across multiple studies with different conditions and populations before acting.
  • Treat industry-funded supplement studies with extra skepticism — their purpose is to show the product works, which creates a built-in conflict of interest; labels like "university tested" are meaningless without context.
  • For time-limited athletes (~6 hours/week), do not default to exclusively Zone 2 base training in winter; insufficient total aerobic stress means higher intensity work becomes necessary to drive adaptation.
  • When evaluating training advice, ask whether the answer given is context-dependent ("it depends") — if the answer is always the same regardless of context, treat the source as unreliable.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The classic hypertrophy rep range (6–15 reps/set) shows only slightly better results for muscle mass than higher or lower rep ranges, given similar total training volumes — the effect is real but not as absolute as commonly stated.
  • Extended Zone 2 base miles in winter have genuine benefits (referenced in Episode 7: "What is Base Really?"), but they are not the only path to fitness improvement and may be inappropriate for time-constrained athletes.
  • The p-value threshold of ≤0.05 in biological sciences is a pragmatic compromise because controlling all variables in human studies is logistically impossible; this means many published results merely clear a low bar and should not be treated as definitive.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Most bro science contains a "tiny core of truth wrapped in a big ball of crap" — the problem is not that the advice is entirely wrong but that it is stated as universally applicable.
  • Placebos are "university tested" by the same logic used to market supplements; the label proves nothing about efficacy.
  • Social media and increasingly sophisticated sports science have made bro science more believable and harder to detect, not less.
  • There are no silver bullets in training; anyone claiming to have found one is "withholding information at best, if not outright lying."
