How High Intensity Interval Training Makes You Faster

Other 5,674 words View transcript

Referenced in

2. VO2max & Oxygen Uptake

Core Message

This early episode examines high-intensity interval training (HIIT) through the lens of two papers: the original Tabata protocol (1996) and a study on ryanodine receptor fragmentation from sprint intervals. The central argument is that HIIT improves both VO2max and anaerobic capacity in untrained or moderately trained athletes, but that as athletes become more trained, a general catch-all stimulus is no longer sufficient — you must target specific physiological weak links. The aerobic adaptations during intermittent intervals are driven primarily by the recovery phases, where the body works to repay oxygen deficit created by anaerobic work. The episode cautions against over-relying on HIIT because excessive anaerobic work can suppress aerobic performance for races longer than roughly five minutes.

Key Training Recommendations

  • For untrained to moderately trained athletes, use Tabata-style intervals (20 seconds on, 10 seconds off, targeting 170% of VO2max power) to simultaneously improve VO2max and anaerobic capacity.
  • Calculate your Tabata target power using your VO2max power (from WKO4/WKO5 VO2max report) and multiply by 1.7; e.g., if VO2max power is 300 W, target 510 W for the 20-second work intervals.
  • For well-trained athletes, move to dedicated VO2max intervals rather than HIIT if the goal is specifically to raise VO2max, since calcium-leak-driven mitochondrial adaptation from sprint intervals does not occur in trained athletes.
  • If your goal is anaerobic capacity specifically, 20-10 Tabata intervals are effective, but be aware that after 6–10 weeks of anaerobic capacity work, improvements plateau.
  • Limit HIIT volume to avoid excessive anaerobic stress that can depress aerobic performance in events longer than ~5 minutes.
  • For new athletes or those with low anaerobic training history, even 3×30-second all-out sprints with 4-minute rest periods can produce meaningful aerobic and anaerobic adaptations.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The Tabata study used moderately trained subjects (VO2max ~48–53 mL/kg/min), so results may not transfer to highly trained endurance athletes.
  • Both the Tabata group and the 60-min/70%-VO2max group increased VO2max by similar amounts, highlighting that near-anything improves untrained people — this is not evidence HIIT is superior for trained athletes.
  • Ryanodine receptor fragmentation (the cellular mechanism for mitochondrial adaptation from sprints) does not occur in well-trained individuals (VO2max ~67–68 mL/kg/min) doing sprint intervals — the stimulus is simply not novel enough.
  • Doing too much HIIT can cause fatigue and carbohydrate depletion that undermines aerobic training quality.
  • The aerobic adaptation during HIIT comes primarily from the recovery between efforts (repaying oxygen debt), not from the intervals themselves.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The aerobic adaptations from HIIT occur largely during the rest periods, not the work intervals themselves — the intervals create oxygen deficit, and the recovery demands drive aerobic adaptation.
  • FTP-style sweet spot work (60 minutes at 70% VO2max) and Tabata intervals produce similar VO2max improvements in untrained people — the "twofer" advantage of HIIT only applies where both aerobic and anaerobic systems are underdeveloped.
  • In elite athletes, improving VO2max and FTP simultaneously may not be possible — improving one can cause the other to decrease (described as a "teeter-totter" by an unnamed physiologist colleague).
  • Anaerobic capacity improvements from training are almost entirely enzymatic (not structural), so they can appear in days to weeks — explaining why athletes see rapid improvements from anaerobic work blocks.
