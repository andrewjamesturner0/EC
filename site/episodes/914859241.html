Ten Minute Tips 9 Drills to Hone Your Sprint Technique

Ten Minute Tips 2,330 words View transcript

Referenced in

11. Equipment & Technique

Core Message

Kolie Moore presents three progressive drills to develop sprint technique on the bike, grounded in the physics of Newton's third law: when you push hard on the pedals, the bike pushes you back, so sprinting requires both force production and force stabilization. The drills progress from big-gear force stabilization, to small-gear high-cadence bike-rocking technique, to a full-power sprint that integrates both skills. The key insight is that sprinting is a technique-limited activity, not just a power-limited one, and specific drill work with proper cues can unlock existing power.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Drill 1 -- Big Gear Drill (Force Stabilization): Start from 20-30 RPM on flat ground or slight uphill; sprint standing for 10-15 seconds; aim to reach no higher than 60-70 RPM; keep core and arms very tight to stabilize force between feet and hands; do this standing (not seated) to remove saddle as a stabilization crutch
  • Drill 2 -- Small Gear Drill (High Cadence / Bike Rocking): Use small chainring and medium-to-small cassette cog; start standing at ~90 RPM and gradually increase cadence until technique breaks down; naturally rock the bike back and forth underneath you; skilled riders can reach ~190-200 RPM standing; optimal duration is 10-20 seconds to allow neural patterning to develop; primary cue is "relax" -- muscles must relax quickly between pedal strokes to contract quickly; watch for chain slap at high speeds
  • Drill 3 -- Max Power / Race Speed Drill: Coast down a hill to 20-25 mph (~40 kph); on flat ground, stand and sprint in the right gear; aim to hit peak power within 1-2 seconds of standing; feel the integration of Drill 1 (tight core/arms stabilizing force) and Drill 2 (relaxed, fluid pedaling at speed); faster sprinters can start at ~30 mph / 50 kph; keep your head up and look where you're going
  • Incorporate 1-2 of these drills into interval ride warmups, 2-3 times per week
  • For a dedicated drill session, do a couple big-gear drills, then small-gear drills, then finish with max-power sprints -- progression on the same day reinforces neural patterning
  • Total sprint time in a quality session: ~60-90 seconds total
  • Do these drills when fresh -- in the first 60-90 minutes of a longer ride
  • Quality over quantity for sprint work; if your sprint is ~1800W, keep drill volume low to avoid digging a fatigue hole; for 800-1200W sprinters, a couple of warmup sprints is fine
  • If the saddle bumps your thighs when standing and rocking the bike, your fit is probably too short -- a longer cockpit keeps center of gravity properly centered and gives room to move; this is especially common on the track
  • Cadence and gearing optimization for sprinting will be covered in a future Watts Doc episode

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Watch out for knee problems with the big-gear drill (high torque at low cadence)
  • Chain slap is a real risk during the small-gear high-cadence drill
  • The host notes he can only do ~190 RPM standing for a couple seconds; his sweet spot for the small-gear drill is 10-15 seconds at ~170-175 RPM -- adjust to your own ability
  • Sprint technique work should be done fresh; fatigued sprinting teaches bad motor patterns
  • Riders with ~1800W sprints need to manage drill volume more carefully than riders at 800-1200W

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The primary cue for high-cadence sprinting is "relax" (and the second cue is "really relax") -- being too tight locks up muscles and prevents the rapid contraction-relaxation cycle needed for high RPM; this is counterintuitive since sprinting feels like it should be about maximal tension
  • Sprinting is as much a stabilization problem (Newton's third law -- the bike is trying to launch you off) as it is a force production problem; without proper core/arm tension, force "leaks" through torso rotation instead of going into the pedals
  • After doing small-gear drills at 170 RPM, normal sprint cadence (110-120 RPM) feels slow, making it easier to think and refine technique at race speeds -- a deliberate perceptual training effect
  • Bike fit for sprinting (especially track) should have a long cockpit to keep center of gravity between contact points; many riders, especially track riders, have fits that are too short for effective standing sprints
