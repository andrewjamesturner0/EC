Perspectives 29 Managing Fitness and Rest During Race Season with Taylor Warren

Perspectives 20,510 words View transcript

Referenced in

12. Racing & Team Tactics

Core Message

Taylor Warren (CS Velo road captain) discusses a breakthrough year: PRs at 13+ years into racing, attributed to higher on-bike volume, dramatically better fueling (90–100 g carb/hr on endurance rides), and a mid-season training block of 6 weeks at 20+ hrs/week. He articulates how to manage fitness across a dense stage-race calendar (50+ race days) through intelligent recovery prioritization, RPE-based training, and a compressed off-season aerobic build.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Push carbohydrate intake to 90–100+ g/hr on endurance rides (even easy ones); Taylor used DIY lemonade in 2L bottles: 100–150 g table sugar + lemon juice + 2 g sea salt per bottle; tubeless setup allowed lower pressure for hot, dusty Boise training (180–220 W rides at 60% FTP)
  • Endurance rides should average 180–185 W (for Taylor's ~300+ W FTP) — "as fast as I can that still feels easy"; confirmed that RPE-guided endurance pace naturally stabilizes around the same value across very different-length days
  • Mid-season break: take 1 week off entirely (Taylor did ~5 hrs of easy mountain bike riding); then do a 6-week volume block (20+ hrs every week); "I would recommend to just about everyone taking a week off in the middle of summer"
  • During a heavy race calendar (Redlands → Gila → Joe Martin = 14 race days in 25 days): prioritize recovery over training; threshold sessions only in 2-week gaps between races; no VO2 max work — racing provides it
  • The off-season build is where 80% of fitness gains happen; maintaining is relatively easy during race season; build the reservoir before the season
  • Signals of readiness to train hard again after racing blocks: motivation to go hard ("I want to train hard" or slight boredom with easy riding); absence of grumpiness when accelerating or going upstairs; not the reverse — if someone refuses to push above threshold it's a signal to give more easy volume
  • Travel management: sync to destination time zone immediately on arrival; eye mask + familiar pillow; normalize the schedule rather than tracking home time zone; adjust circadian rhythm by getting up early rather than going to bed late (if morning person)
  • RPE-based training: "heart rate and power are objective quantifiers of something subjective, which is RPE. Power and heart rate are roundabout ways of training what you're actually trying to train." Use RPE as primary guide, especially for endurance

Caveats & Qualifications

  • 100 g/hr table-sugar bottles may cause gut distress for some; Taylor tolerates high sugar concentrations well; individual gut testing required
  • "Maintaining is relatively easy, whereas building is quite hard" — fitness can decline slightly over a race-heavy stretch even with correct recovery, especially if threshold isn't touched for months
  • Joe Martin crit (late in a very busy stage race) was Taylor's worst result of the year — body doesn't respond well to stage racing when not trained for it; team was built for stage racing but still unprepared for the crit demands
  • Team logistics quality significantly modulates travel stress; bad accommodations, absent food prep, uncertain transfers compound fatigue exponentially

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Taylor: "If you're going to get the same adaptation from riding 180 watts as from 220 watts for endurance rides — and most research shows that's probably the case — then the 220-watt ride just burns more kilojoules and you're more tired, with nothing to show for it."
  • "200 watts" as an endurance target is a human ego/round-number artifact — the body doesn't care about round numbers; the actual aerobically appropriate pace for many 250–350 W FTP riders is 160–185 W
  • Riding 30-hour weeks (140 + 180 mile back-to-back days) at a very easy pace produces PRs in sustained power — the volume itself, independent of intensity, is a performance driver

Evolving Views

  • Taylor shifted from individual-session thinking ("I put a lot of weight into single individual training sessions") to block-level thinking: "every training session is a drop in the water bucket — it's much more beneficial to have that consistent drip than a big splash"
  • Taylor updated his fueling practice from 40–60 g/hr to 90–100 g/hr; the accumulated recovery and energy effect across a whole training block, not just a single ride, was the distinguishing result
