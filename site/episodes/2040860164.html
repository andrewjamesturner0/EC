13. Research & Methodology

Core Message

Host Kolie Moore interviews Jem Arnold, a physiotherapist and PhD candidate at UBC, who is a co-author on a large systematic review and network meta-analysis of individual participant data comparing training intensity distribution models (polarized, pyramidal, threshold, low-intensity) in endurance athletes. The headline finding is that no training intensity distribution model is clearly superior for improving VO2max or time trial performance across all athletes; the only meaningful interaction was that competitive athletes showed slightly greater VO2max gains from polarized training, while recreational athletes showed slightly greater gains from pyramidal. Both hosts emphasize that all effect sizes (~1–2%) sit near the edge of measurement uncertainty, and that research findings should be treated as incrementally informative rather than prescriptive. The second major topic is iliac artery flow limitation (FLIA/endofibrosis) in cyclists — its diagnosis using NIRS and ankle blood pressure during provocative exercise tests, conservative management, and when to suspect it.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Prescribe training intensity with reference to physiological thresholds (lactate threshold 1 and 2, or ventilatory thresholds) rather than fixed percentages of peak values (e.g., % VO2max, % HRmax, % watt-peak). Using threshold-anchored zones reduces heterogeneity in athlete responses and increases the proportion of responders.
  • When prescribing intensity within a three-zone model, use percentage of the delta between anchors: e.g., 50% of the delta between LT1 and LT2 targets the heavy domain; ~75% of the delta between RCP and VO2max targets the severe domain.
  • Competitive athletes (university/provincial/national level and above) may preferentially use polarized training distribution to maximize VO2max gains over a training block (roughly 1–2% additional improvement vs. pyramidal at group level).
  • Recreational/club-level athletes may preferentially use pyramidal intensity distribution (more time in the moderate/tempo zone, less at the extremes) for slightly better VO2max outcomes at group level.
  • Do not over-interpret small week-to-week or test-to-test changes in FTP or VO2max. Day-to-day variability in time trial performance is approximately ±1–3%; VO2max measurement error is ±1–5%. A 5-watt FTP change is within normal measurement uncertainty and should not prompt an immediate training change.
  • When evaluating training response, monitor longer-term trends (monthly or across a full block) and look at convergent indicators (e.g., heart rate at fixed workload trending down, perceived exertion at standard efforts declining) rather than relying on a single point-in-time test value.
  • If a cyclist experiences unilateral leg pain, burning, heaviness, or weakness at high intensity — especially in aero position — have them ruled out for more common musculoskeletal causes first. If those treatments do not resolve the symptoms, pursue referral for sport-specific vascular assessment (provocative exercise test with NIRS and ankle blood pressure), not standard clinical vascular workup designed for older sedentary patients.
  • If FLIA/endofibrosis is suspected or diagnosed, modify training by separating high-intensity work from aero position: do high-intensity intervals in an upright or standing position, and familiarize to the aero position only at low intensity.
  • Power balance asymmetry (e.g., 47:53) in the absence of symptoms is not a cause for concern and does not require correction. Investigate power imbalance only when symptoms are present and you are looking for a contributing cause.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The meta-analysis included ~350 participants from 13 studies; roughly 300 were male. Results are most confidently applicable to trained males of European descent, though Jem Arnold states he is comfortable applying them to all athletes and sexes given that within-individual variation exceeds between-group variation.
  • The average VO2max improvement across all conditions was ~2%, with a range of roughly −20% to +30% across individuals. This wide variability means group-level findings cannot be used to predict individual response.
  • Effect sizes for all intensity distribution comparisons are at or below the day-to-day measurement noise (±1–3% for TT performance; ±1–5% for VO2max), so the practical relevance of choosing one model over another is modest and context-dependent.
  • The competitive vs. recreational interaction effect for VO2max was the only covariate that mattered; age, sex, sport (rowing/running/cycling), and time trial duration did not significantly interact with training model outcomes.
  • Baseline VO2max (as a continuous variable) did not significantly moderate outcomes; it was performance level (competitive vs. recreational classification) that mattered.
  • Studies in sports science are nearly always high or moderate risk of bias due to inability to blind participants to their training intervention; this is a structural limitation of the field.
  • Most intervention studies capture athletes coming off-season or after a deliberate detraining wash-in period, which inflates early gains and limits generalizability to in-season trained athletes.
  • VO2max and time trial performance gains follow a logarithmic time course: rapid early gains (first few weeks), then progressively diminishing returns. Studies running 3–18 weeks therefore tend to capture the steepest part of the curve, especially for less-trained starting points.
  • FLIA (iliac artery flow limitation) is rare. Unilateral leg discomfort at high intensity is far more likely to be musculoskeletal. The condition is typically progressive if provoked repeatedly, but appears unlikely to cause lasting vascular complications after retirement from high-intensity sport (limited long-term data, ~15–20 year follow-up data from Netherlands center).
  • NIRS-based threshold detection matches group-level lactate/ventilatory thresholds well, but individual-level discrepancies can be on the order of 30–40 watts in either direction; do not assume a group-calibrated NIRS breakpoint applies precisely to a given individual.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Polarized training is not clearly better than pyramidal or threshold across all athletes or outcomes when individual participant data (rather than group-level allocated training) are used. An earlier meta-analysis by lead author Rosenblatt using group-level data found polarized > threshold; re-analysis with individual data eliminated this difference, suggesting prior findings were partly an artifact of athletes not actually performing the assigned distribution.
  • Approximately 20% of participants in the included studies performed a different intensity distribution model than the one they were assigned to. This misclassification existed in prior research and may explain some of the between-study discrepancies in conclusions.
  • For recreational athletes, pyramidal (not polarized) training showed slightly greater VO2max improvement. This is the reverse of the popular narrative that polarized training is the gold standard for everyone.
  • Stacking multiple individually small interventions (hard-start intervals, bicarb, creatine, specific interval structures) does not compound to large cumulative gains — collectively they still tend to yield around the same 1–3% effect, likely partly due to temporary placebo/novelty effects that then regress.
  • Coaching routinely leads scientific research because coaches are running individualized experiments continuously in real time; the purpose of research is to follow and explain why things work, not to lead practice. (Jem Arnold's framing: "You have an athlete and you're trying to figure out what intervention responds best to them... coaches and athletes at the top of their games have succeeded because they've found the thing that works.")
  • A VO2max improvement that is the largest effect size in a given meta-analysis funnel plot may simply reflect that the study's training was the most acutely stressful and unsustainable — not that the protocol is optimal for long-term use.
  • Power balance alone (e.g., 47:53 split) does not predict injury risk or performance decrements in healthy athletes. Ranges of 45:55 or greater are common and benign without accompanying symptoms.

Evolving Views

  • Jem Arnold reflects on his earlier blog posts (circa 2019–2020 on Spare Cycles) focused on optimizing interval protocols session by session for VO2max. He now views those nuances (hard-starts, 30-15 intermittent efforts, specific interval timing) as interesting but not meaningfully different from simpler approaches. He says these "don't keep me up at night anymore" and that the literature shows session-to-session programming differences tend to produce only ~1–3% effects — no bigger than other small interventions. He left all original posts up because he does not view them as harmful, but advises readers to treat early content with caution.
  • Kolie Moore notes a similar arc: his most popular post became a "sustainable training template" that pulled back from hyper-optimized interval sessions, returning to fundamentals: high intensity, sprint intervals, low intensity, and threshold — all fitting together over different timeframes. This represents a shift from his earlier 2018–2020 era focus on highly optimized individual sessions.
