1. Exercise Physiology & Molecular Adaptation

Core Message

Fat transport into the mitochondrial matrix requires the CPT1 enzyme and the carnitine shuttle, and this system is regulated both by malonyl-CoA (inhibitor at rest) and by AMPK (which suppresses malonyl-CoA during exercise). At high intensities, free carnitine becomes scarce because it is consumed buffering acetyl groups (as acetylcarnitine), which competitively limits CPT1 and reduces fat oxidation at those intensities — not because fat is unavailable, but because carnitine is occupied. The VLAmax metric, popularized by some coaching platforms, is critiqued as measuring sprint power rather than glycolytic capacity, and the claims about high VLAmax limiting FTP are not supported by evidence. Fat and aerobic power are not necessarily in trade-off — elite cyclists can have both very high sprint power and very high FTP simultaneously.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Training to increase mitochondrial volume increases CPT1 activity and carnitine utilization capacity, enabling more fat import at moderate intensities.
  • At FTP, fat contribution can be 30–50%+ of total energy; training should not deprioritize fat oxidation at intensities near FTP.
  • AMPK activation during exercise suppresses malonyl-CoA, enabling CPT1 to function — this is one reason why exercise is the key regulator of fat oxidation, not diet or fasting.
  • Do not worry about VLAmax as a metric for endurance training; it conflates sprint power with glycolytic strength and the proposed trade-off with FTP is not mechanistically sound.
  • Both sprint power (~2,000W) and high FTP (~400W) can coexist in the same athlete — training for one does not inherently diminish the other.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Carnitine limitation of fat oxidation at high intensities is intensity-dependent: at moderate intensities, adequate free carnitine is available; limitation only becomes relevant near VO2max efforts.
  • Malonyl-CoA inhibition of CPT1 at rest means fat oxidation is genuinely lower at rest and during very light activity, but this resolves rapidly with exercise onset via AMPK activation.
  • Fat oxidation percentage at FTP depends heavily on the individual's training status and mitochondrial density — the 30–50% estimate has a wide range.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The mechanism limiting fat oxidation at high intensities is not substrate competition in the mitochondria but carnitine depletion: carnitine is sequestered as acetylcarnitine (buffering the CoA pool), leaving insufficient free carnitine for CPT1 to transport long-chain fatty acids — this is a transport bottleneck, not a metabolic one.
  • VLAmax is primarily measuring sprint power output, not a distinct glycolytic capacity; the framing that high VLAmax "costs" FTP is critiqued as having no mechanistic support.
  • Elite sprinter-climbers (e.g., 2,000W sprint + 400W FTP) contradict the VLAmax theory's trade-off prediction directly.
