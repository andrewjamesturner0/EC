Perspectives 28 Training Takes and Season Retrospective with Maeghan Easler

Perspectives 16,868 words View transcript

Referenced in

12. Racing & Team Tactics

Core Message

Megan Eastler (pro road cyclist and new Empirical Cycling coach) reviews her breakthrough season after switching from a high-intensity-heavy program to Kolie's higher-volume, lower-intensity approach. The key intervention was increasing weekly volume from ~13 hrs to ~20 hrs (sometimes 30+) and replacing multiple weekly VO2/30-30 sessions with structured endurance + single weekly hard session. She discusses stage race tactics (Redlands, Gila, Joe Martin), TT performance, and the philosophical error of coaching athletes to "not fight for the win."

Key Training Recommendations

  • Replace 2–3 weekly 30-30 sessions (a previous program's approach "to prepare for racing surges") with higher aerobic volume — going from 13 hr/week average to 20–21 hr/week average produced significantly better results across a full season
  • Limit hard sessions to one per week when building for big stage races; "three 30-30 sessions in one week is too much for me"; one hard session per week is enough
  • Long ride target: 7 hrs as a sweet spot; 8 hrs starts to feel like "a job"; 6 hrs not enough for pro-level endurance development
  • Train athletes as if they're fighting for the win — "if you don't have a FTP interval until you start working with [coach], you've missed a major training stimulus"; low-ambition training produces low-ambition results
  • For GC racing: stage 1 is often the worst day (first-time-at-race jitters, positioning errors, altitude adjustment); do not panic; the body often performs better on days 3–5 if managed well
  • In road sprints and crit sprints: when you have the fitness to be in the front group, believe in it — "if I'm in this group and I'm not getting dropped, I should probably sprint"; don't overthink it

Caveats & Qualifications

  • High-intensity prep ("preparing for surges") may make sense for a short criterium like the Highlands circuit race but is counterproductive for sustained 20-min climbs at Redlands Oak Glen or multi-day stage racing
  • Some riders tolerate low-volume/high-intensity well if they have very deep multi-year training history; Megan does not think this was her situation
  • Being in fourth GC at Gila was surprising and required a specific tactical awareness (protecting time gaps on the last day) that Megan had to learn in real time

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Megan's previous coach: "You're not going to be fighting for the win, so let's prepare for you to be pack fill." — Kolie and Megan dismiss this explicitly: training to not-lose produces not-losing results; training to win produces winning capacity
  • High volume in women is not inherently over-training; Megan averaged 21 hrs/week with a peak of 32.5 hrs and describes her year as consistent and progressively improving — dispels assumptions about women needing lower loads

Evolving Views

  • Megan's view on training changed fundamentally: from "ride hard, get faster" (multiple high-intensity sessions per week) to recognizing that aerobic volume is the missing foundation
  • As a new coach, Megan learned to read fatigue signals in others precisely because she experienced what over-intensity felt like personally: "I know how much would put me overboard"
