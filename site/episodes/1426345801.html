Watts Doc 42the Relationship Between Size and Power

Watts Doc 20,594 words View transcript

Core Message

This episode (Watts Doc #42) aims to address the power-versus-weight debate in cycling by grounding it in the biological science of allometric scaling -- how physiological characteristics (metabolic rate, VO2max, aerobic power) change with body size. The hosts begin by establishing that metabolic rate does not scale linearly with body mass: a gram of rat liver consumes ~7x more oxygen than a gram of human liver, and a "Kyle-sized pile of mice" would consume far more O2 than Kyle himself. They introduce the concept of allometric scaling exponents (e.g., basal metabolic rate scales with body mass to the power of ~2/3, as first shown by Max Rubner in 1883), rooted in the geometric fact that surface area scales as the square and volume as the cube of a linear dimension. The episode was building toward applying these principles to explain why bigger cyclists have higher absolute power but not proportionally higher watts/kg, and when watts/kg does and does not matter -- but the transcript is catastrophically corrupted before the main applied discussion.

Key Training Recommendations

  • The episode was truncated before actionable training recommendations were delivered. Based on the surviving content, the hosts were building toward the following framework:
  • Watts per kilogram becomes more important the more climbing is involved (e.g., "for something like Everesting, then it becomes super important because you spend 99% of the time climbing")
  • For flat time trials, absolute watts relative to aerodynamic drag (watts per CdA) matters more than watts per kilogram
  • Bigger riders will naturally have higher absolute power numbers, but this does not mean gaining mass (e.g., gym work to get "swole") will increase FTP -- the hosts strongly imply the relationship is about inherent body size, not deliberately added mass
  • The Coggan watts/kg classification chart (cat 5 through world class, at 5-second, 1-minute, 5-minute, and 20-minute/FTP durations) is referenced as a common but potentially misleading framework for self-assessment

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Allometric scaling means that comparing watts/kg between riders of very different sizes is inherently misleading -- a 60 kg rider and an 85 kg rider with similar speed are not necessarily equally "fit" by watts/kg; the biological expectation from allometric scaling is that the bigger rider will have lower watts/kg even at equal fitness (scaling exponent ~2/3, not 1.0)
  • The Coggan power profiling chart implicitly assumes linear scaling (watts per kg), which allometric scaling theory suggests is incorrect -- power likely scales with mass to the ~0.67 power, not the ~1.0 power that a simple watts/kg division implies
  • A gram of rat liver tissue is ~7x more metabolically active than a gram of human liver tissue -- metabolic rate per unit mass decreases systematically with body size across species, driven by surface-area-to-volume constraints on heat dissipation
