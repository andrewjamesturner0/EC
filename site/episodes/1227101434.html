Watts Doc 36 How Power Meters Make Lactate Testing Nearly Obsolete

Watts Doc 16,884 words View transcript

Core Message

This episode argues that for power meter-equipped cyclists, lactate testing adds little actionable information beyond what can be derived from power data alone, because lactate testing has historically been an attempt to measure the same thing that power meters now measure directly — the work rate above which fatigue accelerates. The episode reviews the history of lactate threshold definitions (LT1, LT2, MLSS), examines the Motter and Heck paper on the 4 mmol/L threshold, and shows through individual data that blood lactate concentration at MLSS varies from 3.0 to 5.5 mmol/L across individuals — making population averages useless for individualized prescription. The episode includes a case study of a high-level athlete (likely a national team cyclist) whose three-minute ramp test showed FTP-range lactate spiking to 16 mmol/L at only 400 W, while his actual FTP was ~370 W, illustrating how ramp-test protocol artifacts can make lactate data completely uninterpretable.

Key Training Recommendations

  • If you train with a power meter and want to identify your FTP, use power data and RPE-based training feedback rather than spending money on lactate testing — the power meter is a more direct and reliable measure of the threshold concept that lactate testing has always been attempting to approximate.
  • If you do want to use lactate testing to corroborate your threshold estimate, prefer MLSS testing (30-minute constant-power tests) over ramp-test lactate protocols — MLSS directly tests the performance criterion rather than approximating from a concentration cutoff.
  • Use your power data to identify where you think your threshold is before doing any lactate testing, then test at that specific power to confirm — "ride at 300 W and see if lactate takes off" — rather than performing a blind graded test from scratch.
  • For athletes whose lactate spikes very high during early stages of a ramp test before stabilizing, this is a sign they need more endurance training to develop mitochondrial mass in their working muscles — not an artifact to be dismissed.
  • Do not assign training zones based on fixed blood lactate concentrations (2 mmol LT1, 4 mmol LT2) without individual verification — individual MLSS concentration ranges from 3.0 to 5.5 mmol/L, and using a population average can lead to significant training errors (one consulted athlete was overtraining on rides prescribed at their "2 mmol power" from a ramp test).
  • Recognize that blood lactate concentration is affected by hydration status (dehydration falsely elevates it), so lactate testing should be done under consistent hydration conditions.
  • For coaches and self-coached athletes: the best indicators that threshold has shifted upward are decoupled RPE (same power feels easier), lower heart rate at the same power, and the athlete's own sense that they can sustain more — use these as the primary signal, then confirm with a power test.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Lactate testing remains a valid and useful tool in a lab research setting where standardization across subjects matters — the critique here is specifically about its value for individualized athlete training.
  • Three-minute step ramp tests underestimate MLSS power for most athletes because lactate has not had time to stabilize — five-minute steps come much closer to MLSS concentrations on average, but still have individual outliers.
  • The Motter and Heck paper found a population average MLSS concentration of 4.02 ± 0.7 mmol/L — but the standard deviation is nearly the size of the measurement error of many lactate meters (±0.2–0.4 mmol/L).
  • Lactate concentrations are diluted by increased blood volume that results from training — a well-trained athlete may show lower blood lactate at a given power simply because their blood volume has expanded, not because their metabolic state has changed.
  • MLSS testing itself has limitations: lactate meter error (±0.2–0.4 mmol/L, higher at high concentrations), coarse power increments (10–30 W steps can miss the true threshold), and the 1 mmol/L criterion for "steady state" may not work for athletes at the extremes of aerobic training status.
  • Kolie self-tested at what he estimated to be his FTP (190 W) and found lactate rising from 4.9 to 6.6 mmol/L over 30 minutes — violating the MLSS criterion — attributing this to his low mitochondrial mass (he rides very little due to a hip injury from being hit by a car).

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Lactate testing, historically, has been an attempt to measure human power output with a blood test — now that we can measure power directly, the blood test is almost redundant for athletes with power meters.
  • The "4 mmol/L threshold" was adopted somewhat arbitrarily: the Motter and Heck paper itself found no significant correlation between MLSS blood lactate concentration and endurance performance (FTP equivalent), then immediately defined FTP as "the running speed at 4 mmol/L" anyway — contradicting the finding it just reported.
  • Lactate is not a metabolic waste product — it is fuel. It enters mitochondria directly via a lactate transporter, is used by the heart in the Krebs cycle, is converted to glucose in the liver, and is re-used by working muscles. The classic post-workout cool-down rationale of "clearing lactate" misunderstands its role.
  • There are probably no true "lactate-producing fibers" and "lactate-consuming fibers" as separate entities — all fibers can both produce and consume lactate depending on mitochondrial density and intracellular conditions, and mitochondria now have known lactate importers that bypass the LDH equilibrium problem.
  • Critical power, despite being a performance-based measure, still does not directly measure the performance criterion it claims to (the fatigue threshold) — it approximates it, and the approximation depends on the test protocol used.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie states that through investigating the metabolism series, he concluded that MLSS is his personal favorite lactate measure — preferable to ramp-test lactate protocols — but that even MLSS falls short of his standard of working reliably for every athlete without assumptions.
  • He acknowledges a personal experimental finding: at his own estimated FTP, his lactate rose 1.7 mmol/L over 30 minutes, technically violating the MLSS criterion — leading him to articulate that the MLSS criterion may not be universal, particularly for athletes at aerobic fitness extremes.
