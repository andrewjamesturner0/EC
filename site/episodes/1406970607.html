Perspectives 21 Behind the New American 40km Itt Record with Cory Lockwood

Perspectives 19,422 words View transcript

Referenced in

11. Equipment & Technique

Core Message

This episode covers Corey Lockwood's successful attempt at the US 40km ITT national record at Moriarty, New Mexico (elevation ~6,500 ft), set November 5, 2022, with a time of 44:54:59. The conversation covers altitude acclimatization, equipment choices made within real-world budget constraints, aero testing methodology without a wind tunnel, TT pacing strategy on an out-and-back course, training structure leading to the record, and the value of VO2max work as a prerequisite to threshold development. It is one of the richest single episodes in this cluster for actionable equipment and technique detail.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Altitude acclimatization (individual variation): Lockwood needs approximately 6–7 days at altitude to feel comfortable after coming from sea level. Pattern: day 1 okay, day 2 bad, day 3 very flat, days 4–5 improving, days 6–7 good. This is based on 10–15+ years of experience training and living at 4,000–6,000 ft. At 7,000–8,000 ft the response is harder and longer. This is distinct from published averages — individual experience is the only reliable guide.
  • Aero bar selection — stiffness priority: Lockwood specifically credits the Aero Coach bars chosen for the record attempt as a "critical component" — they lock in the position so that elbow pads don't move on bumps. When the front end flexes, hip angle changes and head position shifts, making the position non-repeatable and non-confidence-inspiring. Rigid one-piece or carbon aero bars justify their cost.
  • Low-budget aero prep methodology: Without a wind tunnel, test aerodynamics by: (1) riding a consistent flat road segment, (2) changing only one variable at a time, (3) comparing average speed at matched RPE and power, (4) using auditory and tactile feedback (wind noise, pressure on the suit, head load) as a proxy for drag. Return to baseline if the change doesn't help. Build an evidence base one tweak at a time. "Rate of perceived exertion wind tunnel."
  • Pacing an out-and-back TT (negatively split with wind): Day 1 attempt at Moriarty: tailwind on the way out, produced a fast outbound split, but Lockwood was "blown up" ~10 minutes before the finish on the way back and missed the record. Day 2: near-calm outbound (slight headwind), paced more conservatively out (~390–410W avg estimated), got 10 seconds faster to the turnaround than day 1, then benefited from slight tailwind on the return and was ~10 seconds ahead of splits throughout. Lesson: knowing the course prevents over-commitment on the outbound when wind conditions are likely to reverse.
  • Moriarty course technical section: A slight rise out of the turnaround looks manageable but is deceptive at 30+ mph. Going too hard in that 5–10 minute section costs 10+ minutes of compromised pace on the far side of the knoll. Treat it as a patient section — be "fast and aero" through it rather than hammering.
  • Power at altitude for TT pacing: Estimated power: ~390–410W outbound average, ~420–440W return average (20-minute return section). Some power loss at 6,500 ft vs. sea level, but less than many expect for a well-adapted athlete.
  • Gear selection for TTs — 60T chainring: Running a 60T chainring with a 10T minimum cog allows controlled low-cadence pacing on false flats (70 RPM "diesel mode" at ~300W), avoiding the breathing spike that comes from excessive leg speed. Lockwood carries the gear deliberately into sections where he wants to recover speed without elevating heart rate. "Turning over a big diesel gear" on a false flat allows recovery while still carrying 34–35 mph.
  • Cadence and gear interaction: Lockwood typically runs ~95 RPM consistently in training. For TT pacing, he selects cadence deliberately by terrain: accelerate at higher cadence, manage speed at steady-state cadence, recover on false flats by dropping to 70–80 RPM in a big gear. The ability to select precisely 2–3 RPM differences across gears is critical for squeezing seconds out of split times.
  • Training structure for a record attempt: The months before the Moriarty record attempt included: 2 weeks completely off the bike, a gym adaptation phase (RPE-based, basic lifts, ankle mobility), then gradual build with easy riding and increasing tempo and over-under work, then heavy gym (twice per week) concurrent with longer endurance hours (25–30 hrs/week), then transition to a VO2max block (6 sessions/week, 3 double-days/week, ~40 min of VO2 stimulus per day split across 2 sessions, 3-week block). FTP work followed after the VO2 block.
  • VO2max session execution on trainer: Use the flywheel to spin up to 120 RPM first, then add load (resistance mode). Shift down the cassette to maintain 110–115 RPM for the interval duration. The last 60 seconds at maximum sustainable effort is where the adaptation stimulus peaks. For aerobic athletes, starting hard and holding high cadence is critical — dropping to lower cadence lets heart rate recover and reduces the stimulus. Target ~110–115 RPM, not 120 (Lockwood found 105 was insufficient to keep heart rate elevated without recruiting larger motor units that fatigue faster).
  • VO2max session dose for non-elite athletes: 1–2 sessions per week of 3–4 minute intervals is sufficient stimulus for most amateurs. 6 sessions/week (Lockwood's protocol) is appropriate only at his training volume and only after a properly structured aerobic base.
  • Rest after race season: Lockwood took 2 months after the record attempt before FTP testing. Upon testing, FTP was within a couple percent of peak in-season values. Top-end athletes' power numbers are within ~1% year-round; the primary adaptation from rest and rebuilding is endurance depth, not peak watts. Omitting off-season rest eliminates the "headroom" needed to absorb subsequent training load.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Altitude adaptation time is highly individual. 6–7 days worked for Lockwood because of 10–15 years at altitude. Athletes without this background may need significantly longer.
  • Power meter on Lockwood's TT bike was unreliable — all power figures for the record attempt are estimates based on training data and perceived effort, not direct measurement. This is explicitly flagged.
  • 60T chainring and 10T minimum cog produces extreme chain angle on the smallest sprockets — "chain bind" is a real concern. The tradeoff is accepted because the Lockwood rarely uses those gears.
  • VO2max block intensity is "really hard" — the word "savage" is used. Athletes not properly prepared (full aerobic base, strength base, proper rest period) will not respond to this block — they will simply accumulate fatigue.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • An out-and-back TT is not boring — it consists of 15–20 distinct tactical sections each requiring different power, cadence, body position, and strategy. Athletes who don't break down the course this way are leaving significant time available.
  • First-day record attempts often fail not from insufficient fitness but from insufficient course knowledge — specifically, not knowing which sections allow over-commitment and which sections demand patience.
  • Riding too hard in endurance training ("muddying" the zones) is one of the primary training errors even experienced athletes make. It prevents FTP development because high-intensity work can't be executed from a fatigued baseline. The training effect becomes "steady state and tempo" with a ceiling that never rises.
  • VO2max work can be genuinely difficult for aerobic specialists — because their cardiovascular system recovers so fast, they must spin at 110+ RPM to prevent the heart rate from dropping mid-interval. Sprinters have the opposite problem (too much muscle recruitment, blow up immediately).

Evolving Views

  • Lockwood explicitly identifies the "muddy training" pattern — riding endurance too hard, doing too many interval sessions per week (3–5), and not periodizing gym work — as mistakes he made for years before working with Moore. This is a changed view based on accumulated experience.
  • He also notes that he used to do FTP intervals as 4x8 min and "a bazillion" 10-minute efforts rather than a single 60-minute effort. The insight: "if you want to ride your bike for 60 minutes, go ride for 60 minutes." Moving from 4x8 to continuous TTE-style threshold work was a major change.
