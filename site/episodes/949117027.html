Ten Minute Tips 10 How to Read a Scientific Paper

13. Research & Methodology

Core Message

Kyle Helson walks through a practical framework for reading scientific papers, aimed at cyclists and coaches who want to engage directly with the research discussed on the podcast. The episode stresses that paper-reading is a learnable, practiced skill — not innate ability — and advocates an iterative rather than linear reading process. The four core goals when digesting a paper are: (1) understand the problem being studied; (2) understand the methods and metrics used; (3) understand the meaning of the results; and (4) situate the findings within the larger physiological or training context. Critical evaluation of methods, figures, and conclusions is emphasized throughout, including the right to disagree with authors.

Key Training Recommendations

  • When picking up a new paper, start by identifying the authors — recognizing a researcher's specialty helps frame what physiological domain the paper will address before you read a word.
  • Read the abstract and the opening of the introduction first to gauge whether your background knowledge is sufficient; if the first few sentences are incomprehensible, stop and find referenced prior papers to build foundational knowledge.
  • Evaluate the methods/analysis section critically: ask whether the metric or tool used to measure the outcome is actually appropriate for the stated goal (e.g., a 1-minute all-out test is a poor proxy for FTP; a 20-minute FTP test does not assess 1-minute power physiology).
  • When reading figures, first identify axis variables and units, consult the legend, predict what you expect to see based on the text, then assess whether the plotted data actually supports the authors' conclusions — do not accept a best-fit line uncritically.
  • Read figure captions carefully — they often contain new information or clarifications not present in the main text.
  • In the conclusion/discussion section, actively decide whether you agree or disagree with the authors' interpretation based on the data presented; it is acceptable and scientifically appropriate to disagree.
  • Pay attention to sample size: a study of six recreationally active men warrants much less confidence in its conclusions than a larger, more diverse study.
  • Practice reading papers iteratively — re-read sections, go back after reading later sections that recontextualize earlier material, and look up unfamiliar terms via cited references.
  • Practice explaining the paper to a non-expert (e.g., a friend or family member) as a method to test and deepen your own comprehension — this is a standard academic technique used in journal clubs.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • Reading scientific papers is not a linear process from start to finish; treat it as iterative and expect to re-read sections multiple times.
  • Building paper-reading skill can become a rabbit hole — following citations backward can require reading many prior papers; try to skim or selectively read referenced works rather than reading them exhaustively.
  • If a paper is a follow-up to a prior study, compare its methods to the original to understand how the protocol has changed and what assumptions carry over.
  • Theory sections require more time if you are unfamiliar with the subfield; math-heavy content typically appears here.
  • Authors can manipulate data presentation — "best fit lines can be slapped on all sorts of random data points" and data can be massaged to support a desired conclusion; critical reading is the reader's defense.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Scientific papers are "meant to be questioned or investigated further or have follow-up studies done" — published does not mean correct or final.
  • The methods section, not the conclusion, is the most important place to assess whether a paper's findings should be trusted; a flawed measurement tool invalidates otherwise interesting results.
  • Small sample size alone is sufficient reason to substantially discount a paper's conclusions, regardless of statistical significance.
