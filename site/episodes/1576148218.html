Perspectives 26 the Cost of Change with Kathryn Bertine

Perspectives 22,564 words View transcript

Referenced in

9. Psychology & Performance

NOTE: This episode is primarily about women's cycling advocacy and is only tangentially related to performance psychology or coaching. Kathryn Bertine is an author and activist known for co-founding the Le Tour Entier campaign that led to the creation of La Course by Le Tour de France, and for her book "Stand" about systemic corruption in cycling governance. The majority of the episode concerns institutional change, publishing challenges, legal challenges to ASO and UCI, and the personal costs of being a whistleblower. The psychology-relevant portions are extracted below.

Core Message

Bertine describes cycling as "a sport about managing disappointment" — a framing attributed to the former coach of Kolie (a guest referenced in the episode). High-level athletic advocacy requires the same psychological tools as high-level athletic performance: tolerating repeated rejection, maintaining self-belief in the absence of external validation, and reconceptualizing opponents from one-dimensional enemies into three-dimensional people with understandable (if wrong) motivations. She argues that including yourself as a contender — deciding to show up and be counted even without evidence that you will succeed — is itself an act of psychological training that makes performance possible.

Key Training Recommendations

1. "Include yourself as a contender" — before you have evidence that you belong at a level, you have to act as if you do, because the evidence only comes from having acted. This applies both to race entry decisions and to any performance goal where self-doubt precedes action. 2. Reconceptualize intimidating competitors or obstacles as three-dimensional humans: ask what pressures they are operating under, what their actual constraints are, what they fear. This cognitive reframe reduces the paralyzing effect of opposition and opens up strategic thinking. 3. Treat rejection as data rather than verdict — Bertine describes receiving 56 rejections from publishers before "Stand" was published. In athletic contexts: a DNF, a dropped selection, or a poor result is information about a specific day under specific conditions, not a definitive statement about potential.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The training-relevant content in this episode is limited; the primary content is women's cycling advocacy and institutional reform. Recommendations above are inferred from Bertine's personal narrative rather than explicit coaching prescriptions.
  • Bertine is not a sports psychologist; her framework is based on personal experience as an athlete-activist.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The instinct to "know your enemy" by reducing opponents to their worst attributes is tactically inferior to the instinct to understand them as people navigating complex incentives — not because it is morally better, but because it produces better strategy.
  • Cycling, by its structure (weather, tactics, mechanicals, illness, competing on others' schedules), is inherently a sport that produces more disappointment than satisfaction even for elite performers. Psychological resilience in cycling is not about avoiding disappointment but about developing a specific relationship to it.

Evolving Views

  • Bertine describes her view of institutional change having shifted: she initially believed that documenting injustice would produce change through rational persuasion. After years of advocacy she came to understand that institutions change primarily through sustained pressure, coalition-building, and economic threat, not through the quality of the argument.
