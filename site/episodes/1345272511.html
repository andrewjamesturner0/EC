3. Threshold, FTP & Performance Testing

Core Message

Andy Coggan (exercise physiologist, co-developer of FTP, TSS, CTL, and WKO power-duration modelling; researcher at IUPUI) discusses the origins of FTP as a pedagogical construct, the nature of lactate threshold, VO2max training, the power-duration relationship and TTE, VLaMax, and training model quality. The central argument is that FTP is not "60-minute power" but an overarching concept representing the maximal metabolic steady state — a fuzzy physiological boundary that exists regardless of what label is applied. Critically, Coggan argues that there is no single superior method for training VO2max; the classic Hickson 6×5-minute protocol (the study with the highest ever documented VO2max increase, 44%) remains valid, and ISO-power to near-failure is the operative principle. He also argues that the TTE parameter in WKO is primarily a model-fitting parameter, not a reliable physiological estimate, and that training models (including Bannister impulse-response) are limited by model structure, not input quality.

Key Training Recommendations

  • For VO2max development, use ISO-power 5-minute intervals; the benchmark is that you should crack (fail to complete) the last interval approximately half the time — if you always finish all six, the intensity is too low. The Hickson protocol (6×5 minutes, 3 days/week) produced the highest documented VO2max increase (44%) and remains a valid reference.
  • Allow RPE to guide VO2max session intensity rather than a fixed percentage of FTP; "if it feels hard, it is hard" — perceived exertion is the athlete's own subjective experience and cannot be questioned by theory.
  • Collect session RPE (Foster 10-point scale) routinely and compare it to power output; unexpectedly high RPE for a given power, or unexpectedly low power for a given RPE, is an early warning signal to investigate off-bike factors (sleep, life stress, illness).
  • Train for performance and let substrate utilisation sort itself out; do not attempt to engineer fat oxidation by restricting carbohydrate unless preparing specifically for a competition held under glycogen-depletion conditions (which Coggan notes do not exist in real cycling).
  • When prescribing endurance work, think in terms of "glycogen budget" — approximately 2 hours per day of moderately hard training is approximately the maximum that can be fully replenished overnight on a high-carbohydrate diet; plan training loads accordingly.
  • Elite endurance athletes empirically train 15–25 hours per week; training less than ~15 hours/week likely leaves adaptation gains on the table even at high intensity, whereas beyond 25 hours/week the cost-benefit reverses.
  • For strength training, working to near-failure matters more than the specific rep range; trained individuals get comparable strength and hypertrophy gains lifting 30–80% of 1RM to failure.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • TTE as reported in WKO4/5 is primarily a model-fitting parameter, not a reliable estimate of true physiological time to exhaustion at MLSS; it is strongly influenced by which test durations are in the athlete's power profile (e.g., if the athlete only has 20-minute tests, WKO may report TTE just beyond 20 minutes).
  • VO2max training at intensities just below the true maximum is qualitatively indistinguishable from well-executed threshold work in terms of acute fatigue; going only 25 W below VO2max ceiling allows five full intervals in one hour with no unusual fatigue. A truly maximal effort leaves the athlete flu-like for hours.
  • FTP is not fixed at 60 minutes; it is approximately (~) 40 km time-trial power or ~1-hour power; individual variation around that central tendency is large.
  • VLaMax (maximal glycolytic rate) as modelled in some commercial systems is almost certainly not measurable at physiologically meaningful exercise intensities in large muscle-mass whole-body exercise; the maximum enzymatic capacity of glycolysis is so high that a ~20,000 W sprint would be needed to saturate it.
  • Threshold as a percentage of VO2max rises with age: a healthy young cyclist might have maximal lactate steady state at ~75% VO2max, whereas an older trained cyclist might be at ~85% VO2max, meaning less reserve above threshold and a reduced ability to respond to attacks.
  • Correlation studies showing MLSS ≈ CP statistically do not prove the two are physiologically identical; statistical non-significance reflects insufficient power or overlapping confidence intervals, not equivalence.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • FTP is arguably more "real" than critical power, because CP is itself an operationalisation of the same concept (maximal metabolic steady state) and depends heavily on which test durations and which mathematical model are used. FTP is the overarching umbrella under which CP, MLSS, ventilatory threshold, and other constructs all sit.
  • Lactate threshold is not a true threshold in the physiological sense; blood lactate rises exponentially, not in a piecewise step function. The appearance of a "threshold" is a mental convenience. Similarly, LT1 and LT2 as distinct points are not supported by the underlying physiology; "threshold" is better understood as a continuum concept.
  • Fat max is not a useful training target; fat oxidation is highly correlated with threshold, and once you know threshold you know roughly where fat max is. There is no reason to measure fat max separately to prescribe training, and doing so is primarily a revenue-generating activity for labs.
  • Training to increase fat oxidation by restricting carbohydrate does not improve performance; Coggan explicitly endorses the view that you need to supply maximal carbohydrate to maximise training quality, and training quality is the dominant driver of adaptation.
  • The WKO adaptation chart (which Coggan constructed) suggested that high-intensity exercise, not long slow distance, is the superior stimulus for capillarisation (greater shear stress, greater VEGF release); a meta-analysis subsequently confirmed this. The idea that you must do only long slow distance in winter to protect "fragile nascent capillaries" is a myth.
  • Despite thousands of genes being influenced by different exercise intensities, all adaptations ultimately do one of two things: increase maximal force/power, or improve fatigue resistance. Training is therefore far simpler than it is often presented.
  • Tapering improves endurance performance by only 1–5%, with the lower end of that range for longer events (40 km TT, gran fondos). The taper effect is larger for neuromuscular power events than for pure endurance events.

Evolving Views

  • Coggan previously advocated bimodal/polarised VO2max training as part of his "60 for 60" self-experiment (attempting VO2max ≥ 60 mL/kg/min at age 60); a crash in 2014 and a job change in 2016 interrupted that experiment before completion.
  • Coggan notes he initially chose not to expose TTE in the WKO4 interface because he distrusted it as a reliable physiological measure; the decision to display it was made by others at TrainingPeaks, and he remains cautious about placing too much interpretive weight on the number.
