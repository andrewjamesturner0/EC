4. Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

Core Message

This milestone episode addresses training for time-crunched athletes and offers a frank assessment: at around 4–6 hours per week of training, athletes will plateau relatively quickly, and intensity cannot fully substitute for volume over the long term. Kolie states that approximately 14–15 hours per week is the minimum he has observed for year-on-year FTP improvements. The episode covers how to structure limited training time, the role of TSS, the primacy of life stress management, and how to progress threshold and VO2max work within a tight schedule. It also addresses the "two-a-day" approach and the sleep vs. training trade-off.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Manage life stress first: training hard on top of high psychological stress produces poor quality efforts and may not generate the peripheral adaptive signals (calcium flux, NADH/ATP stress, etc.) needed for adaptation.
  • At low volumes (4–6 h/week), more is always more: going from 4 to 6 hours is a 50% increase in stimulus. At these volumes, adding even one endurance ride per week (if easy enough) matters greatly.
  • Two hard sessions per week (7–8/10 RPE or harder) is the right starting point for most time-crunched athletes; sometimes 3, rarely 4.
  • A hard day better than a hard day: favor 2–3 hard sessions with full recovery days over 5 moderate sessions where you never fully recover.
  • For threshold work (sweet spot to FTP), add time-in-zone gradually: e.g., start with 2x20, progress to 2x22.5, 2x25, then consolidate toward longer single intervals. Time-in-zone must be accumulated within a single ride, not split across morning and afternoon sessions.
  • For VO2max work, the key is intensity not duration: aim for 15–20 total interval minutes per session, ensuring you are "gasping like a fish." Individual intervals: 2 minutes minimum, up to 5–6 minutes max for most athletes.
  • Do not pre-plan your training distribution (80/20, etc.) — ask instead whether the stimulus is working and whether you're recovering from it.
  • RPE decoupling is a key indicator of off-bike stress or inadequate recovery: if a normally-manageable 2x20 threshold workout feels like grinding through at a 9/10, back off.
  • Sleep: 98–99.5% of recovery is eating and sleeping. Do not consistently sacrifice sleep for extra training time; a sleep deficit compounds faster than expected.
  • Two-a-day workouts are a valid way to increase volume when single long rides are impossible.
  • One 4–5 hour ride per week is sufficient to keep most athletes progressing in endurance fitness, even if weekday rides are short.
  • Avoid "makeup" workouts: if you missed Tuesday's VO2max session, do not try to stack it on top of Saturday's planned workout.
  • If recovery from a hard ride takes more than 2–3 days at your current low training volume, your body is not adapted to volume and a single hard effort is disproportionately depleting — a sign to build general volume first.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The 14–15 hours/week threshold for year-on-year improvement is a coaching observation, not a universal law; genetics and prior training history affect this substantially.
  • Athletes who have trained at high volume previously can maintain near-permanent aerobic adaptations on much lower volume; the "minimum dose" to maintain past fitness is much smaller than the dose required to build new fitness.
  • Athletes with high genetic endowment may compete at high levels on low volume — do not use their results as normative expectations.
  • TSS is a useful rough tool but creates false equivalences: two 30-second maximal sprints will normalize to a high TSS but are not equivalent to an hour at FTP in aerobic terms. Weekly intensity factor optimization is a path toward overtraining.
  • Zone 2 is best understood as "the hardest pace that still feels easy" — the actual wattage is secondary. Athletes should not peg it at the top of zone 2 by the Coggan table. Even riding 20–30 W below what they think is their LT1 is fine; there is no observed adaptation difference.
  • Sweet spot cannot fully substitute for long rides in race preparation for events like Unbound 200 or similar ultra-endurance events.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • At low training volumes, higher-intensity training taps out faster than people expect (the "ryanodyne receptor fragmentation" effect that produces calcium leak and drives adaptation in novices is absent in well-trained athletes — they must "do it the old-fashioned way and just ride a lot").
  • People who are low-volume often recover from hard efforts more slowly than people doing 20+ hours/week — higher chronic training load improves the body's capacity to absorb subsequent stress.
  • Athletes in excellent aerobic condition frequently feel post-maximal-effort like they "could have gone harder" — this is a sign of good aerobic fitness, not under-effort.
  • When zone 2 is prescribed at 2 mmol/L blood lactate (a common LT1 proxy), many well-trained athletes end up riding at low sweet spot intensities, making every "endurance" ride functionally a tempo session — this is a common setup for overreaching.
  • The LT1 / zone 2 transition does not have a power-curve fingerprint — Andy Coggan designed the zone as a descriptive range, not a prescriptive target, to cover where most athletes' genuine endurance pace would fall.

Evolving Views

  • Kolie acknowledges that "volume is more and more the key driver of continuous aerobic adaptation" with each year he coaches, updating from an earlier emphasis primarily on interval quality.
  • Kolie notes that his views on base training for low-volume athletes are tentative and may "easily change week to year."
