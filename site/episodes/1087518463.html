4. Training Philosophy & Intensity Distribution

Core Message

This AMA covers the mechanics of several training constructs — over-unders, fat metabolism at threshold, training older athletes, anaerobic repeatability, and intensity distribution — while returning repeatedly to the core theme that training distribution should emerge from physiological principles and individual need rather than from preset schemas like 80/20. A notable segment addresses whether and how over-unders work, with a practical prescription for low-cadence overs as a method for recruiting larger motor units. Kolie also provides a concise framework on why even short track events are primarily aerobic.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Over-unders structure: start with 30 seconds over FTP (20–30W above), then 4.5 minutes under (at tempo watts). Do not make the overs and unders symmetrical in duration. The "under" portion should allow genuine recovery; if you are "hanging on by the skin of your teeth" during the under, the prescription is too hard. Total time over threshold across a 1–2 hour race is typically no more than ~25 minutes; over-under prescriptions should respect this scale.
  • Low-cadence over-unders: do the "over" portion at low cadence (obligate recruitment of larger motor units), limit overs to 10–60 seconds, then do unders at normal cadence for recovery. This is an advanced technique — useful primarily for athletes who specifically lack large motor unit oxidative capacity. Start with 2 sets, work up to 4.
  • Anaerobic repeatability workout: 20–30 second max-effort seated efforts, rest 2–3 minutes, 4–6 repetitions. Start with 2 sets, build to 4. For longer anaerobic capacity work: 3-minute on / 3-minute off or 2-minute on / 4-minute off. Key indicator of need: poor repeatability of VO2 max and higher efforts despite high volume likely indicates insufficient work in the larger motor units.
  • FTP work when TTE is short: if you specifically do not want to extend TTE (e.g., for 5–20 minute events), keep doing long rides for aerobic stimulus, prioritize VO2 max work, and do some FTP maintenance (e.g., 2×20) without continually extending time in zone.
  • Once TTE at FTP reaches ~60 minutes or more, shift to VO2 max work — this is a reliable time to push FTP upward.
  • Fasted training: do not combine fasted riding with FTP intervals. Fasted rides are a "strong spice" with some benefit for fat adaptation; limit to once or twice per week maximum, never for high-intensity sessions. When doing fasted rides, use tempo-type (not threshold) intensity. Do not go isocalorically deficient — maintain total caloric intake with fat and protein.
  • For improving FTP when FTP ≈ 80–82% of VO2 max watts: do VO2 max intervals, not more FTP work.
  • Training intensity distribution: do not target a preset distribution (e.g., 80/20, pyramidal). When training goes well, the distribution that emerges often looks pyramidal on inspection, but that was not the design target. Focus on physiological systems, not percentages.
  • 5–10 minute track events are still highly aerobic. Even track endurance riders must develop strong aerobic bases. Anaerobic and sprint capacity matters too, but the aerobic system supplies the dominant portion of energy. Newer track categories run shorter races but as you advance, events lengthen dramatically (40km points races equivalent to a criterium in duration and demand).
  • Older athletes (declining VO2 max): incorporate weight training for bone health and muscle retention, but don't assume switching to "anaerobic focus" will be more productive — individual potential varies.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • 80/20 training is a tool, not a law; Kolie does not calculate distributions — he responds to recovery rates and adaptation signals.
  • Fasted rides are primarily appropriate for athletes who have already maximized training volume; for most athletes, just riding more is the more effective intervention.
  • Eating more does NOT interrupt aerobic adaptation. Eating during FTP intervals enables longer intervals and more total stimulus, which produces better adaptation than fasted shorter intervals.
  • Training repeatability metrics: compare the same interval set over time (not just races); variability index (normalized power ÷ average power) of >1.2 indicates stochastic race-like demands where repeatability matters.
  • Whether sweet spot counts as a "high-intensity" workout depends on whether it is being used in a building context (yes, it counts) or as maintenance (no, it may not need to count against the weekly hard-workout budget).

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Eating food does not blunt aerobic adaptations — this directly contradicts a popular "train-low" hypothesis. Longer intervals with food produce more total adaptation than shorter fasted intervals.
  • Fat burning high-fat diet claim rebutted: increased fat oxidation on a high-fat diet comes primarily from ingested dietary fat, not mobilized adipose stores. Actual endogenous fat mobilization remains approximately the same; caloric deficit (not dietary fat composition) drives adipose fat loss.
  • For repeatability deficits: the cause is likely insufficient work in larger motor units (not lack of anaerobic capacity per se). The prescription is specific repeatability intervals, not more VO2 max volume.

Evolving Views

  • Low-cadence over-unders: Kolie used them heavily for several years with many athletes and has since moved away from them as a default — they work for some athletes in specific circumstances but are not universally warranted.
