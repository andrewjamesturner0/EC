Ten Minute Tips 7 Breathing for Heavy Lifting

Ten Minute Tips 1,604 words View transcript

Referenced in

7. Strength Training

Core Message

Kyle Helson explains the Valsalva maneuver — inhaling a deep belly breath and bracing the core before each rep — as the correct breathing technique for heavy lifting. This creates intra-abdominal pressure that stabilizes the spine and prevents back injuries during squats, deadlifts, and similar compound movements. The technique should be performed per-rep (inhale, brace, lift, exhale), not held across multiple reps. Critics' concerns about blood pressure spikes are acknowledged but largely dismissed for typical training loads where reps take only 1-2 seconds.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Use the Valsalva maneuver (deep belly breath + full core brace) before each heavy rep
  • Breathe with the abdomen, not the shoulders — expand abs, obliques, and low back to create a "cylinder of tension"
  • Exhale and re-breathe between reps; do not hold breath across multiple reps
  • The "inhale on descent, exhale on ascent" cue is acceptable for submaximal warm-up sets but does not produce maximal core stability
  • Practice the bracing technique at home before using it under load — it is a skill that improves with practice
  • Most cyclists never need to attempt true 1RM efforts, so blood pressure concerns from the Valsalva are minimal in practice

Caveats & Qualifications

  • People with hypertension or history of hypertension should consult a medical professional before strength training
  • The hosts explicitly state they are not medical doctors and this is not medical advice
  • Holding the brace for many seconds (prolonged grinding reps) can raise blood pressure via sympathetic nervous system compensation

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • The common advice to "never hold your breath while lifting" is misleading — proper per-rep bracing actually prevents injury by stabilizing the spine
  • The Valsalva initially decreases blood pressure and heart rate before the sympathetic compensation kicks in
