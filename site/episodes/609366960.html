The Watts Doc 5 Can You Lift Heavy While Endurance Training

Watts Doc 6,701 words View transcript

Referenced in

7. Strength Training

Core Message

Heavy strength training and endurance training activate competing molecular pathways (mTOR for muscle growth vs. PGC-1α for aerobic adaptation), and the aerobic pathway tends to take precedence when both are trained simultaneously — a phenomenon known as the "interference effect." The classic Hickson (1980) study demonstrated that concurrent trainees matched strength-only gains through week 6–7 before plateauing and declining, while aerobic gains were unaffected. The practical upshot is that the order, volume, and timing of strength vs. endurance sessions can be strategically managed to favor whichever adaptation is the priority. The host (Colby Moore) shares that in his own coaching he typically avoids heavy lifting in-season for endurance cyclists, while acknowledging year-round lifting can be justified for sprinters and off-road disciplines. Diet — specifically leucine intake distributed across the day — can partially substitute for the lifting stimulus to maintain muscle mass during the competitive season.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Assign heavy strength training (up to 2x/week) during the off-season for endurance athletes; build up to heavy loads before transitioning away from lifting as the race season approaches.
  • For sprinters and power-dependent disciplines (cyclocross, MTB, track), maintain in-season lifting to preserve the neural drive (motor unit recruitment) needed for high-force production, since road racing alone does not provide sufficient high-force stimulus.
  • If you want to suppress muscle growth (avoid gaining mass) while still training in the gym, perform 15 minutes of aerobic work at ~70% VO2max (approximately sweet-spot/tempo) immediately after lifting — this shuts down mTOR signaling.
  • To maintain muscle mass without lifting, consume leucine-rich foods (whey protein, yogurt, milk, cheese) — a single scoop of whey protein is sufficient and more practical than BCAA supplements.
  • Distribute protein intake in ~20 g servings throughout the day rather than concentrating it in one large meal; the body cannot efficiently use very large single boluses and excess nitrogen is excreted.
  • If training load is too high to generate a quality strength stimulus, dedicate a block of the off-season exclusively to strength work rather than trying to force concurrent training year-round.
  • Do not attempt heavy lifting sessions if cumulative fatigue prevents you from reaching ~80% of your 1RM — the strength stimulus is lost, making the session counterproductive.

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The interference effect is dose-dependent: four days per week at ≥80% VO2max (roughly FTP intensity) is sufficient to fully inhibit mTOR and block strength gains; lighter or less frequent aerobic work may allow concurrent adaptation.
  • The strength losses seen in-season are largely neural (the brain loses practice recruiting muscle fibers at maximal effort), not myofibrillar — muscle mass is largely preserved, and strength typically returns quickly after resuming lifting.
  • The fiber-type locking effect (heavy lifting alone without endurance work causes a slow shift toward fast-twitch fiber types) is based on limited long-term data; the host flags this as mechanistically plausible but not definitively proven.
  • The recommendation to do aerobic work before lifting (so that the endurance session is not compromised) works against maintaining a strong strength stimulus; choose order based on which adaptation is the higher priority at that point in the season.
  • The host's observation that athletes' aerobic performances improve rapidly when heavy lifting is removed near race season is based on coaching experience, not controlled data — he explicitly labels it as anecdotal.
  • High-intensity interval training (e.g., 10×6-second maximal sprints) also inhibits mTOR, meaning even short sprint work can suppress the strength stimulus.

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Doing as little as 15 minutes of moderate aerobic work (≈70% VO2max) immediately after lifting is sufficient to shut down mTOR signaling entirely — meaning a brief cooldown ride after a gym session can negate the muscle-building stimulus of that session.
  • Early strength gains in brand-new lifters are primarily neural, not hypertrophic — beginners can rapidly increase load on the bar without meaningfully growing muscle fibers in the first several weeks.
  • Concurrent training (strength + endurance simultaneously) can actually cause both type I and type II muscle fibers to grow due to the combined mechanical and metabolic demand — whereas strength training alone only grows type II fibers. This is a mechanism for hypertrophy without a pure strength stimulus.
  • Heavy lifting year-round without endurance training will gradually shift fiber types toward fast-twitch, effectively "locking in" a sprinter's profile — this can be deliberately exploited by sprinters who want to preserve explosive characteristics.
  • Aerobic training takes evolutionary precedence over strength signaling at the molecular level; when both pathways are activated, PGC-1α suppresses mTOR — endurance adaptation wins by default.
