7. Strength Training

Core Message

Kolie Moore interviews strength and conditioning coaches RJ Boergers and Angelo Gingerelli about how endurance athletes should integrate strength training. The discussion reveals significant disagreement about whether strength training directly improves cycling performance (FTP), with the guests acknowledging they lack conviction that gym strength translates to more power on the pedal. There is broad agreement that strength training's primary value for cyclists is injury prevention, maintaining body alignment and posture, core stability, and reducing kinetic leak -- not directly boosting aerobic metrics.

Key Training Recommendations

  • Keep gym sessions to approximately 40-45 minutes maximum
  • Strength train at least 2 times per week, up to 3-4 depending on schedule
  • When doing two-a-days, do endurance training first when the CNS is fresh; plug strength sessions around cycling
  • In the off-season, prioritize strength training and do endurance work second; during the season, always prioritize cycling
  • For loading: pick a weight where reps 8, 9, and 10 of a set of 10 are "pretty challenging"
  • When tapering endurance volume by 20%, taper the weight room by 20% as well rather than cutting to zero
  • Use RPE/feel for loading rather than percentage-based schemes
  • Introduce new exercises during the off-season to avoid adding soreness from novel stimulus on top of high-volume endurance training
  • Prioritize anti-rotation core exercises (pushup position shoulder taps, plate drags in plank position, banded Pallof press variations) over traditional crunches and planks
  • Include anti-rotation exercises in every workout session
  • Focus on getting "strong enough" with good mobility rather than chasing weight room PRs
  • Allow approximately 2 days of recovery after zone 5 efforts before heavy lifting

Caveats & Qualifications

  • The guests admit: "Will it translate to more power to the pedal? I don't know. And I think we'd be crazy to say that we have some kind of absolute conviction that it does."
  • The research on strength training improving cycling performance is described as "about a 50-50 mix" in the studies
  • The concurrent training effect (Hickson study) shows that after ~7 weeks of combined endurance and strength training, strength gains begin to taper off due to molecular interference
  • The book was written more for a general audience (triathletes, fondo riders) than competitive road racers
  • Editors pushed the authors to make stronger claims than the science supports

Contrarian or Non-Obvious Claims

  • Kolie (the host) recommends people cut out strength training as they get deeper into competitive season, which is the opposite of what the guests advocate
  • Having a six-pack does not mean you have a strong core for cycling -- you can look strong but be functionally weak
  • The concurrent training effect means that strength gains will plateau or reverse after ~7 weeks of concurrent training, undermining the case for heavy in-season lifting
  • Kolie states from personal experience: "I can tell you for a certainty, just weightlifting does not improve your FTP"

Evolving Views

  • Kolie mentions that he used to program more exercises per session but has since moved to fewer exercises, currently starting at about 4 heavy sets and building to about 7
